A Rapidly Shifting Optical Investment Landscape

Two weeks ago I published the 22-company vertical integration matrix for the optical stack. Seven layers, six integration archetypes, and one central thesis.

Vertical integration depth and stock returns move in an inverse relationship.

4-position-deep names like Sumitomo, Nokia, and Cisco averaged +78%, while some 2-position pure-plays ran +900% to +1,700%. Conglomerate discount, size beta, lack of a discovery premium, and cycle timing all stacked up, creating a paradox where the deepest integration produced the lowest returns.

That was the core of v1.0. Group the 22 companies into six integration archetypes, lay out one-year returns side by side, and the pattern was unmistakable at the category level.

Two weeks have passed since that piece went out. The optical sector recently went through a correction and is now showing early signs of re-acceleration. At the same time, several names moved +80% to +300% in a single month. The reason for reopening the 22-company matrix at this point is clear: before the next leg up begins, you need to determine which seats will run again and which have already played out. For a precise read, I re-priced each name individually, category by category, not as averages.

Here is what the one-month returns look like when grouped by category.

Returns are based on April 30, 2026 closing prices, trailing one-month price return. For names with both ADR and local listings, the ticker used in the original article is the basis. Dividends and FX effects are excluded.

Three things jump out immediately.

First, the Downstream IC-to-Photonics group topped the board at +73% average, overtaking Upstream Natives (+46%) for the first time. At the v1.0 snapshot, Upstream Natives dominated at +855% on a one-year average. Compress the window to one month and the order flips.

Second, within the 4-pos Deep Conglomerates, Nokia was the only one that moved. Sumitomo -5%, Cisco +17%, Nokia +56%. Two peers barely budged while Nokia alone matched Downstream-tier returns.

Third, dispersion inside the Materials-Device group is extreme. IPG at +3% versus Sivers at +327%, nearly a 100x gap. Sharing an archetype does not mean moving together.

The question for this piece is simple.

Within the optical map’s 22 names, who outperformed and who didn’t? What does that divergence tell us? And so, within those 22, who do you keep holding, who do you let go, and who do you pick up fresh?

I start by checking where the v1.0 thesis held and where it broke. Then I re-sort all 22 names into four buckets: positions worth holding, positions to hold with caution, positions to trim or avoid entering, and next candidates the market hasn’t fully priced. This is the exercise of turning v1.0’s static map into an actionable decision table.

v1.0 Thesis Validation: What Was Right and What Was Wrong

The Main Thesis: Categories Held, Intra-Category Didn’t

“Vertical integration depth and stock returns move inversely.” At the category-average level, this still works. 4-pos +23%, 3-pos Upstream +46%, 3-pos Downstream +73%, 2-pos Materials-Device +96%. The big picture holds.

Where it broke is inside the categories.

Within 4-pos, only Nokia moved. Within Materials-Device, Sivers and IPG diverged by roughly 100x. Category averages remain meaningful, but which chokepoint a company controls turned out to be the bigger variable at the stock level.

For stock-level decisions, chokepoint ownership, end-market exposure, and narrative fit rank higher than category classification.

Scenario 1 (AI Datacenter Optical Demand Acceleration = Vertical Integration Advantage): Only Nokia Got Priced In

v1.0 grouped Cisco, Nokia, and Sumitomo as beneficiaries of deep vertical integration. One month of data priced in only Nokia. Looking at what separated the three, the market rewarded the company that satisfied three conditions simultaneously.

Optical revenue share has to be meaningful. A Western alternative narrative has to work. And the integration story has to feel fresh.

Nokia checked all three.

Nokia’s rally was driven at its core by accelerating AI datacenter optical demand centered on pluggables and line systems. Q1 optical revenue +20%, AI/cloud revenue +49%, AI/cloud new orders €1B in a single quarter. Network Infrastructure guidance nearly doubled from 6-8% to 12-14%, Optical+IP 18-20%, AI/Cloud TAM CAGR raised to 27%, comparable operating profit +54% (consensus beat by 11%). Infinera synergy €200M for 2026 on track. Nokia used OFC 2026 to push hard on its end-to-end optical stack, from components and pluggables to photonics, systems, and software, built on top of the Infinera acquisition.

That positioning, distinct from Cisco or Sumitomo, is the core of why it got re-rated as an AI optical vendor. The Infinera acquisition is at the one-year mark, so integration results are just starting to materialize.

Sumitomo’s automotive wire harness business accounts for over 50% of revenue, leaving optical exposure too thin. Cisco is locked into the market’s perception as an enterprise networking conglomerate, making an optical vendor reframing difficult. That said, Cisco warrants separate analysis. AI infrastructure orders accelerated from $800M in Q4 FY25 to $1.3B in Q1 FY26 to $2.1B in Q2 FY26, and the Acacia division also showed strong booking growth. That kind of momentum doesn’t get captured by category classification alone. More on this in the stock-level review below.

Scenario 3 (Optical I/O and M&A Acceleration): Pulled Forward by 12-18 Months, and the Pattern Cuts Across Categories

v1.0 placed Scenario 3 at “2027-2028 for mainstream adoption.” The market started pulling that timeline forward. The most explicit trigger was Marvell’s acquisition of Polariton Technologies on April 22, just five days after v1.0 went live.

Polariton is a Swiss company with IP in plasmonics-based silicon photonics modulators. The technology combines plasmonic active devices on top of silicon photonics, enabling modulation up to the THz regime.

Marvell’s stated purpose for the acquisition: scaling optical interconnects to “3.2T and beyond.” Through 1.6T, existing silicon photonics MZMs hold up, but at 3.2T and above, bandwidth, density, and energy-per-bit scaling get significantly harder. That’s why Marvell locked down plasmonics as its next modulation option early.

More important than any single deal is that Marvell’s M&A playbook is accelerating, not winding down. Inphi (2021, DSP), XConn (announced January 2026, closed February, $540M, PCIe + CXL switching), Celestial AI (announced December 2025, closed February 2026, $3.25B upfront plus up to $5.5B at revenue milestones, Photonic Fabric), and Polariton (April 2026). Three connectivity acquisitions in under a year.

On March 17, Marvell shipped the Structera S 30260 (260-lane CXL switch) using XConn IP, getting a first product out within a month of closing. The signal is now clear: for IC giants, optical and connectivity M&A is not a one-and-done asset play. It’s a game of accumulating chokepoints layer by layer to complete the stack.

Modulator IP also got reclassified as a core asset category for the next cycle. Three next-generation modulator candidates exist: plasmonics (Polariton), TFLN (QCi and China’s Liobate, AFR), and EO polymer (LWLG). If Marvell took plasmonics, the market’s next question became: who takes TFLN and EO polymer?

A single acquisition shifted the market’s lens on LWLG and QCi from pure narrative stocks to modulator chokepoint assets.

Trap 4 (LWLG Narrative Stock Warning): Clearly Broken

This is the most obviously wrong call from v1.0. The Trap section warned that “LWLG and QCi move on story, not earnings. When the theme cools, those names drop more than 50%.” Over the past month, LWLG went +101% and QCi +41%. The narrative didn’t cool. Price jumped another level.

It’s important to pinpoint exactly why this was wrong. The market began shifting its view of LWLG and QCi away from pure narrative stocks. After the Polariton acquisition, companies with photonic chokepoint assets started looking less like story-only plays and more like holders of strategic optionality.

LWLG’s EO polymer offers an ultra-low-power advantage with sub-1V modulator drive voltage. QCi’s TFLN sits in a balanced sweet spot at 1-2V. Polariton’s plasmonics leads on high-bandwidth. But Polariton and LWLG aren’t simply competitors. Polariton’s plasmonic-organic hybrid (POH) architecture itself uses EO polymer as the active material inside its plasmonic slot.

The two companies have been co-developing plasmonic MZMs since a joint record-performance demonstration at ECOC 2021 alongside ETH Zurich. By 2024 they had reached a single-channel 437 Gbps demonstration. With Marvell acquiring Polariton, this collaboration history and the related technical optionality entered the market’s field of view.

That said, no formal supply or licensing relationship between Marvell and LWLG has been announced. This should be treated as strategic optionality, not commercial fact.

Parts of the original call still hold. LWLG remains pre-commercial. FY25 revenue was $237K, with a 2027 production ramp target. QCi posted under $0.4M in FY24 revenue. If manufacturing ramp fails, the multiple compresses fast. Short-term volatility is still high. But the framework of “narrative cools, stock drops 50%” is retired. If it drops, the trigger will be a failed production validation or receding acquisition potential, not narrative cooling.

Separate. High-Risk Watchlist

POET Technologies (POET, +27% / -47% just before publication).

The biggest variable for this name materialized just before this piece was written.

On April 23, Marvell cancelled a POET purchase order related to Celestial AI, citing confidentiality breach. When disclosed on April 27, POET dropped -47% in a single day, the largest single-day decline in company history.

On April 14, a PFIC status + US redomicile announcement had already been made, and immediately after the cancellation disclosure, several shareholder rights law firms announced investigations and potential litigation. The only remaining order of note is a separate customer’s $5M order.

The Optical Interposer thesis itself survives, and the Foxconn FIT 800G/1.6T collaboration is ongoing, but customer concentration risk and governance/disclosure timing risk surfaced simultaneously.

POET is a separate high-volatility/governance-risk watchlist. Decision-making on this name requires a standalone judgment on commercial relationship recovery prospects and legal overhang, not the optical cycle thesis.

So Where Should You Stand Within the 22?

Using the validation above, all 22 names get re-sorted into four buckets. Classification criteria first: the sorting is not by magnitude of price gain but by the evidence quality behind the move and visibility of the next catalyst.

Bucket 1. Keep Holding (Add or Hold)

Positions where the thesis got stronger. These ran hard over the past month, but the evidence behind the move is still compounding through follow-on catalysts, leaving room for further multiple expansion.

Nokia (NOK, +56%).

The first name the market priced in under Scenario 1. Q1 optical revenue +20%, AI/cloud revenue +49%, AI/cloud new orders €1B in a single quarter.

Network Infrastructure guidance nearly doubled from 6-8% to 12-14%, Optical+IP 18-20%, AI/Cloud TAM CAGR raised to 27%, comparable operating profit +54% (consensus beat by 11%). Infinera synergy €200M for 2026 on track. The multi-rail amplifier (8x density) and full InP/SiPh stack unveiled at OFC 2026, spanning 13 application profiles with 70% TCO reduction, are just entering the revenue contribution stage. If the divergence where Nokia alone moves within the 4-pos group continues into Q2 and Q3, price has room for another leg. The invalidation signal is clear: if Optical Networks growth decelerates starting Q2, or if Infinera integration fails to flow through to margins, the Nokia-only divergence thesis breaks.

Sivers (SIVE.ST, +327%).

The market priced the ELS chokepoint thesis harder here than anywhere else. On April 16 the company formally announced it is evaluating a Nasdaq New York dual listing, with the Q1 interim report scheduled for May 20.

However, the annual report was delayed from April 27 to May 15 due to PCAOB audit uplift work required for the dual listing, and the AGM was pushed from May 27 to June 15. Revenue reallocation, inventory, and share option expense adjustments were flagged as possibilities during the audit process.

The dual listing is a powerful re-rating catalyst, but the audit delay and potential financial statement adjustments could act as short-term volatility triggers. This is the case that most cleanly demonstrates why “which chokepoint you own” matters more than category averages, breaking the Materials-Device category’s general assumptions about weak cycle exposure.

Position sizing needs to account for both volatility and audit timeline. The invalidation signal: the dual listing falls through, the May report fails to deliver more specifics on ELS customer disclosures, or audit results produce revenue adjustments that run counter to the thesis.

Marvell (MRVL, +55%, YTD +85%).

Three connectivity acquisitions in under a year (XConn $540M, Celestial AI $3.25B upfront plus up to $5.5B in earnout, Polariton).

The Structera S 30260 shipped within a month of XConn closing, proving integration speed. Celestial AI’s Photonic Fabric is expected to begin meaningful revenue contribution in H2 FY2028 (calendar H2 2027).

Polariton’s plasmonics is a 3.2T-era card, so revenue contribution is 2028 or later. Two multiple expansion paths are the base case and one is optionality: Photonic Fabric production and Polariton integration results are the base case; the market beginning to speculate on the next M&A target (TFLN or EO polymer space) is the optionality.

The invalidation signal: Photonic Fabric production timeline slips from H2 FY2028, or post-Polariton integration shows no visibility on first revenue contribution. The POET purchase order cancellation shows that existing supplier restructuring is also underway, so the balance between integrating acquired assets and managing the existing ecosystem deserves monitoring.

LWLG (+101%).

The biggest beneficiary of Trap 4’s retirement. Tower Semi PH18 integration was announced on March 11 (the stock jumped +41% intraday on March 12), GFS PDK listing followed on March 16, and a fourth Fortune Global 500 company entered Stage 3 on February 24. The Polariton acquisition elevated the strategic value of the entire modulator IP category, and because Polariton’s POH architecture uses EO polymer as its active material, LWLG’s strategic optionality expanded in tandem.

However, no direct supply or licensing relationship between Marvell and LWLG has been officially confirmed, so this should be treated as speculative upside rather than commercial fact. Keep position sizes small, but this belongs in Bucket 1. The invalidation signal: Stage 3 customers fail to advance to production contracts (Stage 4), the 2027 ramp timeline slips, or Marvell redirects R&D post-Polariton integration in a way that reduces polymer dependency.

Bucket 2. Hold with Caution (Hold but Watch)

The thesis worked, but the company’s own gate is approaching and direction depends on results. Or, strong fundamentals but expectations are already high enough that results announcements carry elevated volatility.

Astera Labs (ALAB, +83%).

v1.0’s explicit call that “the photonic scale-up roadmap is the catalyst” landed. In the Q4 2025 8-K, the company announced its acquisition of aiXscale Photonics (fiber-chip coupling), joining the photonic asset integration pattern.

FY2025 revenue $852.5M (+115% YoY), Q4 $270.6M (+92% YoY), Q1 2026 guidance $286-297M, 5-year SAM expansion to $25B (10x). Given the revenue growth trajectory, a forward P/E around 80x carries some justification, but risks like ESUN consortium exclusion and Trainium4’s adoption of NVLink Fusion remain.

The next gate is when Scorpio X-Series design wins land in hyperscaler production lineups.

Coherent (COHR, +29%).

Placing a one-year champion in Bucket 3 just because it already ran doesn’t square with fundamentals this strong. Q2 FY26 marked the fifth consecutive beat. The datacenter segment posted a book-to-bill ratio exceeding 4x, providing demand visibility through 2027. 6-inch InP wafer capacity reached 80% of target, Q3 FY26 guidance accelerated to $1.7-1.84B, S&P 500 inclusion came on March 23, and the Munich business unit divestiture strengthened margins.

At OFC 2026 the product lineup was deep: 6.4T socketed SiPh CPO + ELS, multimode VCSEL CPO, 400G InP modulator on silicon, founding member of both XPO and Open CPX MSAs, multi-rail optical transport (4 C+L band rails in 1RU).

The immediate next gate is the Q3 FY26 report on May 6. With a $2B NVIDIA tie-up locking in part of the long-term upside, near-term price surprises are harder to come by, but if Q3 results push visibility one step further, the multiple has room for another move.

Lumentum (LITE, +18%).

Same logic as Coherent.

Q2 FY26 revenue $665.5M (+65.5% YoY), operating margin expansion of +1,700 bps, OCS backlog $400M+, CPO incremental multi-hundred-million-dollar order booked for H1 CY2027 delivery. Q3 FY26 guidance $780-830M (+85% YoY), operating margin 30-31%, EPS $2.15-2.35.

The CEO’s comments were striking: guidance implies +85% YoY, yet with OCS and CPO the company is “only at the starting line.” Cloud & Networking now accounts for 88% of revenue, making Lumentum effectively a cloud pure-play. The immediate next gate is the Q3 FY26 report on May 5.

Credo (CRDO, +81%).

Within the Downstream trio, this is the name that needs to show optical revenue fastest. Q3 FY2026 revenue $407M (+202% YoY, +52% QoQ), gross margin 68.6%, EPS $1.07, the strongest quarter in company history. The core AEC business is growing hard enough to provide a thick cushion. The DustPhotonics acquisition drew a strong market reaction upon announcement. The deal structure is $750M cash + 0.92M shares + up to 3.21M earnout shares, putting total deal value at potentially $1.3B. DustPhotonics’ meaningful revenue contribution is an FY2027-and-beyond story; until then, integration costs and opex growth could pressure margins near-term. Amazon concentration at 40-64% of quarterly revenue is the single biggest risk. The next gate is the Q4 FY2026 report on June 3.

AAOI (+90%).

Three variables fired simultaneously: sovereignty narrative, size beta, and AEC ramp. The gap versus category peers (LITE +18%, COHR +29%) is wide enough that losing even one or two of those variables could compress the stock quickly.

On top of the $1B 2026 revenue guidance issued on February 26 (more than doubling the prior year’s $455M), a sequence of catalysts followed from mid-March: a $200M+ first hyperscale 1.6T order, a $124M 800G order, Houston-area footprint expansion to 900,000 sq ft (Pearland 2 buildings + Sugar Land 210K + 154K leased), a production target of 500K-700K units per month, and laser fab capacity growth of approximately 350% (by end-2027).

Based on management commentary, the next gate is less about demand confirmation and more about capacity ramp execution. Expanded capacity was described as insufficient to meet demand through mid-2027. The immediate gate is Q1 2026 results on May 7. The $250M ATM equity offering is a latent dilution risk.

QCi (QUBT, +41%).

The Polariton acquisition elevated the TFLN modulator category, and QCi is one of the rare names on US public markets offering relatively direct TFLN foundry exposure. Tempe AZ Fab 1 is operational at small-batch manufacturing stage.

The December 2025 Luminar Semiconductor acquisition ($110M all-cash, closed February 2) added lasers, detectors, and advanced packaging, so QCi is no longer a TFLN-only foundry but is transitioning into a vertically integrated photonics platform. Pro forma liquidity rose to approximately $1.5B after a $750M private placement in 2025, partially de-risking the pre-revenue profile.

However, FY24 revenue was under $0.4M and FY25 Q4 revenue was $198K, so absolute numbers remain very small. The next gates: Tempe foundry first-revenue visibility, and progress on the 3.2 Tbps optical engine co-development with POET announced November 11, 2025. Note that POET’s commercial momentum has been shaken since the Marvell order cancellation, so this collaboration’s significance also needs re-evaluation.

Ciena (CIEN, +27%).

This is not just a portfolio stability name. It should be viewed as direct exposure to the AI optical transport supercycle. Q1 FY2026 revenue $1.43B (+33% YoY), adjusted EPS $1.35 (+111% YoY), $7B record backlog (sequential +$2B).

Direct Cloud Provider revenue +76% YoY, 42% of total. WaveLogic 6 Extreme is the industry-leading 1.6 Tb/s coherent solution, adding 18 customers in Q1 alone (total now 90). Scale-across deployments are secured with 3 hyperscalers (each deal “hundreds of millions”+), and RLS revenue rose +80% YoY. FY2026 guidance $5.9-6.3B (+28% YoY), Q2 guidance $1.5B. At OFC 2026 in April, the company unveiled Vesta 200 6.4T CPX, hyper-rail photonics (32x density), and 2nm 1600ZR/ZR+.

The Nubis Communications acquisition added CPO/NPO + electrical redriver assets, following the same pattern as Marvell-Polariton, Astera-aiXscale, Credo-DustPhotonics, and Semtech-HieFo. S&P 500 re-inclusion came on February 9. The post-Q1 14% sell-off wasn’t a thesis break; it happened because whisper numbers ran above guidance. Expectation burden and supply-chain capex pressure amplify near-term volatility.

Bucket 3. Trim or Hold Off on New Entry (Reduce or Hold Off)

Names that have already run their course for now, have exited the primary cycle, or have weakened near-term momentum.

Innolight (300308, +42%) / Eoptolink (300502, +14%).

Core names in the v1.0 fab-lite category. With the 1.6T ramp accelerating from H2 2026, this is technically more of a mix-shift phase than a late-cycle entry. Innolight is frequently cited as one of the strongest Chinese optical module contenders in the GB200 and 1.6T transition.

Its lineup diversification across 1.6T DR8/2xFR4 (3nm DSP), 1.6T 2xLR4 (TFLN MZM), and 1.6T VCSEL 2VR4 puts it in a favorable position for the cycle transition. Eoptolink posted 2025 revenue of 24.84B yuan and net income of 9.53B yuan (+235.9% YoY), and Q1 2026 net income grew +77% YoY. However, Q4 came in -13% sequentially with EPS surprise -12.7% and revenue surprise -14.9%, missing market expectations. The stock dropped -15% after the report.

Goldman Sachs flagged FX losses from yuan strength as a key contributor. The Bucket 3 classification here is less about “800G has peaked” and more about “valuation premium burden + FX and macro variables + 1.6T mix-shift transition volatility.”

IPG Photonics (IPGP, +3%) / nLIGHT (LASR, +20%).

Both sit in the Materials-Device category but their end-market exposure diverges from this AI datacenter optical cycle. IPG’s overwhelming industrial laser exposure is why it returned 1/30th of the category average (+96%) over the past month.

This is the cleanest case where v1.0’s explicit diagnosis of “industrial/defense laser, not datacenter-exposed” played out. nLIGHT is a different story. With the Q1 report coming May 7, Q4 2025 delivered revenue of $81.2M (+71% YoY), A&D revenue of $56.3M (+87% YoY), a 70kW-class laser weapon system unveiling, a $50M missile-sensing contract, and $162M in A&D backlog.

Direct AI datacenter optical exposure is limited, but the company is deeply embedded in a different cycle. Within the optical map framework, Bucket 3 (outside the datacenter cycle) is correct, but for stock-level decisions, the defense thesis warrants separate evaluation.

Bucket 4. New Candidates / Trigger Watch

Names within the 22-company matrix that haven’t moved as much but sit on a thesis that says they should. This bucket splits into two: active initiates where entry is justified now, and trigger-watch names that need a specific event to move.

4A. Active Initiate

Cisco (CSCO, +17%).

v1.0 grouped this under 4-pos conglomerate dilution logic, but while the one-month price barely moved, fundamentals accelerated sharply. AI infrastructure orders went from $800M in Q4 FY25, to $1.3B in Q1 FY26, to $2.1B in Q2 FY26, reaching the full FY25 level in a single quarter. FY26 AI orders guided above $5B, AI infrastructure revenue above $3B. The Q2 mix split roughly 60/40 between systems and optics, indicating optical share is ramping in earnest.

On the earnings call, management stated that Acacia posted its strongest quarter ever with triple-digit booking growth. They added that all major hyperscalers are deploying coherent pluggables. In March the company launched the 102.4 Tbps G300 chip, 1.6T OSFP, and 800G LPO pluggable (built on Cisco’s silicon photonics platform). Neocloud/sovereign/enterprise pipeline exceeds $2.5B, and the AMD-HUMAIN JV targets 1 GW of AI infrastructure by 2030. Forward P/E of 18.7x sits below the 5-year average of 22x, an asymmetric setup.

The caveat is that optical revenue doesn’t break out independently in Cisco’s financial reporting, so the stock should be treated not as a pure optical play but as an AI networking + optics catch-up play. This is no longer a trigger-watch name. The trigger has already fired; the multiple just hasn’t caught up. The invalidation signal: hyperscaler AI order acceleration stalls in Q3, or Acacia booking momentum breaks.

MACOM (MTSI, +23%) / Semtech (SMTC, +31%).

Both are tracking near the category average (+27%). v1.0’s thesis that “in an EML supply shortage, IC companies with their own laser fabs differentiate” hasn’t fully priced in yet. MACOM posted Q1 FY26 revenue of $271.6M (+24.5% YoY), book-to-bill of 1.3x (strongest since Q3 2021), datacenter guidance raised from 20% to 35-40%, and is progressing its 1.6T entry and 200G/lane photodetector capacity expansion. Semtech posted Q4 FY26 revenue of $274.4M, FY26 total $1.05B, datacenter $223M (+58% YoY), and guided FY27 datacenter above +50%.

The HieFo acquisition is the key catalyst: Semtech content per module goes from high-single-digit dollars at 800G to approximately $80 at 3.2T, a 10x expansion. NVIDIA demonstrated a 1.6T DR8 transceiver built on Semtech TIA + laser driver. Both names are at the stage where first signals of IC-Laser category reframing begin entering price.

The invalidation signal: Semtech’s first quarter post-HieFo fails to show integration synergies, or MACOM’s optical revenue mix expansion decelerates.

Yuanjie (688498.SS, +48%).

The hottest candidate in the China optical second wave. The first trigger from v1.0’s Scenario 5 (Hong Kong secondary listing wave) has already fired. In March the company filed its Hong Kong listing application.

On April 17 the stock reached CNY 1,403, surpassing Kweichow Moutai to become the highest-priced stock on China’s A-share market (market cap approximately $13.7B). 2025 revenue CNY 601M (+138.5% YoY), net income CNY 191M (loss to profit turnaround), datacenter revenue share above 50%, gross margin 72.21%. The next trigger is not “will they file for an HK listing” but HK listing approval, pricing, and post-listing valuation re-rating.

Political risk (US-China friction extending to the optical supply chain) calls for careful position sizing. The invalidation signal: the HK listing is derailed by political risk, or US export restrictions and hyperscaler supply-chain de-risking extend broadly to Chinese optical vendors.

Aeluma (ALMU, +81%).

One of the few US-listed pure-play names offering exposure to a 12-inch III-V-on-Si platform. Small market cap means the +81% over one month carries structural asset value that is still in the process of category reframing. The SWIR photodetector + quantum dot laser combination extends beyond datacenter applications into LiDAR, quantum computing, and defense.

Recent catalysts include a NASA award (April), $4M+ in government contracts (April 14, non-dilutive funding), partnerships with Tower Semiconductor and Sumitomo Chemical Advanced Technology, AIM Photonics full industry membership, and DARPA/DOE collaborations. This is a name that recalls the AXT re-rating template (AXT ran +6,837% on the InP wafer cycle).

Volatility is very high. The invalidation signal: 12-inch III-V-on-Si fails to convert a commercial customer ramp into first revenue within 2026.

4B. Wait for Trigger

Sumitomo Electric (5802.T, -5%).

With Nokia at +56% among its 4-pos peers, Sumitomo is theoretically in the same framework. But it satisfies almost none of the three conditions Nokia met (optical revenue share, Western narrative, integration freshness). Automotive wire harness accounts for over 50% of revenue, leaving optical exposure thin.

As a Japanese company, the Western narrative doesn’t apply. And its integration story is decades old. That said, Sumitomo’s absolute optical revenue is dominant. Its participation alongside Corning, AFL, and TeraHop in forming the SDM4 MCF MSA (4-core multicore fiber) on March 11 opens a new narrative angle around multicore fiber leadership. If a trigger materializes (e.g., reports of an optical business unit spin-off IPO, hyperscaler long-term contract disclosure, or mainstream MCF adoption for AI datacenters), a reaction becomes possible. Without a trigger, it stays where it is. This is a watchlist seat, not an active entry.

Four Buckets at a Glance

The Message From This Review

First, the v1.0 category framework survived its first market test, but the dominant variable for stock-level decisions has shifted to intra-category divergence.

What determines stock-level performance is not “depth of vertical integration” but “which chokepoint you own,” “Western alternative narrative fit,” “freshness of integration assets,” “optical revenue share,” and “end-market exposure.” In a cycle where IPG at +3% and Sivers at +327% coexist in the same category, category-level betting is already too blunt a framework.

Second, look again at the names that moved the most (Sivers +327%, LWLG +101%, AAOI +90%, Astera +83%, Credo +81%, Aeluma +81%).

They land in Bucket 1 or Bucket 2. The naive mean-reversion logic of “it already ran, so it’s due for a pullback” does not work in this cycle. The judgment call is not about the magnitude of the move but about whether evidence behind the move keeps compounding. At the same time, Coherent and Lumentum, which showed one-month deceleration, have fundamentals that are actually accelerating. For those names, price momentum is not the variable. Their own gates (Q3 reports on May 5 and 6) are the immediate determinant.

Third, photonic asset acquisition through M &A has become a pattern that cuts across categories.

Marvell-Polariton, Astera-aiXscale, Credo-DustPhotonics, Ciena-Nubis, Semtech-HieFo. The Downstream IC trio, an optical transport vendor, and an IC-Laser veteran are all moving in the same direction simultaneously. This means v1.0’s “depth of vertical integration” as a static framework is becoming dynamic. In the next cycle, “current integration depth” may matter less than “the direction and speed at which a company is accelerating integration.”

Fourth, the most interesting position in this cycle is Bucket 4A’s Cisco.

A name trapped under the 4-pos conglomerate dilution label saw AI infrastructure orders accelerate ($800M to $1.3B to $2.1B) in a single quarter while trading at a forward P/E of 18.7x, below the 5-year average of 22x. It was a seat where one trigger could produce an asymmetric reaction. Now it’s been reclassified: the trigger has already fired, the multiple just hasn’t caught up.

Sumitomo stays on trigger watch, but the MCF MSA formation adds a new narrative angle.

Ultimately this review says one thing. The optical cycle isn’t over, but the phase where you simply follow first-wave laser and module winners is passing. What matters now is not avoiding names that have already run, but distinguishing between names where the reason for the run extends into the next layer of evidence and names where the run ends at momentum. And at the same time, companies accelerating photonic asset integration through M&A are increasingly likely to capture the next cycle’s rewards.

If the optical map v1.0 was a static map, this review is the exercise of deciding where to stand within that map right now. The 22-company matrix is the same, but the positioning within it has already been rearranged once.
