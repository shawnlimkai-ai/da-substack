1. The Limits of Copper, and the Shift to Light

Most of what connects chips inside a data center today is copper cable. But copper has hit a physical wall. Once data transmission speeds reach 200Gb/s per lane, the distance copper can carry a signal without degradation drops to under one meter. “Per lane” refers to a single electrical channel. In practice, transceivers bundle multiple lanes together to build total bandwidth. An 800G transceiver (800 gigabits per second, or 100 gigabytes per second), for example, is built as “200G x 4 lanes” or “100G x 8 lanes.”

The physical housing for these transceivers is called a form factor. The most widely used today is the QSFP-DD (Quad Small Form-factor Pluggable Double Density).

Breaking down the name: SFP (Small Form-factor Pluggable) was originally a single-lane standard. QSFP (Quad SFP) bundles four of them. Add DD (Double Density), and the electrical connector is doubled into two rows, bringing the lane count to eight. Same slot size, twice the bandwidth. It’s hot-swappable, meaning you can plug it into the front panel of a switch and pull it out again. 100G per lane x 8 lanes gives you 800G QSFP-DD. 200G per lane x 8 lanes gives you 1.6T QSFP-DD.

Worth noting: QSFP-DD doesn’t imply optical or copper. It’s just the shell, the form factor. What you put inside is what determines the technology.

Copper’s problem shows up when you go from 800G to 1.6T and beyond. Once per-lane speed crosses 200G, signal degradation in copper gets severe fast. At 800G, copper DAC cables could cover several meters. At 1.6T (200G x 8 lanes), the usable distance shrinks to under one meter, and even then you need a retimer chip to maintain signal integrity. At 3.2T (400G x 8 lanes), copper struggles with distances measured in centimeters. In the 1.6T world and beyond, optical interconnects aren’t a nice-to-have. They’re the only option.

2. Lasers and Modulators: The Concepts You Need to Know First

What a Laser Does

Inside a data center, a laser does exactly one thing: it generates light. That light travels through optical fiber , bouncing off the fiber walls, covering hundreds of meters or several kilometers. Once you encode data (ones and zeros) onto that light , you can transmit information faster, farther, and at lower power than copper wire.

Here are the three concepts you need to understand:

  1. What a laser is

  2. What optical fiber is

  3. What it means to put data on top of light (more on this in the modulation section)

LASER stands for L ight A mplification by S timulated E mission of R adiation.

Here’s how it works: when you run current through a certain semiconductor material, electrons absorb energy and jump to a higher state. When they fall back down, they release photons, which is light. Those photons bounce back and forth between mirror-like surfaces inside the chip, amplifying as they go, until they exit through one end. That’s your laser beam. The key difference from an ordinary LED is that a laser emits light at a single wavelength, a single “color.” That precise, coherent light is what makes long-distance transmission possible.

The light a laser generates travels through optical fiber , which is a glass (silica, SiO2) cable drawn thinner than a human hair. At its center is a core where light travels. Surrounding the core is a cladding with a different refractive index. Because the core has a higher refractive index than the cladding, light undergoes total internal reflection inside the core, staying in and propagating forward. Copper carries electrical signals; optical fiber carries light signals. Unlike copper, fiber is immune to electromagnetic interference, and its signal loss is so low that it can carry data for tens of kilometers.

Two Semiconductors, Two Fiber Types

Optical fiber comes in two types depending on core diameter, and which type you use depends on which laser you're running. Data center lasers use two families of semiconductor materials.

And it is commonly referred to as a III-V compound semiconductor.

A III-V semiconductor is a compound semiconductor made by combining Group III and Group V elements from the periodic table. These materials have a direct bandgap, which makes them highly efficient at converting electricity into light , so they are very well suited for making lasers.

GaAs (Gallium Arsenide): A compound semiconductor made from gallium and arsenic. It emits light at 850nm , in the infrared, invisible to the eye. This wavelength works well in multimode fiber (MMF) , which has a relatively wide core diameter of around 50 micrometers, roughly half the thickness of a human hair. A wider core lets light enter at multiple angles simultaneously (hence “multimode”), making it easy to manufacture and align, but the multiple light paths interfere with each other over distance, causing signal degradation quickly. The usable range tops out at 100 to 300 meters.

InP (Indium Phosphide): A compound semiconductor made from indium and phosphorus. It emits at 1310nm or 1550nm , wavelengths optimized for single-mode fiber (SMF) , which has a core diameter of about 9 micrometers. With only one path for light to travel, there’s no interference, and the signal can travel several to tens of kilometers without breaking down.

To summarize:

GaAs produces 850nm light, works in multimode fiber , and is used for short distances.

InP produces 1310nm or 1550nm light, works in single-mode fiber , and handles long distances.

Laser Structures: VCSEL vs. DFB

There are two types of lasers used in data centers. They differ in the direction light comes out of the chip, the semiconductor material they’re made from, and the distance they can cover.

VCSEL (Vertical-Cavity Surface-Emitting Laser)

Light exits from the top of the chip. Made from GaAs , operates at 850nm wavelength, and works exclusively with multimode fiber. Reach is about 100 to 300 meters.

VCSELs have three key advantages.

First, they’re cheap. Because light comes out the top, you can test thousands of chips simultaneously while they’re still on the wafer (the semiconductor disc) before cutting them apart. Catching defects early keeps mass production costs low.

Second, they consume very little power. The distance light travels back and forth inside the chip is extremely short, so it takes very little current to achieve lasing.

Third, the beam comes out in a circular shape, which makes it easy to couple into a fiber.

The most familiar application isn’t in data centers.

It’s the iPhone’s Face ID.

The sensor that projects 30,000 infrared dots onto your face to map it in 3D uses a VCSEL as its light source. Supplying hundreds of millions of VCSELs per year to Apple is how Coherent built its economies of scale, and that manufacturing expertise carried directly into developing high speed VCSELs for data centers.

DFB (Distributed Feedback Laser)

Light exits from the side of the chip, traveling horizontally. Made from InP (indium phosphide), operates at 1310nm or 1550nm wavelength, and works exclusively with single mode fiber. Reach extends from several kilometers to tens of kilometers.

The reason DFB exists comes down to one thing: wavelength purity. A basic laser (called an FP laser, or Fabry-Perot laser) emits multiple wavelengths of light at the same time. When different wavelengths travel together through a fiber, each one moves at a slightly different speed, causing the signal to spread out over distance. This is called chromatic dispersion. Over long distances the signal smears beyond recognition. DFB solves this by etching a fine corrugated pattern called a “diffraction grating” directly into the chip. Think of it as an optical filter built into the laser itself. It selects exactly one wavelength and suppresses everything else. A single wavelength beam is resistant to chromatic dispersion, which is what makes high speed, long distance transmission possible.

The EML , CW laser , and DML that come up later in this article are all built on top of the DFB structure. The only thing that changes between them is how data gets encoded onto the light.

EML : Integrates a modulator right next to the DFB for high-speed, long-distance links.

CW Laser : Runs the DFB at constant output with no modulation at the source, leaving that job to a separate silicon chip.

DML : Drives the DFB by switching the current directly, the simplest approach of the three.

_ Now, DFB is the foundation of every long reach laser in an AI data center._

Modulation: Various Ways to Put Data on Top of Light

The laser generates light. Now you need to encode digital data, ones and zeros, onto that light. This is called modulation. Turn the light on and you have a 1. Turn it off and you have a 0. That’s the simplest form of modulation, called NRZ (Non-Return-to-Zero) : consecutive ones don’t cause the signal to drop back to zero between them, it stays on. One bit per symbol. More complex modulation divides light intensity into four levels, transmitting two bits at once. This is PAM4 (Pulse Amplitude Modulation, 4-level) , where 00, 01, 10, and 11 are each represented by a different brightness level.

The key question is where and how modulation happens. That single choice determines a laser’s performance, cost, and use case. There are five modulation approaches that matter in AI data centers.

① Direct Modulation: The simplest approach. You rapidly ramp the current flowing through the laser up and down, and the light output follows, getting brighter and dimmer accordingly. No separate modulator chip is needed, which keeps the design simple and cheap. The downside is that when the current changes, the laser’s emission wavelength wobbles along with it. This is called chirp. When chirp is large, the chromatic dispersion problem described earlier gets worse and the signal spreads out over long distances. Both VCSELs and DMLs (Directly Modulated Lasers, which are just DFB lasers operated with direct modulation) use this method.

② EAM (Electro-Absorption Modulator): A modulator built right next to a DFB laser on the same InP chip. The DFB fires a constant beam of light, and the EAM sitting beside it either lets that light pass through or absorbs it depending on the voltage applied, creating the 1s and 0s. Think of the DFB as a faucet that’s always running and the EAM as a valve on the pipe. Open the valve and water (light) flows out. Close it and the flow stops. Because the current driving the DFB stays constant, chirp is minimal, which makes this combination strong for high speed, long distance links.

DFB + EAM = EML (Electro-Absorption Modulated Laser)

It’s the workhorse behind today’s 800G and 1.6T pluggable modules, and it’s also the source of the supply bottleneck.

③ MZM (Mach-Zehnder Modulator): This one splits incoming light into two paths and then recombines them. Apply voltage to one path and the light traveling through it speeds up or slows down slightly (its phase shifts). When the two paths merge back together, if they’re in phase the light comes through strong (= 1). If they’re out of phase the light cancels itself out (= 0). What really matters is what material you build this modulator on. Build it on silicon (SiPh) and you can mass produce it in a standard CMOS fab. Build it on InP and you get better performance.

The combination of a CW laser plus a silicon MZM is rapidly emerging as the main challenger to EML.

④ MRM (Micro-Ring Modulator): A tiny circular ring, just tens of micrometers across, sits next to a straight optical waveguide (the path light travels through). Under certain conditions light gets pulled into the ring and disappears from the main path (= 0). Apply a voltage that slightly shifts those conditions and the light ignores the ring and passes straight through (= 1).

It does the same job as an MZM but in a much smaller footprint, so you can pack more of them onto a single chip.

Built on silicon. This is the modulator NVIDIA plans to use in its TSMC COUPE based CPO.

Both MZM and MRM are primarily built on silicon (SiPh). But a next generation material is gaining serious attention:

TFLN (Thin-Film Lithium Niobate)

The idea is to take a special crystal called lithium niobate (LiNbO₃), slice it into a film just a few hundred nanometers thick, and fabricate an MZM-style modulator on top of it. The modulation method itself is the same as a standard MZM. What changes is the material underneath. A silicon MZM works by physically moving electrons around to alter the speed of light passing through it. TFLN works differently. An electric field acts directly on the crystal structure to change the speed of light (this is called the Pockels effect). No electrons need to move, so the response is dramatically faster. Bandwidth exceeds 100 GHz (silicon MZMs top out at a few tens of GHz), drive voltage is lower, and static power consumption is nearly zero.

It's drawing attention as the modulator material for the 3.2T era, where 400G per lane speeds will be needed.

POET Technologies and QCi are co-developing a 3.2T optical engine based on TFLN, and foundries like HyperLight (US) and Lightium (Switzerland) are already producing TFLN devices. The catch is that this material is difficult to process and manufacturing yields haven't caught up to silicon yet.

Putting Multiple Colors on One Fiber: WDM

Everything described so far assumes one laser, one wavelength, one fiber. To increase bandwidth, you add more fibers. Sending 800G requires eight fibers (100G x 8). Sending 1.6T also requires eight fibers (200G x 8). More fibers mean more connectors, more space, more cost.

WDM (Wavelength Division Multiplexing) is the solution. Instead of one wavelength per fiber, you put multiple wavelengths on the same fiber simultaneously. Different wavelengths of light don’t interfere with each other inside the fiber, so each one carries its own independent data stream. On the receiving end, filters separate the wavelengths and recover each signal individually. Think of it like a highway where red cars, blue cars, and green cars all travel at the same time. Different colors don’t collide, and at the destination you sort them by color.

With WDM, the same number of fibers can carry several times the bandwidth. Put four wavelengths on one fiber and two fibers do the work of eight. Fewer fibers means fewer connectors, simpler assembly, and less space consumed. In CPO, attaching dozens of fibers directly to a chip is physically difficult, and WDM directly alleviates that constraint.

WDM requires lasers that emit multiple wavelengths simultaneously. That’s the reason multi-wavelength lasers from companies like Scintil, Ayar Labs, Ranovus, and Xscape matter so much in the CPO roadmap.

3. The Battlefield Map: Who Uses What, Where, and How

The laser structures and modulation approaches covered above play out in actual data centers through specific combinations. To understand them, you need to look at three axes simultaneously:

  1. Where it’s used (application)

  2. What form it takes (form factor)

  3. What laser and modulator combination it uses (technology).

At every intersection of these three axes sits a different technology, a different company, a different investment opportunity.

Scale-Up: GPU to GPU, Under 2 Meters

This is the domain of direct GPU-to-GPU connectivity. NVIDIA calls this NVLink. GPUs on the same board, or within the same rack, exchange data as fast as possible.

NVIDIA NVLink 6 runs at 400G per lane per SerDes, with a per-GPU bandwidth ceiling of 3.6TB/s. At these speeds, copper signal quality degrades sharply with distance.

Today: copper dominates.

Broadcom argues that continued SerDes advancement will keep copper dominant for intra-rack, ultra-short distances through 2028. It’s cheap, proven, and works. No lasers needed, so it’s simpler than optical. Copper still owns this space.

The inflection point: multi-rack scale-up forces optical.

The problem arises when scale-up configurations extend beyond a single rack.

In a 576-GPU cluster built from eight NVIDIA NVL72 systems, GPU-to-GPU data has to cross rack boundaries. Copper can’t simultaneously satisfy the bandwidth and reach requirements at that distance.

TrendForce projects that optical interconnects for multi-rack scale-up could emerge with the Rubin Ultra or Feynman generations.

Expected combinations when optical arrives:

When optical enters this space, the form factor will be CPO (Co-Packaged Optics, integrating the optical engine into the same package as the switch or GPU) or NPO (Near-Package Optics, placing the optical module immediately adjacent to the chip but not in the same package).

Short distances call for designs attached directly to the chip, not pluggable modules. Three laser and modulation combinations are candidates.

First, VCSEL plus direct modulation , using multimode fiber for distances of a few meters. GaAs VCSEL arrays targeting around 1pJ/bit energy efficiency are in development. Coherent’s demonstration of a multimode VCSEL-based socketed CPO at OFC 2026 is the most notable example.

Second, CW laser (InP) plus MRM (SiPh) , which is NVIDIA’s roadmap, placing micro-ring modulators on a SiPh die on top of TSMC’s COUPE platform. Single-mode fiber covers greater distances than VCSEL, and the architecture supports future WDM expansion (multiple wavelengths on one fiber).

Third, multi-wavelength CW plus WDM. Multi-wavelength sources such as Ayar Labs’ SuperNova, Scintil’s LEAF Light, and Ranovus’ Quantum Dot laser target this space. Multiple wavelengths on one fiber reduces fiber count while increasing bandwidth density. WDM-based CPO, however, is expected to take until 2027 to 2028 to deploy, after single-wavelength CPO is validated first.

[Damnang Note] MicroLED: The Wildcard Without Lasers

One company is taking a completely different approach. Avicena uses GaN (gallium nitride)-based blue MicroLED arrays to transmit data over multicore fiber bundles, hundreds of thin fibers in a single cable, for parallel transmission. No laser involved. Think of tiny displays shooting data and cameras on the other end receiving it.

At OFC 2026, they unveiled the LightBundle eKit: 320 MicroLED channels, 3.5Gbps per channel, a 256 active plus 64 spare configuration delivering a 512Gbps link, with 896Gbps coming in Q2. Tx power is 200 femtojoules per bit, a thousand times smaller than a picojoule, which is dramatically lower than laser-based solutions. TSMC is the production partner for the photodetector arrays, SK Hynix has invested, and the CEO is Marco Chisari, former EVP at Samsung Foundry.

The limitations: maximum range around 10 meters and per-channel speed of 3.5Gbps makes it hard to be a primary solution at scale. The positioning is scale-in (die-to-die within a package) or ultra-short-range scale-up, as a copper replacement. It’s less a direct threat to the laser market and more a wildcard: if this scales, the demand structure for lasers could change fundamentally.

Scale-Out: Switch to Switch, 10 Meters to 10 Kilometers

This is the switch-to-switch domain. Ethernet and InfiniBand (the high-performance computing network protocol) territory. This is the largest current market, and the main battleground for EML vs CW + SiPh.

In this space, the laser and modulation combination varies by form factor. And those form factors themselves are evolving from pluggable to NPO to CPO.

Pluggable: Today’s Mainstream

Pluggable refers to transceiver modules that slot into the front panel of a switch and can be swapped out when needed. They come in standard form factors like QSFP-DD and OSFP (Octal Small Form-factor Pluggable, a slightly larger form factor than QSFP-DD that allows for higher power and better thermal headroom). When one fails, you pull it and plug in a replacement. That simplicity is why pluggable still accounts for more than 90% of the market.

EML + DSP (current mainstream)

The 800G DR8 module is the representative example.

The name breaks down as follows: 800 total gigabits per second, DR (Data Center Reach, roughly 500 meters to 2 kilometers), transmitted across 8 lanes.

Inside the module are eight 100G EML chips, plus a DSP (Digital Signal Processor). The DSP conditions the electrical signal into modulation formats like PAM4 on the transmit side and corrects signal distortion and errors on the receive side. The whole assembly comes in an OSFP form factor.

An EML bare die runs $10 to $20. Packaged into a TOSA (Transmitter Optical Sub-Assembly, which integrates the laser die with lenses and a fiber coupling interface), prices reach $50 to $100 or more. With eight of them in a single module, the laser cost overwhelmingly dominates the bill of materials.

200G EML is the only commercially available laser that can support 1.6T today. That’s why demand is exploding and supply is short. NVIDIA preemptively locking up EML capacity is where the “laser shortage” originates.

Primary suppliers: Lumentum, Coherent, Broadcom, Mitsubishi Electric, Sumitomo Electric.

CW + SiPh MZM + DSP (fast growing)

This combination is surging as the alternative to scarce EML. An InP CW laser emits a constant beam of light, and an MZM inside a SiPh PIC (Photonic Integrated Circuit, a chip that integrates modulators, waveguides, and photodetectors on silicon) handles the modulation.

An 800G SiPh module takes two to four CW lasers. At $8 to $10 per 70mW CW laser, the laser cost per module comes to $20 to $40, less than half of what EML costs. SiPh PICs are fabbed in standard CMOS fabs, which makes supply scaling far more flexible.

SiPh 800G module penetration has jumped from roughly 10% in 2024 to 20 to 30% in 2025.

Key players: Marvell (SiPh engine design), China Innolight (largest SiPh module shipper), Coherent and Lumentum (CW laser supply).

DML + DSP (legacy)

Primarily used in 100G QSFP28 modules. The simplest structure available: a DFB laser driven with direct modulation. Four lanes at 25Gbps each make 100G. High chirp rules it out for long distances, but it still fills the gap where VCSEL falls short and EML would be overkill. Its share of new AI data center deployments is shrinking.

EML or CW+SiPh with LPO (removing the DSP)

The DSP described above consumes nearly half of a transceiver's total power. A 1.6T module draws over 30 watts, and a significant portion of that goes to the DSP alone.

LPO (Linear Pluggable Optics) is an architecture that removes the DSP entirely or strips it down to the bare minimum. The logic is simple: once the SerDes (Serializer/Deserializer, the circuit that converts data between serial and parallel formats) inside the switch ASIC becomes powerful enough to handle signal integrity on its own, there's no reason for the transceiver to run another round of digital processing. Power drops from 30W+ to roughly 10W. Latency drops from around 200ns to under 5ns. When thousands of GPUs need to stay in sync during AI training, that latency difference has a direct impact on overall cluster efficiency. LPO works on top of any laser technology. EML or CW+SiPh, it doesn't matter. It's a system-level choice, not a laser choice. Broadcom and Marvell are both pushing LPO-compatible SerDes.

NPO: The Transitional Phase Between Pluggable and CPO

NPO places the optical engine right next to the ASIC chip, but not inside the same package. Unlike pluggable, which sits at the front panel of the switch, NPO sits on the board, adjacent to the chip. This shortens the electrical signal path compared to pluggable, improving power and latency, while maintaining flexibility that full CPO integration doesn’t allow.

Two NPO configurations are under discussion.

First, socketed optical engines , which the Open CPX MSA is standardizing. The optical engine plugs into a socket and can be swapped out if it fails. CW plus SiPh MZM is expected to become the dominant combination here.

Second, solderable optical engines , the approach LightSpeed Photonics announced at OFC 2026, where the optical engine is directly soldered to the board. Assembly becomes simpler and thermal performance improves, but replacement is much harder.

The laser and modulation combination in NPO is basically the same as in pluggable CW plus SiPh. The key architectural difference is where the laser lives. In pluggable, the laser is inside the module. In NPO, it’s possible to supply laser light from a separate unit called an ELS (External Laser Source) , physically isolating the heat-sensitive laser from the thermal load of the ASIC. ELS modules typically come in an ELSFP (External Laser Small Form-factor Pluggable) form factor.

CPO: The Real Transition After 2027

CPO integrates the optical engine into the same package as the switch ASIC. The electrical signal path is dramatically shorter than pluggable or NPO, minimizing power and latency.

NVIDIA’s Spectrum-X and Quantum-X CPO switches are the first deployments in this space. CPO penetration in AI data centers sits at roughly 0.5% in 2026, but TrendForce projects it will reach 35% by 2030.

In CPO, the laser must always be external. The ASIC generates enormous heat, and lasers are sensitive to heat, so performance would be unstable inside the same package. That’s why the fundamental CPO architecture is external CW laser (ELS) plus SiPh modulator inside the package.

Current CPO development and deployment uses the following combinations:

Single-wavelength CW plus MRM (first-generation CPO). NVIDIA places a 200G PAM4 micro-ring modulator on a SiPh die on top of TSMC’s COUPE (Compact Universal Photonic Engine) platform. This is the first CPO architecture to deploy. Broadcom also has COUPE-based CPO on its roadmap.

Single-wavelength CW plus MZM (first-generation CPO alternative). A variant using MZM instead of MRM. MZM is larger than MRM, but wavelength stability is higher and temperature sensitivity is lower. Broadcom is pursuing this in parallel.

VCSEL plus direct modulation (short-range CPO). Coherent’s multimode VCSEL-based socketed CPO demonstrated at OFC 2026. Range is limited to multimode fiber distances, but for intra-rack deployments, VCSEL’s low-power advantage is meaningful.

Multi-wavelength CW plus WDM (second-generation CPO, 2027 to 2028 and beyond). Multiple wavelengths on one fiber can dramatically increase bandwidth density while reducing fiber count. Scintil Photonics (LEAF Light, the world’s first single-chip multi-wavelength laser source), Ayar Labs (SuperNova), Ranovus (Quantum Dot multi-wavelength laser), and Xscape Photonics (programmable comb laser) are all targeting this space. The OCI MSA is defining an NRZ plus WDM-based, “silicon-centric” optical standard, which means these startups’ technologies could become core to the standard architecture.

Future: TFLN modulator (post-3.2T). When 400G/lane and beyond is required in the 3.2T era, TFLN modulators could break through the bandwidth limits of silicon MZM and MRM. POET plus QCi are developing a TFLN-based 3.2T optical engine targeting completion in the second half of 2026.

4. The Player Map: Who Owns Which Technology

Here’s where each company sits across the laser technologies and light source types covered in this article. Many players span multiple technologies. That’s the nature of this market: a handful of large players with full stacks, alongside a large number of startups going all-in on one technology.

The EML Camp: Current King, but Surrounded

EML is the core light source in today’s 800G/1.6T pluggable modules. Demand is exploding, supply is tight, and cash is flowing. Right now.

Coherent (COHR): The closest thing to a full-stack company in this space, with EML, CW, VCSEL, SiPh PIC, and photodiode capabilities all in-house. The former II-VI’s acquisition of Coherent completed the vertical integration. Coherent was the only company at OFC 2026 to simultaneously demonstrate 1.6T transceivers using four different technologies: SiPh, InP CW, InP EML, and GaAs VCSEL. They also showed compatibility with three different DSP vendor solutions. They operate and are expanding three 6-inch InP fabs in Järfälla (Sweden), Sherman (Texas), and Zürich (Switzerland). New products include a 400mW CW laser and 200G/400G photodiodes. OFC 2026 also featured a 400G Differential EML and 400G silicon PIC MZM for 3.2T applications. Founding member of both XPO MSA and Open CPX MSA. Received NVIDIA’s $2B investment at $257 per share.

Lumentum (LITE): A critical EML supplier. Lumentum lasers go into NVIDIA’s Spectrum-X switches. Received NVIDIA’s $2B investment, with confirmed plans for a new device fab in the United States and a multi-billion dollar purchase commitment included. Not the same full-stack breadth as Coherent, but the laser component technology is best-in-class. The investment price of $695 per share reflects the market pricing in a premium for Lumentum’s near-monopoly position in laser supply.

Mitsubishi Electric, Sumitomo Electric: Japan’s core EML suppliers. Both are among the companies targeted in NVIDIA’s capacity lock-in. Mitsubishi Electric, in particular, has a partnership with POET Technologies for joint optical module development.

CW Laser plus Silicon Photonics Camp: EML’s Challenger

EML scarcity is what has driven this camp’s explosive growth. The ecosystem breaks into three layers: CW laser suppliers, SiPh PIC designers, and the companies that assemble both into finished modules.

CW laser supply: Coherent and Lumentum both produce EML and CW, straddling both camps. China’s Yuanjie and Shijia are mass-producing CW lasers at scale. Sweden’s Sivers Semiconductors (SIVE.ST) specializes in DFB lasers, supplying laser arrays for CW and ELS applications. They have partnerships with POET Technologies, O-Net Technologies, and Enablence for co-developing ELS modules, and are reported to supply lasers to Ayar Labs as well. At OFC 2026, they announced a CPO-targeting ELS module jointly with O-Net and Enablence.

SiPh PIC design and manufacturing: Marvell (MRVL) is at the front. Their 1.6T SiPh light engine won the Lightwave Innovation Reviews 2026 award. They also demonstrated a 6.4T 3D SiPh engine (32 channels at 200G), with TIA and driver integrated in the same device.

OpenLight , spun out of Juniper Networks in 2022, holds a distinctive position. They use heterogeneous integration to put III-V material on silicon, integrating a laser, EA modulator, amplifier, and photodetector into a single SiPh chip. No external CW laser required. At OFC 2026, they demonstrated a 400G/lane EA modulator combined with a MACOM driver, and also showed a 1.6T DR8 evaluation board connected to a Marvell Ara DSP. Not yet at mass production, but among the most advanced commercial players pursuing laser integration on silicon.

SiPh module assembly and shipping: China Innolight is the largest shipper of SiPh-based 800G/1.6T modules, aggressively lowering module prices by sourcing CW lasers externally. AOI (Applied Optoelectronics, AAOI) is preparing for CPO/NPO architectures and demonstrated 1.6T transceivers and 6.4T on-board optics at OFC 2026, along with a new narrow-linewidth pump laser.

MACOM (MTSI): Doesn’t make lasers directly, but supplies 200G photodiodes, high-speed drivers, and TIAs that are critical components in the SiPh ecosystem. MACOM drivers appeared in OpenLight’s OFC 2026 demonstrations.

The VCSEL Camp: Short Range and the Scale-Up Future

Coherent dominates here too. The economies of scale built from supplying iPhone Face ID are being converted into 100G and 200G data center VCSELs. At OFC 2026, they demonstrated a 200G GaAs VCSEL-based 1.6T transceiver and a multimode VCSEL-based socketed CPO.

Broadcom also develops 100G VCSELs in-house for its pluggable modules.

The VCSEL market is close to a duopoly between Coherent and Broadcom, with Lumentum maintaining a VCSEL portfolio as well.

Multi-Wavelength Laser and CPO Light Source Camp: Betting on the Next Generation

CPO requires lasers that emit multiple wavelengths simultaneously. In WDM-based CPO, putting multiple wavelengths on a single fiber reduces fiber count while increasing bandwidth density. Several startups are competing for this multi-wavelength source market.

Ayar Labs: TeraPHY chiplet and SuperNova multi-wavelength source (16 wavelengths, 16 ports). The chiplet attaches directly next to an ASIC or GPU to provide optical I/O. Compliant with CW-WDM and UCIe (Universal Chiplet Interconnect Express, the standard chiplet interconnect interface). Invested in by NVIDIA, AMD, and Intel ($155M Series D). Transitioning its roadmap from GlobalFoundries Fotonix to TSMC COUPE. The most advanced player in optical chiplets.

Scintil Photonics: Based in Grenoble, France. LEAF Light, claimed to be the world’s first single-chip multi-wavelength laser source, targeting DWDM (Dense Wavelength Division Multiplexing) CPO. Uses a proprietary SHIP (SCINTIL Heterogeneous Integrated Photonics) process to integrate III-V epitaxially on SiPh. Foundried at Tower Semiconductor. Received NVIDIA investment in September 2025. Aiming squarely at WDM-based CPO deployment in the 2027 to 2028 timeframe. Announced a DWDM laser source evaluation kit at OFC 2026.

Xscape Photonics: Emerged from stealth in late 2024. Core technology is a programmable comb laser : a single external CW laser optically pumps multiple wavelengths (4 to 16, ultimately up to 128) on-chip simultaneously. Fabricated on Tower Semiconductor’s PH18 platform, and fully compatible with existing SiPh modulators and detectors. The differentiation: multi-wavelength output without needing EML or separate III-V integration. Closed a $37M round just before OFC 2026 for a total Series A of $81M, with NVIDIA among its investors. Announced their 8-wavelength ELSFP product “FalconX,” directly targeting GPU-to-GPU and GPU-to-HBM optical links.

Ranovus: Based in Ottawa, Canada. Quantum Dot multi-wavelength laser technology. Quantum dots are nanometer-scale semiconductor crystals that emit different wavelengths depending on their size, enabling simultaneous multi-wavelength output. Odin CPO 3.0 platform targeting 4pJ/bit including the laser. Collaborating with MediaTek on 6.4T CPO development.

Optical Integration Platforms: The Companies That Assemble the Lasers

These companies don’t make lasers, but they integrate lasers with other optical components on a single platform to create a finished “optical engine.” In the laser war, their role is to answer the question of how to integrate lasers into a manufacturable, scalable form.

POET Technologies (POET): Core technology is the Optical Interposer : a silicon wafer with optical waveguides, filters, and copper/aluminum interconnects built in, onto which InP lasers, photodetectors, and driver ICs are placed. The key differentiator is passive alignment. Traditional optical assembly requires active alignment of laser to fiber at micrometer precision. POET’s interposer is designed so that components placed by pick-and-place at the wafer level automatically align. Wire bonds are also eliminated. The result is a CMOS-compatible mass-production-friendly structure.

Products include Teralight (1.6T transceiver optical engine, Lightwave Innovation Reviews 2026 winner) and Blazar (high-power multi-channel ELS using wafer-level chip-scale technology to effectively scale InP supply). At OFC 2026, they also unveiled a next-generation ELS called Starlight , with live demonstrations of both products. It was POET’s largest OFC booth ever.

The partner network is broad: Foxconn , Luxshare (optical module assembly), LITEON Technology (co-development with prototype late 2026, mass production 2027), Lessengers (1.6T 2×DR4 transceiver co-development, samples Q2 2026), Sivers Semiconductors (DFB laser-integrated light engine), QCi/Quantum Computing (TFLN modulator-based 3.2T optical engine), and Mitsubishi Electric. Cash position of $150M with no debt. That said, revenue remains negligible ($298K in Q3 2025), and production validation remains the critical milestone.

Lightmatter: Passage 3D CPO platform. Optical engine on a photonic interposer, manufactured in collaboration with GUC (Global Unichip Corp., a TSMC subsidiary) for hyperscaler CPO solutions. Founding member of OCI MSA. Long-term vision is “photonic fabric,” including optical switching.

Nubis Communications: A startup specializing in rack-to-rack interconnect optical engines. Initial product was a 16x112Gbps (1.6Tbps) chiplet. Acquired by Ciena in 2025. Combined with Ciena’s Hyper-Rail scale-across architecture, the acquisition adds CPO capability to Ciena’s portfolio.

LightSpeed Photonics: Announced what it claims is the industry’s first solderable near-packaged optical interconnect technology at OFC 2026. Unlike the socket-based approach typical of NPO, this method solders the optical engine directly to the substrate, simplifying assembly and improving thermal performance.

Photon Bridge: Demonstrated wafer-scale laser production at OFC 2026, with 30mW output per channel. A new entrant, but the direction of scaling laser production to wafer level is drawing attention from the industry.

Upstream: Materials, Foundries, and Equipment

These are the picks-and-shovels players in the laser war. Regardless of which laser technology wins, upstream benefits.

AXT (AXTI): InP substrate supplier. EML, CW lasers, and photodiodes all start with InP epitaxy. When laser demand rises, InP substrate demand rises with it.

IntelliEPI (iET), VPEC: InP epitaxy foundries. As laser companies redirect their own fab capacity to laser production, outsourcing epitaxy to these shops creates a spillover benefit for them.

Tower Semiconductor: The leading optical foundry. Offers SiPh plus III-V integration on its PH18 platform. Foundry partner for both Scintil Photonics and Xscape Photonics. TSMC’s entry into the SiPh market through COUPE has introduced competition, but Tower’s accumulated process expertise doesn’t disappear easily.

TSMC: Entering the SiPh market through COUPE (Compact Universal Photonic Engine) 3D packaging. NVIDIA’s CPO switch optical engines are fabricated on COUPE. Ayar Labs also shifted its roadmap to COUPE. Avicena’s MicroLED photodetector arrays are also made at TSMC. The dominant logic foundry is extending its reach into photonics.

GlobalFoundries: Fotonix platform for monolithic SiPh integration. Ayar Labs’ first-generation TeraPHY chiplet was made here. Less advanced than TSMC COUPE in terms of node access, but more mature in photonics-specific process development.

CEA-Leti plus NcodiN: Announced at OFC 2026 a collaboration to industrialize optical interposers on 300mm photonics processes. A European public research institution (CEA-Leti) and a startup (NcodiN) declaring they will mass-produce next-generation optical interposers at 300mm wafer scale. Also a European push toward monolithic III-V on silicon.

Fabrinet (FN): The largest contract manufacturer for optical modules and transceivers. Assembles transceivers at scale for Coherent, Lumentum, and others. Doesn’t own laser technology, but every laser camp’s production volume flows through Fabrinet. Structured to benefit regardless of which technology wins.

5. The Investment View: Who Wins

Near Term (2026 to 2027)

EML supply remains constrained. Pluggable modules still account for more than 90% of the market. The direct beneficiaries in this window are Lumentum and Coherent. NVIDIA’s $2B investments and multi-billion dollar purchase commitments give both companies revenue visibility extending well into 2027 and beyond. Broadcom bundles its own EML and VCSEL capability with switching ASICs to capture system-level revenue.

LPO adoption will expand, reducing per-transceiver power and cost, but this is a negative for DSP vendors (Broadcom and Marvell’s DSP businesses) and doesn’t reduce laser demand. If anything, lower unit prices from LPO drive higher module volumes, sustaining or increasing laser demand.

On the upstream side, AXT benefits from rising InP substrate demand. Epitaxy foundries IntelliEPI and VPEC benefit for the same reason.

Medium Term (2027 to 2029)

CW plus SiPh begins taking real share from EML. Once SiPh module penetration crosses 30%, the game changes. Marvell (SiPh engine design) and China Innolight (SiPh module mass production) are the primary players for this phase.

Early CPO adoption begins. When NVIDIA’s Rubin-generation scale-out CPO goes into production deployment, the external laser source market opens. As the TSMC COUPE-based optical engine ecosystem forms, Tower Semiconductor and GlobalFoundries also benefit.

The three MSA standards will crystallize, and which laser architecture “wins” will become increasingly clear during this period.

Long Term (2029 and Beyond)

CPO and OIO (Optical I/O, directly integrating optical I/O into ASICs or GPUs) scale up for real. The era of multi-wavelength lasers arrives: Scintil, Ranovus, and Ayar Labs. WDM-based CPO delivers higher bandwidth density than single-wavelength CPO, and putting multiple wavelengths on one fiber reduces fiber attachment count.

And the most fundamental technology shift could arrive during this period: Monolithic III-V on Silicon. Directly growing III-V compound semiconductors (InP, GaAs, etc.) on silicon wafers via epitaxial growth, embedding lasers inside SiPh chips.

Why does this matter? Today’s CW plus SiPh architecture has one particularly painful process step: bonding a separately fabricated InP laser chip to a SiPh chip and aligning them optically with micrometer precision. That process is slow, expensive, and low-yield. Monolithic integration eliminates the bonding and alignment step entirely. If you can grow laser material directly on a 300mm silicon wafer via MOCVD (Metal-Organic Chemical Vapor Deposition), you can leverage the entire CMOS fab’s mass-production infrastructure. No external laser needed. The ultimate cost reduction.

It’s still largely in research territory, and overcoming the lattice mismatch between silicon and III-V is the central technical challenge. But if it reaches production, the line between “laser company” and “semiconductor company” disappears entirely. The laser becomes part of the silicon chip, not a separate component. Logic foundries like TSMC, Intel, and GlobalFoundries would be in the business of making lasers.

At that point, the current pluggable transceiver market structure could come apart. The EML that lived inside pluggables would evolve to external laser sources, then to co-packaged lasers, and ultimately to monolithic integrated lasers. Each step reshapes the value chain.

Risks

There are variables you can’t afford to ignore. First is the hyperscaler capex cycle. Some analysts expect one round of 20 to 30% capex pullback as AI infrastructure investment pauses to breathe. That introduces volume forecast uncertainty for laser companies.

Technology transition timing is also a risk. If CPO adoption comes slower than expected, pluggable modules (and EML) have a longer runway, and CW plus SiPh penetration slows. If CPO comes faster than expected, pluggable-focused companies take a hit.

The pricing aggression from Chinese SiPh players is another variable. China Innolight and others are pushing SiPh module prices lower aggressively, which could compress margins for US and European laser companies.

Whoever Controls the Light Controls AI

The evolution of data center optics compresses into four phases.

Today is phase one: EML-based pluggable modules are the mainstream.

Phase two is already running in parallel: CW plus SiPh is pressing in to address EML scarcity.

Phase three begins around 2027 to 2028: CPO and external laser sources get standardized, and the laser moves outside the ASIC.

Phase four could arrive post-2030: III-V grown directly on silicon, with the laser embedded inside the chip.

HBM broke the memory bottleneck. Lasers have to break the I/O bottleneck before AI infrastructure can move to the next level. NVIDIA’s $4B bet, and the three MSAs that appeared simultaneously at OFC 2026, are proof that the whole industry has accepted this transition.

One question remains.

Who makes the light. The answer will determine who wins the next decade.
