On May 6, the KOSPI broke through 7,000. Samsung Electronics joined the trillion-dollar market cap club. SK hynix hit an all-time high. Foreign investors net-bought over 3 trillion won in a single day.

The numbers matter less than the direction. Global investors are turning their attention to Korea. The country’s position in the AI semiconductor supply chain is being re-rated, from “memory cycle trade” to “structural pillar of AI infrastructure.”

But most investors still stop at Samsung Electronics and SK hynix. Below these two giants, dozens of companies form the supply chain across equipment, materials, test, packaging, power, and optics, each connected to entirely different demand drivers. That part is not well understood.

This article is a map that breaks that supply chain apart.

It covers why Korea is still undervalued in AI semiconductor investing, where the opportunities are, and what the market is still missing from the vantage point of Silicon Valley. It takes the single basket called "semiconductor-related stocks" and splits it into four categories, covering the story, the names, what the market is missing, and finally, the companies I am watching most closely in each category.

Why Global Investors Are Pouring Into Korea

Earnings Have Reached the Same Tier as Global Big Tech

Samsung Electronics posted 57.2 trillion won in operating profit for Q1. That puts it in the same earnings tier as Nvidia, Microsoft, and Apple. It was the first time in the history of Korean corporations that quarterly revenue exceeded 100 trillion won and operating profit exceeded 50 trillion won simultaneously. SK hynix also posted a record Q1 operating profit of 37.6 trillion won at a 72% operating margin.

Combined Q1 operating profit for the two companies: 94.8 trillion won. Among major KOSPI-listed companies that have reported Q1 earnings, these two account for an overwhelming share.

And yet the valuation relative to these earnings is still low. Samsung Electronics far exceeded TSMC in Q1 operating profit. But its market cap is roughly 50% of TSMC’s.

Even combining Samsung and SK hynix, their Q1 earnings significantly exceeded TSMC’s, but as of mid-April their combined market cap was only around 70% of TSMC’s. Global investors saw these numbers and started looking at Korea again.

Memory Has Become Core AI Infrastructure

The center of gravity in AI is shifting from Training to Inference. If Training was a fight centered on massive compute, Inference brings memory bandwidth, capacity, and power efficiency into the picture as critical variables.

Serving large language models in real time requires loading tens of billions of parameters into GPU memory, and the memory needed scales proportionally with user count. That memory is HBM.

HBM is an area where Korea is overwhelmingly strong. SK hynix and Samsung Electronics combined account for well over half the global market. Looking at DRAM overall, Samsung and SK hynix are core suppliers covering the majority of global share.

The Cloud Big 4 (Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta) are expected to push 2026 CAPEX past $700 billion. Not all of that goes to GPUs. Data centers, power, networking, cooling, and memory all grow together. Within that, HBM and high-capacity server memory have established themselves as core components of AI infrastructure. The bigger the AI market gets, the stronger Korea’s memory companies’ leverage becomes.

Transitioning from Cycle to Structure

The most fundamental reason global investors are pouring into Korea is this: at least in HBM and high-value AI server memory, a different structure is emerging compared to the old commodity DRAM cycle.

In the past, DRAM prices surged and crashed with supply and demand. The market learned this pattern and applied cyclical multiples to memory companies. What is happening now is different. Long-term supply agreements spanning 3 to 5 years are being signed with Big Tech.

This is an evolution toward TSMC-style order-based production. Earnings volatility is dampening and visibility is expanding. Investors are starting to recognize this, and capital is flowing in, but valuations have not fully caught up. A gap remains between cyclical multiples and the multiples appropriate for structural demand companies. The narrowing of that gap is the investment opportunity.

Things Listed Here That You Cannot Buy Elsewhere

There are many markets where you can invest in AI semiconductors. But in most of them, you only get broad exposure. Buy the GPU designer and you are betting on AI as a whole. Buy the big equipment company and you are betting on semiconductor capex as a whole.

Stocks that give you pure exposure to specific bottlenecks like “HBM test complexity increase” or “ultra-high-voltage transformer supply imbalance” exist in some other markets, but it is rare to find a market like Korea where bottlenecks around AI memory and power infrastructure are tightly mapped across multiple independent listed companies.

Korea is different.

HBM test sockets, probe cards, burn-in equipment, ultra-high-voltage transformers, AI server MLCCs. Niche companies connected to AI infrastructure bottlenecks exist as independent listed entities.

If the U.S. market is the “chip + cloud” layer of AI, Korea is the “memory + infrastructure” layer. They sit on the same AI capex but give access to a different tier, and within that tier you can pick individual bottlenecks.

The tightening of U.S. restrictions on China is also pushing up the global share of Korean companies, reinforcing this structure.

What You Need to Know Before Coming In

Money flowing in does not mean risks are absent.

The Korea Discount is real.

Chaebol governance, disregard for minority shareholder rights, low dividend payout ratios, opaque decision-making. The reason Samsung Electronics trades at a lower valuation than TSMC is not just about cycle perception. There is a governance discount baked in. Recent moves around the Value-Up Program and increased shareholder returns are encouraging, but this is not a problem that changes overnight.

You need to know this going in.

This is an illustrative example to help readers understand the structure.

That said, the discount does not apply equally to every stock. Large conglomerates with significant owner risk and mid/small-cap equipment and component companies that live on technology carry different types of discount. So investing in Korea’s AI semiconductor space is not simply about “it’s cheap.” It is a game of understanding why it is cheap and identifying which stocks can escape that discount.

Structure of This Map

This article divides the Korean AI semiconductor ecosystem into four categories. Even stocks grouped under the same “semiconductor-related” label move at entirely different timings and in different directions depending on which demand they connect to.

Category 1. Samsung Electronics Ecosystem.

Stocks that move when Samsung’s capex moves. This is the segment where the IDM’s power drives memory, HBM4, foundry, and AVP (Advanced Packaging) simultaneously.

Category 2. SK hynix Ecosystem.

Stocks that ride along when SK hynix’s HBM sells. TC bonding, test, substrates. Test complexity increases non-linearly as stack count rises, and the market has not yet fully priced this in.

Category 3. Data Center Power Infrastructure.

Stocks that benefit when AI data centers get built. If HBM is the bottleneck inside the server, transformers are the bottleneck before the server even turns on. The category with the highest earnings visibility in this map.

Category 4. AI Server Components & Optical Interconnects.

Stocks that rise when the components connecting chip to chip inside the server get upgraded. MLCCs, substrates, optical transceivers. Not the chip itself, but the infrastructure required for the chip to function.

Below, each category covers why this space deserves attention now, which stocks connect to which bottlenecks, what the market is missing, and the conditions under which the thesis breaks, all at once. Then, at the end, I walk through the names I am watching most closely in each category. The aim is to surface things hidden behind the market's existing frames, things that only become visible with deeper technical understanding, and use them to find investment opportunities.

Investment Map at a Glance

Category 1: Samsung Electronics Ecosystem

To understand Samsung Electronics, you have to start with the IDM (Integrated Device Manufacturer) structure. Samsung is the only company in the world that owns memory, foundry, and advanced packaging all in-house. TSMC only does foundry. SK hynix only does memory. Intel is an IDM but exited memory. Samsung alone can provide a turnkey service: fabricate the logic chip, stack HBM next to it, and package both together.

This structure has long been treated as a discount factor. “They do everything, so they do nothing well.” But what is happening now is the first time the IDM structure is converting into a premium.

_ Investing in Samsung is not one bet. It is two different bets._

Thesis A: Memory + HBM catch-up.

The DS division posted 53.7 trillion won in Q1 operating profit. An all-time record. DRAM prices are rising, HBM volume is increasing, and long-term supply agreements are being signed.

Samsung is in the qualification process for NVIDIA’s next-generation GPU with HBM4, and plans to more than triple HBM revenue year-over-year. The transition to leading-edge memory nodes (1c/1d-nano DRAM, 300+ layer V-NAND) is underway, bringing equipment replacement demand with it. The market broadly recognizes this direction.

Thesis B: Foundry + AVP turnaround. This is the contrarian play. Signals are emerging that Samsung Foundry’s 2nm GAA process yields are improving. As TSMC raises leading-edge pricing year after year, a structural need exists among fabless customers looking for a “TSMC alternative.”

Samsung is preparing SF2Z, a 2nm process with BSPDN (backside power delivery network) technology targeted for 2027. From the customer’s perspective, Samsung’s pitch is that its integrated AI solution (foundry + memory + packaging turnkey) can shorten development timelines.

The Tesla AI6 win is a signal that this turnkey strategy is translating into real customer engagement.

Samsung is also strengthening 2.5D/3D packaging and heterogeneous integration through the AVP organization within the DS division. When AVP starts generating meaningful revenue, packaging equipment and materials demand within the Samsung ecosystem becomes a new capex axis.

What the market is missing. The market views Samsung Foundry through a single frame: “2nm yield.” Yields are not there yet, so the entire related stock group stays suppressed. But AVP is a separate business from foundry yield.

Even if yields are not ready today, equipment and materials demand for AVP is generated independently. The fact that this new axis is growing has not been priced in. While everyone stares at 2nm yield, another axis is rising.

Stocks That Benefit from This Story

Whether the weight falls on Thesis A (memory/HBM) or Thesis B (foundry/AVP) changes the stock selection. Some straddle both.

Wonik IPS (KRX: 240810). Supplies CVD/ALD equipment to both Samsung’s memory and foundry lines. One of the first equipment companies to receive orders when Samsung capex ramps. Estimated Samsung revenue exposure exceeds 70%, which is both a strength and a risk. When Samsung invests, Wonik gets direct upside. When Samsung delays, Wonik takes a direct hit. This stock straddles both Thesis A and Thesis B, making it the first name to look at for a broad bet on the Samsung Electronics ecosystem.

Jusung Engineering (KRX: 036930). Holds a distinctive position in ALD (atomic layer deposition) equipment. The notable point is that it straddles both Samsung and SK hynix. It supplies ALD equipment to Samsung’s memory and foundry lines while also seeing ALD orders materialize from SK hynix’s leading-edge DRAM node transitions.

Significant China revenue exposure is a double-edged sword under the current U.S.-China regulatory environment, but ALD’s share is expanding rapidly in 300+ layer V-NAND, meaning the technical demand itself is structural. One of the few names that rides Category 1 and Category 2 simultaneously.

HPSP (KRX: 403870). Has built a near-monopoly position in high-pressure hydrogen annealing (HPA) equipment. Operating margin above 50%. That margin comes from the position itself. High foundry exposure makes this the maximum leverage play on Thesis B. If the probability of Samsung Foundry’s recovery ticks up even slightly, the multiple can open up early.

KC Tech (KRX: 281820). Supplies CMP equipment and slurry. CMP goes into every semiconductor process, so this name does not depend on a specific scenario. But CMP is also essential in HBM’s TSV process. After drilling TSVs, the silicon surface must be planarized, and as stack count increases, so do the number of TSVs and the number of CMP steps. A quiet name that straddles Samsung and SK hynix while also receiving HBM upside.

Doosan Tesna (KRX: 131970). A wafer test specialist. Broke ground on its Pyeongtaek Plant 2 with a 230.3 billion won investment, preparing for expanded system semiconductor volumes. If Samsung Foundry recovers, test volumes increase directly. A name that connects straight to Thesis B. If you believe in the foundry recovery scenario, this is one to watch alongside HPSP.

Hana Micron (KRX: 067310). An OSAT (outsourced semiconductor assembly and test) company specializing in back-end processes, packaging and test.

This is not a company with revenue directly tied to Samsung Foundry.

Its primary revenue comes from outsourced back-end processing of commodity DRAM/NAND for Samsung Memory and SK hynix.

But there is a reason this name belongs in the Samsung ecosystem. The connection I see is this: the more Samsung pushes its strategy of “handling everything from HBM to foundry to packaging under one roof,” the more advanced packaging capacity gets prioritized for high-value integrated products. The result is that commodity memory back-end capacity gets structurally displaced. This is not temporary overflow.

It is a strategic reallocation of priorities.

The more Samsung Foundry succeeds, the more commodity memory OSAT outsourcing grows, and Hana Micron is the primary recipient of that outsourcing. It has both Samsung Electronics and SK hynix as customers, and its Vietnam subsidiary is ramping utilization on dedicated SK hynix lines.

If Wonik IPS is “the company that installs equipment when Samsung builds a fab,” Hana Micron is “the company that gets busier as Samsung Foundry succeeds and memory back-end gets pushed out.” A completely different angle of beneficiary within the same Samsung ecosystem.

Dongjin Semichem (KRX: 005290), Soulbrain (KRX: 357780), Hansol Chemical (KRX: 014680). These three are best viewed as a group. Photoresist, etchant, precursors. Once qualified, they are not easily replaced, and as long as the fab runs, revenue keeps coming. Consumable businesses. They do not offer explosive leverage to capex cycles, but the downside is supported. Many supply both Samsung and SK hynix, so they are tied to overall Korean memory utilization rates rather than any specific scenario. Positions suited for investors who want to reduce volatility.

Triggers and risks for this thesis.

The trigger is Samsung’s 2nm yield improvement translating into actual customer tape-outs and production volume, or AVP being confirmed as an independent capex axis. For this thesis to break, Samsung Foundry would need to fail to land meaningful customers for three or more years while AVP also fails to generate visible revenue.

Category 2: SK hynix Ecosystem

SK hynix’s story is simple. HBM. Q1 operating profit of 37.6 trillion won, 72% operating margin. Global number one in HBM3E, with HBM4 transition underway.

_ The important part is elsewhere._

_ The most undervalued segment of the HBM supply chain is test._

HBM is a structure where DRAM dies are stacked vertically. At 12 layers, 12 dies are connected through TSVs and bonded via thermocompression (TC). The market already recognizes that TC bonder demand rises with HBM expansion. Hanmi Semiconductor’s 5x stock price is proof of that.

But test is different.

As stack count rises, the test burden does not simply scale linearly like bonding passes. Pre-stack KGD screening, post-stack interconnect verification, and stress testing under thermal and power conditions all increase together.

Even if individual die yield is 99%, stacking 12 layers gives 0.99^12 = 88.6%. At 16 layers, 0.99^16 = 85.1%. If even one layer is defective, the remaining good dies are scrapped along with it. In practice, actual HBM yield incorporates redundancy, repair schemes, and process-level screening, so it is not determined by simple multiplication.

But the direction is clear: as stack count increases, the economics of securing KGD become more critical.

Why HBM Is So Hard to Make

HBM is everywhere in the conversation right now. Stock forums, news outlets, casual dinner tables — everyone’s heard that “HBM is in short supply” or “NVIDIA can’t build enough GPUs because of HBM.”

Read full story

What the market is missing. The market has completed its learning as “HBM = bonding equipment.” But the test value chain does not fit this frame.

First, analytical tool limitations.

Brokerages analyze semiconductors through a “capex to orders” lens. Bonding equipment gets order disclosures and fits neatly into this model. Test sockets are not equipment you buy once when expanding a line. They are consumables that wear out and get replaced every day the fab runs. They do not show up in order-tracking models, and there are no disclosures.

Second, value dispersion.

In bonding, Hanmi Semiconductor held near-monopoly share. In test, value splits across burn-in, probe cards, sockets, handlers, and ATE time. There is no “Hanmi Semiconductor of test.” You cannot make a single-stock bet, so the story is not clean and reports do not get written. A basket approach is the right one.

Third, test time as a hidden bottleneck.

As stack count rises, the sequential chain of per-die burn-in, KGD screening, post-stack test, and final test gets longer. Daily throughput per ATE drops, and there are hard limits on how much At-Speed testing can be shortened. Burn-in cannot be reduced.

When this reality sets in, test cost becomes a critical line item governing HBM unit economics and yield economics.

Stocks That Benefit from This Story

Following the HBM manufacturing process reveals the beneficiary names. Walking through in process order.

HBM starts with making the DRAM wafer. Once the wafer comes out, individual dies need to be verified. If you stack a defective die, the good dies stacked with it die too. The first gate is burn-in test. DI Corporation (KRX: 003160) sits here. Its subsidiary Digital Frontier has passed SK hynix qualification for an HBM4 wafer-level burn-in tester. This is equipment that applies high temperature and high voltage to screen out early failures. Starting with HBM4, a new category of liquid-cooled burn-in is opening up. Only dies that pass burn-in move to the next step.

Dies that pass burn-in go through wafer-level functional testing. This is where probe cards are used. LEENO Industrial (KRX: 058470) makes them. Probe pins contact each die on the wafer to inspect electrical characteristics. These are not HBM-specific and are also used in general semiconductor testing, so dependency on the HBM-only scenario is lower. The downside is correspondingly supported.

Only known-good dies (KGD) that pass functional testing move to the stacking process. This is where Hanmi Semiconductor (KRX: 042700) enters. Its TC (thermocompression) bonders bond dies one layer at a time. It supplies SK hynix at near-monopoly levels. This is the stock where the market first recognized HBM upside. It has already risen significantly, but the HBM generational transition (HBM4 to HBM4E) is not finished. Each generation brings higher stack counts, more bonding passes, and repeat bonder orders. The risk is valuation. The current price already has significant HBM4 expectations baked in.

After stacking is complete, the entire stack is tested again. Individual dies were good, but once stacked, TSV connection failures, thermal issues, and interconnect defects can emerge. At this stage, Techwing (KRX: 089030) and its Cube Prober are drawing attention. Traditional handlers test after packaging is complete. The Cube Prober verifies stacks at the post-bonding stage, before final packaging. It has passed SK hynix qualification, and discussions are underway with two additional global HBM manufacturers. As stack count rises, the value of “screening after stacking” grows.

In final package test, the completed HBM goes on an ATE (automatic test equipment) for 100% inspection. The component that physically connects the chip to the ATE is the test socket. ISC (KRX: 095340) makes them. The key point is that test sockets are consumables. They are consumed as long as HBM is produced. Hanmi Semiconductor’s bonders are equipment that customers buy when expanding lines. Once expansion is done, orders stop. ISC’s sockets are different. They wear out and get replaced every day the fab runs. They are consumables directly tied to production volume. The revenue structure is fundamentally different.

HBM stacks that pass testing are mounted onto substrates. Haesung DS (KRX: 195870) supplies lead frames and packaging substrates. It has a dual-driver structure with demand growing from both HBM packages and automotive power semiconductors. No matter how well the dies are stacked and tested, the package is not complete without a substrate to mount them on.

Triggers and risks for this thesis. HBM4 mass production ramp in H2 2026 through 2027 is the re-rating trigger. Three conditions weaken this thesis: HBM4 ramp comes in slower than expected, customers aggressively pressure test socket and burn-in equipment ASPs, or test parallelism improves enough that equipment and consumable demand growth comes in below expectations.

Category 3: Data Center Power Infrastructure

This category looks at AI capex not from the chip side, but from the power infrastructure required to turn those chips on.

The power density of AI data centers is 5 to 10 times that of traditional data centers. When a 1GW-class data center goes up, ultra-high-voltage transformers, switchgear, and power cables are all needed. A significant portion of the capex Big Tech spends on AI flows into power infrastructure. The budget for buying GPUs and the budget for buying transformers come from the same line.

The ultra-high-voltage transformer market has abnormally high barriers to entry. Training a skilled engineer takes 10 years, and even if you build a new factory, you cannot produce without the workforce. With the U.S. excluding Chinese-made transformers, demand for Korean-made units is so strong that buyers voluntarily absorb 18 to 20% tariffs.

What the market is missing. The market views power equipment as industrial cyclicals. “Expensive” is the consensus. The logic is that cyclicals peak and then roll over, so you should not pay a high P/E. This frame is incomplete. Of course, prices and margins will eventually cycle. But the current phase has AI data center buildout, U.S. grid replacement, China exclusion, and long lead times all operating simultaneously, extending visibility beyond what is typical for industrial cycles. Order backlogs are stacked to five or more years, and buyers are absorbing tariffs just to get product. This is closer to “elevated and sustained for years” than “up and then back down.”

Stocks That Benefit from This Story

HD Hyundai Electric (KRX: 267260). Approximately 15% market share in the U.S. ultra-high-voltage transformer market, leading the pack. Q1 orders of $1.79 billion, achieving 42.6% of the full-year target in a single quarter. Order backlog of $7.89 billion. Q1 operating margin of 24.9%. The fact that net profit growth (35.4%) far exceeded revenue growth (2.1%) means the high-margin product mix is improving. The Alabama factory expansion will increase production capacity by 50%, and growing U.S. local production also reduces tariff risk.

A sharp deterioration in earnings looks unlikely. However, the stock price may react earlier to the direction of new order growth and margins than to actual earnings. Even with years of backlog stacked up, the moment new order momentum decelerates, the market starts suspecting peak-out.

Hyosung Heavy Industries (KRX: 298040). Holds approximately 50% of cumulative installed references in the U.S. 765kV ultra-high-voltage transformer market, placing it at number one. Its subsidiary Hyosung HICO Memphis is currently the only dedicated 765kV production facility in the United States. 765kV is the highest voltage class within ultra-high-voltage, and there is only one factory in the U.S. that can make it.

Hitachi is building a second facility in South Boston, but first shipments are not expected until 2028. That means Hyosung is the sole U.S.-based producer in the 765kV market for at least two more years. Order backlog of 13.8 trillion won. Cumulative Memphis factory investment exceeding $300 million. If HD Hyundai Electric is strong in “general ultra-high-voltage,” Hyosung Heavy Industries holds a physical moat as “the only U.S.-based producer at the highest voltage class.”

LS ELECTRIC (KRX: 010120). The fastest-growing among the Big 3. Q1 revenue of 1.38 trillion won and operating profit of 126.6 billion won, both quarterly all-time highs. Year-over-year revenue growth of 33.4% and operating profit growth of 45.3%. North American revenue surged roughly 80% year-over-year to approximately 300 billion won.

Where HD Hyundai Electric and Hyosung Heavy Industries focus on ultra-high-voltage transformers, LS ELECTRIC’s portfolio extends to medium-voltage equipment including switchgear and distribution panels. When a data center goes up, it does not just need transformers. It needs the distribution equipment to route that power.

Combined order backlog across the three companies exceeds 30 trillion won. At least five years of work secured. Timing matters too. The total capacity expansion investment announced by North American transformer OEMs exceeds $3.5 billion, but most of the EHV-class (345kV, 500kV, 765kV) additions come online in 2028.

Expansions arriving in 2026 and 2027 are mostly medium-voltage and distribution-class. Supply is tightest during 2026 and 2027, when demand is strongest, and by 2028 when supply increases, demand may already have stepped up another level. This 1 to 2 year supply-demand mismatch is the structural backdrop supporting power equipment companies’ margins right now.

Iljin Electric (KRX: 103590) is smaller than the Big 3 but produces transformers and power equipment, riding the same cycle. Taihan Cable & Solution (KRX: 001440) and LS Cable & System are power cable companies. Data center construction requires massive quantities of power cables, and demand for submarine cables and ultra-high-voltage transmission cables is also growing alongside AI infrastructure expansion.

Category 4: AI Server Components & Optical Interconnects

This category bundles two different stories. One is the components needed for chips to function inside AI servers (MLCCs, substrates). The other is the optical interconnects connecting server to server. The common thread: “not the chip itself, but the infrastructure surrounding the chip.”

Story A: AI Server Components

A single AI server cannot run on chips alone. No matter how good the GPU is, it will not function without MLCCs to deliver stable power, and it cannot be packaged without a substrate. AI servers use 10 to 15 times more MLCCs than standard servers, and as chips get larger, substrates are being pushed toward larger area and higher layer counts. As chip performance rises, demand for these components scales proportionally.

Samsung Electro-Mechanics (KRX: 009150). Two core businesses.

MLCC. Samsung Electro-Mechanics competes with Japan’s Murata for global number one or two in high-temperature, high-voltage MLCCs for AI servers. Only these two companies in the world can supply this market. It is a duopoly. Reports that Murata is considering MLCC price increases are a signal that supply cannot keep up with demand. AI server MLCC margins are estimated above 30%.

FC-BGA package substrates. FC-BGA is essential for packaging high-performance processors in AI servers. Partnerships have been established with major global AI companies, and the line is approaching full utilization in H2.

And the next catalyst: Glass Substrate.

Existing organic substrates are hitting limits on warpage and thermal expansion as AI chips grow larger. Glass substrates solve this. Samsung Electro-Mechanics has a pilot line running at its Sejong site and has established a joint venture with Sumitomo Chemical Group to internalize the core material, glass core.

Full-scale production is targeted for 2027. Intel continues to push its glass substrate roadmap as well, but Korean companies are ahead on commercialization speed. That said, glass substrates are not yet at a stage of meaningful revenue contribution. The 2027 production target needs to be confirmed through actual customer adoption and yield stabilization.

What the market is missing. The market views Samsung Electro-Mechanics through “MLCC price.” Price goes up, stock is good. Price goes down, stock is bad. This frame obscures the identity-shifting catalyst that is glass substrate. If glass substrates are commercialized, Samsung Electro-Mechanics’ valuation could shift from “MLCC cyclical” to “next-generation packaging platform company.” It is still at the optionality stage, but as long as the market only watches “MLCC price,” this catalyst remains unpriced.

Simmtech (KRX: 222800). Specializes in FC-BGA substrates. Supply is tight and expansion lead times are long, meaning the supply-demand imbalance is expected to persist for a while.

SKC (KRX: 011790). Its subsidiary Absolics is one of the furthest along in the glass substrate commercialization race. Absolics is unlisted, so to bet on the glass substrate theme, you have to buy the parent company, SKC. Absolics completed the world’s first semiconductor glass substrate mass production factory in Georgia, U.S., and received $75 million in CHIPS Act funding.

However, production timing has slipped versus the original plan, which is a risk. Samsung Electro-Mechanics is a latecomer but is closing the gap aggressively by internalizing glass core. The glass substrate market is forming a “first-mover SKC vs. fast-follower Samsung Electro-Mechanics” dynamic.

Doosan Corporation (KRX: 000150). A low-profile name, but its Electronics BG (electronic materials division) plays a critical role in the NVIDIA supply chain. It supplies CCL (copper clad laminate), which is a core material for semiconductor package substrates and server motherboards.

Demand for high-performance CCL is surging as AI server substrates move toward larger area and higher layer counts. The interesting part is the valuation. Analysis suggests that the Electronics BG’s standalone business value alone approaches or exceeds Doosan Corporation’s entire market cap. That means the remaining business units are being discounted.

ISU Petasys (KRX: 007660). Specializes in MLB (multilayer board) for AI servers. Supplies high-layer-count PCBs for networking equipment and servers. If Samsung Electro-Mechanics and Simmtech are “the substrate directly under the chip (FC-BGA),” ISU Petasys is “the substrate holding the entire server together (MLB).” Different layers, but an AI server needs both to function.

Story B: Optical Interconnects

The way servers connect to each other inside data centers is changing. A transition from electrical signal (copper) based to optical signal (optical) based.

As AI cluster scale grows, bandwidth requirements between servers surge. Electrical connections suffer increasing signal loss and power consumption over distance.

Optical interconnects solve this. 800G optical transceivers are the current mainstream, and the generational transition to 1.6T is underway.

Longer term, the direction is CPO (Co-Packaged Optics), where the optical engine is embedded directly inside the switch chip package.

Oi Solution (KRX: 138080). Among Korean optical companies, the name with the clearest optionality for AI data centers and the CPO transition. It is mass-producing 800G and 1.6T optical transceivers and unveiled a CPO-specific external light source module (ELSFP) at OFC 2026.

The biggest differentiator is its internalization of the laser diode (LD) chip.

The risk is that ongoing net losses persist due to interest costs from convertible bond (CB) issuance and derivative valuation losses.

It has turned profitable at the operating income level, but reaching net income profitability will take more time. The scale gap versus global tier 1 players is also a factor.

The market has dismissed Korean optical stocks as “too small versus global peers,” but “small company” and “company with technical differentiation” are not the same thing.

Optosys (KRX: 109080). An optical link solutions company. Financially solid with consistent shareholder returns. Its direct positioning in the high-speed optical transceiver market for AI data centers is weaker than Oi Solution’s, but it is a name to watch for the combination of stability from its existing video transmission business plus expansion potential from its optical technology base into data centers.

Lightron (KRX: 069540). Produces optical components and modules. As a component supplier rather than a finished product (transceiver) company, it is tied more to overall volume expansion than direct generational transition upside.

_ Optical interconnects are the watchlist zone of this category._

The direction is right.

The shift from copper to optical inside data centers is a technically certain trend.

The issue is timing and scale.

Whether CPO reaches mainstream hyperscaler adoption in 2027 or 2030 completely changes the investment outcome.

And even when CPO ramps, the fact that Korean companies are smaller in scale than global tier 1 players (Coherent, Lumentum) is real.

Technical differentiation alone is not enough. The capacity to take volume and the customer relationships to back it up must follow for earnings to materialize.

At this stage, “the direction looks right so size up gradually as confirmation comes” is the better approach than “go big on conviction.”

Triggers and risks for this thesis. For AI server components, MLCC price increases and FC-BGA supply tightness are near-term triggers.

Glass substrate 2027 production and CPO hyperscaler adoption are medium-term triggers.

For this thesis to break, MLCC cycle expectations would need to already be priced into the stock, glass substrate production would need to keep getting delayed, or CPO adoption would need to come in later than expected.

Core picks are names with high earnings visibility that can survive across multiple scenarios. Asymmetric picks are names the market may re-rate if the thesis is right, but with significant risks that need confirmation.

Samsung Electronics Ecosystem.

_ Core:_ Wonik IPS (KRX: 240810).

The equipment company that receives orders first when Samsung capex opens up. Because it supplies CVD/ALD equipment to both memory and foundry, it benefits regardless of whether Thesis A (memory) or Thesis B (foundry) moves first. The most direct proxy if you are entering the Samsung ecosystem. The risk is Samsung revenue exposure above 70%. If Samsung delays investment, it takes a direct hit. But that concentration is exactly why it is the Core pick for this category. It is a Samsung ecosystem bet, so it should be tied to Samsung.

_ Core:_ KC Tech (KRX: 281820).

Different in character from Wonik IPS.

CMP is a foundational process used across memory, foundry, and advanced packaging, so it receives upside from SK hynix as well, not just Samsung. Strictly speaking, it is closer to a “Korean semiconductor overall” broad pick than a “Samsung ecosystem” pick. So the explosive upside within the Samsung ecosystem is lower than Wonik IPS. In exchange, it survives across any scenario. If Wonik IPS is “the name that moves first when Samsung moves,” KC Tech is “the name that holds up even when Samsung does not move.”

_ Asymmetric:_ HPSP (KRX: 403870).

Operating margin above 50%, but the stock is suppressed. The reason is singular: the market is skeptical about Samsung Foundry. High foundry exposure means even a slight uptick in Samsung Foundry recovery probability can open up the multiple. The chain is short. “Samsung Foundry recovers, HPSP orders increase.” If wrong, you wait, but TSMC-directed revenue keeps the floor from breaking.

_ Asymmetric:_ Hana Micron (KRX: 067310).

A completely different angle from HPSP. As Samsung Foundry succeeds, advanced packaging capacity gets prioritized for high-value products, and commodity memory back-end gets outsourced through structural reallocation. Hana Micron is the primary recipient. The chain is long. “Samsung Foundry succeeds, internal capacity reallocates, OSAT overflow, Hana Micron benefits.” Four steps. But if the market has not yet seen this connection, it is also the name with the most re-rating room.

SK hynix Ecosystem.

_ Core:_ ISC (KRX: 095340).

The cleanest thesis in this category. Test sockets are not equipment sold once during line expansion. They are components consumed repeatedly as long as production continues. As HBM volume grows, test time lengthens, and KGD and final package test become more critical, ISC benefits from utilization rather than capex. This is not an HBM capex bet. It is closer to an AI memory utilization bet.

_ Core:_ Hanmi Semiconductor (KRX: 042700).

Already up big. “Is it right to put this as Core now?” is a fair question.

I keep it here because the HBM generational transition is not over. Each transition from HBM4 to HBM4E brings higher stack counts, more bonding passes, and repeat bonder orders. This is not one-time upside but structural demand that recurs with each generation.

If ISC is “consumables tied to production volume,” Hanmi Semiconductor is “equipment tied to generational transitions.” Two names within the same HBM ecosystem that benefit at different timings.

That said, significant HBM4 expectations are already priced into the current stock.

_ Asymmetric:_ DI Corporation (KRX: 003160).

A name positioned at the front end of the HBM test bottleneck: burn-in. As KGD verification becomes more critical with HBM4, the strategic value of burn-in equipment rises. The potential to open a new category in liquid-cooled burn-in is the draw. But equipment names are sensitive to order timing and customer adoption decisions. Higher risk than ISC, but potentially bigger moves if the thesis lands.

_ Asymmetric:_ Techwing (KRX: 089030).

If DI Corporation covers burn-in (pre-stack), Techwing covers the Cube Prober (post-stack). This is equipment that verifies stacks at the post-bonding stage, and its value grows as stack count rises. It has passed SK hynix qualification and is in discussions with additional global HBM manufacturers. Together with DI Corporation, it covers two new equipment categories that HBM4 is opening up.

The strength here is not narrative but numbers. $7.89 billion order backlog, 24.9% operating margin, 15% U.S. ultra-high-voltage transformer market share, expanding local production. If this thesis is right, the market could re-rate Hyosung from a simple industrial name to a U.S. ultra-high-voltage grid capacity play. But with elevated expectations, any inflection in new order growth or margin direction can move the stock first. I only put one Asymmetric here because there is no need to force a second.

AI Server Components & Optical Interconnects.

_ Core: *_*Samsung Electro-Mechanics (KRX: 009150).

The market views it as an MLCC cyclical, but in AI servers, both MLCC volume and product mix are different. Layer in FC-BGA and glass substrate optionality, and the balance between current earnings base and future optionality is the best in this category.

The name with the clearest CPO transition optionality among Korean optical stocks. It has LD chip internalization and a CPO external light source module as differentiators.

High risk, high reward until hyperscaler adoption and net income profitability are confirmed.

Glass substrate optionality through subsidiary Absolics.

Conclusion

The core message of this article is to stop viewing Korean semiconductors as a single theme.

Samsung Electronics and SK hynix are just the starting point. Below them are points where AI capex decomposes into real bottlenecks: bonding, test, CMP, substrate, transformer, MLCC, optics. The appeal of the Korean market is that these bottlenecks are expressed as independent listed companies.

In each category, the market is looking through familiar frames. “2nm yield,” “bonding equipment,” “cyclical,” “MLCC price.”

The changes that sit outside those frames (AVP, test consumables, structural supply-demand imbalance, glass substrate) are not yet fully priced in. Looking at consumables when the market counts capex orders. Capturing a basket when the market hunts for a single name. Seeing structure when the market sees a cycle. Watching the next generation when the market watches the current product. Standing outside the frame is how you find the opportunity.

The opportunity in AI semiconductor investing lies not in “what has already risen” but in “where the next bottleneck is moving.” I hope this map serves as a starting point for that judgment.
