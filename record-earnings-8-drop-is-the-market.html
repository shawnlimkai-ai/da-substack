1. Why an 8% drop on a record print

Teradyne reported its largest quarter ever on April 28. Q1 2026 revenue came in at $1.282B, up 87% year over year. Non-GAAP EPS hit $2.56, beating the $2.11 consensus by 21%. In the press release, CEO Greg Smith said about 70% of the quarter’s revenue was linked to AI demand.

By the numbers it was hard to find a flaw. The market reaction said the opposite. After the print Teradyne fell about 8.7% in the after-hours session. Combined with the 5.4% drop already booked in the regular session, the one-day selling pressure was significant.

The reason isn’t that Q1 was bad. Q1 was too good. The problem is what comes next.

Teradyne guided Q2 revenue to a range of $1.15B to $1.25B. Midpoint is roughly $1.2B. In absolute terms, still very strong. Year over year, that’s 75%-plus growth. In the ATE industry, that kind of growth rate isn’t common.

But the market wasn’t looking at the absolute number. It was looking at the direction. Q1 revenue was $1.282B. Q2 guidance midpoint is $1.2B. Translated into plain English, the company essentially said, “Next quarter is still good, but it could be lower than this one.” That’s a sequential decline. Year over year still high growth, but quarter over quarter the line is bending down.

In a normal setup that’s not a big deal. But Teradyne had already run hard on AI test optimism. The stock was up more than 400% over the past year and had nearly doubled YTD. When a name has already moved that much, the bar shifts. Good earnings aren’t enough. The market also wants confidence that the next quarter will be better. The Q2 guide didn’t deliver that confidence.

Then OpenAI added fuel. The same day, the WSJ reported that OpenAI was missing its revenue targets. SoftBank fell nearly 10% in Tokyo. Oracle, CoreWeave, AMD, Nvidia, basically the entire AI infra basket, traded heavy. The market started asking the question again. Can the AI capex spent so far actually convert into revenue and profit.

Teradyne’s drop was the overlap of those two things. The Q1 numbers themselves were strong. The Q2 guide created the worry that “this quarter might have been the short-term peak,” and the OpenAI news scaled that worry up to AI infrastructure as a whole.

So what the market was selling wasn’t Q1. It was uncertainty about H2.

Now the real question is one thing. Is this drop a simple valuation reset on a stock that ran too far, or is it a signal that the AI test cycle is actually approaching peak. To answer that, you have to stop looking at Teradyne as just an ATE equipment maker, and start looking at it as the test layer that AI infrastructure has to pass through.

2. What Teradyne actually does

A semiconductor can’t be sold the moment it leaves the fab. Each die on the wafer has to be checked. After packaging, it has to be checked again. If it’s an advanced package with HBM bonded to a logic die, the assembled package has to be verified. AI accelerators with high power and heat get one more round of validation under conditions closer to the real system.

The equipment that runs this work is ATE. ATE stands for Automated Test Equipment, the gear that pushes signals into a chip and automatically verifies that the expected response comes back.

Testing happens at multiple stages. Wafer probe is the step that checks each die on the wafer before it’s diced. A die is the individual chip cut from the wafer. Final test is the step that checks the chip after packaging. SLT, or System Level Test, runs the chip on a board or in a system, under conditions close to real operation.

In the past, wafer probe and final test could catch most issues. With AI accelerators the story shifts. Dies are getting bigger. HBM is bonded on top. High-speed I/O lanes multiply. Power and heat climb. Some failures don’t show up at the chip-level test. They only appear once the chip is on a board, running something close to a real workload. That’s why SLT is becoming more important.

Look at Teradyne’s revenue mix and you can see this shift in the numbers. Of Q1 2026’s $1.282B revenue, Semiconductor Test was $1.111B, or 87% of the total. In the past, mobile SoC test was a major leg of the business. Today, the center of gravity is moving to AI accelerator SoCs and HBM memory. In Q4 2025, SoC test revenue was $647M, up 47% sequentially. Memory test revenue was $206M, up 61% sequentially, an all-time quarterly high. For full-year 2025, the Compute category grew 90% year over year and became the largest contributor.

Teradyne’s core platforms map to this trend. UltraFLEXplus is used for high-performance SoCs and AI accelerator test. The Magnum series handles high-bandwidth memory test like HBM. SLT solutions sit on top of these, extending coverage from the chip level into system-level verification.

3. One-time burst, or structural bottleneck

The question narrows to one thing.

Are Teradyne’s recent numbers a temporary burst from AI capex landing all at once, or are they structural test demand that has to keep growing as AI chips get bigger, more expensive, and more complex.

The difference matters. If the answer is the first one, Teradyne is a textbook cycle stock. Customers pile in tools for a stretch, then enter a digestion phase, and revenue rolls over. The market reacted strongly to the Q2 guide for exactly this reason. With the stock already up sharply, the doubt crept in: “Was Q1 the peak.”

If the answer is the second one, the story is different. As AI infrastructure grows, test stops being a cost line that gets squeezed and becomes a more important bottleneck. The more expensive a GPU gets, the taller the HBM stack, the more complex the package, the more high-speed I/O lanes there are, the higher the cost of catching a failure late. Customers end up testing at more steps, in conditions closer to real operation, and for longer.

From here, this article works through that question. Why some customers are starting to view 100% SLT as the cheapest option. Why HBM4 is creating new test insertions. And why Teradyne’s moves over the last 13 months point in the same direction.

4. When testing one AI accelerator becomes an insurance premium

The reason AI accelerator test demand is structurally rising is simple. Chips are getting bigger. Packages are more expensive. And real operating conditions are far harsher.

In the past, you could catch most issues at the wafer stage and final test. AI accelerators are different. Dies are pushing reticle limits. Multiple HBM stacks sit on the same package. High-speed I/O scales from tens to hundreds of lanes. Power has climbed to the kilowatt range. In that kind of structure, some defects don’t show up at chip-level test. They only appear once the chip is on a board or in a server.

This is why SLT, or System Level Test, has become important. SLT runs the chip in conditions close to a real system. It’s not just feeding signals into pins and reading responses. On a board, you’re checking power, heat, high-speed signaling, and behavior under workloads that resemble actual operation, all at the same time.

First reason: dies are getting too big

NVIDIA Blackwell B200 puts two reticle-limit-class dies into a single GPU package, linked by a high-bandwidth die-to-die interconnect. The reticle limit is the largest area you can pattern in a single lithography exposure, roughly 858㎟. In plain terms, a single die is now sized near the physical limit of what can be manufactured.

The bigger the die, the harder it is to verify everything at wafer probe. Wafer probe is the test step before the wafer is diced. With a larger die, more signals have to be touched in a single contact, more area has to be measured, and the number of dies you can test in parallel goes down.

Then there’s the high-speed I/O problem. AI accelerators move enormous amounts of data between GPUs, between GPUs and HBM, and between GPUs and the network. The circuit responsible for that pipe is the SerDes. SerDes is the high-speed transmit-and-receive block that bridges the inside and outside of a chip, and a single die can have tens to hundreds of lanes.

When people say 800G or 1.6T link, that’s the aggregate. The actual lanes underneath are 112G or 224G SerDes, bundled to reach those speeds. The issue is that lane speed is going up, and lane count is going up. Validating signal integrity, jitter, crosstalk, and power-noise sensitivity for every one of those lanes at production speed during wafer probe is getting steadily harder.

So some failures get pushed to final test, and some that final test misses get pushed all the way to system. CEO Greg Smith made a related point on the Q1 2025 call. When AI accelerators are placed on servers, the failure rate exceeds the acceptable level, and certain failures only emerge when running an actual training workload. The failures are shifting from simple manufacturing defects to marginal failures that only show up under real-use conditions.

Second reason: bonding HBM raises the cost of failure

AI accelerators are no longer a story about a single GPU. Multiple HBM stacks sit next to the GPU, with an interposer and substrate underneath. Micron said it began volume shipments in Q1 2026 of HBM4 36GB 12H designed for NVIDIA Vera Rubin. 12H means 12 DRAM dies stacked. After that comes 16-Hi HBM4.

The problem with this structure is cost. An HBM stack contains many DRAM dies and TSVs. TSVs are the vertical microscale channels that connect dies above and below. If even one of them fails, the entire stack can fail. And if that failure is found after attaching the stack to a GPU package, the cost climbs dramatically.

In the past, picking a known good die was the focus. That’s no longer enough. You have to confirm known good die, known good HBM stack, known good interposer, known good package, and known good system, step by step. The later you find a defect, the more money you throw away.

If a single HBM stack has a problem and it’s already packaged with an expensive GPU, interposer, and substrate, you’re not just throwing away one piece of memory. You’re affecting the yield and rework cost of an AI accelerator package worth tens of thousands of dollars. In the advanced packaging era, test stops being a final QA stage and becomes part of the yield ramp itself.

Third reason: power and thermal conditions have gotten brutal

Per the NVIDIA DGX B200 documentation, the B200 GPU is designed around a power envelope that can hit roughly 1kW depending on conditions. With Rubin, that power and thermal load is likely to grow.

Testing a 1kW-class chip isn’t just about checking whether signals come and go cleanly. You have to deliver power stably, control temperature precisely, and confirm whether performance wavers under real operation. Power delivery and thermal control become part of the test itself.

That makes test time per device longer. And longer test time means you need more testers to push the same volume. That’s where the rising tester demand for AI accelerator production actually comes from.

Tester demand isn’t determined by unit volume alone

If you only think about ATE demand in terms of chip shipments, you’ll miss the picture. AI accelerators don’t ship in the hundreds of millions like mobile SoCs. Yet tester demand can still grow. The reason is that tester demand is roughly a function of these four variables.

Tester demand = unit volume × number of test insertions × test time per insertion ÷ parallelism

Parallelism is the number of dies or devices a single tester can check at the same time.

In plain terms: more test stages, longer time per stage, fewer chips you can test in parallel. Even if shipment volume is much smaller than mobile chips, tester demand can be larger.

For AI accelerators, all three are happening at once. Test stages are rising: wafer sort, HBM post-stack test, package test, board test, SLT. Test time per stage is rising because of high-speed I/O, power, and thermal conditions. And parallelism is dropping because dies and packages are larger.

This is why AI test demand isn’t a simple volume story. The drivers aren’t unit volume. They’re test insertions, test time, and parallelism.

So 100% SLT becomes the insurance premium

The numbers below aren’t an official company cost figure. They’re a rough sensitivity model based on the ASP of high-end AI accelerators, board-level rework, and rack-level downtime.

A single AI accelerator like H100 or B200 sells for roughly $20K to $40K. If SLT costs $200 to $500 per chip, that’s about 1% to 2% of the chip’s value.

Now flip it. A chip that skipped SLT goes into a server, lands in a data center, and fails during a real workload. The cost structure is now completely different. It’s not just the chip. It’s the board, memory, power supply, cabling, rack space, rework hours, and the opportunity cost of the data center slot the server was occupying. The all-in cost of one late failure can exceed $50K.

That changes the math. If SLT costs $200 to $500, and a late-stage failure costs $50K, then even a failure rate of 0.5% makes 100% SLT economically rational. The money you save by skipping the test is smaller than the loss from one failure caught too late.

So in AI accelerators, SLT isn’t an extra cost. It’s closer to an insurance premium that protects an expensive package and an expensive system.

And when this shift spreads through the industry, the meaning grows. SLT stops being a niche insertion for a few products and becomes a mainstream test insertion for high-end AI accelerators. If every device has to go through one more system-level test, the underlying test equipment demand steps up.

This is why Teradyne is hard to view as just an ATE cycle stock. The bigger and more complex AI chips get, the more test customers need. And that test doesn’t end at the wafer. It runs through package, board, and system.

5. The HBM4 wafer sort win and a turning point for the memory business

Teradyne’s memory business has been a niche for a long time. Another incumbent ATE vendor has held an overwhelming share of HBM memory test, and Teradyne’s Magnum platform has played a supporting role. The Q4 2025 memory revenue of $206M was a record quarter for the company, but it was only 23% of the $883M Semi Test total.

Then in Q1 2025, something meaningful happened. The Memory Business Unit landed a new HBM4 wafer sort customer. CEO Smith called it out directly on the Q1 2025 call. “This quarter we received an order for HBM4 performance test from a major DRAM manufacturer. This is our first DRAM wafer sort win at this customer. Shipments are expected to begin in the second half of 2025.”

Which test insertion is it

What kind of insertion this win covers matters. The CEO walked through it in detail on the call. The HBM4 memory test flow goes like this.

(1) Every DRAM wafer goes through core test.

(2) The wafer is diced. Dicing is the process of cutting the wafer into individual dies.

(3) Dies are stacked on a substrate wafer. Twelve dies are stacked one on top of another.

(4) The stacked HBM is put through a high-speed performance test as a whole.

(5) Finally, additional test runs on the singulated stack.

Teradyne’s win is at step (4): post-stack wafer level performance test.

Why step (4) matters

Back when I was doing HBM-related verification work at a previous employer, this came up directly. The HBM had passed its known-good stamp at outgoing inspection, and the accelerator die had cleared both wafer probe and final test. But once the two parts were assembled into a single package and run on system test, a portion of them still failed at a recurring rate. A yield drop that was invisible at the chip-level tests was only revealed at the system stage.

That kind of failure is hard to deal with. Narrowing down to the actual root cause is itself difficult. When the system shows a failure, you first have to determine whether it’s an internal defect in the HBM die, a TSV open, a signal issue at the interface between accelerator and HBM, or a board-level power delivery problem. Disassembling components that have been bonded into a single package and re-testing them stand-alone is also nontrivial.

The cost explodes at this step. The same defect that would have ended in scrapping a single die at the wafer stage drags in the accelerator package, the HBM stack, the interposer, the substrate, and the board parts once it reaches the system stage. On top of scrap cost, you take on debug labor, equipment time, and shipment delays. Comparing the cost of handling the same failure, system-stage detection runs in the tens of multiples of wafer-stage detection.

The value of post-stack wafer test sits exactly here. If you run a high-speed performance test on the wafer with the stack already on top, and you screen out the failures before the stack is bonded to the accelerator package, you cut the failure off at that point. Scrap cost and root-cause analysis cost both stop at single-digit step counts. Step (4), where Teradyne’s win is, is precisely the inspection point that catches the most expensive failures.

Memory test TAM is expanding

In Q2 2025 the momentum continued. CEO Smith said, “Following Q1’s post-stack wafer test win, in Q2 we received a win at post-stack singulated die test as well.” He flagged a new dynamic. “Some HBM suppliers are introducing additional test insertions on the singulated stack. This insertion hasn’t fully landed across the industry yet, but we view it as an important future driver of memory test TAM growth.”

In other words, new test insertions are being added to HBM. With 16-Hi entering volume, the verification stages on the stack itself are increasing. That means memory test TAM isn’t just growing in proportion to HBM capacity. It’s getting multiplied again by insertion additions.

In the Q1 2026 release, management noted that memory test revenue hit a record quarterly high, and that AI-related revenue had risen to 70%. The fact that Teradyne is starting to take share in memory test starting from HBM4 is the kind of event that can change this company’s revenue mix. If memory test moves from 23% of Semi Test to 30 to 35%, the revenue mix becomes more diversified and cycle volatility decreases.

6. Wafer to data center stack: from ATE company to test infrastructure company

Teradyne’s recent moves can look scattered if you take them one news item at a time. One is a software acquisition. One is photonics test. Others are board-level test or high-speed I/O test. But put them on the timeline of how a product gets built, and the direction is fairly clear.

Teradyne is no longer a company that wants to stay only in wafer probe and final test. From the moment chip design ends and the test program is created, through the wafer-stage die check, through HBM and photonics verification, through AI server board and high-speed link validation, all the way to the final system level test, the company is taking position at every point where test is needed.

Start with software. In April 2026 Teradyne acquired TestInsight. TestInsight develops test program creation, validation, and pattern conversion. A test program is closer to an instruction sheet that tells the ATE how to test a chip. It defines which pin gets which signal and what response is expected. Pattern conversion takes the verification patterns generated during design and translates them into the form an ATE can actually execute.

Why this matters: AI accelerator product cycles are getting shorter. If too much time passes between tape-out and production ramp, customers can miss their market window. So building test programs faster and getting validation flows ready before silicon arrives has become more important. Teradyne acquiring TestInsight isn’t just a software bolt-on. It’s a move to reach further inside the customer’s design flow.

Next is photonics. In March 2025 Teradyne announced, together with ficonTEC, a double-sided wafer probe test cell for silicon photonics. It’s the equipment for testing wafers where PIC and EIC are joined by hybrid bonding, in a production setting. PIC is the photonic chip that handles signals as light. EIC is the electronic chip that controls it. As the industry moves toward CPO and optical engines, both have to be verified together.

Following that, Teradyne acquired Quantifi Photonics. Quantifi specializes in photonic IC test instrumentation. If the ficonTEC partnership was an external collaboration on the production cell side, the Quantifi acquisition is closer to bringing photonics test capability in-house.

On top of that flow, Photon 100 launched in March 2026. Photon 100 is an opto-electric ATE platform aimed at silicon photonics and CPO production. It’s designed to verify at the wafer stage, the optical engine stage, and the co-packaged module stage.

For CPO to actually reach volume, you have to look at optical and electrical signals together. Fiber alignment has to be maintained at micron precision. Thermal behavior after packaging has to be checked. Traditional electrical ATE alone can’t handle this. That’s why Teradyne is pushing photonics test as a separate axis.

That said, this isn’t likely to be a major 2026 revenue driver. The CPO and silicon photonics production market is still forming. Photon 100 is better seen as option value for 2027 and beyond rather than a near-term revenue line. The pace at which AI networking architecture moves toward optical interconnect and CPO, and the timing of meaningful adoption from hyperscalers and switch vendors, are what determine the upside.

There’s also a shift at the board level. In March 2026 Teradyne announced Omnyx. Omnyx is a PCBA and sub-assembly test platform aimed at AI and data center hardware. Traditional board test centered on ICT, In-Circuit Test. ICT is strong at detecting structural and parametric faults: whether components are correctly soldered, whether resistance and voltage values are within range.

But AI server boards aren’t simple PCBs. The board carries 800G and 1.6T high-speed signals, and some issues only show up under actual software workloads or mission-mode operation. Traditional ICT can’t catch these reliably. If they’re caught at final functional test, rework costs balloon.

Omnyx tries to fill that gap. It pulls structural test, parametric test, high-speed interconnect test, and functional test into a single production platform. Even if a chip is fine, a board-level issue still drives up SLT cost. Teradyne is reinforcing board-level test because this stage is becoming more important during AI server ramp.

On the high-speed I/O side there’s the MultiLane JV. In January 2026 Teradyne agreed with MultiLane to form a joint venture, MultiLane Test Products. MultiLane has strength in high-speed I/O test. The JV targets the 112G and 224G SerDes lane test and validation demand that sits underneath 800G and 1.6T links.

Omnyx and MultiLane look like they overlap, but their roles are different. Omnyx targets the high-speed interconnect and functional behavior happening inside an AI server board, at the production stage. MultiLane is closer to link validation that runs board to board, across cables, transceivers, and switch level. As AI infrastructure scales, the connections between chips and between systems become as important as the performance of any single chip. Teradyne is going after the test of those connections too.

Stack everything up and Teradyne’s expansion direction is straightforward.

When design ends and the test program has to be built, there’s TestInsight. At wafer and package stage, there’s UltraFLEXplus and Magnum. In HBM4, the company is widening its memory insertion footprint with post-stack wafer test and singulated stack test. In photonics, ficonTEC, Quantifi, and Photon 100 are getting silicon photonics and CPO test ready. At the board level, there’s Omnyx. On high-speed link validation, there’s the MultiLane JV. At the end, there’s SLT.

Compress this into one line. Teradyne is moving from a chip test company to an AI hardware test infrastructure company.

7. Conclusion and watch list

Teradyne’s 8% drop didn’t happen because the company’s earnings were bad. The numbers were strong. The problem was the price. The stock had run up over 400% in the past year, and the market had already priced in a long stretch of AI test cycle.

In that setup, good earnings aren’t enough. Investors also want to confirm that growth holds in the next quarter, that orders don’t bend in H2, and that today’s AI demand is structural rather than a one-time capex burst. The Q2 guide looked lower than Q1, the OpenAI news landed the same day, and the market shifted toward doubt first.

So what to watch from here isn’t just whether Q2 revenue lands. The bigger question is the shape of H2. Management has already said roughly 60% of 2026 revenue is concentrated in the first half. That tells you H1 demand is very strong, but it also tells you H2 visibility isn’t fully open yet. Over the next two to three quarters, it should become clear whether this is a normal quarterly fluctuation or the early signal of a cycle peak.

I’ll be watching five things from here.

First, whether the AI-related revenue mix stays high in Q2 and Q3. In Q1 about 70% of revenue was tied to AI. If that share drops fast, it’s a signal of AI capex digestion.

Second, whether the new HBM4 customer ramp converts into actual revenue. Teradyne has said HBM4 shipments would begin in the second half of 2025, and Q1 2026 memory test revenue hit a record. The question is whether that’s a one-quarter event or the signal of a share shift in the HBM4 generation.

Third, whether post-stack singulated stack test spreads across the HBM industry. If it stays an extra inspection step at a few customers, the impact is limited. If it becomes a new standard insertion in HBM4 and 16-Hi, memory test TAM itself expands.

Fourth, whether the MultiLane JV and Omnyx convert into actual customers. MultiLane sits on the high-speed I/O validation side. Omnyx sits on AI server board-level test. Both are pieces of Teradyne’s push beyond chip test into data center hardware test. Meaningful customer announcements here would make the expansion thesis far more credible.

Fifth, when Photon 100’s first major customer arrives. CPO and silicon photonics test is still an early market. But once a hyperscaler or optical engine vendor moves into volume preparation and adopts Teradyne’s platform, photonics test stops being a long-term option and becomes the next growth axis.

If those five break Teradyne’s way, the mid-term EPS model of $9.5 to $11 the company has put forward becomes a reachable number. If AI revenue mix slips, if the HBM4 ramp comes in lighter than expected, and if Omnyx and Photon 100 fail to build customer traction, valuation pressure resurfaces.

Share

In the end, the question this drop posed isn’t simply, “Is the AI cycle over.” The more accurate question is whether Teradyne is a company that posted a strong stretch within an existing ATE cycle, or a company moving into a position where it captures more test insertions as AI hardware gets more complex.

My read leans toward the second. Teradyne is hard to view today as just a vendor of wafer probe and final test equipment. As test scope widens across AI accelerators, HBM, advanced packaging, board-level hardware, high-speed I/O, and photonics, where the company is trying to anchor itself is becoming clearer. Q2 guidance and H2 visibility can shake the valuation, but the direction of the business itself doesn’t look broken.

That’s why I don’t read this drop as a thesis break. It looks more like an event where expectations that had been priced in too quickly cooled off once. The bigger AI infrastructure gets, expanding from GPU to HBM, to packaging, to board, to networking, to photonics, the more every layer has to pass through one gate. That gate is test. Teradyne is widening that gate from the chip level to the system level. From here, the question isn’t whether the direction is right. It’s whether the market’s expected pace shows up in the revenue numbers.
