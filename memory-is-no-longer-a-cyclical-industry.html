“This Is Not a Memory Super Cycle”

The phrase “super cycle” gets thrown around a lot when people describe today’s memory market. Demand is strong, prices are up, and sentiment around HBM is running high, so the label sticks naturally. But what I heard at this seminar was a direct challenge to that framing. Super cycle is a term rooted in how people used to look at memory, and the argument is that we’ve moved into territory that framework simply can’t explain.

The old memory industry ran on textbook cyclicality. A slight supply surplus would crater prices. Prices fell, companies eventually cut investment. Supply tightened, prices bounced back. Prices rose, everyone rushed to add capacity again. This loop, supply glut, price collapse, investment pullback, supply shortage, price recovery, repeat, played out on a multi-year cadence. Memory earned its reputation as the quintessential cyclical industry.

But the three major players don’t move like that anymore. Strong demand doesn’t automatically trigger aggressive capacity expansion the way it once did. The reason is simple: they’ve all paid for it. They went big during the up-cycle, got hit with supply gluts and price collapses, and bled for it. That experience left a mark. Today, the priority has shifted from chasing market share through volume to profitability, capital efficiency, and customer mix.

This tendency gets even stronger with products like HBM, where supply is inherently constrained and customer requirements are complex. Memory makers can’t just route limited production capacity wherever demand appears. Deciding which customer gets allocated capacity, and for how long, is directly tied to margin. Long-term supply agreements, in this context, aren’t just about locking in volume. They’re a signal that memory companies are managing supply far more strategically than before. It’s no longer an industry where anyone can just make more and sell more when prices go up.

This is also where long-term contracts take on a different meaning. In the old memory market, spot prices and short-term deals drove the cycle. Now, with AI infrastructure buildout and surging demand for high-value memory like HBM, securing stable supply has become critical on the customer side. And for memory companies, with massive upfront investment, process transitions, and packaging resource allocation all in play, demand visibility matters enormously. Both sides benefit. Long-term contracts are becoming a mechanism for reducing uncertainty and stabilizing supply chains, not just a sales vehicle.

These structural shifts, stacking on top of each other, are pulling memory further away from its classic cyclical pattern. That doesn’t mean price volatility disappears. Memory is still subject to supply and demand dynamics and will continue to fluctuate with market conditions. But the character of that volatility is different now. The old dynamic, where a good upcycle would trigger industry-wide overinvestment and eventually collapse everything, is becoming far less likely.

The regional president’s view was clear. Memory isn’t in the middle of a super cycle that pops and resets. It’s transforming into an industry that can command structurally higher value on a sustained basis. In his words, it’s moving closer to a structural growth industry than a commodity business that just rides the macro. He said it half-jokingly, but he floated the idea that returns from memory investments could potentially outpace index returns going forward.

The key question in today’s memory market isn’t where this cycle peaks. It’s that the nature of the industry itself has already changed. Viewing it through the lens of past cycles means missing what’s actually happening. Memory is no longer an industry you analyze by the cycle. It’s one you read by understanding structural change.

The Three Requirements to Buy HBM

HBM today is not something you can just buy because you want it. According to what I heard, if you want to purchase HBM from SK Hynix, you need all three of the following at the same time.

First, access to TSMC’s advanced packaging capacity.

Second, the data center power to actually run those chips at scale.

Third, the capital to fund all of it.

Each of these is hard on its own. The real problem is that you need all three simultaneously. Securing HBM is not the finish line. You still have to build the GPU or AI accelerator, package it, integrate it into servers and racks, and run it in a large-scale data center. HBM is not a standalone memory market. It’s a market where packaging, systems, infrastructure, and capital are all bound together.

That’s why the realistic pool of HBM buyers has effectively narrowed to hyperscalers and players at that tier. NVIDIA, Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta. Not just because their AI demand is large, but because they can actually claim TSMC packaging resources, absorb multi-billion dollar infrastructure investments, and secure power capacity that could eventually reach the GW scale. HBM is a technology market, yes, but it’s equally a market of supply chain control, infrastructure mobilization, and financial endurance.

This also explains why NVIDIA holds the position it does. NVIDIA’s dominance isn’t just about GPU performance. NVIDIA is the most concentrated source of demand, the most powerful ecosystem operator, and the most certain destination for HBM. From a memory company’s perspective, NVIDIA isn’t just a buyer of chips. It’s the player that can convert HBM allocation into system revenue fastest, at the largest scale, with the highest degree of certainty. In other words, the customer that generates the most impact per unit of supply.

AI chip startups, by contrast, are structurally disadvantaged. No matter how good the chip design, insufficient HBM access makes it nearly impossible to build a competitive system. Even after taping out, lack of TSMC packaging capacity delays product shipment. On top of that, there’s large-scale customer qualification and real-world data center power constraints to clear.

The implication is significant. In the AI semiconductor market, “good technology equals good investment” no longer holds as a simple formula. Technology is a necessary condition, not a sufficient one. What matters far more is who can secure stable HBM supply, who can claim packaging capacity, and who can mobilize power and capital to convert it into actual revenue. This is what HBM absolute supremacy looks like in practice.

cHBM: Memory Goes Foundry

Anyone who’s read my previous articles already knows this, but memory companies including SK Hynix are engaged in active cHBM co-design discussions with hyperscalers. The reason is straightforward. In AI systems, the bottleneck is no longer compute performance alone. Data movement, power efficiency, packaging area, thermal management, and system-level optimization are all becoming critical. Producing large volumes of good memory is no longer enough. The era of deeply customizing memory to fit what each customer’s system architecture actually requires has arrived.

This is where the character of the memory industry begins to shift.

In standard product-driven memory, cost reduction and yield competition were the core game. Who could make more, cheaper, more reliably was what mattered. cHBM changes the equation. The base die takes on more logic. Custom design work increases. Packaging complexity rises. Validation scope expands. That naturally inflates development costs and NRE-type expenses, and raises the share of co-design work between customer and supplier. Memory moves away from pure manufacturing logic and starts incorporating design services, advanced packaging, and system optimization as core parts of the business.

This is why I don’t read cHBM as simply “HBM getting more sophisticated.”

It’s a signal that the memory industry is moving from commodity to solution business. Beyond that, it means memory companies are beginning to behave, in certain domains, like foundries.

What It Takes to Build a 1GW Data Center

People occasionally push back with something like this: “Fine, HBM I get. But DRAM is still a commodity, and structurally memory is still a cyclical industry, isn’t it?”

It was, historically. DRAM was the textbook commodity. Supply up, prices collapse. Prices collapse, capex contracts. Supply tightens, prices recover. Prices recover, aggressive capacity expansion begins again. That’s why many people still believe that even if HBM is an exception, the broader memory market including DRAM will eventually revert to its old cyclical pattern.

Let me ask a question.

How many DRAM wafers do you think it takes to build a single 1GW data center?

According to what I heard at the seminar today, roughly 350,000 wafers.

Let that sink in.

What makes this number even more striking is that those 350,000 wafers are only the DRAM portion. Data centers don’t run on DRAM alone. You need GPUs, HBM, packaging, networking, SSDs, power infrastructure, and cooling systems, all at once. Building a 1GW data center isn’t a matter of adding a few more servers. It means simultaneously moving the entire semiconductor supply chain and the entire power infrastructure.

That’s the critical point.

The memory cycles of the past moved within the relatively predictable boundaries of IT demand driven by PCs, smartphones, and servers. Supply fluctuated against that demand, and prices swung accordingly. AI data center buildout is a different animal entirely. The absolute scale of demand is enormous, and it’s not a one-off event. It’s tied to the long-term infrastructure investment plans of the largest companies in the world. With Microsoft, Google, Amazon, and Meta all talking about GW-scale data centers, memory demand can no longer be reasonably framed as something that dips and recovers on the old pattern.

That doesn’t mean DRAM price volatility goes away. DRAM still carries commodity characteristics and will respond to market conditions. But the context driving that volatility is fundamentally different now. The magnitude of demand, the identity of the buyers, the way supply is being managed, and the structure of long-term contracts have all changed. Memory will still be volatile, but it is absolutely not a cyclical industry in the way that term used to mean.

Is Hybrid Bonding Mandatory for HBM?

Hybrid bonding came up at the seminar too. It’s a topic I care about, so I asked a few more pointed questions in the 1-on-1 afterward.

As most of you know, hybrid bonding in HBM is fundamentally different from what we’re talking about with logic chips like AMD’s 3D V-Cache. Same name, but the technical difficulty and spec requirements are essentially different games.

I’ll cover the technical details of hybrid bonding in a dedicated article later.

HBM has been built on MR-MUF-based TC bonding up through HBM3E. That approach has held up this far. But moving into HBM4E and HBM5 and beyond, the situation changes. I/O counts have scaled sharply, from 1,024 on HBM3 to 2,048 on HBM4, and they’ll keep climbing.

Fitting more connections into the same footprint means continuously reducing die-to-die interconnect pitch. The industry expects HBM4 microbump pitch to approach 10um, and as you move into that range, the existing approach runs into rapidly growing challenges around alignment error, bridging, parasitics, thermal stress, and yield. Hybrid bonding at this point is no longer a distant future option. For HBM4E, HBM5, and generations beyond, it’s effectively mandatory.

This wasn’t from today’s seminar, but from conversations with multiple SK Hynix and Samsung engineers: samples with hybrid bonding applied to HBM4 and HBM4E are already being evaluated. Equipment costs and other practical constraints make immediate full adoption difficult, but the expectation is that HBM5 is where hybrid bonding goes mainstream.

One other interesting point: this trend intensifies as you move toward 3D HBM and cHBM. Starting from HBM4, the base die goes beyond a simple interposer role to carry more complex controllers and PHY. The industry has already moved decisively toward custom base die built on logic process nodes. As the boundary between memory and logic blurs, the simultaneous demands for higher interconnect density, lower power, and tighter signal integrity make hybrid bonding not a choice but a structural requirement.

3D DRAM and the China vs. Korea Dynamic

3D DRAM was the most striking part of today’s seminar.

The regional president used the analogy of gasoline cars versus EVs to explain how China, rather than trying to close the gap in conventional memory through a head-on fight, jumped straight into 3D DRAM. And in that domain, he said, China is well ahead of Korea. SK Hynix is fully aware of this gap and has deployed a significant number of engineers to close it.

Why does this matter? Because planar DRAM is starting to show its limits. Scaling and cell structure optimization have carried density improvements this far, but going forward, the trajectory for DRAM increasingly resembles what happened to NAND: stacking. Packing more cells into the same wafer area through vertical integration will become the key lever for cost competitiveness and long-term market position.

This is where 6F2 and 4F2 enter the conversation. These figures describe cell area efficiency in DRAM, where F represents feature size. Moving from 6F2 to 4F2 means fitting more cells into the same footprint, which translates directly into higher bit density per wafer and has downstream effects on productivity, cost structure, and profitability. It’s not a technical footnote. It’s a foundational shift in how competitive advantage gets built.

If planar scaling and miniaturization defined the previous era of DRAM competition, 3D DRAM architecture is what defines the next. And when that transition arrives, what matters most isn’t who ships first. It’s who has the more efficient cell structure. From that angle, the shift from 6F2 to 4F2 is a core variable in determining how the next generation of DRAM shakes out.

What struck me most wasn’t the technology itself. It was that this isn’t being treated as a distant research initiative. The fact that SK Hynix is actively concerned and has put real resources into catching up tells you how important this is. Companies don’t commit headcount and budget to things that might matter someday. They do it for the next competitive battleground they know is coming.

3D DRAM is not just another next-generation candidate on a roadmap.

It’s the most important card available to players who have been behind, to flip the table on the existing order. Closing the gap the same way the leaders built it is essentially impossible. But when the underlying structure changes, the game resets. NAND redefined competition when it went vertical. If 3D DRAM reaches the same inflection point, we’ll be looking at a completely new competitive landscape.

The standard take on Chinese memory companies is “still limited,” “significant gap in advanced nodes.” That’s accurate if you’re looking at today’s commercial position. But if they’re already generating meaningful results in a structural technology transition, the competitive picture a few years out could look nothing like today.

That’s why this was the part of the seminar that stayed with me the longest.

PIM’s Limits and Google

PIM(Processor In Memory) came up too, given how frequently the topic circulates in the industry.

Most people look at PIM and think, “compute happens inside the memory, so it must be faster and more efficient.” The direction is right. Reducing data movement, alleviating memory bandwidth bottlenecks, and delivering real advantages for specific workloads are all legitimate. The problem is that PIM’s biggest constraint isn’t the technology itself. It’s the dynamics among the companies that would have to change the existing paradigm to make it work.

For PIM to function properly, swapping out the hardware alone isn’t enough.

When compute partially moves to the memory side, the software stack has to move with it. How developers write programs, what compilers and runtimes are needed, how roles are divided with existing accelerators: all of it has to be redefined. Layering PIM on top of CUDA or ROCm as-is doesn’t surface its advantages.

Security is also a serious challenge.

Compute happening inside memory means the boundary between storage and processing blurs. That forces a reexamination of access control, data integrity, isolation, and side-channel vulnerabilities from a new angle. You can’t adopt this based on performance alone. Especially in hyperscaler or enterprise environments, technologies that haven’t fully addressed security and operability don’t get adopted regardless of how promising they look.

And then there’s the ecosystem.

Which technologies win in an industry ultimately comes down to who can integrate them most completely. Hardware-only companies fall short. Software-only companies fall short. Infrastructure alone isn’t enough. You need the ability to tie it all together, all the way through to actual services and developer experience.

At that point, who comes to mind?

What follows is speculation on my part.

The company best positioned to make PIM work, with the software stack, security posture, and ecosystem to execute it, is Google.

I came away with a strong suspicion that SK Hynix is already in fairly concrete PIM architecture discussions with Google, or at least exploring co-development in that direction. The logic is simple. PIM is not a technology a memory company can complete on its own. It requires memory architecture, compute architecture, software stack, security, and real-world service integration to all click into place simultaneously. The company most realistically capable of meeting all those conditions is Google, which means Google is probably also the most natural partner for SK Hynix to go deep with first.

Take it further: if a PIM architecture gets customized specifically for certain workloads and a TPU architecture, the story gets genuinely interesting. Beyond reducing memory bandwidth bottlenecks, there’s real potential to cut data movement at its source, improve both power efficiency and latency, and produce meaningful system-level performance gains for TPU. PIM’s real leverage isn’t as a general-purpose technology. It’s when it gets shaped specifically around one hyperscaler’s AI stack. If the first combination to pull that off is SK Hynix memory paired with Google’s TPU, that’s not just a memory innovation. That could be the starting point for rewriting AI system architecture from the ground up.

SK Hynix ADR, and Micron

After the seminar, I asked what was probably the most sensitive question of the night.

I want to buy SK Hynix stock so badly. Any sense of when an ADR might happen?

He was visibly reluctant to answer in a public setting. He said he’d talk to me separately later. The nature of the question was clearly sensitive, and it wasn’t the kind of thing that could be addressed openly in front of a room.

In the 1-on-1 that followed, I brought it up again.

He answered with a bit of humor:

“Just buy Micron.”

Everything from here is my own speculation. I want to be absolutely clear: this is not a statement of SK Hynix’s official position on anything. He wasn’t giving a serious, formal answer about ADR prospects. This was almost an offhand remark, and everything that follows is a story I’ve constructed around one throwaway line. Treat it as entertainment, not signal.

My read was that ADR is not a simple undertaking, and it doesn’t look like something that gets resolved in the near term.

When I think about why, a few things come up. There are structural governance complexities within the SK Group. The Korean financial regulator and capital markets framework have to be factored in. Beyond that, the question of how an ADR listing affects existing Korean shareholders and what strategic considerations the company brings to that decision adds several more layers of complexity. “There’s U.S. investor demand, therefore do an ADR” doesn’t come close to capturing how complicated this actually is.

That might be subtly affecting valuations in ways worth thinking about. Micron has posted strong results and demonstrated clear exposure to AI demand, yet the stock has been choppy. Part of that is attributable to heavy capex expansion and concerns about memory cycle peak. But SK Hynix ADR speculation has almost certainly been a factor too. There’s a credible argument that Micron has long carried a structural premium as effectively the only large-cap memory stock accessible to U.S. investors. If that premium starts to erode, even in expectation, it shows up in price. And if an ADR turns out to be harder to pull off than the market assumed...?

But again, please don’t read more into this than it deserves. The regional president was not making an official statement. He gave no concrete answer about ADR. This was closer to a joke thrown out in conversation, and most of what follows it is my own imagination filling in the gaps. Read it as one possible story built on a short offhand comment, nothing more.

Closing: The Memory Industry’s Paradigm Is Shifting

One thing I left today’s seminar more certain of than when I walked in:

Memory is fundamentally different now.

Supply is constrained. Demand is structurally growing. The three major players no longer invest recklessly the way they once did. HBM is moving toward hybrid bonding and 3D architectures, raising the technology barrier with each generation. And the customers this market ultimately converges on are the hyperscalers.

Drop the cyclical framework. Today’s memory industry is a completely different game, one where structural supply constraint meets accelerating technological innovation.
