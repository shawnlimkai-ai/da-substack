What Happened This Week

Feb 17–18 (background): WD secondary offering. Western Digital executed a secondary offering of its remaining SanDisk stake. Roughly 5.82 million shares were priced at $545 per share, with the deal closing on February 19. SanDisk received none of the proceeds. The ownership structure shift had already begun.

March 20: Lock-up expiry. Separately from the February offering, a lock-up period expired on March 20, making over 2 million insider-held shares eligible for sale. The stock slipped from $772 to below $740. Given that the stock had rallied 25% in the same week, the lock-up expiry became a convenient excuse for profit-taking.

March 20: Micron earnings fallout. On the same day, Micron reported quarterly results and highlighted its capex plans. Concerns about increased NAND supply spread across the entire memory sector. SanDisk fell 5%.

March 23: Profit-taking + valuation warnings. With the stock price above the average analyst target of $761, the perception that “it’s more expensive than consensus” took hold. The broader market rallied on geopolitical developments (Iran tensions), but SanDisk moved in the opposite direction. Down 3.76%.

March 25: Double punch. First, SanDisk announced a $1B equity investment in Taiwan’s Nanya Technology. Down 6%. Second, Google announced TurboQuant. “Does AI need less memory now?” The entire memory sector sold off.

March 26 (today): Selling continues. Far from recovering yesterday’s losses, the stock dropped another 8.13%, hitting $623. The Nasdaq briefly attempted a rebound intraday before rolling over again, and SanDisk caught both the broader market weakness and memory sector dumping at the same time. The aftershocks of the Nanya deal and TurboQuant hadn’t faded, and a broader risk-off move piled on top. Down 20% from the $772 high. Roughly $20B in market cap gone in one week.

Now let’s figure out what’s noise and what’s real.

Noise: Lock-up, Profit-Taking, Nanya

Lock-up expiry and profit-taking are supply events. Short-term traders exiting a 25x stock is natural. It’s not structural change. The stock is still well above its 50-day moving average of $593.

The Nanya investment deserves a closer look. The market knocked the stock down 6% on this deal, so let’s be precise about what actually happened.

SanDisk, through its subsidiary SanDisk Technologies, acquired a 3.9% stake in Taiwan’s Nanya Technology for $1B. The deal involved roughly 139 million newly issued shares at a 15% discount to Nanya’s 30-day average price, structured as a private placement. Under Taiwanese securities law, there’s a 3-year lock-up. Alongside the equity investment, SanDisk and Nanya signed a multi-year strategic DRAM supply agreement.

One important piece of context. SanDisk wasn’t the only participant in this private placement.

It was part of a $2.5B (NT$78.7B) round that included SanDisk at $1B (roughly 139 million shares, 3.9%), Solidigm (SK hynix subsidiary) at $500M (roughly 71.39 million shares, 2%), Kioxia at $500M (70 million shares, 2%), and Cisco at $500M (71.5 million shares, 2%). All at the same price of NT$223.9 per share. This wasn’t an exclusive SanDisk deal. It was an industry event where the three major NAND players (SanDisk, Kioxia, Solidigm) plus a networking giant (Cisco) simultaneously locked in DRAM supply. All three SSD makers signed separate DRAM supply agreements with Nanya.

Three SSD manufacturers locking in DRAM sourcing at the same time sends a strong signal: the entire industry recognizes a DRAM bottleneck.

Why Nanya? Nanya Technology is a Taiwan-based pure-play DRAM maker. As Samsung, SK hynix, and Micron have shifted resources toward DDR5 and HBM, deprioritizing DDR4 production, Nanya has emerged as a critical supplier in the DDR4 market. TrendForce spot pricing shows DDR4/16G trading above DDR5/16G, an inversion. The older-generation DDR4 costs more because the big three have structurally reduced DDR4 output.

Nanya’s Q4 2025 results reflect this trend. Per Nanya’s own disclosures, the company posted record quarterly EPS, with gross margin climbing to 49% (up roughly 30 percentage points sequentially). DRAM ASP also rose by double digits QoQ. The company plans to significantly expand 2026 capex year over year, pushing 1C/1D node transitions and new fab equipment installation (targeting early 2027). DDR5 still accounts for only about 10% of total shipments, but next-generation DDR5 product development is underway.

According to industry reports, China’s CXMT has been moving to reduce its DDR4 production mix, a trend that is further strengthening Nanya’s position in the DDR4 market.

The market’s negative reaction makes sense. $1B exceeds last quarter’s adjusted free cash flow of $843M. Cash on hand was $1.54B, so there’s still $500M+ left after the investment, but it raises questions about short-term financial flexibility. The “why is a pure NAND company putting $1B into a DRAM company?” reaction is understandable. A 3.9% minority stake doesn’t give you much of a voice in management either.

But from an engineering standpoint, this deal has logic. Three angles.

First, DRAM cache is critical for high-performance enterprise SSDs. Enterprise SSD controllers keep the FTL (Flash Translation Layer) mapping table in DRAM to deliver random read/write performance. If DRAM supply is constrained, it creates a bottleneck for high-performance eSSD production. In the current environment of structurally tight DDR4 supply, locking in multi-year DRAM sourcing is a practical move to protect eSSD production continuity.

Second, and this is a strategic interpretation rather than confirmed fact, it could be groundwork for the HBF era. HBF stacks NAND dies like HBM and places them on an interposer next to the GPU. In this architecture, the ability to work with both DRAM and NAND becomes important. SanDisk securing a DRAM supply relationship through Nanya could serve as the foundation for a DRAM-side partnership when HBF reaches commercialization. SanDisk hasn’t officially drawn this connection, so treat this as a hypothesis for now.

Third, the valuation angle. They bought Nanya shares at a 15% discount to the 30-day average. Nanya’s earnings are surging, and DDR4 supply shortages are expected to persist through at least the first half of 2026. There’s a 3-year lock-up, but Nanya’s enterprise value is likely to be higher in three years than it is today.

Let me also flag the risks. A 3.9% minority stake means limited strategic control. There’s Taiwan geopolitical exposure. If DRAM prices roll over, the value of the Nanya stake drops. The 3-year lock-up means zero liquidity.

If it doesn’t, the “why did they spend $1B?” discomfort lingers.

All three of these (lock-up, profit-taking, Nanya) are predominantly short-term supply dynamics. Lock-up and profit-taking are temporary selling pressure. Nanya is a strategically rational deal that suffered from poor market communication. Based on currently available information, none of these events have directly impaired SanDisk’s NAND business fundamentals.

Half Real: Micron Capex and Supply Concerns

Micron’s aggressive capex announcement deserves more serious consideration. The market’s worry goes like this: “Micron pours money into NAND, supply increases, prices drop, SanDisk margins collapse.”

But the narrative the market is running with and the message Micron itself delivered point in opposite directions. On the same earnings call, Micron said that “data center NAND demand exceeds supply for the foreseeable future” and projected industry NAND bit shipment growth of roughly 20% for 2026. They also noted that initial output from their new Singapore NAND fab won’t come until the second half of 2028. In other words, Micron itself is saying “even with our capex increase, supply tightness persists for the time being,” while the market looked at the capex number in isolation and panicked about oversupply.

Let’s also look at this from a technical standpoint. There are only two ways to actually increase NAND bit supply. Build a new fab, or stack more layers in existing fabs to increase bit density per die. The first takes 12 to 18 months. The second requires a process node migration, and during the transition, yield stabilization and validation can actually cause bit output to temporarily decline.

Looking at the composition of Micron’s NAND capex increase (+63% YoY), per Micron’s own disclosures, the bulk of the increase goes toward cleanroom and equipment spending for the Tongluo fab and new US fabs. This is long-term infrastructure investment, not something that translates into immediate bit output growth. The US ID1 fab won’t produce meaningful output before 2027, and the Singapore fab won’t before the second half of 2028.

The current situation across manufacturers: Samsung and SK hynix are either reducing or freezing NAND investment while concentrating capital on HBM. According to industry research (TrendForce and others), 2026 NAND capex growth is projected in the low single digits, significantly below DRAM capex growth. NAND is being intentionally deprioritized.

The state of the layer stacking race also matters. Based on each company’s public disclosures: SK hynix is in mass production at 321-layer QLC, Samsung is producing V9 (290-layer class) TLC, Micron is shipping 276-layer G9 QLC. SanDisk/Kioxia are in production with BiCS 8 (218 to 232 layers), with BiCS 9 (300-layer class) targeted for 2026 and BiCS 10 (332 layers) originally scheduled for the second half of 2027, though there are reports (Nikkei, unofficial) that AI demand is pushing the timeline forward.

It’s true that SanDisk/Kioxia trail SK hynix by roughly a year on layer count. That means a cost-per-bit disadvantage and potentially weaker positioning in eSSD price competition. But flip it around: the 218-layer to 300+ layer transition gives them significant room to increase bit density per die. When this technology transition executes, the same fabs produce more bits, cost per bit improves, and margins expand. SK hynix at 321 layers is approaching the limits of how much additional efficiency it can extract, while SanDisk/Kioxia still have steps left to climb.

This is where CBA (CMOS directly Bonded to Array) matters. In simple terms, it’s a technology that fabricates the memory array and logic circuitry on separate wafers, then bonds them together using hybrid bonding. Traditional 3D NAND built both on the same wafer. CBA separates them so each can be optimized independently. The array for high-density stacking, the logic for high-speed interfaces. This architecture enables high-speed interfaces like Toggle DDR 6.0 (4.8 GT/s). YMTC commercialized this first under the name Xtacking, and SanDisk/Kioxia adopted it starting with BiCS 8. It directly enables eSSD performance differentiation and, as I’ll discuss later, serves as a technical prerequisite for HBF.

_ Bottom line: Micron’s capex increase gave the market a narrative for supply concerns, but Micron itself says demand exceeds supply, and new fab output won’t arrive until 2027 at the earliest. This doesn’t change the 2026 supply-demand picture._

The Real Question: Is Google TurboQuant a Threat?

This is the most interesting variable in the current selloff. The market reacted instantly. The entire memory sector dropped. The “AI needs less memory” narrative spread.

Let me share my assessment as an engineer who has actually looked at TurboQuant.

You need to understand exactly what TurboQuant does. When LLMs process long contexts, the KV cache that stores key-value pairs from previous tokens eats up GPU memory. TurboQuant is a compression algorithm combining PolarQuant (which eliminates normalization constants via polar coordinate transformation) and QJL (1-bit residual correction). Breaking down Google’s reported results precisely: at 3-bit compression, zero accuracy loss and 6x+ KV cache memory reduction. Separately, at a 4-bit configuration on H100, up to 8x speedup in attention computation. The 3-bit memory savings and 8x speed boost were not achieved simultaneously. They come from different configurations.

Technically impressive. No question about it.

But the market’s reaction missed the scope. What TurboQuant reduces is the KV cache sitting in the GPU’s HBM. This could affect DRAM (HBM) demand. The same HBM capacity can handle longer contexts. But the market sold SanDisk, a NAND company, right alongside it.

What SanDisk sells is SSDs. What’s stored on SSDs is not KV cache. It’s model weights, training data, checkpoints, inference datasets. TurboQuant is not a model weight compression technique. It compresses KV cache. Model weight compression is a separate set of quantization techniques (INT4, INT8, GPTQ, etc.) that already exist and are already baked into NAND demand forecasts.

In my assessment, TurboQuant’s direct impact on NAND/SSD demand appears limited. KV cache resides in GPU HBM or system DRAM, which sits in a different tier of the memory hierarchy from the model weights and training data stored on SSDs. That said, this is my interpretation as of now, not a settled conclusion. The second and third-order effects TurboQuant could trigger (GPU efficiency gains → data center architecture changes → storage configuration adjustments) are still playing out and worth monitoring.

There could be indirect effects. If KV cache shrinks, the same GPU memory can run larger models for inference. In theory, “fewer GPUs needed → slower data center buildout → indirect decline in eSSD demand” is a possible second-order effect. But this logic ignores the Jevons Paradox, where efficiency gains actually increase total demand. When compression lets you do more with the same resources, historically usage doesn’t shrink. It expands. Longer contexts, larger models, more agents. When efficiency goes up, applications proliferate.

My assessment: TurboQuant is a medium-term point of attention for DRAM/HBM demand, but its direct impact on NAND/SSD appears limited. The portion of SanDisk’s decline attributable to TurboQuant is most likely the result of the market failing to distinguish between DRAM and NAND and selling “memory” as a single bucket.

A pivot top sell signal triggered on March 19 at $772. In the week since, the stock has dropped 20% with volume increasing on down days. Heavy volume on selloffs is a warning sign that selling pressure goes beyond simple profit-taking. The broader market turning weak today is real, but SanDisk’s 8% is several times the Nasdaq’s decline. A beta of 2.74 doing exactly what it does.

The high beta accumulated during a 25x run-up amplifies downside volatility in a correction. This isn’t a SanDisk-specific problem. Long-term moving average signals are still on buy. But the 50-day ($593) is only about 5% away. If it bounces before testing the 50-day, the uptrend resumes. If the 50-day breaks, the trend-reversal scenario opens up.

So How Are the Fundamentals?

Strip away the noise and look at SanDisk’s actual business.

Most recent quarter (FY2026 Q2): Revenue $3.025B, up 61% YoY. GAAP net income $803M, GAAP EPS $5.15 (7x the prior quarter). Non-GAAP EPS $6.20. Gross margin surged from 29.9% to 51.1% in a single quarter, a 21 percentage point increase. Consumer revenue $907M (QoQ +39%), data center revenue $440M (QoQ +64%). Adjusted free cash flow $843M (FCF margin 27.9%), turning cash flow positive. Debt ratio 0.06.

Q3 guidance: Revenue $4.4B to $4.8B. Gross margin 65% to 67%. EPS $12 to $14.

This guidance is the key. If they deliver, the annualized non-GAAP EPS run rate hits $50. At today’s price of $623, the forward P/E based on that guidance run rate drops to roughly 12x. A caveat: that assumes the Q3 guidance pace holds for four consecutive quarters. (P/E figures in this article are based on non-GAAP EPS unless otherwise noted.) Annualizing last quarter’s non-GAAP EPS of $6.20 gives roughly $25, which puts the trailing P/E at about 25x. The forward P/E of roughly 18x shown on Yahoo Finance is based on pre-Q3-guidance consensus. These three numbers differ because the underlying assumptions differ, not because they contradict each other.

Let me revisit the mechanics behind the margin explosion. When NAND ASP rises, you’re selling wafers you already manufactured at higher prices. Cost is fixed. Only the selling price goes up. Q1 2026 NAND contract prices rose 33% to 38% QoQ across all categories (TrendForce). Kingston’s data center SSD head reported that NAND wafer prices were up 246% YoY. As long as this pricing environment holds, margins keep expanding.

Can NAND supply-demand sustain these prices? Three pillars.

First, AI data center storage demand. Data centers will consume over 70% of the world’s memory chips in 2026 (IDC). QLC plays a central role here. AI inference workloads write model weights once and read them tens of thousands of times, which means QLC’s weakness, low write endurance, isn’t an issue. Demand for high-capacity QLC enterprise SSDs is exploding, but yield and validation are tougher than TLC, so supply can’t keep up with demand. This technical bottleneck is what sustains prices.

Second, manufacturer capex discipline. The trauma of the 2022 to 2023 downturn. Both Samsung and SK hynix are concentrating capital on HBM while deprioritizing NAND. Per TrendForce estimates, 2026 NAND capex growth is single digits YoY. With demand growth outpacing supply growth, this gap is pushing prices higher.

Third, HDD supply shortage. Seagate says “all nearline HDD supply for 2026 is allocated.” This is the most transient driver. It could ease by the second half of 2027.

Kioxia’s CEO stated publicly that “the era of cheap 1TB SSDs is over,” and per multiple industry reports, most major NAND makers have largely exhausted their 2026 allocations. There are also reports that some hyperscalers have moved to secure long-term volumes. Major research firms including TrendForce and IDC project supply shortages persisting through at least the first half of 2027.

It Went Up 25x, So Why Is the Multiple This Low?

When a stock goes up 25x, you’d expect the valuation to be sky-high. But SanDisk’s trailing P/E, annualizing last quarter’s non-GAAP EPS of $6.20, sits at roughly 25x. That’s well below the semiconductor sector average of about 41x. Compared to AI semis (NVIDIA 40x+, Broadcom 35x+, Marvell 45x+), it’s roughly half.

The reason the multiple is this low despite a 25x move is straightforward. Earnings have risen nearly as fast as the stock. GAAP EPS went from $0.75 the prior quarter to $5.15. Non-GAAP EPS went from $1.22 to $6.20. Q3 guidance calls for non-GAAP EPS of $12 to $14. The denominator (earnings) has been chasing the numerator (price), compressing the P/E. If Q3 guidance is sustained for four quarters, annualized non-GAAP EPS hits a $50 pace, and the guidance run-rate P/E drops to 12x. A week ago this was “a low multiple for a cyclical.” Now it’s “a low multiple by any standard.”

The reason the market still won’t give it a higher multiple comes down to classification. The market treats SanDisk as a “memory cyclical.” The convention for memory cyclicals is not to apply high multiples to peak earnings, because the assumption is that earnings are about to roll over.

My view is that this classification is only half right. SanDisk is cyclical, no denying that. But the assumption that the trough of this cycle will look like previous troughs is likely wrong. Structural storage demand from AI inference has raised the earnings floor, and the industry’s HBM-first strategy is structurally constraining NAND supply.

In the past, consumer electronics drove NAND demand, and that was highly cyclical. The growth engine now is data center eSSD. TrendForce projects that AI-related NAND consumption will account for 20% of total bits and 34% of revenue in 2026. That demand doesn’t depend on smartphone shipment volumes.

The core question is whether these earnings are peak or the new normal. The market says peak. I say it’s closer to a new normal with a higher floor. This difference in judgment is what creates the gap in multiples, and that’s where the investment opportunity may lie.

The Kioxia comparison fits this context. Same fabs, same NAND, 50:50 wafer split. They jointly operate Flash Ventures (9 fabs, roughly one-third of global NAND bits), yet market caps differ by over 50%. The justification for SanDisk’s premium is Nasdaq liquidity, the SanDisk brand, and direct access to North American enterprise customers. The current correction is narrowing that gap, and it’s getting closer to a reasonable level.

HBF: An Option Not Yet Priced In

There’s one more reason to view SanDisk as more than a cyclical. HBF (High Bandwidth Flash).

Just as HBM stacks DRAM dies using TSVs and connects them to the GPU at ultra-high bandwidth, HBF stacks NAND dies using TSVs and places them directly on the interposer next to the GPU. The core innovation is a parallel sub-array architecture. The NAND’s fundamental structure is partitioned into many independent sub-arrays to enable simultaneous parallel access, combined with DDR synchronous signaling through hundreds to thousands of pins, similar to HBM.

What makes this possible is the CBA technology I described earlier. Without CBA, which separates memory and logic for independent optimization, you can’t fit sub-array-level controllers and high-speed PHY inside the NAND package. The fact that SanDisk has been using CBA in production since BiCS 8 means the key prerequisite for HBF is already in place. This technology readiness is what makes HBF a viable product candidate rather than a lab concept.

Let me start with confirmed facts. SanDisk and SK hynix have signed an MoU for HBF standardization and established a workstream within OCP (Open Compute Project). HBF’s goal is to deliver HBM-class bandwidth with 8x to 16x the capacity at a lower price point than HBM. SanDisk has outlined a timeline of initial samples in the second half of 2026, with AI inference device integration samples in early 2027. SK hynix presented an H3 architecture (HBM + HBF hybrid) at IEEE, showing a simulation of 2.69x improvement in performance per watt versus an HBM-only configuration.

Keep in mind that specific spec numbers (bandwidth, maximum capacity, etc.) are still pre-production simulation-stage estimates. There are also industry reports about Samsung’s participation in HBF, but the only officially confirmed MoU is between SanDisk and SK hynix.

The limitations are clear. Write endurance of roughly 100,000 P/E cycles restricts it to read-heavy workloads. Read latency of 10μs is in a different league from DRAM. But AI inference, where model weights are read repeatedly, is the largest market, and these constraints align perfectly with that use case.

_ And here’s where the relationship with TurboQuant becomes ironically positive. If TurboQuant compresses KV cache and frees up HBM capacity on the GPU, that freed-up space creates room for HBF. TurboQuant isn’t an enemy of HBF. It’s a complement_.

Connecting this to the investment thesis. HBF’s near-term revenue contribution is zero. Analyst estimates on market size vary, and since it’s pre-production, pinning down a TAM precisely is difficult. But if HBF materializes, SanDisk’s industry classification changes.

From “storage cyclical” to “AI memory hierarchy player.” That’s the most powerful catalyst for a multiple re-rating. As long as the market views SanDisk as a cyclical, option value for something two to three years out barely shows up in the multiple. It’s an asymmetric option: upside if it works, status quo if it doesn’t.

When Does the Cycle Turn?

The answer to this question is ultimately what determines the valuation. Optimism alone isn’t worth your subscription. Let me be specific.

The second half of 2027 is the checkpoint. Three things could converge.

First, new capacity coming online. Kioxia has announced plans to double NAND capacity by 2029. Micron’s US ID1 fab targets 2027 production, and the Singapore fab targets the second half of 2028. If current price levels hold, the incentive to build more only gets stronger.

Second, HDD supply recovery. Seagate has indicated it will start taking 2027 first-half orders, which would reduce the SSD substitution demand.

Third, a slowdown in AI investment. If recession fears grow or AI ROI skepticism emerges, hyperscaler capex could pull back.

But “easing” is not the same as “glut.” As long as Samsung and SK hynix maintain their HBM-first strategy, a full-blown oversupply like past cycles is unlikely. YMTC (China, shipping 294 layers, targeting 15% of global bit share by end of 2026) is a wildcard, but sanctions limit direct access to global markets. Fulfilling Chinese domestic demand could create an indirect easing effect, but it’s not enough to cause a price collapse.

SanDisk-specific risks need a separate look too. The 50:50 joint venture structure creates a ceiling on volume when demand surges. Being a pure NAND play means there’s no DRAM buffer during downturns. Samsung lost 11 trillion won on NAND in 2023, but DRAM carried them through. SanDisk doesn’t have that safety net. Integrated memory companies (Samsung, SK hynix, Micron) can afford to bleed in NAND competition with DRAM profits, and the pure NAND player is the first to feel the pain.

So, Is It Done?

For it to be done, either NAND supply-demand has to flip or AI investment has to break. Neither has sufficient evidence right now. What’s ending is the short-term momentum that took it to $772, not the structural story of NAND supply tightening.

But “it’s not done” doesn’t mean “buy it now.” The 50-day test is right in front of us, and if it fails, another 20% to 30% downside is possible. Catching it without confirmation of a bottom is catching a falling knife.

May 13 earnings is the inflection point. If the Q3 numbers meet guidance, this week’s selloff might become a “wish I’d bought it then” moment a year from now. If they miss, it means the market was right to call peak earnings.

Right now, you watch and wait.
