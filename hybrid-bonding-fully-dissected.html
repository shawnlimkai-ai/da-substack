1. What Is Hybrid Bonding

The old way: bumps

The most traditional method for connecting chip to chip is the bump. A bump is a tiny solder ball that protrudes from the chip surface. You align the bumps on chip A with the bumps on chip B, apply heat and pressure, the solder melts, and the two chips form an electrical connection.

TCB (Thermo-Compression Bonding), currently the dominant method in HBM, works exactly this way. A filler material, either NCF (Non-Conductive Film) or MUF (Molded Underfill), is injected between chips to fill the gaps around the bumps. SK Hynix's MR-MUF (Mass Reflow Molded Underfill) is the best-in-class implementation of this approach.

The problem is that bumps take up space. If the bump pitch (center-to-center distance between bumps) is 40μm, there is an unavoidable gap of tens of microns between chips. That’s fine when you’re stacking four dies. When you need to stack 12, 16, or eventually 20, those accumulated gaps become a hard constraint on total package height.

Hybrid bonding: eliminate the bump

Hybrid bonding removes bumps entirely. Instead of a solder ball, the chip surface exposes bare copper pads surrounded by a dielectric (SiO₂ or SiCN). When two chips are pressed face-to-face, the dielectrics bond first at room temperature or low temperature. A subsequent annealing step heats the assembly, causing the copper pads to expand thermally and form a direct metal-to-metal bond.

Why “hybrid”?

Because dielectric bonding and metal bonding happen simultaneously. It’s not purely copper bonding, and it’s not purely dielectric bonding. The two mechanisms work in tandem.

What that actually means:

First, no bumps means virtually zero gap between chips. You can fit more dies within the same total height. Second, bump pitch was 40μm; with hybrid bonding it can drop below 10μm, and ultimately to sub-micron. Interconnect density goes up by orders of magnitude. Third, removing the bump as an intermediary lowers electrical resistance, speeds up signal transmission, and reduces power consumption. Fourth, bumps were a thermal bottleneck between chips; hybrid bonding also improves heat transfer across the die-to-die interface.

In short: hybrid bonding enables 3D stacking that is denser, faster, more interconnect-rich, and thermally superior.

Pitch: what it is, and why it matters

Earlier I said bump pitch goes from 40μm down to below 10μm. Here’s what that actually means.

Pitch is the center-to-center distance between adjacent connection points. Think of a grid: the spacing between intersections is the pitch. The tighter the spacing, the more connection points you can fit in a given area.

The numbers tell the story. At 40μm pitch, you can fit 25 connection points along a 1mm line. Extend that to a 1mm × 1mm area and you get roughly 625 total connections. At 10μm pitch? 100 per millimeter, 10,000 per square millimeter. Cut the pitch by 4x and interconnect density jumps 16x. Push hybrid bonding to 1μm pitch and you get one million connections per square millimeter — 1,600x the density of a 40μm bump.

Why does this matter for HBM? The HBM4 interface is 2,048 bits wide, double the 1,024 bits of HBM3E. Bit count is the number of “lanes” available to send and receive data simultaneously. More lanes means higher bandwidth. HBM4’s per-stack bandwidth of 2TB/s comes directly from that wider interface.

The challenge is fitting 2,048+ I/O pins into the physical footprint of an HBM die. The area is limited. At 40μm pitch, you simply cannot pack that many I/Os into the available space. HBM4 already requires pitch to be pushed well below 40μm, and future generations will need to approach 10μm. At that point, bump-based TC Bonding hits a wall — the physical size of the bump itself is the bottleneck.

Hybrid bonding solves this at the root. Copper pads sit flush with the chip surface rather than protruding, so the pads themselves can be made much smaller. Smaller pads mean tighter pitch, which means more I/Os in the same area. Below 10μm pitch, you can fit tens of thousands of connection points — more than enough to support 2,048 bits and beyond.

The chain of logic: tighter pitch → more connections per unit area → wider interface → higher bandwidth. HBM bandwidth requirements are growing explosively with each generation. The physical foundation for that bandwidth is pitch. Hybrid bonding is what breaks through the physical limits on pitch.

One more thing. Tighter pitch doesn’t just add connection density. Shorter interconnect paths reduce parasitic capacitance and inductance, which speeds up signal transmission and lowers power consumption. HBM4’s per-pin data rate of 8Gb/s is built on exactly these physical improvements.

The non-negotiable prerequisite: atomic-level surface flatness

For hybrid bonding to work, chip surfaces must be flat at the atomic scale. TC Bonding can tolerate surface irregularity because molten solder compensates for height variations. Hybrid bonding has no such buffer. Surface roughness must be on the order of a few angstroms (1Å = 0.1nm).

The process that achieves this flatness is CMP (Chemical Mechanical Planarization). CMP combines chemical reaction and physical polishing to bring copper pad height and surrounding dielectric height into alignment at the nanometer level. Applied Materials (AMAT) holds a dominant position in the CMP equipment market for hybrid bonding applications. Competitors like Japan’s EBARA exist, but AMAT’s technical lead in hybrid bonding-specific CMP is firmly established. The fact that the real gating factor in hybrid bonding is not the bonder itself but the upstream CMP process will come up again when we get to the beneficiary analysis.

2. Bonding Methods: W2W, D2W, and D2D

Before getting into the Hybrid bonding applications, it helps to understand the three primary bonding methods. W2W, D2W, and D2D are not concepts unique to hybrid bonding; they apply to bonding broadly. But in practice, they come up far more often in the hybrid bonding context. The reason is that hybrid bonding is extremely sensitive to alignment error, particle contamination, and yield loss, which means the choice of assembly format has an outsized impact on both yield and cost.

The Three Methods: W2W, D2W, and D2D

W2W (Wafer-to-Wafer)

Two fully fabricated wafers are aligned and bonded face-to-face as complete units. Alignment and bonding occur in separate chambers, which is why this method delivers the highest alignment accuracy, throughput, and bond yield of the three.

The constraint is that both wafers must have identically sized dies, which rules out heterogeneous integration. And because entire wafers are bonded at once, there’s no opportunity to screen out defective dies beforehand. W2W works best for small, high-yield chips where the yield loss from bonding a bad die to a good one is manageable.

Applications: Sony CIS, YMTC Xtacking (3D NAND), Micro-LED microdisplays, 3D DRAM CBA.

D2W (Die-to-Wafer)

Individual dies are placed and bonded onto a target wafer. This enables KGD (Known Good Die) selection and allows dies of different sizes, thicknesses, and process nodes to be combined on a single wafer, making it the method of choice for heterogeneous integration. D2W splits into two distinct sub-approaches.

DP-D2W (Direct Placement D2W) : Individual dies are picked, flipped, and placed directly onto the target wafer one at a time using a high-precision flip-chip bonder. Maximum flexibility for mixing different die types, but the lowest throughput of the D2W approaches. Each die requires individual surface cleaning and activation, which makes particle control a persistent challenge. Besi’s hybrid bonder is the leading equipment platform for this method.

Applications: HBM stacking (future generations), select TSMC SoIC configurations.

Co-D2W (Collective D2W) : Individual dies are first placed onto a carrier wafer, then the carrier and target wafer are bonded together in a single W2W-like step. This improves throughput over DP-D2W and partially inherits W2W alignment advantages, but adds process steps for carrier preparation, temporary bonding, and carrier separation. EVG is the key equipment supplier for this approach.

Applications: Silicon photonics, TSMC SoIC (reconstituted method).

A notable variant here is Reconstituted W2W, used in TSMC’s SoIC platform. Dies for both the top and bottom layers are individually placed onto separate carrier wafers, and the two carriers are then bonded W2W. AMD’s 3D V-Cache is built this way. A single V-Cache chip requires five bond steps: bottom CPU die to carrier, V-Cache chiplet to carrier, two dummy silicon pieces to carrier, and finally wafer-on-wafer.

D2D (Die-to-Die)

An individual die is bonded directly onto another individual die without a wafer substrate. Complete flexibility, but the lowest throughput of the three methods and significant alignment challenges. Currently limited to research and prototyping. IBM demonstrated D2D hybrid bonding at 2.5μm pitch at IEDM 2024.

Applications: Research and prototype stage only.

With that framework in place, here’s how each major application maps onto these methods, and why some are dramatically harder than others.

3. Where Hybrid Bonding Is Applied, and Why Some Are Harder Than Others

CMOS Image Sensors (CIS): where hybrid bonding was born

CIS was the first application to reach mass production with hybrid bonding. Sony’s IMX260, which shipped in the Samsung Galaxy S7 in 2016, was the first commercial product, built on Xperi’s (now Adeia) DBI (Direct Bond Interconnect) technology under license.

Hybrid bonding in CIS is relatively straightforward. The reason is simple: it uses wafer-to-wafer (W2W) bonding. The pixel array wafer and the signal processing logic wafer are bonded as complete wafers. Both wafers have matching chip sizes, and image sensors tend to have relatively high yields, making W2W viable. W2W bonds an entire wafer at once, which drives high throughput, and alignment precision of around 200nm is sufficient.

Key point: CIS is a two-layer stack. You bond two wafers. That’s it.

Logic/HPC (3D V-Cache, chiplets): the D2W domain

AMD’s 3D V-Cache is the defining example here. TSMC’s SoIC (System on Integrated Chip) platform is used to stack an SRAM cache die on top of a CPU die. Intel’s Foveros Direct falls in the same category.

These applications require die-to-wafer (D2W) bonding. Why not W2W? Because the two dies being bonded are different sizes.

The V-Cache die and the CPU die have different footprints. Combining chips of different dimensions requires placing individual dies onto a wafer one at a time.

D2W is technically much harder than W2W. Handling individual dies increases particulate contamination risk. The dicing process can introduce edge damage. Throughput is significantly lower than W2W.

The tradeoff is that D2W allows KGD (Known Good Die) selection, letting you screen out defective dies before bonding. With W2W, you bond entire wafers together, so a bad die on one side kills the good die it’s bonded to on the other.

Key point: Logic/HPC uses D2W, requires sub-10μm pitch, and demands alignment accuracy below 100nm. Typically two to three layers.

3D NAND: already in production

In 3D NAND, some manufacturers have already introduced hybrid bonding type wafer bonding technologies into mass production. Representative examples include YMTC’s Xtacking and Kioxia’s CBA. In these structures, the memory cell array wafer and the peripheral logic wafer are manufactured separately and then bonded together using a wafer to wafer approach.

3D NAND is relatively well suited for W2W in this kind of memory on logic structure, and in that sense it shares some similarities with CIS. However, NAND scaling beyond 400 layers cannot be explained by hybrid bonding alone. High aspect ratio etching, deck stacking, and changes in peripheral circuit architecture also play important roles together. Therefore, it is more accurate to say that hybrid bonding is becoming one of the key enablers for NAND generations beyond 400 layers.

Key point: 3D NAND is a two-layer W2W bond, similar in structure to CIS, and comparatively straightforward to implement.

Micro-LED: a new front opening in display

There’s another area where hybrid bonding is quietly making inroads: Micro-LED microdisplays.

Micro-LED is a display technology where inorganic semiconductor (GaN-based) material emits the light. It’s far brighter than OLED, capable of millions of nits, and superior on both longevity and power efficiency. The reason OLED hits a wall in AR glasses is brightness. Seeing augmented reality imagery clearly outdoors requires tens of thousands of nits or more. OLED can’t get there. Micro-LED can.

The challenge is connecting the Micro-LED chips to their CMOS driver circuits (the backplane). AR glasses displays pack millions of pixels into a 0.1 to 0.5 inch form factor, which puts pixel pitch at 5 to 10μm or below. At that density, conventional indium bump or solder flip-chip bonding simply can’t meet the required alignment accuracy or interconnect density. Bonding a GaN Micro-LED wafer to a CMOS wafer using W2W hybrid bonding solves this: no bumps, pitch below 5μm, and hundreds of millions of connections formed in a single step.

Structurally, this is close to CIS. W2W, two layers, matched wafer sizes. The unique challenge compared to CIS is that GaN (a III-V compound semiconductor) and silicon are different materials with different coefficients of thermal expansion. Managing the strain that arises from that CTE mismatch is a problem CIS doesn’t have.

From an investment standpoint, two things matter here. First, if the AR glasses market opens up seriously starting in 2027, Micro-LED microdisplay demand spikes, and that entire market converts into hybrid bonding equipment demand. While HBM hybrid bonding adoption slips by a year or two, Micro-LED can fill the gap in equipment vendor revenue. Second, there is ongoing research into Micro-LED-based optical interconnects, where the electrical connections between GPU and HBM in AI servers would be replaced by Micro-LED optical links. Hybrid bonding at sub-5μm pitch is cited as a core enabling technology for this. Still early-stage, but it could be what opens the generation after CPO.

Key point: Micro-LED is W2W two-layer, but the heterogeneous (GaN + Si) bond introduces a unique set of challenges that CIS doesn’t face. It’s a new axis of hybrid bonding equipment demand that could come online alongside the AR glasses market.

HBM: the hardest and most consequential battleground

HBM hybrid bonding is fundamentally different from the three applications above. Instead of two layers, you’re stacking 12, 16, and eventually 20+ DRAM dies in a single package. D2W bonding is unavoidable, and you have to repeat it a dozen or more times.

And the need for hybrid bonding in HBM isn’t just about height reduction. HBM4’s 2,048-bit interface means the number of die-to-die connection pins has exploded. As explained in the pitch section, fitting that many I/Os into a constrained die area requires pitch to approach 10μm. The demands of height reduction and I/O density are both pushing toward hybrid bonding simultaneously.

Why this is a nightmare is covered in detail in the next section.

3D DRAM (CBA): the chapter hybrid bonding is about to open

There’s one more application that deserves attention here. 3D DRAM. It’s easy to conflate with HBM, but the two are fundamentally different.

HBM is physically stacking multiple conventional DRAM dies. Each individual die is still a traditional planar (2D) structure. You take a finished planar DRAM chip, drill it with TSVs (Through-Silicon Vias), and stack it vertically. HBM is a packaging technology.

3D DRAM is different. It stacks the DRAM cells themselves vertically, following the same logic that led 3D NAND to stack flash cells hundreds of layers high. This is a front-end architectural change, not a packaging change.

The most-discussed architecture for 3D DRAM is CBA (CMOS Bonded Array). The concept is straightforward. Today, a DRAM chip has both the memory cell array and the peripheral logic circuits (sense amplifiers, decoders, I/O circuits) on the same die. Because they share the same substrate, the design rules are a compromise. You want cells as small as possible, but the logic circuits have different optimization requirements. Having both on the same wafer means neither can be fully optimized.

CBA separates them physically. Build the cell array on one wafer and the peripheral logic on another, then bond them with hybrid bonding. Each wafer can be independently optimized. The cell array wafer focuses on minimum cell size (4F² structures), the logic wafer focuses on high-performance circuit design.

What Samsung demonstrated at ISSCC in March 2026 is exactly this: a 16Gb DRAM using a 4F² VCT (Vertical Channel Transistor) structure, with the cell array and peripheral circuits fabricated on separate wafers and joined by hybrid bonding. SK Hynix presented a similar approach at VLSI 2025. Still research-stage, but the direction is clear.

The important detail: CBA uses W2W hybrid bonding. Cell wafer and logic wafer, same size, bonded as complete wafers. Same category as CIS and 3D NAND. Much more tractable than repeating D2W sixteen times for an HBM stack. W2W hybrid bonding is already proven in production.

Why both threads matter

Either way, hybrid bonding is essential to both tracks.

Track one is HBM stacking: D2W stacking of 16 to 20 conventional DRAM dies, driven by pitch density and height constraints. Hard, but unavoidable.

Track two is DRAM cell architecture: W2W CBA bonding that separates cell and logic for independent optimization. Technically easier, already demonstrated.

The timelines differ. HBM hybrid bonding is targeting mass production around 2027 to 2028 with HBM4E. 3D DRAM CBA will see early products in a similar timeframe, but full-volume production isn’t expected until 2030 and beyond. Yole projects that the industry won’t be able to avoid migrating to 3D DRAM until the 0c/0d nodes around 2033 to 2034.

From an investor perspective, the implication is that hybrid bonding’s TAM is not just HBM. 3D DRAM, 3D NAND, logic chiplets, and CIS are all expanding their use of hybrid bonding in parallel. Even if HBM adoption slips by a year or two, other applications provide a floor for equipment vendor revenue. That’s why the long-term investment cases for Besi and AMAT don’t depend on any single application.

4. Why HBM Hybrid Bonding Is So Much Harder

Problem 1: The layer count is categorically different

CIS: two layers. 3D V-Cache: two or three layers. NAND: two layers. HBM: sixteen layers. And headed to twenty.

It took TSMC years to stabilize two-layer D2W hybrid bonding for SoIC production. Now try doing that sixteen times in a row. Each step’s yield compounds. If single-step yield is 99%, after sixteen steps overall yield is 0.99^16, roughly 85%. If single-step yield drops to 98%, overall yield falls to 0.98^16, about 72%. A one percentage point drop at any single step cascades into a multi-percentage-point hit on the final stack.

Problem 2: Ultra-thin die handling

Fitting sixteen layers into a 775μm package height requires individual DRAM dies to be thinned to roughly 30μm. For reference, a human hair is about 70μm thick. You’re handling silicon that’s less than half the thickness of a hair, without breaking it, while maintaining atomic-level surface flatness.

Dies that thin are extremely susceptible to warpage. A warped die misaligns the hybrid bonding surfaces. Misalignment means copper pads don’t make contact, and the bond fails. TC Bonding has solder bumps to absorb some height variation. Hybrid bonding has no such tolerance.

Problem 3: CMP at scale

The success of hybrid bonding depends on CMP. Sixteen layers means CMP at every layer. But conventional DRAM manufacturing didn’t require CMP at this frequency. With TC Bonding, CMP isn’t needed at all for this step. The bump provides enough compliance that extreme surface flatness isn’t required.

Adding hybrid bonding to HBM means inserting sixteen or more CMP steps that didn’t exist before. That increases cycle time, cost, and yield risk. AMAT’s dominant position in CMP equipment also creates a potential supply bottleneck.

Problem 4: Cleanroom requirements

TC Bonding runs in a conventional back-end packaging environment. The cleanroom class requirements are lower than front-end logic fabrication. Hybrid bonding is different. You’re dealing with atomic-scale surfaces. A single particle can cause a bond failure. The environment effectively needs front-end-class cleanliness.

For memory manufacturers, that means major capital investment. Existing packaging lines can’t be repurposed directly. Hybrid bonding-specific lines need to be built from scratch.

Problem 5: Cost

A hybrid bonder costs roughly $3 million per tool. A TC bonder runs $1 to $2 million. Equipment alone is 1.5 to 3x more expensive. Layer on top of that the added CMP costs, the front-end-class cleanroom maintenance, and the yield loss costs, and total cost of ownership is several times higher than the current approach.

HBM is already expensive relative to standard DRAM. Add the hybrid bonding cost premium and the price goes higher still. Whether NVIDIA absorbs that increase or passes it through to GPU pricing involves the interests of the entire supply chain.

Why memory manufacturers would rather not do this right now

Five technical challenges laid out. The core reasons memory manufacturers want to delay hybrid bonding come down to four things.

First, the process cost is prohibitive. Tool cost alone is 1.5 to 3x higher than TC bonding. Add CMP steps, front-end cleanroom build-out, and new materials, and TCO is several times the current baseline. Memory is fundamentally a cost business. Voluntarily adopting a technology that blows up your cost structure is a hard sell, even for a premium product.

Second, yield isn’t there yet. It took TSMC years to stabilize two- to three-layer D2W hybrid bonding in production. Memory manufacturers are still at the prototype stage for HBM-scale stacking. Getting sixteen-layer D2W to production yield has a long road ahead. Running at low yield means destroying large numbers of expensive DRAM dies. HBM DRAM dies cost significantly more than commodity DRAM, so yield-driven scrap is an economically painful scenario for any management team.

Third, the infrastructure has to be built from the ground up. TC bonding works on existing back-end packaging lines. Hybrid bonding doesn’t. You need CMP equipment, hybrid bonders, surface activation tools, annealing equipment, and new inline inspection systems. Cleanroom class needs to step up from back-end to front-end levels. This is hundreds of millions of dollars in capex. Not retrofitting a few machines, but closer to building a new factory.

Fourth, and most pragmatically: the current technology still works. SK Hynix’s MR-MUF is delivering industry-leading yields on 12-layer HBM3E stacks today. JEDEC’s relaxation of the HBM4 package thickness to 775μm means TC bonding can handle 16-layer HBM4. Engineering organizations don’t voluntarily abandon proven technology for unproven alternatives without a compelling reason. SK Hynix’s strategy of pushing hybrid bonding to HBM4E (20-layer) and maximizing the life of MR-MUF is entirely rational.

These four factors reinforce each other. High cost means low-yield production is more painful. Improving yield requires more infrastructure investment. Justifying that investment is hard when the existing technology still works. From a sound business perspective, delay is the rational call.

5. Will HBM Actually Use Hybrid Bonding?

I asked numerous engineers I personally know at SK Hynix, Samsung, and Micron, all currently working in the HBM field.

“So, are you actually going to do hybrid bonding for HBM?”

And every single one of them answered the same way.

“Of course! ...Eventually.”

That “eventually” captures the entire reality of the industry in one word.

Nobody disputes the direction. The question is timing.

Why hybrid bonding is coming anyway

The previous section laid out the reasons memory manufacturers want to delay. They’re rational and well-founded. But there’s an expiration date on the delay strategy. Four forces are pushing back from the other direction.

First, physical limits. The simplest and most powerful argument. Beyond 20 layers, TC bonding’s economics and technical feasibility deteriorate sharply. Accumulated bump gaps push package height past JEDEC spec. As of March 2026, JEDEC is discussing further thickness relaxation to 825 to 900μm for HBM4E and HBM5, but even that has limits. An HBM stack needs to match the height of the co-packaged GPU die for coplanarity. 775μm is the standard silicon wafer thickness; going above that forces the logic side to be raised artificially, which complicates interposer design and introduces signal integrity problems. Thicker stacks also make thermal management worse. Thickness relaxation is buying time, not solving anything.

Second, I/O density requirements. HBM4 is 2,048 bits wide. Future generations will go wider. As described in the pitch section, fitting that many I/Os into a limited die area requires pitch below 10μm. The physical bump pitch limit for TC bonding is around 10μm. Below that, the bump itself becomes the bottleneck. Only hybrid bonding supports sub-10μm and ultimately sub-micron pitch. Falling behind on bandwidth means not getting designed into NVIDIA’s next GPU. For memory manufacturers, that’s existential.

Third, thermal performance. This one gets underestimated, but it may matter more in the long run than height reduction. The bumps and underfill materials in TC bonding have much lower thermal conductivity than silicon. They create a thermal resistance bottleneck in the heat path from inside the stack to the outside world. Hybrid bonding’s direct copper-to-copper, dielectric-to-dielectric interface dramatically reduces that thermal resistance. AI accelerator power consumption is rising with every generation, and thermal management is already a hard constraint. Needham named Besi as a top semiconductor pick in January 2026, and one of their core arguments was that thermal issues in 16-layer stacks could pull forward hybrid bonding adoption.

Fourth, competitive dynamics. If any one of the three memory manufacturers achieves hybrid bonding production first, the other two have to follow. That’s exactly why Samsung is being aggressive. Having failed to match SK Hynix on TC-NCF-based TC bonding, Samsung’s strategy is to seize the lead on hybrid bonding before the game resets. If Samsung lands hybrid bonding production in HBM4E, SK Hynix and Micron have no choice but to respond. Technology transitions have this cascade structure: one company succeeds, and the others get pulled along. “Not wanting to do it” and “being able to avoid it” are different questions.

The memory manufacturers have rational, defensible reasons to delay. But physical limits, I/O density demands, thermal constraints, and competitive pressure are all pushing from the other direction. The reasons to delay are about cost and risk. The reasons it has to happen are about physics and competition. Cost and risk get resolved over time. Physics doesn’t negotiate.

Timeline

The original industry consensus was that HBM4 (16-layer) would require hybrid bonding. But JEDEC’s decision to relax the HBM4 package thickness from 720μm to 775μm made it possible to build 16-layer HBM4 with conventional TC bonding. Hybrid bonding adoption got pushed back by one generation.

Where things stand in March 2026:

SK Hynix appears to be the most conservative of the three. Publicly, the company has completed development and mass production readiness for 12 layer HBM4 using its existing MR MUF approach, and has also showcased 16 Hi HBM4. For now, hybrid bonding still appears to be targeted more toward HBM4E, where the layer count and packaging constraints become much more severe.

Samsung is the most aggressive. The company has strongly signaled hybrid bonding adoption starting with 16-layer HBM4E. In July 2024, Samsung EVP Kim Dae-woo presented the company’s next-generation semiconductor packaging roadmap and raised the possibility of applying hybrid bonding in HBM4E. Samsung’s equipment subsidiary Semes has produced 16-layer HBM samples with hybrid bonding and confirmed they function correctly. That said, this remains a technology roadmap signal and a sample demonstration, not a confirmed production commitment. Whether it actually goes into mass production depends on where yield and cost land. Samsung’s motivation is clear: it hasn’t been able to catch SK Hynix on TC-NCF-based TC bonding. Hybrid bonding is a reset of the game board, and Samsung wants to get there first.

Micron has not disclosed a specific hybrid bonding production timeline. They licensed Adeia’s hybrid bonding IP in 2022, but the current focus is on ramping HBM4 using TC bonding. Among the three, Micron is most likely to be last.

Note: Everything above is a summary of what can be pieced together from public reporting. To go deeper, I personally spoke with engineers currently working at SK Hynix and Samsung's memory divisions about hybrid bonding. Their position was that they're targeting HBM4E for initial adoption, but given the cost challenges, it may realistically not happen at scale until HBM5. What matters most, though, is what I was able to confirm directly from them: technically, they can no longer afford to delay.

6. Beneficiary Analysis: Who Makes the Money

Hybrid bonding is still early in mass production adoption. But equipment orders move one to two years ahead of production ramps. Orders are already flowing in 2026, and serious production is expected in 2027 to 2028 with HBM4E and HBM5. Here’s how to think about the beneficiaries.

Tier 1: Besi (BESI, Euronext Amsterdam)

The clear number-one in hybrid bonding equipment. Per Besi’s own disclosures, the company’s current hybrid bonding roadmap includes 100nm class tools in deployment and 50nm class tools under development, with the longer term goal of supporting sub 4μm pitch.

Based on Needham estimates, Samsung and Micron have Besi hybrid bonders installed, with additional deployments expected through 2026. Per Financial Times reporting, Besi’s hybrid bonding revenue is projected to grow from roughly €36 million in 2023 to approximately €476 million in 2026, potentially representing a third of total company revenue. These are analyst estimates, so actual figures will vary depending on how HBM adoption timing plays out.

In March 2026, Reuters reported that Besi has received takeover approaches, with Lam Research named as one of the parties in discussions. AMAT, which already holds a 9% stake as Besi’s largest shareholder, is also flagged as a potential interested party. No formal bid has been confirmed, but the news alone sent Besi’s stock up more than 10% in a session. The Netherlands government’s Vifo Act (which gives the government review authority over foreign acquisitions of strategic technology companies) remains a wildcard, but the episode was a clear market revaluation of Besi’s strategic importance.

Risk: Continued delays in HBM hybrid bonding adoption can reset near-term revenue expectations downward. Tool pricing in the $1.5 to $2.5 million range makes the revenue line sensitive to customer capex cycles.

Tier 1: Applied Materials (AMAT, NASDAQ)

The dominant leader in CMP equipment for hybrid bonding applications. Competitors like EBARA exist, but AMAT’s technical advantage in hybrid bonding-specific CMP is firmly established. Wherever hybrid bonding is applied, CMP is required. Every additional CMP step across every application goes through AMAT tools.

Beyond CMP, AMAT also supplies deposition, etch, and metrology equipment. As hybrid bonding takes on an increasingly front-end character, AMAT equipment gets pulled deeper into packaging lines.

The 9% Besi stake provides indirect upside from Besi’s growth. Per AMAT’s official guidance, the company guided Q2 fiscal year 2026 revenue at approximately $7.65 billion, significantly above the roughly $7.03 billion Wall Street had expected. Management disclosed that production expansion is being planned for the second half of 2026 in anticipation of increased demand.

Risk: Per available reporting, China revenue is estimated to have dropped from roughly 45% of total in 2024 to approximately 25% in Q4 2025. Continued export control tightening could push it lower.

Tier 2: EV Group (EVG, private)

An Austrian company with a strong position in W2W hybrid bonding. EVG is a key equipment supplier for CIS and 3D NAND applications. Not publicly listed, so no direct investment access. But tracking EVG’s technology developments matters for understanding how Besi and ASMPT are positioned in their respective segments.

Tier 2: ASMPT (0522.HK)

A semiconductor back-end equipment company that spun out from ASM International (not to be confused with ASML, which is a separate company entirely). Currently dominant in TC bonding for HBM, with next-generation tools in development, including the AOR TCB (Fluxless TCB) and hybrid bonder commercialization. ASMPT is a follower to Besi on hybrid bonding, but it’s working to convert its TC bonding installed base at HBM customers into a hybrid bonding position as the transition happens.

Tier 2: Adeia (ADEA, NASDAQ)

The company that owns the foundational IP for hybrid bonding. After acquiring Ziptronix’s DBI (Direct Bond Interconnect) technology in 2015, Adeia has been licensing it to Sony, YMTC, Micron, Kioxia, and others. As hybrid bonding proliferates, the royalty stream grows. TSMC and Intel have developed independent IP, but most memory manufacturers either license Adeia’s portfolio or are building their own patent position. YMTC in particular has been aggressively expanding its hybrid bonding patent portfolio.

Tier 3: KLA (KLAC, NASDAQ) / Onto Innovation (ONTO, NASDAQ)

Hybrid bonding creates a step-function increase in demand for inline inspection and metrology. Post-bond void detection, alignment error measurement, and interface analysis all require specialized tools using optical interferometry, acoustic microscopy, and infrared transmission inspection. Inspection tool revenue tracks equipment revenue broadly, so these companies benefit in proportion to the overall ramp.

Tier 3: Hanmi Semiconductor (042700.KQ)

The dominant TC bonder supplier today, with a large installed base at SK Hynix and Micron. There’s a scenario where TC bonder revenue at Hanmi gets displaced as hybrid bonding ramps; Hanmi is developing hybrid bonder capability to address this. In the meantime, TC bonding remaining as the primary approach continues to benefit Hanmi by keeping TC bonder demand elevated. The delayed hybrid bonding adoption timeline is, paradoxically, good for Hanmi near-term. The long-term question is how fast it can make the transition and how competitive its hybrid bonder turns out to be.

Summary

The most durable beneficiaries across all hybrid bonding applications are AMAT (the leading CMP supplier) and Besi (the top hybrid bonder vendor). These two companies benefit wherever hybrid bonding gets applied: CIS, logic chiplets, HBM, NAND, Micro-LED. Everything else varies by application and timing.

Closing: This Is No Longer a Question of “Will It Happen”

Hybrid bonding has more than ten years of production history in CIS and over three years in logic chiplets. The technology works. The challenge in HBM is specific: the extreme condition of stacking 16 to 20 layers in a single package is what makes it hard.

JEDEC’s thickness relaxation kept TC bonding viable through the HBM4 generation. But from HBM4E at 20 layers onward, TC bonding’s economics and technical feasibility deteriorate sharply, and hybrid bonding becomes the most viable path forward. The entire industry is in preparation mode and equipment orders are already moving.

This technology shift is rewiring the semiconductor supply chain. As packaging increasingly takes on front-end characteristics, CMP vendors, bonder vendors, inspection vendors, and IP licensors all get new growth vectors. At the same time, TC bonder vendors are racing a transition whose pace they don’t fully control.

Whether you’re an investor or an engineer, hybrid bonding is now a prerequisite for understanding how the semiconductor industry works. Asking “will it happen” is behind the curve. The real questions are: when, at what yield, and who gets to production first.
