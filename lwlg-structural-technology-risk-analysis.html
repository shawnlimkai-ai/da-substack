1. Background: Why Next-Generation Modulators Are Needed

Data transfer between servers in data centers happens as light traveling through optical fiber. The optical transceiver that handles this conversion contains a modulator. It takes electrical signals (0s and 1s) and changes the properties of light to encode them.

With the AI era, the speed demanded from modulators has surged. Per-lane rates are moving from 100G to 200G to 400G, and full transceiver targets are 1.6Tbps and 3.2Tbps.

The mainstream silicon modulator works by injecting or removing electrons inside a waveguide to change its refractive index (carrier plasma dispersion). Because electrons have to physically move, there’s a speed ceiling, and the modulation process absorbs light, degrading signal quality. In mainstream silicon implementations, these tradeoffs escalate sharply beyond 200G/lane. Today, DSP (digital signal processing) chips compensate for these limits, but DSPs themselves consume a lot of power.

This is where demand for next-generation modulators comes from. LWLG’s EO polymer is one of the candidates.

2. EO Polymer Modulators: Where LWLG’s Technology Actually Stands

Perkinamine

LWLG’s core asset is a material platform called Perkinamine. EO stands for “electro-optic,” meaning the material changes its optical properties when an electric field is applied. It’s a specialized organic polymer.

The actual substance consists of chromophores (specialized organic molecules that interact with light) embedded in a polymer matrix. When you apply an electric field to these chromophores, the electron distribution in the molecules shifts, which changes the speed (refractive index) of light passing through. This is called the Pockels effect. Unlike silicon modulators that physically move electrons around, the electric field itself changes the material’s properties, which makes it much faster.

The key performance metric for EO polymers is r33 (the electro-optic coefficient). It measures how much the refractive index changes per unit of applied electric field. A higher r33 means you can modulate light with less voltage, and you can achieve the same performance in a smaller device.

How It’s Made

Inside a silicon photonics chip, you create a narrow gap (slot waveguide) in the waveguide and fill it with Perkinamine. The filling method is spin-coating: you drop liquid polymer onto the wafer and spin it at high speed to spread a thin, uniform film.

After filling, a process called poling is required. This aligns the chromophore molecules in one direction. If the molecules are randomly oriented, the electro-optic effects cancel out. You heat the material and apply a strong electric field to align the molecules, then cool it down so they lock in place.

The resulting modulator operates in an MZI (Mach-Zehnder Interferometer) structure. Light is split into two paths, voltage is applied to one path to shift its phase, then the two paths are recombined. When the phases match, the output is bright (1). When they’re opposite, it goes dark (0).

_ The key advantage is that you can use existing silicon photonics foundry 300mm lines to fabricate the silicon waveguide, then simply add spin-coating and poling steps._

_ No special wafers needed._

Published Specs and Validation Status

Here’s what LWLG has disclosed about Perkinamine’s specs.

Bandwidth target is 110GHz or above, with 200Gbps/lane demonstrated. The exact r33 value is undisclosed, but academic literature reports EO polymer r33 above 65 pm/V. For comparison, LiNbO3 has an r33 of about 31 pm/V, so EO polymers are roughly 2x higher. The material does not use rare earth elements, reducing related geopolitical supply risk.

For reliability testing, LWLG announced in July 2025 that it passed Telcordia GR-468 85/85 testing: 1,000 hours at 85°C and 85% humidity. This is an accelerated life test that extrapolates long-term durability from short-term harsh conditions. It is not the same as guaranteeing 25 years of real-world operation.

The Tg (glass transition temperature, the point where the polymer softens and loses its electro-optic properties) was reported in LWLG’s 2024 technical documentation as composite Tg 170°C and lot average Tg 186.76°C.

The fact that Tg used to be undisclosed was a common criticism, so publishing these numbers is a positive step. However, this is company-reported data, not independently verified. Whether a Tg of 170°C holds up for 10 or 20 years in a data center environment can only be confirmed after actual volume production.

Current Development Stage

At the material level, basic performance has been demonstrated to some degree.

At the device (chip) level, production has not been qualified. Engineering tapeouts at Tower and GF are planned for mid-2026.

At the module (transceiver) level, no finished product has ever been shown. LWLG is a material/IP company and doesn’t build transceivers itself, but no customer-built transceiver using an LWLG modulator has been publicly shown either.

Per the company’s February 2026 update,

four Fortune Global 500 customers are at Stage 3 (prototype to product).

In the 10-K filing as of January 2026, it was three. The February 24 update brought it to four.

Roughly 15 customers are in Stages 1 through 2. Zero customers have reached Stage 4 (formal production decision).

All customer names are undisclosed.

The company projects the earliest high-volume commercial production revenue in 2027.

Structural Weaknesses

These are the problems that have blocked EO polymer commercialization for 30 years.

Tg and thermal stability.

If the Tg is 170°C (per company data), there’s adequate margin for pluggable environments (70 to 85°C). But in CPO environments where ASIC junction temperatures exceed 100°C, whether a 170°C Tg provides sufficient long-term margin is a separate question. And again, this figure is self-reported, not independently verified.

Photo-oxidation.

Organic materials degrade when exposed to light over time through reaction with oxygen. Data center equipment runs 24/7. The CEO mentioned on the Q4 2025 earnings call that they had expanded data on this, but did not disclose specific numbers.

Poling stability.

The molecular alignment can relax over time, especially at elevated temperatures.

Production yield.

Uniformly filling polymer into slot waveguides and performing consistent poling across an entire 300mm wafer is a high-difficulty process. Lab-scale success and production-scale yield are entirely different problems.

3. What the PDK News Actually Means

The March 2026 news that sent LWLG’s stock soaring was the Tower Semiconductor and GlobalFoundries announcements. These were bundled together as “PDK milestones,” but the actual content reflects different stages of progress.

Tower Semiconductor: Development Agreement (March 12)

What was signed with Tower is a development agreement. The goal is to integrate LWLG’s modulator reference design into Tower’s PH18 silicon photonics PDK (the component library and design rule set that chip designers use). Multiple engineering tapeouts are planned for 2026 to validate 200G and 400G modulator performance. The stock jumped 41% in a single day.

The key point: this is an agreement to proceed with integration. It is not an announcement that integration is complete.

GlobalFoundries: “Now Available” in PDK (March 16)

The GF announcement used different language. LWLG’s modulator platform is “now available” in the GDSFactory PDK. This means designers can already incorporate the LWLG modulator into their chip designs through this PDK. The announcement also mentioned that GF is optimizing its 300mm process for slot waveguide fabrication.

This is one step ahead of Tower, and it signals foundry-level investment in the process.

What PDK Entry Does Not Mean

Being in a PDK means “it’s on the menu.” It does not mean “orders have come in.” PDKs contain dozens to hundreds of device options, and only a fraction of those end up in actual customer products. No customer has publicly disclosed adopting the LWLG modulator in a product design, and production yield has not been validated.

Tower’s PH18 platform already has its own built-in silicon modulators. Over the past 12 months, Tower has been expanding its ecosystem across the SiPho stack through six partnerships: Alcyon Photonics (design IP), LightIC (FMCW LiDAR), NVIDIA (1.6T optical modules), Scintil Photonics (DWDM lasers), and Salience Labs (optical switching). Among these, LWLG is the only external modulator material partner. But that does not mean Tower has gone all-in on EO polymers. It added one more option on top of its existing silicon modulators, something customers can reach for when they need higher performance.

Tower’s risk here is close to zero. LWLG builds the reference design and puts it in the PDK. If a customer picks it, Tower manufactures. If nobody picks it, that’s the end of it.

That said, registering a device in a PDK is not cost-free. The foundry has to verify process compatibility, write design rules, and commit engineering resources. Given that Tower is investing $920M in CapEx for its SiPho business and put LWLG into the PDK after technical due diligence, it is at least a signal that foundry-level validation of technical feasibility has been passed.

Still, the market is reading this news as “volume production is getting close,” but from PDK inclusion to actual production revenue, there is a long road ahead: customer adoption, trial design, tapeout, performance validation, and production decision. In the semiconductor industry, this process typically takes 2 to 3 years or more.

The Difference from HyperLight

During the same week (March 11 to 13, 2026), TFLN company HyperLight also announced a major partnership. Comparing the details reveals a maturity gap.

HyperLight signed a “strategic manufacturing partnership for high-volume foundry production” with UMC (a global top-5 foundry). This is not just putting a device in a PDK.

It’s a commitment to allocate 6-inch and 8-inch production lines to TFLN chiplet manufacturing. On top of that, Jabil (one of the world’s largest electronics manufacturing services companies) is handling system integration and assembly. And HyperLight has already demonstrated a 1.6T-DR8 transceiver reference module and announced a 400G/lane PIC as a product.

LWLG is at the stage of getting a design into a PDK. HyperLight has secured production lines and an assembly partner. Both made “partnered with a foundry” news the same week, but they are not at the same place.

4. Competition: EO Polymer Is Not the Only Player in This Market

The high-speed optical modulator market for 200G/lane and beyond has at least four competing technology tracks. Here are the three competing with LWLG’s EO polymer.

Silicon Photonics Modulators

Silicon modulators have physical limits, but massive companies are pushing through with DSP + PAM4 + advanced design to reach 200G/lane, and there’s no sign they’re giving up the mainstream position anytime soon. Recent research has even demonstrated extending native silicon photonic device bandwidth beyond 100GHz. The limits are real, but it’s too early to say “silicon is done.”

Broadcom’s Tomahawk 5-Bailly is the industry’s first production CPO. Its third-generation CPO (200G/lane) was announced, and Tomahawk 6 Davidson integrates 200G/lane CPO into a 102.4Tbps switch. Marvell demonstrated a 1.6T silicon photonics light engine (200G x 8 channels) at OFC 2025. NVIDIA unveiled a silicon photonics CPO switch at GTC 2025, built with TSMC.

Trillion-dollar companies are investing billions, and they already have products in production.

III-V (InP) Modulators

InP-based EAMs (modulators that change light absorption when voltage is applied) are also strong contenders. Lumentum is a leading confirmed supplier currently scale-shipping 200G/lane EMLs. NVIDIA’s multi-billion dollar investments in Lumentum and Coherent back this line.

OpenLight presented a 1.6T DR8 evaluation board that integrates lasers, modulators, amplifiers, and photodetectors on a single chip by placing InP on silicon. EO polymers only provide the modulator and need a separate laser, which is a structural difference from InP’s single-chip integration capability.

TFLN (Thin-Film Lithium Niobate)

This is the most directly comparable technology to LWLG. It uses the same Pockels effect but with an inorganic crystal (LiNbO3) instead.

HyperLight: Harvard spinoff. $37M Series B led by Summit Partners (September 2024). Partnerships with UMC + Wavetek + Jabil. 6-inch production line operational, 8-inch pilot line secured. Shipping a 145GHz packaged intensity modulator. Shipping a 110GHz IQ modulator. Announced a 448Gbps/lane IMDD PIC. Demonstrated a 1.6T-DR8 transceiver. Unifying pluggable, coherent, and CPO on a single platform.

POET Technologies + QCi: POET is an optical interposer platform company. It’s integrating QCi’s TFLN modulator onto its platform to develop a 3.2Tbps optical engine (targeted for completion in H2 2026). POET’s CEO has stated that “among high-speed modulator materials currently in development, including silicon, InP, and advanced polymers, TFLN is the most promising for early commercialization.” A company that builds optical engine platforms chose TFLN over EO polymer.

Liobate (China): Exhibited 1.6T/3.2T DR8 and 110GHz modulators as products at OFC.

EO Polymer vs. TFLN

Thermal stability: LiNbO3 has a Curie temperature of about 1,140°C. The concept of Tg doesn’t even apply. At the material level, it is far more favorable in CPO environments (100°C+). Actual CPO systems still face packaging, assembly, and cooling challenges at the system level, but the raw thermal margin of the material is in a different league. EO polymer’s Tg of 170°C provides adequate margin in pluggable environments, but its long-term margin in CPO remains unproven. TFLN has a structural advantage in thermal stability.

r33: EO polymer (65+ pm/V) is roughly 2x that of LiNbO3 (31 pm/V). EO polymer has the edge for low-voltage operation and miniaturization.

Bandwidth: LWLG is targeting 110GHz or above. Meanwhile, HyperLight announced a 145GHz packaged intensity modulator as a product in March 2026, supporting 448Gbps/lane IMDD. Its IQ modulator has reached 125GHz. Where LWLG is at “110GHz target,” TFLN is already shipping at 145GHz. TFLN is ahead on bandwidth too.

Long-term reliability: LiNbO3 is an inorganic crystal with decades of proven use in telecommunications equipment. It has none of the organic-material-specific degradation issues like photo-oxidation or poling instability. The publicly visible commercialization trajectory is also further along for TFLN.

Process integration: EO polymers can be directly applied to existing silicon wafers via spin-coating. TFLN requires fabricating chiplets on a separate wafer and bonding them. EO polymer has the advantage in process simplicity.

Supply chain: Perkinamine does not use rare earth elements. TFLN wafer supply is concentrated among a small number of vendors, creating supply chain concentration risk.

EO polymer’s clear advantages are process integration ease and high r33. Whether those two strengths are decisive enough to offset the remaining weaknesses (thermal, reliability, ecosystem) is the central technical question.

5. Product-by-Product Analysis: Where It Can Win and Where It Loses

Pluggable Transceivers: Most Realistic, but Not Alone

These are the modules you plug into and unplug from the front panel of a switch. Commercial-grade operating temperature is 0 to 70°C, industrial-grade is -40 to 85°C. Since they’re physically separated from the ASIC, the thermal environment is the mildest.

Having passed Telcordia GR-468 85/85, and with a Tg of 170°C, there’s adequate thermal margin for pluggable environments. One of LWLG’s Tier-1 customer programs targets 1.6T pluggable (200G/lane).

The problem is that competing products already exist in this market. Marvell has demonstrated a silicon-based 1.6T light engine. OpenLight has demonstrated an InP-based 1.6T DR8. HyperLight has demonstrated a TFLN-based 1.6T-DR8 reference module. LWLG has never demonstrated a completed 1.6T transceiver. It has material and reference designs, but no module built with them has been publicly shown.

For EO polymer to gain a foothold in this market, it needs to prove manufacturing cost advantages based on spin-coating. On technical performance alone, the competition is tough.

NPO (Near-Package Optics): The Market Itself Hasn’t Formed Yet

This is a configuration where the optical engine sits on the same board near the ASIC, rather than inside the ASIC package. Estimated operating temperature is 70 to 90°C. The thermal environment is similar to pluggable, so technically it’s not a bad fit.

However, NPO itself is not yet standardized. It received significant attention at OFC 2026 with vendor presentations, but standardization and large-scale deployment paths are still early-stage. The industry could skip NPO entirely and go from LPO straight to CPO. LWLG has not announced that it’s explicitly targeting NPO. This is double uncertainty: an uncertain technology in an uncertain market.

CPO (Co-Packaged Optics): The Biggest Opportunity and the Biggest Contradiction

CPO places the optical engine inside the same package as the ASIC, minimizing electrical paths and eliminating DSPs for 40 to 70% power savings. LWLG’s second Fortune 500 customer program targets 400Gb/s CPO, and this is the segment where the market assigns LWLG its biggest premium.

But not all CPO is the same. The thermal environment varies dramatically depending on the type of ASIC, and EO polymer’s applicability changes accordingly.

Switch ASIC + CPO (scale-out). This is co-packaging optical engines with network switch chips. Broadcom’s Tomahawk 5-Bailly is the first production CPO, and Tomahawk 6 Davidson integrates 200G/lane CPO into a 102.4Tbps switch. Switch ASICs typically run at 300 to 500W TDP, and because the die area is large, power density (W/cm²) stays in a manageable range. The optical engine sits on the ASIC substrate in a socketable form, thermally separated from the ASIC die through an interposer or substrate. Junction temperatures are high (85 to 105°C), but thermal design margin is achievable.

Assuming Perkinamine’s Tg of 170°C, switch CPO offers tens of degrees of thermal margin. That said, this is a steady-state number. How poling stability changes under sustained thermal stress, and how photo-oxidation accumulates over time, requires separate validation. Switch CPO is the CPO segment where EO polymer has the highest chance of technical viability.

XPU (GPU/AI Accelerator) + CPO (scale-up). This is co-packaging optical engines directly with AI accelerator chips. The CPO switch NVIDIA unveiled at GTC 2025 moves in this direction. Marvell’s custom XPU architecture places HBM, compute dies, and 3D SiPho engines on a single substrate. This is for scale-up fabric, using optics to overcome copper’s reach limits in direct GPU-to-GPU connections like NVLink.

The thermal environment here changes drastically. AI accelerator TDPs exceed 700W and are approaching 1,000W. Power density relative to die area approaches or exceeds 100W/cm². The optical engine sits on the same substrate, within millimeters of the die. The entire substrate heats up, and the thermal radiation and conduction hitting the optical engine are on a completely different level from switch CPO. Some XPU CPO designs already require direct liquid cooling.

Even with a Tg of 170°C, the actual temperature near the ASIC die in an XPU CPO environment, and how much poling relaxation occurs after tens of thousands of hours at that temperature, are entirely different questions. LiNbO3 (TFLN), being an inorganic crystal with a Curie temperature of 1,140°C, simply does not have this concern at the material level. No data demonstrating that EO polymer can function in XPU CPO has ever been published.

To summarize, in switch CPO, EO polymer may be technically viable, but silicon is already in production there. In XPU CPO, the thermal environment is the most hostile to EO polymer. The very reason the market assigns LWLG its biggest premium (CPO) is, when you dig into the details, a segment where it either faces entrenched silicon competition (switch CPO) or its greatest technical uncertainty (XPU CPO). This disconnect is the core risk in the current valuation.

6. So Why Did This Stock Rally? Valuation and Checkpoints

Market cap approximately $1.58B (at $10.6 per share). FY2025 revenue $236,855. P/S ratio roughly 6,600x.

Viewed as an option: success case market cap $5B x 30% probability of success = $1.5B expected market cap. This assumes a blue-sky TAM where the optical transceiver market reaches $100B long-term, with EO polymer capturing 10% of the material layer. For reference, per LightCounting, the 100G+ Ethernet transceiver + CPO market is roughly $16.5B in 2025 and $26B in 2026. $100B is a scenario, not reality.

Factoring in the three other competing technology tracks, a realistic market share is 3 to 5%. Success case market cap $1.5 to 2.5B, with 30% success probability yielding an expected market cap of $450 to 750M. That’s less than half the current market cap.

So Why Did the Stock Go Up?

From a 52-week low of $0.79 (April 2025) to above $10. That’s more than a 12x move. It didn’t happen overnight. Catalysts stacked up over six months.

Starting in the second half of 2025, CPO emerged as “the next big thing” in data centers. When NVIDIA publicly unveiled a CPO switch at GTC 2025, the entire optical connectivity theme caught fire. LWLG rode this wave with the combination of “AI + optics + new materials.” In November, the first Fortune 500 Stage 3 announcement came out, pushing the stock into the $3 to 4 range.

On March 2, 2026, NVIDIA announced investments of $2B each in Lumentum and Coherent, totaling $4B. This had nothing to do with LWLG directly. NVIDIA’s money went to InP (Lumentum) and silicon photonics (Coherent). Not a single dollar went to EO polymers. But the market didn’t distinguish between technologies. The headline “NVIDIA is putting $4B into optical infrastructure” lifted the entire AI optics sector, and LWLG rose with it. The stock rallied on news of money flowing to competing technologies as if it were a beneficiary.

Nine days later, on March 11, the Tower development agreement was announced. Regular session +15%, after-hours +67%. The GF PDK “now available” news on March 16 stacked on top, pushing the stock into the $8 to 10 range.

Retail community speculation about customers added fuel along the way. LWLG does not disclose its Fortune Global 500 customer names, but NVIDIA, Broadcom, and Microsoft have been floated.

With Tower already working with NVIDIA on a 1.6T optical module collaboration and LWLG now in Tower’s PDK, speculation circulated that “LWLG is joining the Tower to NVIDIA supply chain.”

On Stocktwits, posts like “the NVIDIA-Tower collaboration could send LWLG up another 10x” gained significant traction. Nothing has been confirmed, but the speculation itself created momentum.

Structural conditions also lined up. At $0.79, the market cap was roughly $120M. In a small cap with limited float, momentum buying and short covering can combine to produce explosive moves.

And paradoxically, having no revenue acted as a shield. With no results to disappoint each quarter, the dream-premium holds. Earnings calls are filled with qualitative updates like “Stage 3 customers have increased” or “tapeouts are underway.”

Ultimately, LWLG’s 12x rally was not driven by “the technology being validated.” It was the result of AI optics theme momentum, the sector rally triggered by NVIDIA’s $4B, Tower/GF headlines, customer speculation, and small-cap dynamics compounding over six months. The fact that NVIDIA’s money went to competing technologies (InP, silicon) is the clearest evidence that the market is trading on theme, not distinguishing between technologies.

7. What It Takes for This Stock Price to Hold

LWLG’s business model itself is attractive. It’s a capital-light structure built on material sales, process licensing, and royalties, supplying materials and IP to foundries without manufacturing directly. Once production starts, margins relative to revenue could be very high. It’s structurally similar to how ARM maintains high margins by licensing chip design IP rather than manufacturing chips itself. The fact that r33 is 2x that of TFLN means the driving voltage is half, and in AI data centers where power is the biggest constraint, that’s an advantage you can’t ignore. Spin-coating-based 300mm process compatibility also offers a cost structure differentiation versus TFLN’s chiplet bonding approach.

But for this model to become reality, the checkpoints that need to be cleared are specific.

  1. The next earnings call (estimated around May 7) needs to show concrete progress on Stage 3 and tapeout timelines.

  2. The H2 2026 Tower/GF tapeouts need to confirm 200G/400G target performance and production-viable yields.

  3. And LWLG needs to show customer adoption results before HyperLight announces a production contract with a hyperscaler first.

ARM has billions of dollars in revenue. LWLG has $237K. The gap between the model’s appeal and the current reality is wide. The first signal that this gap is narrowing will come from the tapeout results. Until then, the market cap sitting on a 6,600x P/S ratio is sustained by expectations, not by technical validation.

Conclusion

EO polymer’s r33 and spin-coating process compatibility are real strengths.

But in the most realistic market for those strengths (pluggable), silicon, InP, and TFLN already have transceiver-level products. In the market where the most money is at stake (CPO), the 30-year-old thermal weakness matters most.

Getting into a PDK is progress, but it’s a long way from production, and HyperLight’s ecosystem, announced the same week, is a step further along.

A company that has spent 30 years repeating “next time will be different” now faces the question of whether this time really is different.

The answer won’t come until the 2026 tapeout results are in.
