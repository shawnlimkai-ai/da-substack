1. What POET Showed at OFC 2026

Let me go through the product lineup POET brought to OFC in technical detail. The company runs two parallel lines of business.

One is optical engines , the core chipsets that generate, modulate, and receive light, built on top of a platform called the Optical Interposer.

The other is ELS (External Light Source), standalone laser modules.

The main event for live demos at OFC 2026 was ELS. The Blazar and Starlight ELS products were front and center in the booth, while Teralight was the awards and display focus.

First: What Is the Optical Interposer

An “interposer” in the semiconductor industry is an intermediate substrate that mounts multiple chips in one package and lets them communicate with each other. Electrical interposers have been around for years. HBM memory getting placed next to a GPU runs on a silicon interposer. TSMC’s CoWoS is the canonical example.

POET’s Optical Interposer takes that concept into the domain of light. Instead of routing electrical signals, it routes optical signals on the chip. Lasers, modulators (the devices that encode data onto light), photodetectors (which convert received light back into electrical signals), and driver ICs (the electronics that drive the lasers and modulators) are all integrated onto a single interposer platform and connected through embedded optical waveguides, the channels that carry light.

This Optical Interposer is the foundation of everything POET builds. Understanding it makes every product much clearer.

Product 1: Blazar — Hybrid ELS

Blazar is POET’s ELS product. To understand why ELS exists: as the industry moves toward CPO (Co-Packaged Optics, where optical engines are placed in the same package as the switch chip), putting a laser inside that package becomes a serious problem. GPUs and switch chips dump hundreds of watts of heat. Lasers are extremely temperature-sensitive. If the wavelength drifts, data transmission breaks down entirely. So you pull the laser out of the package, put it in a thermally isolated module. That’s ELS.

Blazar takes a structurally different approach from DFB (Distributed Feedback) lasers, the current mainstream for ELS. A DFB laser has a diffraction grating etched inside the chip itself, a microstructure that amplifies only a specific wavelength. It’s proven technology, but building it means assembling and testing each laser individually, which keeps costs high and scalability limited.

Blazar separates that structure. The optical gain portion of the laser (the InP-based gain medium) is split from the wavelength-selection portion (the grating), and the grating is etched directly into the Optical Interposer’s waveguide. The InP chip handles optical amplification only; everything else is handled by the interposer. That’s why it’s called a hybrid laser.

Three advantages follow from this. The InP chip is smaller, so you get more chips from the same wafer, improving InP utilization (InP is a globally constrained rare material). Wafer-level packaging (WLCSP) makes it possible, and POET claims costs come down by an order of magnitude compared to DFB. The design supports channel outputs above 100mW and multi-wavelength operation simultaneously.

What changed from OFC 2025: Last year, Blazar was shown only to select customers behind closed doors. This year was the first public live demo. In twelve months, the technology maturity went from “private prototype” to “public demo.” That’s one real step forward.

Product 2: Starlight — 8-Channel Compact ELS

Starlight is the upgraded successor to POET’s first ELS product. It sits in the same ELS category as Blazar but is positioned differently.

Blazar is a “next-generation architecture” product designed for CPO and chip-to-chip links. Starlight is an “industry standard” product, built to the ELSFP (External Light Source Form Factor Pluggable) specification that the industry is currently standardizing around. It packages eight high-power laser channels into a single compact engine and was demoed integrated into a working optical engine.

The difference between Blazar and Starlight in short:

Blazar targets CPO and chip-to-chip links. It’s based on POET’s proprietary architecture, with a separated InP gain medium and interposer grating. Still early in commercialization.

Starlight is compatible with the pluggable ELS standard (ELSFP), built around eight multiplexed channels, and is an extension of the lightbar line already being supplied to Celestial AI (now Marvell). Commercially, it’s ahead of Blazar.

Product 3: Teralight 1.6T — The Main Optical Engine That Keeps Winning Awards

Teralight is not an ELS. It’s a complete optical engine that generates light, transmits data by encoding it onto that light, and receives incoming optical signals and decodes them back into data.

Co-developed with Mitsubishi Electric, this 1.6T (1.6 Terabit/s) transceiver engine’s core differentiator is that the 1.6T transmit engine uses only four laser chips instead of eight. That’s possible because of Mitsubishi’s 2x200G EML (Electro-absorption Modulated Laser) design, where the laser and modulator are integrated into a single chip, with one EML handling two 200G channels simultaneously. You’re cutting the count of the most expensive component in the transceiver in half.

Why that matters: With InP supply tight globally, cutting laser count in half means you can build twice as many transceivers from the same InP supply. In a market where InP constraints are a real problem across the industry, that’s a genuine advantage, not a marketing bullet.

Technical specs: 106 Gbaud PAM4 modulation, clean optical eye diagrams across all eight lanes, coupling loss below 0.5dB to single-mode fiber via a Spot-Size Converter (SSC), and a single board design that supports both 1.6T DR8 and 2xFR4 pluggable modules.

How Teralight relates to Blazar and Starlight: Teralight includes the laser inside the engine. Blazar and Starlight are the laser pulled out and packaged separately. In CPO deployments, the laser has to move outside the package, so you need Blazar or Starlight. In conventional pluggable transceivers, you need Teralight with the laser included. POET covers both directions of where the market is going.

Award history: Best Product Innovation at the January 2026 Infostone Awards in China, Lightwave Innovation Reviews Elite Score of 4.5 in February (among the highest scores given to any submission). The award ceremony was held at OFC 2026.

2. Two Partnerships That Landed During OFC Week

March 16: Strategic Collaboration with LITEON Technology

LITEON is a large Taiwanese optoelectronics company. They make LEDs, optical components, and power supplies, and they have actual volume manufacturing infrastructure. This isn’t a startup partnership.

The collaboration: co-develop next-generation optical communications modules for AI data centers, built on POET’s Optical Interposer platform. The design integrates lasers, optical components, driver electronics, and coupling structures into a single compact module. Timeline is development starting in 2026, prototype by end of 2026, and mass production in 2027. The stock moved +0.73% on the announcement day, though it hit -8.7% intraday at one point.

March 17: 1.6T 2×DR4 Transceiver Co-Development with Lessengers

Lessengers is a South Korean optical connectivity technology company. Their core technology is DOW (Direct Optical Wiring), which simplifies packaging by routing optical component connections flexibly.

The two companies are building a 1.6T 2×DR4 transceiver module. The difference between DR8 and 2×DR4: DR8 carries 200G per lane across eight lanes to reach 1.6T. 2×DR4 organizes four lanes into two groups. Same 1.6T throughput, different connector configuration. LightCounting forecasts the 1.6T DR8 market at over 125 million units from 2027 to 2031, and 2×DR4 attacks the same market with a different connector arrangement. Sample target is Q2 2026.

What Both Announcements Have in Common: No Financial Terms

Neither partnership disclosed the contract value, whether it’s structured as NRE (Non-Recurring Engineering fees), a JDA (Joint Development Agreement), or what revenue sharing looks like at production scale. Nothing.

Over the past year, POET has announced partnerships with Foxconn, Celestial AI, Sivers Semiconductors, QCi, Semtech, Mitsubishi Electric, and now LITEON and Lessengers. Eight or more names. Motley Fool captured the market’s reaction precisely in a March 19 article: investors are rotating toward companies that generate real revenue, and POET still gets treated as a story stock. The pattern of the stock selling off after AI partnership announcements keeps repeating. The market doesn’t want partner names. It wants PO dollar amounts.

3. What the Numbers Say

Let me look directly at the gap between the technology and the financial statements.

Revenue Trend, Last Five Quarters

The direction is up. But the absolute scale is tiny. $298K in Q3 2025 is roughly the payroll for one silicon valley engineer. Q1 through Q3 2025 combined revenue is around $734K.

Q3 2025 net loss: $9.4M (-$0.11 per share). R&D spending alone is $3.7M per quarter. Operating cash flow: -$2.8M. Q2 2025 showed a one-quarter net income of $6.3M due to a derivative liability adjustment, but that was a non-cash item with no operational significance.

Cash and Dilution

POET completed a $150M equity raise in January 2026, issuing 20,689,656 common shares. Combined with previous cash, the company holds $300M or more. At roughly $10M quarterly cash burn, that’s seven to eight years of runway on pure cash alone. Near-term survival risk is low.

That said, the Use of Proceeds disclosure for the $150M raise lists “corporate development, targeted acquisitions, scaling R&D, and expanding operations.” This is not a defensive raise. There’s an offensive intent here, including the possibility of acquisitions.

Production Orders

Q3 2025 included $5.6M in production orders across two customers. In October 2025, POET announced a separate $5M production order for 800G optical engines, with shipment scheduled for H2 2026. Combined pipeline: $10M or more in booked orders.

Valuation

Market cap: approximately $940M. Price-to-book: 14.2x (more than triple the U.S. semiconductor sector average of 4.5x). For a company with $763K in trailing four-quarter revenue, a sub-billion-dollar market cap means 100% of the valuation is priced on future expectations.

4. Where Each Product Actually Stands

Now let me combine the OFC information and partnership details to assess where each product actually is in the market. Not “here’s what the technology does” but “who puts this in their BOM, why, and under what conditions.”

Optical Interposer: The Problem Isn’t the Technology, It’s the Supply Chain Position

Real companies with real name recognition are interested in the Optical Interposer. But “interested” and “designed into the BOM” are entirely different things. Here’s where each customer actually is.

Foxconn Interconnect Technology (FIT): The contract manufacturing subsidiary of Foxconn, the world’s largest EMS company. FIT selected POET in May 2024. The original roadmap called for design completion in 2024, six months of sampling, and production orders in H2 2025. Current status: final design sample stage. 1.6T transceiver samples targeted for late 2025, production targeted for late 2026.

The key question is why FIT chose POET. FIT sells transceivers in volume to hyperscalers. In that market, competitiveness equals BOM cost. If POET’s “70% assembly cost reduction” claim holds in production, FIT can build the same-spec module cheaper than competitors. For a module maker, that’s an extremely compelling incentive. The counterargument: if production yield falls short of expectations, total cost actually goes up. And hyperscaler customers may be cautious about optical engines from an unproven supplier. For FIT, POET is a bet that looks like it should work but hasn’t been proven yet.

Luxshare Tech: A large Chinese data center equipment supplier and the furthest along among POET’s customer base. One 800G transceiver design is complete, five more in development. At OFC 2025, Luxshare ran an 800G DR/FR module based on POET engines at its own booth (4905). The key fact: POET’s receive optical engine has already passed qualification at Luxshare. Final transmit engine samples were delivered in March 2025, and Luxshare is using those to build complete modules for qualification by its end customer, a hyperscaler.

That qualification pipeline is what matters. RX engine qualified, TX engine sampled, module built, end-customer qualification, then PO. Right now we’re at the “module build to end-customer qualification” stage. That process typically takes three to six months. If everything goes smoothly, a meaningful production PO from Luxshare is possible in H1 2026.

Celestial AI (now Marvell Technology): Marvell acquired Celestial AI in December 2025 for $3.25B (up to $5.5B with earnouts). POET had been supplying Starlight lightbars to Celestial. On the earnings call, Marvell CEO Matt Murphy explicitly named optical interconnects as a core bottleneck in AI infrastructure. A $60B semiconductor company became an indirect customer.

Going one level deeper: Celestial AI’s “Photonic Fabric” architecture requires an external light source by design. POET’s Starlight fills that role. The more Marvell integrates Celestial’s technology into its custom ASIC platform, the greater its dependency on POET’s lightbar could become. Marvell could try to bring light source production in-house or switch suppliers, but there’s no strong incentive to sever a working supply relationship, especially when supply stability matters most during a CPO transition.

Adtran: European network equipment company. Developing a MicroMux Quattro 100G LR4 module. $500K production order. Volume production targeted for mid-2026. Small in scale, but potentially significant as the first publicly confirmed instance of a POET optical engine inside a commercial product.

QCi (Quantum Computing Inc.): Joint development of a TFLN (Thin-Film Lithium Niobate) 400G/lane modulator implemented on the Optical Interposer. A completed engine would reach 3.2Tbps. Targeted for completion in H2 2026. This isn’t near-term revenue, but it’s technical preparation for the 3.2T era. The 3.2T pluggable and CPO market is projected at $12B through 2030.

The real bottleneck in mass production isn’t yield, it’s the qualification chain. POET’s claims around passive alignment, eliminated wire bonds, and 70% cost reduction matter at production scale, but the more immediate constraint is the length of the qualification chain. POET builds the engine. Luxshare or FIT builds the module. The hyperscaler tests and approves the module. Each of those steps takes three to six months. Any single delay pushes out the entire timeline. POET’s wafer-level assembly and test line at Globetronics in Malaysia is operational, with Silterra handling wafer fabrication. The manufacturing infrastructure is coming together. The question is whether that infrastructure consistently produces output that meets customer specs.

ELS (Blazar/Starlight): Who Buys POET’s Lasers and Why

The ELS market is growing fast alongside CPO adoption. Bank of America’s post-OFC 2026 report projects AI optics TAM expanding from $18B in 2025 to $90B in 2030. Bernstein estimates large-scale CPO shipments beginning in late 2026 to 2027 for scale-out scenarios and H2 2028 for scale-up.

On March 2, 2026, NVIDIA put $2B into Coherent and another $2B into Lumentum, $4B total. These aren’t passive financial investments. They come with multi-year large-scale purchase agreements and priority access to production capacity. NVIDIA effectively locked up CPO laser supply. You need to read that $4B in supply chain terms, not capital markets terms.

What happens to the rest of the market when NVIDIA ties up Lumentum and Coherent? When Broadcom, Cisco, and hyperscalers running custom ASICs (Google TPU, Amazon Trainium, Microsoft Maia) try to adopt CPO, there’s a laser supply gap. McKinsey projects 800G transceivers running 40 to 60% short of demand through 2027, and 1.6T running 30 to 40% short through 2029. That supply gap is an opportunity for whoever can supply lasers to “the non-NVIDIA world.”

For Blazar and Starlight to capture that opportunity, two conditions have to be met.

Condition 1: Price. If POET’s claim of order-of-magnitude cost reduction versus DFB holds up, module makers can’t ignore it. At hyperscale data center volumes, you’re talking hundreds of thousands to millions of transceivers. The laser accounts for roughly 30 to 40% of the module BOM. An order-of-magnitude reduction in laser cost rewrites the entire module economics. That’s why Foxconn FIT and Luxshare chose POET.

Condition 2: Reliability qualification. Data center optical components have to pass reliability standards like Telcordia GR-468, covering temperature cycling, humidity, vibration, and lifetime testing. This typically takes six to twelve months. No matter how well Blazar performs in a demo, it can’t enter any system vendor’s BOM without clearing this qualification. Based on currently available public information, the status of Blazar’s reliability qualification is not confirmed. That’s one of the core questions that needs to come up on the next earnings call.

Starlight is already being supplied to Celestial AI (now Marvell) in lightbar form, which means at least one customer has either completed or is progressing through qualification. Starlight’s commercialization is ahead of Blazar’s.

The Sivers Semiconductors collaboration is also worth watching. The project combines Sivers DFB lasers with the POET Optical Interposer to build light-engine sub-systems for pluggable transceivers and ELS applications. Prototype targeted for H1 2026, production-ready by end of 2026. This shows POET is simultaneously pursuing its own laser technology in Blazar while also creating integration pathways for external lasers. That’s a practical approach.

Outlook: Blazar moving from private demo at OFC 2025 to public demo at OFC 2026 is real progress. But Lumentum has already shipped 8 million PICs, is in volume production of 200G EMLs, and just received $2B from NVIDIA. The scale gap is enormous. For POET to capture meaningful share in this market, the realistic path is to go after Tier 2 module makers with price competitiveness while Lumentum and Coherent are tied up serving NVIDIA. Speed matters here. By around 2027, the CPO supply chain starts to solidify. POET has to complete qualification and get into BOMs before that window closes. Whether time is on POET’s side will be determined by the next two or three quarters.

Teralight: The Most Commercially Advanced Product, the Nearest Path to Revenue

Teralight is furthest along of the three. Luxshare has already completed a transceiver design around this engine, and Foxconn FIT is using it as the basis for its own module development. The majority of the $5.6M and $5M production orders are most likely Teralight-series optical engines.

A comment CRO Raju Kankipati made when announcing the $5M production order is worth sitting with. He said the customer is “integrating POET across its ecosystem,” not just buying one product. That phrase points to a platform-level relationship rather than a component vendor arrangement. The customer wasn’t identified, but that level of integration language suggests something structural.

The 1.6T market trajectory is not in question. LightCounting projects 125 million or more 1.6T DR8 units from 2027 to 2031 alone. LightCounting also projects the optical transceiver, LPO, and CPO market for AI to double from $5B in 2024 to $10B in 2026. Teralight’s four-laser structure via Mitsubishi’s 2x200G EML cuts InP usage in half, and the Optical Interposer’s wafer-level assembly adds further cost reduction. The theoretical cost advantage from combining both is real. “Theoretical” being the operative word. Production has to prove it out.

5. Intel, Marvell, and the Acquisition Scenario

Intel: A Competitor That Also Justifies POET’s Existence

Intel has been investing in Silicon Photonics (SiPh) for over twenty years. The company has shipped more than eight million PICs and over 32 million on-chip lasers. At OFC 2024, Intel demoed its OCI (Optical Compute Interconnect) chiplet co-packaged directly with its own CPU, showing bidirectional 4Tbps live.

Intel’s approach is fundamentally different from POET’s. Intel uses heterogeneous integration, directly bonding InP lasers onto silicon wafers. POET uses hybrid integration, placing separate laser chips onto an interposer. Intel’s method achieves higher integration density and leverages 300mm fabs, but managing yield with the thermal expansion mismatch between InP and silicon is complex. POET’s method offers more flexibility but may be at a disadvantage in integration density.

One interesting detail: Intel uses the term “optical interposer” in its own patents. It describes an optical fan-out interposer architecture with embedded waveguides. Intel also has pending patents on EMIB-based CPO packaging and glass substrate optical interposers.

Intel’s current situation adds another layer to this picture. Intel is pursuing a partial spin-off of its foundry business (IFS) and seeking strategic investment. The Silicon Photonics division (IPS) is one of Intel’s better-performing units, but its strategic position within a restructuring Intel is fluid. Nothing has been publicly confirmed, but there’s periodic industry speculation about IPS operating as a more independent entity or expanding external partnerships.

The chance of POET getting directly into the Intel ecosystem in the near term is low. Intel has its own SiPh platform and is pursuing a vertically integrated strategy. But there’s an indirect path. As IFS grows its external foundry business, those customers adopting CPO don’t have to use Intel’s own SiPh. And as Intel evolves toward glass-interposer-based CPO, demand for external light source modules could emerge.

Looked at from a wider angle, Intel’s existence is paradoxically positive for POET. The harder Intel pushes silicon photonics, the faster the whole industry moves toward CPO. Faster CPO adoption means more ELS demand. As long as Intel isn’t directly attacking the ELS market (Intel’s current focus is integrated OCI, not ELS), POET can surf the wave Intel is generating.

Marvell: The Most Realistic and Most Near-Term Path

Far more realistic than the Intel scenario is Marvell. Let me go deeper on the structural connection created by the Celestial AI acquisition.

Celestial AI’s “Photonic Fabric” is a technology for chip-to-chip optical interconnects. One of its core architectural requirements is an external light source. POET’s Starlight lightbar was filling that role. If that supply relationship continues after Marvell’s acquisition, POET is indirectly integrated into Marvell’s custom ASIC platform.

Marvell’s incentive to keep using POET is straightforward: Marvell is a chip design company, not an optical component manufacturer. Even if Marvell internalizes Celestial’s technology, there’s little reason or capability to bring light source production in-house. That doesn’t fit Marvell’s business model. Keeping a qualified external supplier is the rational choice.

The counterargument is valid: Marvell could source CW lasers from Lumentum or Coherent, or redesign Celestial’s technology in ways that reduce POET dependency. But during a fast-moving CPO transition, deliberately replacing a working supply relationship creates unnecessary risk.

When you think through Marvell’s custom ASIC customer list, the potential scale becomes clearer. Amazon, Google, and Microsoft commission custom silicon from Marvell. If those customers adopt CPO, the package could look like Marvell ASIC plus Celestial Photonic Fabric plus POET lightbar as an integrated offering. Current public information can’t confirm that, but the structural logic of the supply chain points in that direction.

The Acquisition Scenario

Multiple analysts covering POET on Seeking Alpha have mentioned acquisition potential. These are rumors and speculation, and so is everything I'm about to say — all of it extrapolated from public filings, press releases, and conference transcripts, nothing more. But trace the supply chain interests and you get to scenarios that are plausible, not far-fetched.

Scenario A: Marvell vertical integration. Marvell just paid $3.25B for Celestial AI. Acquiring POET, the core light source supplier for Celestial’s technology, gives Marvell a full stack: ASIC plus Photonic Fabric plus light source. At POET’s current $940M market cap, a 40 to 50% premium puts the acquisition at $1.3B to $1.4B. Against Marvell’s $60B market cap, that’s digestible. One detail that supports this scenario: POET’s Use of Proceeds disclosure for the $150M raise explicitly mentions “targeted acquisitions.” Management is clearly thinking about the M&A landscape in both directions, as an acquirer and as a potential acquisition target.

Scenario B: Coherent cost improvement play. Coherent holds roughly 25% of the transceiver market by volume, and is simultaneously under gross margin pressure from Chinese competition, particularly Innolight. If POET’s 70% assembly cost reduction claim holds, acquiring POET gives Coherent a structural cost advantage. Volume leadership plus cost structure improvement is a compelling combination. That said, Coherent just received $2B from NVIDIA and may not have appetite for another major acquisition immediately.

Scenario C: Nobody acquires POET. POET proves out mass production independently and grows into a mid-sized optical engine and ELS supplier. With $300M in cash, it has the time. In this scenario, POET’s value compounds purely through revenue growth, without an acquisition premium. Slower, but it compounds without further dilution.

All three scenarios share one premise: POET has to prove out mass production. Without that, neither acquisition value nor standalone growth value means much.

6. What the Upcoming Earnings Really Are

This Isn’t a Revenue Event. It’s a Credibility Event.

Check the company’s official announcement, but Q4 2025 earnings are likely coming in the last week of March.

External revenue estimates vary. Investing.com shows $2.2M; other sources are lower. EPS consensus per TipRanks is around -$0.06. In Q3 2025, POET missed both revenue and EPS expectations, and the stock fell 12.87% that day.

But the headline revenue number isn’t the main event this time.

The market isn’t asking “how much did you make.” It’s asking “are you actually capable of making money.” This is a credibility event, and three things inside the report matter more than the total revenue line.

First, revenue composition. What percentage of total revenue comes from product shipments versus NRE (design services fees). NRE is one-time. Product revenue is what makes the “production ramp” story credible. Through Q3, NRE made up the majority of revenue. If product revenue is a meaningfully larger share in Q4, that’s a more important signal than the total number.

Second, actual revenue conversion from production orders. POET has $5.6M plus $5M in booked production orders. How much of that shipped and was recognized as revenue in Q4? A purchase order and revenue recognition are different things. The timing depends on shipment dates, acceptance criteria, and recognition policies. If specific numbers come out here, we’ll be able to measure POET’s PO-to-revenue conversion velocity for the first time.

Third, 2026 ramp visibility. The market is more interested in how revenue scales through 2026 than in the Q4 number by itself. Foxconn FIT and Luxshare production schedules, utilization at the Malaysia facility, additional production order pipeline. How specific management gets on these in the earnings call will likely determine what happens to the stock. A beat with vague guidance can still push the stock down. An in-line quarter with a concrete ramp roadmap can push it up.

Three Scenarios

Scenario 1: Numbers are modest but commercial signals are strong. Revenue comes in at $1M to $1.5M, below the upper range of estimates, but the call includes specific commercial milestones: a new production order announced, a Foxconn FIT production schedule confirmed, another customer qualification completed. The initial market reaction is mild disappointment, followed by a gradual recovery over several days as the call commentary gets processed. This is the scenario I think is most likely. Given the pace of POET’s revenue conversion so far, an explosive number seems unlikely. But management is coming off OFC and has more to say about the customer pipeline than at any prior quarter.

Scenario 2: Numbers beat but future guidance is vague. Revenue comes in above $2M and beats estimates, but the 2026 guidance is something along the lines of “we expect revenue to ramp.” The market’s response: “the number is fine, but what’s next?” Short-term spike followed by a pullback within days. This would be a management communication mistake. CEO Suresh Venkatesan is an experienced semiconductor executive; this kind of mistake seems unlikely from him.

Scenario 3: Numbers miss but strategic catalysts reignite expectations. Revenue comes in below $1M and disappoints, but at the same time a new strategic partnership (a hyperscaler-tier customer, or a design win with a major systems company) or renewed M&A speculation re-anchors the stock at current levels. Weakness in Q4 gets offset by “something big in 2026” narrative. This is the highest-variance scenario.

Across all three, the one thing to watch consistently is management tone on the call. The frequency and specificity of “confident,” “on track,” and “ahead of schedule.” This is more likely to be a quarter where words move the stock more than numbers.

The Bottom Line

POET is not an easy stock.

A company with $763K in trailing four-quarter revenue trading at a $940M market cap means the price is entirely about the future. P/B at 14.2x is more than triple the sector average.

But reducing POET to a “high-growth expectation stock” misses what it actually is. POET is a listed option on a massive supply chain transition, a $90B TAM opening up as the optics industry restructures around CPO. There are very few publicly traded companies with proven platform technology and a name-brand customer pipeline sitting inside that transition. POET is one of them.

For anyone holding a position, this is a patience game. Next week’s earnings will set the short-term direction, but the real value inflection point is H2 2026 into 2027, when qualifications complete, production starts, and quarterly revenue crosses $10M for the first time.

Until then, you’re sitting in the “technology is real, revenue isn’t yet” phase. The valley of death for semiconductor startups. POET has $300M in cash, which means it has enough water to cross the valley. The question is how efficiently that cash gets deployed on the way to the other side.

What OFC 2026 confirmed is that the technology is real. What next week’s earnings need to show is that the company has started making money from it. And once that evidence starts accumulating, the valuation begins to make sense.
