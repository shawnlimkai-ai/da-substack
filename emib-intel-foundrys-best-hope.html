EMIB: Intel Foundry's Best Hope

Can This Packaging Technology Save Intel?

The bottleneck in the semiconductor industry has shifted. For a long time, almost everything came down to how small you could make a transistor. That’s no longer the whole story. Increasingly, what matters is how fast and efficiently you can connect multiple chips inside a single package. Packaging has become a critical competitive axis.

The dominant technology in this space is TSMC’s CoWoS (Chip on Wafer on Substrate). NVIDIA’s AI GPUs, AMD’s accelerators, Broadcom’s custom ASICs — most high-performance AI chips today are packaged using CoWoS. The problem is supply can’t keep up with demand. By industry estimates, NVIDIA absorbs a substantial portion of available CoWoS capacity, leaving other customers struggling to secure allocation. This has been going on for a while now.

Into this gap steps Intel’s EMIB (Embedded Multi-die Interconnect Bridge). In March 2026, Intel CFO David Zinsner told the Morgan Stanley conference that the packaging business was shaping up to be significantly larger than originally anticipated. Multiple outlets reported that Intel’s EMIB was being evaluated for NVIDIA’s next-generation architecture.

This piece breaks down what EMIB is, how it differs from TSMC’s approach, and what it could mean for Intel Foundry.

1. Why Advanced Packaging Suddenly Matters

Some context first.

AI-era chips can’t perform on their own. Take a single NVIDIA GPU: the logic die handling computation and the HBM (High Bandwidth Memory) storing data need to exchange information at extreme speeds inside one package. Advanced packaging is what makes that connection possible.

The old approach was monolithic design — pack everything into one large chip. But as chips get bigger, defect rates climb and costs spike. So the industry changed course. Instead of one large chip, you build multiple smaller chips (chiplets), each optimized for a specific function, and assemble them within a package. Think of it as LEGO blocks. The quality of the connections between those blocks determines total system performance.

2. CoWoS vs EMIB: Highway vs Bridge

There are several ways to connect chips inside a package. The two that matter most right now are TSMC’s CoWoS and Intel’s EMIB. To understand the difference, you need to understand interposers.

What Is an Interposer?

An interposer is essentially a middleman substrate placed between chips. You put a compute chip and a memory chip on top of this substrate and route data between them through extremely fine metal traces embedded in the substrate. Because the traces are much denser and shorter than what you’d find on a standard PCB, data moves faster and power consumption drops.

The question is what you make the interposer out of. Silicon enables extremely fine traces and delivers excellent performance, but silicon interposers are expensive. They’re fabricated on semiconductor wafers using processes similar to chip manufacturing itself. Larger interposers mean higher defect probability and sharply rising costs.

CoWoS: TSMC’s Approach

TSMC’s CoWoS places a large silicon slab beneath all the chips in the package, routing inter-chip communication through extremely fine traces embedded in that slab. Think of it as building a city-wide highway network: high-speed movement is available everywhere, but construction costs are steep.

CoWoS comes in several variants. CoWoS-S uses a solid silicon interposer and is what powers chips like the NVIDIA H100 and AMD MI300. It tops out at roughly 2,700mm² due to the reticle limit, the maximum area a lithography tool can expose in one shot. CoWoS-L extends beyond that constraint by placing small silicon chips (LSI chips) only at the high-speed interfaces and filling the rest with organic RDL. CoWoS-R goes further, replacing the silicon interposer entirely with an organic RDL layer, trading performance for cost.

Worth noting: CoWoS-L’s approach of placing silicon only where needed looks a lot like EMIB. AnandTech called it “TSMC’s answer to EMIB.” That TSMC built it at all is an implicit acknowledgment that full-area silicon interposers have limits.

EMIB: Intel’s Approach

Intel’s EMIB starts from the opposite premise. No interposer. Instead, small silicon bridges are embedded directly into the package substrate, appearing only where two chips need to talk at high speed. Everything else runs over standard organic substrate. If CoWoS is a city-wide highway network, EMIB is a bridge at a river crossing.

The practical difference shows up in utilization. Intel puts EMIB bridge wafer utilization at around 90%, versus roughly 60% for large interposers. Small pieces tile cleanly on a wafer with minimal waste, and since a significant chunk of CoWoS packaging cost comes from the silicon interposer, eliminating it changes the cost structure.

EMIB has been in production since 2017, starting with the Stratix 10 FPGA and running through server CPUs including Sapphire Rapids and Granite Rapids. Years of high-volume production history matter when an external customer is deciding whether to trust a packaging partner with their flagship chip.

What’s Actually Different

Cost : CoWoS carries higher packaging costs due to the large-area silicon interposer. EMIB is structurally cheaper because that interposer doesn’t exist. Actual cost differences vary by design and configuration, so blanket comparisons are imprecise.

Interconnect density : CoWoS allows traces across the entire interposer area, giving it an advantage in inter-chip bandwidth density. For GPUs requiring extreme throughput, CoWoS remains the better option.

Package scalability : EMIB scales by adding bridges wherever they’re needed. Intel’s EMIB-T specification supports a 120mm x 180mm package with 38 or more bridges. CoWoS-S is constrained by the reticle limit on interposer size, and while CoWoS-L extends that, it hasn’t matched EMIB’s scalability.

Design flexibility : EMIB allows mixing chips from entirely different process nodes in one package. A compute die on 3nm and an I/O die on 12nm can coexist. Each bridge connection can be individually optimized. For custom ASIC design, this flexibility has real value.

CoWoS and EMIB are not substitutes for each other. They address different use cases. For flagship GPU training chips demanding peak interconnect density, CoWoS wins. For custom ASICs where cost efficiency and design flexibility matter more, EMIB is the better fit. What’s interesting is that CoWoS is evolving toward EMIB’s core idea (local silicon bridges rather than full-area interposers). The principle of “connect only where you need it” is being absorbed by the broader industry.

3. How EMIB Is Evolving

EMIB is not static. As AI chip requirements change rapidly, the technology has branched in several directions.

Better Power Stability: EMIB-M

AI chips consume enormous power, and the problem isn’t consumption per se but the instability that comes with it. Noise on the power delivery network directly affects chip performance and reliability.

EMIB-M addresses this by embedding MIM capacitors (Metal-Insulator-Metal Capacitors) inside the bridge itself. Capacitors store charge temporarily and release it on demand. Integrating them into the interconnect acts as a power buffer, stabilizing supply to the chips. Think of it as embedding a voltage regulator directly into the connection layer.

Vertical Power Delivery: EMIB-T

The more significant evolution is EMIB-T , where “T” stands for TSV (Through-Silicon Via).

In conventional EMIB, signals and power travel laterally along the bridge surface, taking a detour to reach their destination. It’s like going from a building’s basement to its roof by walking up an external staircase. EMIB-T drills vertical through-holes (TSVs) through the bridge itself, allowing signals to travel straight through. The elevator shaft, rather than the fire escape.

Two things improve substantially. First, the power delivery path shortens and resistance drops. In the original EMIB “cantilever” geometry, power had to travel around the bridge to reach the chip. In EMIB-T, power comes from below through TSVs directly. For high-wattage AI chips, this is a meaningful difference. Second, integration with next-generation HBM4 and HBM4e becomes more natural. HBM itself stacks multiple DRAM layers using TSVs, so having TSVs in the connecting bridge simplifies the interface geometry.

EMIB-T’s specs are notable. Packages up to 120mm x 180mm with 38+ bridges and 12+ large dies. Bump pitch has tightened from 55μm in the first generation to 45μm and now 35μm, with 25μm in development. It also supports glass substrates in addition to organic ones, which matters because glass offers better thermal stability and enables finer routing, making it one of the key materials directions for next-generation packaging.

Combining Vertical and Horizontal: 3.5D

This is where Intel’s Foveros technology enters the picture.

If EMIB connects chips horizontally (the 2.5D model), Foveros stacks chips vertically (3D). Die on top of die, connected by TSVs and microbumps. The latest version, Foveros Direct, enables copper-to-copper direct bonding without bumps at all, which is what the industry calls hybrid bonding. Foveros is an Intel-developed technology that entered production in 2019 with the Lakefield processor and was later used in Meteor Lake.

Combine the two and you get 3.5D : chiplet stacks assembled via Foveros, then interconnected horizontally via EMIB. Apartment towers linked by bridges. This configuration allows vertical density increases while preserving horizontal scalability. Intel’s latest server CPU, Clearwater Forest, uses this 3.5D architecture: 18A process logic chiplets stacked on a base die via Foveros, with EMIB connecting those modules horizontally.

A Naming Collision Worth Noting

Broadcom also calls its XDSiP platform “3.5D,” which creates confusion. The underlying technology is completely different. Broadcom’s 3.5D is built on TSMC’s stack: CoWoS-L (LSI bridge + RDL interposer) for horizontal connectivity, and TSMC’s SoIC (System on Integrated Chips) for vertical stacking. SoIC is TSMC’s wafer-level direct bonding technology for 3D integration, the equivalent of Foveros in the TSMC ecosystem.

To summarize:

  • Intel’s 3.5D = EMIB (horizontal) + Foveros (vertical). Both are Intel technologies.

  • Broadcom’s 3.5D = CoWoS-L (horizontal) + SoIC (vertical). Both are TSMC technologies.

Same label, entirely different technology stacks. Always check whose technology sits underneath when you see “3.5D.”

4. EMIB and Optical Interconnects

The way chips communicate across data centers is changing. Traditional electrical signaling over copper traces is hitting walls on both bandwidth and energy efficiency. CPO (Co-Packaged Optics) is emerging as a response: optical engines placed directly inside a chip package, transmitting data via light instead of copper.

Realizing CPO requires placing a photonic integrated circuit (PIC) very close to the compute die inside a package. EMIB is one viable mechanism for that connection.

The most widely cited public demonstration is Intel and Ayar Labs integrating an Intel FPGA with Ayar Labs’ TeraPHY optical I/O chiplet using EMIB in a single package. The demo achieved high bandwidth between the two chips at extremely short distances. Compared to silicon-interposer-based approaches, EMIB offers lower parasitic capacitance from TSVs and a smaller footprint, which matter for optical signaling.

Intel has invested more than 20 years in silicon photonics. The company has shipped over 8 million PICs and has published OCI (Optical Compute Interconnect) chiplet prototypes. The forward-looking roadmap involves stacking optical chiplets via Foveros, then connecting those stacks to compute dies via EMIB.

In other words, EMIB has a potential role not just in electrical chip-to-chip connections but as the interface between optical and compute elements. Intel’s combination of packaging and silicon photonics capabilities puts it in a position that other packaging vendors can’t easily replicate. That said, the path from demonstration to high-volume production involves considerable validation work that hasn’t been completed yet.

5. Why Now

EMIB has existed since 2017. So why is industry attention spiking in 2025 and 2026? The technology itself hasn’t changed dramatically. The market environment has shifted in EMIB’s favor.

CoWoS capacity constraints are structural. TSMC is expanding aggressively, but the consensus view is that demand growth from NVIDIA and others will continue to outpace new capacity for the foreseeable future.

Hyperscalers are designing their own ASICs at scale. Google TPU, Meta MTIA, AWS Trainium. These chips don’t always need extreme interconnect density. They need cost efficiency and design flexibility. That’s exactly where EMIB has a structural edge.

Interest in domestic U.S. packaging is real. Today, wafers fabbed at TSMC’s Arizona facility still ship to Taiwan for packaging. Intel operates advanced packaging capacity in New Mexico and is expanding it. For high-volume production today, Intel has the most substantial U.S.-based advanced packaging infrastructure in place. TSMC and Amkor are planning U.S. packaging facilities, so this lead isn’t permanent, but the window exists.

Government support is a tailwind. Through the CHIPS and Science Act, Intel has received significant federal subsidies and tax benefits. The U.S. government has made domestic semiconductor manufacturing a stated priority, and Intel is one of its primary beneficiaries.

6. The Customer Pipeline: Names in Circulation

Several names are circulating in connection with EMIB. Here’s what’s been reported publicly.

NVIDIA Feynman — Updated after GTC 2026

In January 2026, DigiTimes reported that NVIDIA was exploring Intel’s process and EMIB packaging for Feynman’s I/O die. The tone was exploratory.

That changed at GTC 2026. On March 16, Jensen Huang officially confirmed Feynman as NVIDIA’s 2028 GPU generation, built on TSMC’s A16 (1.6nm) node. Jon Peddie Research, summarizing the keynote, wrote that “Intel supplies EMIB advanced packaging for the I/O die.” No hedging language. A notable shift from January.

Feynman brings several firsts for NVIDIA: 3D die stacking on GPU silicon, custom HBM (likely a proprietary HBM4E or HBM5 variant), a new Rosa CPU, Groq’s LP40 LPU on the same platform, and an NVL1152 rack architecture with 8x the density of Rubin.

On the packaging side, ETNews and TrendForce reported on March 18 that Intel is scaling EMIB packages to 120×120mm (up from the 100×100mm standard used in Blackwell), targeting 12+ HBM stacks per package. By 2028, Intel aims for 120×180mm packages with up to 24 HBM stacks, timed for Feynman’s launch.

One caveat: whether Huang named Intel EMIB on stage or Jon Peddie Research inferred it from context is not fully confirmed. NVIDIA’s official GTC press materials do not explicitly mention Intel EMIB. The gap between analyst interpretation and official confirmation still matters. But the direction, from “exploring” in January to analysts treating it as settled in March, is hard to dismiss.

Google TPU

DARPA advanced packaging expert Dr. Phil Garrou and TrendForce have both covered reports suggesting Intel could supply packaging for future generations of Google’s TPU. TPU is a purpose-built inference and training ASIC, not a general GPU. The cost structure and design flexibility that EMIB offers align well with custom ASIC development cycles.

Apple, MediaTek, Qualcomm

Reports have emerged that Apple is evaluating Intel’s 18A-P process for server-side custom chips and exploring EMIB packaging. This appears to be at the exploratory stage. There are also rumors about MediaTek evaluating Intel’s next-generation process and packaging, and Qualcomm posted job listings requiring EMIB experience for data center-related work.

The fact that these names are in circulation at all reflects a different level of industry interest than existed even two years ago. Interest is not orders. Until confirmed customer announcements are made, the pipeline should not be sized too aggressively.

7. Packaging as the On-Ramp to Wafers: The IDM Structural Advantage

Step back and look at the bigger picture. EMIB packaging revenue matters on its own, but the real question is whether it can pull wafer orders along with it.

A similar playbook is already running. At Samsung.

Samsung has long used its memory business as a lever for foundry orders. The clearest example is the HBM logic die, the controller chip at the bottom of the HBM stack. Starting with HBM4, this die requires advanced foundry processes rather than conventional DRAM fabrication. Samsung is manufacturing HBM4 logic dies on its own 4nm node and evaluating 2nm for HBM4E. Compared to SK hynix sourcing from TSMC at 12nm, that is aggressive positioning. The internal HBM volume lifts fab utilization, better utilization improves yields and costs, and lower costs let Samsung offer more competitive pricing to external customers. A virtuous cycle where memory demand strengthens the foundry. Samsung CTO Song Jae-hyuk said at SEMICON Korea 2026 that having competitiveness across memory, foundry, and packaging is “very advantageous for making AI semiconductors.”

The signal that this strategy is working came this week. And it came loudly.

AMD CEO Lisa Su visited South Korea on March 18, her first trip in the 12 years since becoming CEO in 2014. She toured Samsung’s Pyeongtaek campus with DS Division chief Jun Young-hyun and Foundry Business head Han Jin-man, then held a three-hour dinner with Chairman Lee Jae-yong. The two companies signed an MOU covering memory supply, foundry orders, and joint research on next-generation chips. Samsung becomes a priority HBM4 supplier for AMD’s AI chips, and the partnership expands from memory into wafer manufacturing. Industry sources indicate discussions are underway to produce AMD’s next-gen chips on Samsung’s 2nm SF2P process. Visiting Korea during the same week as NVIDIA’s GTC 2026 and linking Samsung and Naver in a single itinerary is being read by industry observers as an effort to build a counter-NVIDIA coalition.

The bundling logic is plain to see. AMD already sources HBM3E from Samsung and Samsung holds a strong position in HBM4. Memory from Samsung, so wafers from Samsung too, for optimization advantages. Following Tesla AI6, Apple image sensors, and NVIDIA’s Groq 3 LPU (Samsung 4nm foundry), adding AMD would structurally reshape Samsung Foundry’s customer base.

Intel is trying to apply the same logic through packaging.

TSMC is fundamentally a wafer company. It does packaging, but the core of the business is wafer foundry. Intel is different. Wafer fabrication, packaging, and test under one roof, the IDM structure. That is why Intel calls itself a “systems foundry,” bundling process technology, packaging, IP, and test into a single service.

In this structure, EMIB can serve as the entry point. Once a customer commits to Intel for packaging, the pitch follows naturally: if you are packaging here anyway, why not run the wafers on 18A or 14A too? Design optimization, logistics, and lead time all benefit from a single source. For the customer, getting wafers and packaging from one place simplifies design rule compatibility, shipping costs, and quality control.

If Samsung is using memory to pull in foundry orders, Intel is trying to use packaging to pull in wafer orders. Both are leveraging their strongest asset as the front door to capture customers across the full value chain. Samsung reached the stage this week where the CEO flew in personally and signed an MOU. Intel is not there yet, but the structural logic is the same.

Of course, both strategies come with a prerequisite. The wafer process itself has to be competitive. If Intel’s 18A and 14A cannot match rival nodes on yield and performance, the bundling pitch falls apart. Customers may take packaging from Intel but keep wafers at TSMC, a “split workflow” that leaves Intel with only the lower-margin packaging revenue. The same applies to Samsung. If 2nm yields do not meet AMD’s bar, the memory partnership alone will not close a wafer deal.

8. How TSMC Sees This

TSMC’s perspective is worth understanding.

According to DigiTimes’ January 2026 report, some within TSMC don’t view certain orders shifting to Intel purely as a negative. Reduced concentration concerns lower regulatory risk. Political pressure from the U.S. government eases somewhat. The orders at risk of shifting appear to be less central than flagship GPU compute dies, and the internal read is that customers who experience alternatives will more sharply appreciate TSMC’s value. The quote from a supply chain source was pointed: customers who try other foundries will come to understand what TSMC actually delivers.

The key fact is that, at present, Intel EMIB does not threaten TSMC’s core business. Packaging flagship GPU compute dies via EMIB is not technically feasible today, and TSMC is well aware of that.

9. Risks That Can’t Be Skipped

Intel’s execution track record is the biggest variable. Over the past decade, Intel has lost significant industry trust through repeated process roadmap delays. EMIB itself has production validation, but Intel has no established track record handling high-volume external customer orders. CEO Lip-Bu Tan’s stated position, that Intel won’t invest in 14A capacity without customer commitments in hand, reflects capital discipline but also means the roadmap contracts if customers don’t show up.

TSMC is not standing still. TSMC is dramatically expanding its U.S. investment footprint and constructing packaging capacity in Arizona. When those facilities come online, Intel’s U.S. packaging advantage erodes. The timeline is roughly 2028 to 2029.

The Amkor outsourcing arrangement cuts both ways. Near-term, it’s a necessary capacity move. Long-term, if EMIB becomes something Amkor does rather than something uniquely Intel does, the premium Intel can charge diminishes.

Can packaging revenue fix foundry losses? Packaging carries lower margins than wafer manufacturing. Intel Foundry has been running substantial operating losses through recent quarters. Even meaningful packaging revenue won’t reverse the loss structure on its own. Wafer revenue has to follow for the economics to work.

10. Who Else Benefits: The Downstream Chain

If EMIB gains real traction, Intel isn’t the only winner. Benefits flow through the supply chain. Here’s where the structural advantages sit.

Most Direct: Amkor (AMKR)

Amkor is Intel EMIB’s sole external assembly partner. Its Icheon K5 facility in Korea is already operational, with Portugal and Arizona (expected 2028) expanding the footprint to three geographies. Being the only OSAT capable of EMIB assembly globally is the key position. When Intel’s packaging deals scale up, revenue follows directly. The Arizona campus involves large upfront investment with first revenue coming after 2028, so this is more a medium-term thesis than an immediate catalyst. Customer concentration risk warrants consideration.

ABF Substrate Vendors: The Most Interesting Segment

Whether the packaging technology is EMIB or CoWoS, one of the shared bottlenecks is high-performance ABF (Ajinomoto Build-up Film) substrates. DigiTimes explicitly reported in March 2026 that Intel’s EMIB momentum is restoring capital investment confidence across the IC substrate sector.

This segment is interesting because it benefits from CoWoS expansion and EMIB expansion simultaneously. It’s the pickaxe-and-shovel position in the advanced packaging gold rush.

Ibiden (4062.T) is Intel’s core substrate partner and has announced roughly 500 billion yen in advanced substrate capacity investment. AI server substrate revenue has grown sharply year-over-year, though overall guidance has been trimmed due to weakness in PC and conventional server demand. As the AI revenue mix grows, Ibiden’s economics improve.

Shinko Electric (6967.T) is Japan’s other ABF substrate leader alongside Ibiden, with a concentrated focus on HPC and AI segments.

Unimicron (3037.TW) holds the top market share position in ABF substrates in Taiwan, sitting squarely inside the TSMC CoWoS supply chain while also positioned to capture EMIB volume.

These three companies account for a substantial portion of the global ABF market.

Packaging Equipment

Expanding advanced packaging volume drives equipment demand.

Besi (BESI.AS) is positioned in hybrid bonding and die bonding equipment. Regardless of whether the packaging is EMIB, CoWoS, or Foveros, capacity expansion generates equipment orders. Today, most of the reflected value in Besi tracks TSMC CoWoS capacity additions.

Intel EMIB upside may not yet be priced in.

Glass Substrates: A Longer-Duration Theme

Intel has confirmed EMIB-T compatibility with glass substrates in addition to organic ones. Glass offers better thermal stability and enables finer routing, making it one of the primary material directions for next-generation packaging.

AGC (5201.T) and Corning (GLW) are both developing semiconductor-grade glass substrates, but volume production is still years away. This is a long-duration positioning theme, not near-term catalysts.

The central takeaway from the downstream chain is that the most defensible position is where CoWoS and EMIB both create demand, such as ABF substrates.

If EMIB succeeds, you win. If EMIB disappoints but CoWoS continues expanding, you still win. The underlying bet is on the growth of advanced packaging as a category, not on a specific technology horse.

Conclusion: How Much Weight Can This Bridge Carry?

EMIB’s proper framing is not as a CoWoS replacement. It’s a complement that can capture demand CoWoS cannot satisfy.

As this piece has argued, packaging is not just a revenue line. It’s potentially an on-ramp to wafer business. The same way Samsung is leveraging memory to pull foundry orders, Intel is trying to leverage packaging to pull wafers. Whether this “systems foundry” strategy holds together matters more than packaging revenue in isolation. And the prerequisite for any of it working is competitive wafer process technology in 18A and 14A.

The things to watch from here: whether Intel announces confirmed EMIB customer wins in the second half of 2026; whether the Amkor partnership delivers capacity expansion on schedule; whether packaging relationships convert into wafer orders; and whether Intel Foundry can approach breakeven by the end of 2027.

One thing is clear regardless of how these questions resolve: advanced packaging is no longer back-end work. It’s a primary axis of semiconductor competitiveness. Where Intel can take EMIB in 2026 and 2027 will be one of the more consequential tests of that reality.
