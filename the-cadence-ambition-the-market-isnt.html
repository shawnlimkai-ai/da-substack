What EDA Is, and Where Cadence Sits in It

To understand the Cadence story, you have to start with the EDA market structure.

EDA is the umbrella term for chip design software. Every step of building a chip, from spec definition to RTL coding, functional verification, synthesis, physical design, tape-out, and post-silicon test, has an engineer sitting in front of an EDA tool. Modern semiconductors don’t get built without EDA.

The market is effectively a three-way oligopoly. Synopsys, Cadence, and Siemens EDA (the former Mentor). Synopsys and Cadence are the top two, and in practice a single company’s chip design flow uses tools from both. Each has different strengths.

Synopsys is strong on the front end. VCS for RTL simulation, Fusion Compiler for synthesis, PrimeTime for static timing analysis. The IP portfolio is much larger, and after acquiring Ansys for $35 billion in 2025, Synopsys also extended into multiphysics simulation.

Cadence is strong on the back end and in analog. Virtuoso for analog and custom IC design, Innovus for P&R. The IP business is smaller than Synopsys but growing fast.

In adjacent areas, physical verification (LVS, DRC) belongs to Siemens Calibre. EM/IR analysis belongs to Ansys RedHawk (now under Synopsys). Emulation is a three-way fight.

Simplified: Cadence has historically been the back-end and analog leader. And in the AI chip boom, the money is flowing hardest into the front end and IP, exactly where Cadence is weakest. Almost everything Cadence has done over the past few years is a move to fix that gap. The CadenceLIVE announcements are an interim result of that effort.

CadenceLIVE Announcement: Confirmed Facts and Interpretation

Three things were officially confirmed in the announcement.

One, Cadence introduced a new system called AgentStack. AgentStack extends the existing ChipStack AI Super Agent so that not just RTL and verification, but also physical design, custom and analog design, migration, and system-level workflows are all wrapped in a single agent orchestration layer.

Two, NVIDIA is participating as an early partner for AgentStack, applying it to its own semiconductor and system design flows and providing real-world feedback. That’s the official wording. It’s not “NVIDIA has standardized on AgentStack.”

Three, Cadence claimed that integration with NVIDIA CUDA-X, AI Physics, and Omniverse libraries can deliver up to 100x acceleration for engineering workflows. This is Cadence’s own claim, not an industry-validated number.

How you read these three depends on the reader. Here’s how I read them.

First, AgentStack looks less like a frontal assault on Synopsys’s front-end fortress and more like an attempt to change the frame of the game. Beating decades of accumulated VCS and Design Compiler dominance tool-against-tool is close to impossible. But AgentStack shifts the competition from “which tool runs faster” to “can an agent run the entire design flow for you.” If that frame shift takes hold, the relative value of the accumulated front-end advantage erodes. Whether it actually takes hold is a separate question.

Second, NVIDIA’s early-partner participation is a meaningful signal but needs careful reading. NVIDIA already invested $2 billion in Synopsys in December 2025 and signed a strategic partnership. So this isn’t NVIDIA leaving Synopsys for Cadence. NVIDIA is keeping deep relationships with both companies and adding itself as an early partner on AgentStack. For Cadence, the gain is being able to combine NVIDIA’s GPU infrastructure and Nemotron LLM into its own AI agent products. For NVIDIA, the gain is the ability to mix Cadence’s back-end and analog strengths into its design flow.

Third, the Google Cloud and Gemini integration broadens Cadence’s LLM optionality. ChipStack AI Super Agent already supports a range of frontier models, including NVIDIA Nemotron and OpenAI GPT. Adding Gemini strengthens Cadence’s positioning as an agent platform that isn’t locked into any single LLM vendor.

That covers the surface-level announcement content.

But the real weight of AgentStack isn’t in the announcement itself.

It shows up when you connect it to the bigger play Cadence has been quietly preparing over the past two years.

If that connection holds, Cadence is no longer a company that just sells tools.

And at that point, the “EDA vendor” frame the market currently applies has to be rewritten too.

Where Cadence Is Heading: Custom Silicon Services

This is where the real thesis of the article begins.

Pull up the Cadence website and Chiplet Design Services, Custom Silicon Services, and Full Chip Design Outsourcing are sitting right there in the service portfolio. So this isn’t a tool-only company. It’s already publicly running a business that designs chips on behalf of customers. The question is how big and how strategic this business gets going forward.

To find that answer, you have to thread together what Cadence has been doing over the past two years into a single line. M&A, AI strategy, IP expansion. My read is that all three converge on a single point: custom silicon services. And what I’m hearing in the field confirms that this direction is actually in motion. Let me walk through each one.

First thread: Acquiring design headcount and engineering capability

One of the clearest patterns Cadence has shown over the past two years is the systematic buildup of design headcount and engineering capability.

The starting point is the January 2024 acquisition of Invecas. A Santa Clara-based design house with its core engineering team in Hyderabad, India. They’ve handled customer specs all the way from architecture definition through tape-out and foundry coordination on a turnkey basis. Cadence’s acquisition release at the time explicitly stated the purpose was to expand system design engineering offerings. This wasn’t a simple technology bolt-on. It’s better read as a move to pull design service capability, the ability to perform design work on behalf of customers, directly in-house.

The acquisitions that followed point the same direction. Intrinsix had aerospace and defense ASIC design experience. Apex Semiconductor was meaningful from a design engineering headcount perspective. Each deal may look small on its own. But string them together and the picture is clear. Cadence is maintaining the outer shape of a tool company while steadily building up the people and the organization needed to actually perform design work inside it.

The February 2026 close on Hexagon’s Design & Engineering business is a different kind of deal. At roughly €2.7 billion, it brought in over 1,100 engineers along with multiphysics assets like MSC Software, Nastran, and Adams. Cadence’s official positioning for this one leans toward physical AI and system design and analysis. In other words, the primary significance of this deal isn’t adding ASIC design headcount. It’s about massively reinforcing Cadence’s ability to compete in the multiphysics space that Synopsys has been expanding through Ansys.

What matters more, though, is the possibility that these assets connect to custom silicon services over the medium to long term. As we move deeper into the chiplet era, semiconductor design no longer ends at purely electrical work. Packaging, thermal, and mechanical behavior all have to be solved inside a single flow. Seen that way, AgentStack extending its orchestration scope to system-level design and the Hexagon acquisition aren’t separate events. They’re closer to a signal that Cadence is heading toward an integrated platform that ties electrical, mechanical, and thermal together.

Second thread: AI turns design service into a scalable business

No matter how much design headcount you acquire, a people-centric business eventually hits scaling limits. As project count grows, you need to attach more engineers, which runs straight into margin and operational efficiency walls. This is exactly why Cadence’s AI strategy matters. It’s not just about adding AI features to tools. It’s about making the design service business itself, a fundamentally people-driven operation, more scalable.

The first thing to look at is Cerebrus. Cadence Cerebrus Intelligent Chip Explorer is a digital implementation optimization tool built on reinforcement learning, and it’s already deep into real-world use. Per Cadence’s numbers, as of Q1 2025 Cerebrus had been used in over 1,000 cumulative tape-outs, and over 50% of advanced node designs are on the platform. So Cerebrus isn’t experimental AI. It’s a productivity tool embedded in production flows.

The next step was ChipStack. Cadence acquired ChipStack in November 2025 and officially launched ChipStack AI Super Agent in February 2026. It’s an agentic system aimed at reading spec documents and generating RTL and verification artifacts. Then AgentStack, unveiled at this CadenceLIVE, sits on top as a higher-level orchestration layer. Where ChipStack focused on RTL and verification, AgentStack extends to physical design, analog, migration, and system-level workflows, showing a picture of connecting the entire design process.

The important question is why this AI layer connects to custom silicon services. In my view the structure is fairly straightforward. If Cadence grows its internal design organization and layers Cerebrus, ChipStack, and AgentStack on top, the possibility opens up for the same number of engineers to handle more projects. AI stops being an add-on that helps sell tools and starts acting as a multiplier on the design service organization’s productivity. Think of it as laying AI leverage on top of the design headcount acquired through Invecas and others.

There’s also a part here that has to be read without exaggeration. The 100x speedup and 10x productivity numbers Cadence has put out are the company’s claims. Whether those effects actually reproduce in production flows will take another one to two years of concrete case data to judge. That said, NVIDIA signing on as an early partner is meaningful. At minimum, it means one of the most complex and demanding design environments in the industry showed enough interest to test this approach.

Third thread: Analog IP as the differentiating weapon

Even with design headcount secured and AI lifting productivity, customers still have one question left. Why hand this to Cadence instead of an existing design house. One of Cadence’s strongest answers to that question is analog and interface IP.

Modern SoCs are never purely digital chips. The moment a chip sends or receives data externally, generates clocks, or manages power, analog blocks are involved. SerDes for high-speed interfaces, PLLs for clock generation, ADCs and DACs for signal conversion, on-chip power management. These blocks are required in nearly every high-performance chip, they’re hard to design, and accumulated experience matters enormously. Digital has been progressively automated. Analog is still a field where experienced engineers’ instincts and iterative tuning carry a lot of weight.

Cadence has held a strong position in this space for a long time. Virtuoso covers analog, mixed-signal, RF, and silicon photonics as a broad custom design environment. Spectre is one of the most widely used analog circuit simulators in the industry. Years of building these tools have accumulated inside Cadence not just software capability but a deep understanding of analog design problems themselves. Building a tool means you see, up close and repeatedly, the bottlenecks and failure modes that tool addresses.

What matters is that this accumulated know-how is now connecting beyond tool competitiveness into the IP business. Cadence’s IP segment grew about 25% YoY in 2025, with the portfolio centered on HBM, UCIe, PCIe, DDR, and SerDes. As it happens, every one of those is an area directly benefiting from AI infrastructure investment. HBM is the AI accelerator memory interface. UCIe is the core connectivity standard for chiplet architectures. PCIe and SerDes are the high-speed links underpinning server and data center systems end to end.

This is where IP becomes an important differentiator for custom silicon services. Existing design houses handle these interfaces too. But most of them assemble customer chips by combining tools and IP sourced from external vendors like Cadence or Synopsys. When Cadence performs the design service directly, the story changes. It can bundle HBM, UCIe, PCIe, and SerDes IP that’s been optimized for its own design environment together with the design service itself. For the customer, that means they’re not just renting headcount. They’re getting a package where tools, IP, design flow, and optimization experience come bound together.

How strong a competitive advantage this combination actually becomes remains to be seen. But structurally at least, Cadence is closer to a design platform that brings its own tools and its own IP into the engagement than it is to a simple design service shop. And that’s a fundamentally different starting point from existing design houses.

Where the three threads converge

To sum up, the changes Cadence has been building over the past two years compress into three axes. Through Invecas, Intrinsix, Apex, and Hexagon, it secured design headcount and multiphysics assets. Through Cerebrus, ChipStack, and AgentStack, it built the AI layer to lift that headcount’s productivity. And with HBM, UCIe, PCIe, and SerDes IP on top, it now has the weapons to differentiate the design service offering.

When the three threads merge, the picture changes quite a bit. EDA tools, IP, AI agents, an actual design organization, and system simulation assets all land inside Cadence under one roof. In other words, it moves past being a company that supplies tools and toward a platform that can cover a customer’s chip design more broadly.

This position is also structurally different from the existing competitive landscape. Synopsys still has a powerful EDA and IP portfolio, but it isn’t fronting design service the way Cadence is. On the other side, design houses like Alchip and GUC have strong capability in actually designing customer chips, but they don’t have in-house EDA tools, an AI layer, and multiphysics assets all under the same company.

Whether this combination translates into real business results still needs more time. But looking purely at the structure, Cadence is moving into a fairly unique position on the border between EDA company and design house. And at that point, it becomes hard to see this company as just another EDA vendor.

What’s Being Sensed in the Field

The picture so far can be assembled mostly from public material. But public material alone always leaves one thing unresolved: whether this change is actually translating into organizational movement on the ground, or whether it’s still sitting at the level of announcements and slide decks. What’s interesting is that what I’ve been hearing from semiconductor professionals around me over the past few months lines up pretty closely with where the public data points.

On the North American side, there are reports of a Samsung-origin senior leader building out an ASIC and design-service team inside Cadence’s US org. Moves at this seniority level typically don’t stop with one person. There’s a high chance they pull along networks of design engineers they’ve worked with over many years. If this is accurate, it reads as a signal that Cadence is putting down a real North American design service base. If Invecas secured the India-side foundation and Hexagon broadened the European engineering assets, strengthening the North American org is close to the last piece of that puzzle.

Stories about talent movement from the Synopsys and Ansys side track in a similar vein. As restructuring and reorgs rolled through the Ansys integration process in late 2025, some senior people are reportedly moving to competitors including Cadence. Among them, engineers with multiphysics experience fit with the simulation assets Cadence is expanding, and people with front-end or design automation backgrounds could land in the AI-driven design organization. These aren’t at the level of officially confirmed personnel announcements. But the reading that uncertainty created by a competitor’s integration process is working as a talent acquisition opportunity for Cadence is reasonable enough.

Take any one of these stories alone and it could be an individual rumor. But when the public strategy and the talent flows being sensed in the field keep pointing the same direction over and over, it’s hard to chalk that up to coincidence. My weight falls on the side that says Cadence is actually building out a design service organization.

How Big Is This Market

If Cadence is heading toward custom silicon services, the obvious follow-up question is how big this market is and whether there’s room for Cadence to enter.

Start by cutting away the market Cadence isn’t going after. The high end of the AI server ASIC market is dominated by a small number of large big-tech players. Their customers are hyperscalers, who already have thousands of in-house chip designers and work with co-design partners on multi-generation, multi-year contracts. Cadence squeezing into that market as a new entrant isn’t realistic.

What Cadence is going after is the tier below that. Automotive OEMs that need a custom autonomous driving SoC but don’t have an in-house design team. Defense contractors that need dedicated security chips but lack EDA flow experience. Mid-size AI chip startups developing inference silicon but short on analog IP and physical design depth. Industrial equipment makers trying to convert FPGAs to ASICs. For these customers, the business model of the major ASIC players doesn’t fit.

Let’s size the market. Estimates for the global ASIC design service market vary by research firm. Under a narrow definition, it runs from about $7.4 billion (2025) to $12.4 billion (2035). Under a broader definition (including digital + analog + verification + prototype services), it ranges from $18.9 billion (2026) to $30.7 billion (2033). Either way, the projected CAGR is around 5 to 8%.

The biggest players in this market today are Taiwan-based design houses. GUC (Global Unichip) has TTM revenue of about $1.09 billion and a market cap around $11.4 billion. Alchip posted Q2 2024 revenue alone of $421 million, with AI/HPC making up 91% of the mix. Both are growing fast on hyperscaler custom silicon (Google TPU, AWS Trainium, etc.). Per TrendForce, ASIC-based AI servers will account for 27.8% of total AI server shipments in 2026, approaching 40% by 2030.

The key point is that this market isn’t hyperscaler-only. Automotive, industrial, defense, telecom, mid-tier AI chip, and other non-hyperscaler demand makes up a significant portion of this market, and that share is growing fast as AI adoption spreads. That’s the segment Cadence is targeting. While GUC and Alchip focus on large hyperscaler deals, there’s currently almost no company positioned to offer non-hyperscaler customers an integrated package of tools + IP + AI agents + design service.

China and Asia are particularly large opportunity areas. The Chinese semiconductor market is forecast to grow from about $182.8 billion in 2024 to $430 billion by 2033. That said, Cadence’s China revenue exposure was around 13% in 2025, so it’s limited, and US-China export controls are a structural risk hanging over it at the same time. A market with both opportunity and risk on a large scale.

If This Scenario Is Right, What Changes Structurally

If the picture drawn above actually works, what changes in Cadence’s business model isn’t just revenue growth. The structure itself changes.

First, revenue becomes multi-layered. Right now tool license fees are the main thing. In the new model, the same customer can be charged tool license + IP license + design service fee at the same time. And once a chip designed by Cadence, using Cadence IP, built on Cadence tools, is in production, the cost of switching to another vendor at the next generation becomes extreme.

Second, an AI flywheel could form. The design projects Cadence performs internally become training data for Cerebrus, ChipStack, and AgentStack. As projects accumulate, AI improves, and improved AI brings in more customers. For that structure to actually spin, though, data governance and customer IP protection have to be solved first.

Third, the tools themselves improve faster. When you design real chips with your own tools, limitations and bugs surface faster than they would through external customer reports. The design service org effectively becomes an internal testbed for the entire EDA portfolio.

Whether all three of these spin at once, or only some of them, isn’t clear yet.

Risks

The risks to this scenario are also clear.

Neutrality. The most expensive asset an EDA vendor holds is the position of “neutral tool supplier.” The deeper Cadence goes into design service, the more it competes with design house customers who buy and use its tools. It’s the classic platform-versus-partner conflict, where the platform operator competes with partners running their business on it. Losing that balance could damage the core EDA business.

Margins. Cadence’s non-GAAP operating margin is 44.6%. Design service is fundamentally a people-centric business. As its share grows, blended margins compress. The key variable is how much per-engineer productivity gets lifted by AI, and those numbers haven’t been validated yet.

Capability. Whether a company that’s good at building tools can become a team that uses those tools to design great chips is a separate proposition. Acquisitions brought in experienced teams, but whether they keep their independent design house agility once embedded in a large corporation is something only time answers.

Geopolitics. Cadence’s China subsidiary has a track record of agreeing to over $140 million in fines for illegal exports to NUDT. China is the biggest growth opportunity and the biggest regulatory risk at the same time.

Investor View: Where the Numbers Put This Right Now

Cadence FY2025 results.

Revenue $5.297 billion, up 14% YoY. Non-GAAP operating margin 44.6%. Record backlog $7.8 billion. 2026 revenue guidance $5.9 billion to $6.0 billion, with the Hexagon contribution not yet reflected.

By segment, Core EDA is 70%, Semiconductor IP is 14%, and System Design and Analysis is 16%. The IP segment is growing at 25% YoY, and if this trend continues for a few more years, the shape of the revenue mix itself starts to look different.

The valuation is already expensive. As of April 15, the P/E is in the low 90s, with a market cap around $84 billion. The market has already priced in a substantial portion of Cadence’s AI tailwind.

In my view, there are two triggers for additional re-rating.

First, IP revenue growth pushing past 25% and breaking through 30%. How far HBM, UCIe, PCIe, and SerDes demand extends during the AI ASIC investment cycle is something that doesn’t have a visible ceiling yet.

Second, design service revenue being broken out as a separate segment. Right now it’s mixed inside Core EDA and invisible from outside. The point at which Cadence discloses “Design Services” or “Integrated Solutions” as a standalone segment will be the inflection where the market reframes how it looks at this company.

The risk triggers are also clear. Expanded China restrictions. Synopsys counter-attacking in simulation after Ansys integration. AgentStack failing to deliver the expected productivity in real use. The premium multiple Cadence carries right now leaves almost no tolerance for an earnings miss.

Conclusion

The market sees Cadence as an “EDA beneficiary in the AI era.” My frame is different.

Put everything Cadence has executed over the past two years into one picture, and where this company is heading isn’t “EDA vendor riding AI.” It’s closer to “a semiconductor design platform that directly designs customer chips.” And it’s hard to say this direction is fully reflected in the market’s valuation. The design service business has never been broken out as a separate segment. AgentStack’s track record in production is short. The Hexagon integration impact is just starting.

Of course this picture might be wrong. If neutrality erodes, core customers walk. If design service margins disappoint, the valuation actually gets pressured instead of re-rated.

One thing is certain. The line between tool maker and chip designer is blurring across the entire EDA industry. The clearest specimen of that direction right now is Cadence.
