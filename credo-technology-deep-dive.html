1. Credo’s Technical Moat: Everything Starts with SerDes

The first thing to understand about Credo is not what products they sell. What matters more is where this company repeatedly generates competitive advantage.

In Credo’s case, the starting point is clear: SerDes IP.

SerDes: Where Every Connection Begins

Inside a data center, data is constantly moving. GPU to GPU, server to switch, rack to rack. Inside the chip, data travels simultaneously across dozens or hundreds of parallel lines. But the moment it needs to leave the chip, you run into problems. Routing hundreds of lines out of a chip makes the cables too thick, and electrical interference between the lines becomes severe.

The solution is to combine multiple parallel signals into a single high-speed serial signal (Serialize), send it, and then unpack it on the receiving end (Deserialize).

The circuit block that handles this conversion is the SerDes (Serializer/Deserializer). Everywhere a “connection” exists inside a data center, SerDes is involved.

Credo designs its own SerDes IP in-house across every generation, from 28Gbps all the way up to 224Gbps. Other large semiconductor companies have their own SerDes too, but for those companies, SerDes is just one component among dozens of product lines. For Credo, SerDes is the entire reason the company exists. Out of 622 total employees, roughly 500+ are engineers, and that R&D firepower is focused squarely on SerDes.

AEC: The Market Credo Created

The first product Credo built on top of this SerDes IP was the AEC (Active Electrical Cable).

The most basic way to connect servers and switches inside a data center is copper cabling. As speeds moved to 800G and beyond, conventional “passive” copper cables started falling apart. Signals degrade and distort over distance. You can make the cable thicker to compensate, but then it gets as thick as a finger, and you are limited to 1 to 2 meters of reach.

Credo solved this problem in a completely different way. They put a small chip inside the connector at each end of the cable. That chip contains Credo’s SerDes and a DSP (Digital Signal Processor, a processor that mathematically restores distorted signals). Because the chip regenerates the signal mid-path, you can use thin copper wire (32AWG) and still transmit up to 7 meters, while cutting cable bulk by up to 75% compared to DAC.

That is the AEC, or “active electrical cable.” Credo created this product category, and they still dominate it.

In an AI data center, cutting cable volume by 75% is not a nice-to-have. In 120kW and 200kW racks where liquid cooling pipes and cables compete for the same physical space, this is the difference between being able to build the system or not.

But the real strength of AEC is not the size reduction. It is reliability.

Credo calls this “ZeroFlap.” A link flap is a momentary connection drop caused by signal instability. In an AI fabric with tens of thousands of GPUs and link counts in the hundred-thousand range, even a single unstable link can wreck an entire training run. Millions of dollars in training costs gone in an instant. Credo claims its ZeroFlap solution delivers up to 1,000x higher reliability compared to legacy interconnect solutions, and real deployment data from hyperscaler customers backs that up.

Mature Process Node Strategy: Why Credo’s Margins Are High

Another key technical advantage is Credo’s manufacturing strategy. The company deliberately fabricates its chips on mature process nodes rather than cutting-edge ones. With the AI boom creating chronic capacity shortages at leading-edge fabs, Credo is relatively free from supply chain bottlenecks.

This works because the SerDes design capability itself is strong enough. When you can squeeze performance out of circuit design optimization, you do not need the latest process node to hit competitive power/performance ratios. Non-GAAP gross margins in the 67 to 68% range are partly a product of this strategy.

2. The Stock Crash, and the Rebound

A company with this kind of technical moat saw its stock drop from $213 to the $80s. Revenue was hitting all-time highs (FY26 Q3 revenue of $407M, up 201% YoY) and the stock fell nearly 60%. The reason boils down to one thing:

the market reclassified Credo as a “copper company.”

In early 2026, Jensen Huang emphasized the importance of optical interconnects at NVIDIA’s GTC, and NVIDIA made $4B in strategic investments in Lumentum and Coherent. The market built a “the copper era is ending” narrative, and Credo, whose revenue comes mostly from AEC, took a direct hit. On top of that, you had the valuation overhang of 100x+ forward P/E, insider selling, high volatility at a beta of 2.72, and macro headwinds piling on.

But from the $80s, Credo has come all the way back to $157.

That is a 67% bounce from the March low of $94 in just two weeks.

Jefferies initiated coverage with a Buy rating and the stock jumped 12% in a single day. After the DustPhotonics acquisition was announced, it popped another ~20%. This is not just a technical bounce. The “copper company” narrative is starting to crack.

Most analysis stops here.

Evaluate Credo through the “copper vs. optical” lens, call the DustPhotonics acquisition a signal of optical expansion, and move on.

But the way I see it, the market is missing something bigger.

AEC was just the first product Credo built on its SerDes IP. On top of that same SerDes IP sit six product lines in total: optical DSP, PCIe retimer, memory gearbox, optical transceiver, and active LED cable.

The portfolio covers every connection distance from chip-to-memory (inches) to long-haul data center links (kilometers). And with yesterday’s DustPhotonics acquisition bringing Silicon Photonics PIC in-house, the vertical integration from copper to optical is now complete. Very few connectivity-focused companies have this kind of structure.

What exactly DustPhotonics’ SiPho PIC is, and why Credo spent $750M on it right now. The difference between a company that only has DSP and one that also owns PIC on the road to CPO (Co-Packaged Optics). Why Credo has structural advantages over Astera Labs and Marvell during the optical transition, and where its biggest vulnerability lies. These are the structural differences that most Wall Street reports fail to explain. I will break them down below.

3. The Bigger Picture Behind the DustPhotonics Acquisition

A Portfolio That Covers Every Segment from One SerDes IP

As I explained in Section 1, AEC is a product that puts Credo’s SerDes IP inside a copper cable. But the problems that SerDes needs to solve are not limited to copper cables. The same class of problem repeats across every segment where signals travel inside a data center. Credo has been building products for each of these segments, one by one.

When you move to fiber optic cables, light replaces electrical signals, but optical signals also distort during transmission. The chip that restores those signals is the optical DSP, and SerDes sits inside that DSP as well. Credo’s optical DSP lineup, Cardinal, supports up to 1.6T at 224Gbps/lane. This chip maintains signal quality inside optical transceivers.

Problems also exist inside the server itself. On the PCIe interface connecting GPU and CPU, signal integrity degrades through copper traces on the board as speeds increase. Credo’s PCIe Gen6 retimer regenerates those signals mid-path, and once again, SerDes is at the core.

At even shorter distances, between chip and memory (inches), there are bottlenecks too. In AI inference, where memory capacity and bandwidth are insufficient, Credo’s OmniConnect Weaver gearbox addresses the problem with 112G VSR SerDes. This chip increases LPDDR5X memory I/O density by 10x.

And for the gap between AEC’s 7-meter reach and optical’s long-distance coverage, the 7 to 30 meter range, a micro-LED-based ALC (Active LED Cable) is in development.

Across all of these products, PILOT (Predictive Integrity Link Optimization and Telemetry) software is integrated. PILOT is a diagnostic platform that predicts and prevents link flaps before they happen. Once a hyperscaler builds its data center management infrastructure on PILOT, switching to another vendor’s products becomes difficult.

To sum it up: Credo already had a portfolio covering every connection distance, from chip-to-memory (inches) to data center long-haul (kilometers), all on a single SerDes IP + PILOT foundation. But one piece was missing. The PIC (Photonic Integrated Circuit), the core component that converts electrical signals to light in optical communications. That was the one part Credo was still sourcing externally. It was the only point where Credo could not fully control the performance and cost of its optical products.

The DustPhotonics acquisition fills that last gap.

DustPhotonics’ Technology: Silicon Photonics PIC and Carmel 8

DustPhotonics is an Israel-based company specializing in Silicon Photonics PICs. Understanding their technology makes it clear why Credo picked this particular company.

Start with the architecture of a conventional optical transceiver.

The standard approach is to mount an individual laser for each channel. Eight channels means eight lasers. Each laser is sensitive to temperature and current, and if one fails, that channel dies. The part count is high, heat output is significant, and reliability management is complex.

DustPhotonics’ SiPho PIC supports both integrated laser and external laser configurations. In both configurations, 2 lasers each serve 4 lanes, cutting the laser count to one-quarter of the conventional per-channel approach with 8 individual lasers. In the external laser model, you can freely choose your laser vendor. And the PIC chip itself is manufactured in a pure silicon process, meaning it can be mass-produced in existing semiconductor fabs. Part count drops, system architecture simplifies, and power consumption falls. Fewer lasers also means fewer potential sources of link flaps.

Carmel 8 is an 800G DR8 transmit PIC with integrated CW lasers, and has reportedly achieved production-grade signal quality at 8-channel 100G/lane PAM4. DustPhotonics’ full portfolio covers 400G (Carmel 4) through 1.6T (Oz8, Kfir200), with a roadmap extending to 3.2T, and is designed to work with both NPO (Near Package Optics) and CPO (Co-Packaged Optics) architectures.

PAM4 eye diagram measurement of the DustPhotonics Carmel 8. Wider and cleaner eye openings indicate better signal quality. The metrics shown on the left panel, including a TDECQ of 1.15 dB and RLM of 0.986, demonstrate comfortable margin against 100G/lane PAM4 specifications, suggesting production-ready signal integrity.

The deal is valued at $750M in cash plus approximately 0.92M shares of Credo stock, with performance-linked earnout payments of up to 3.21M additional shares. Chairman Avigdor Willenz previously sold Habana Labs to Intel and Annapurna Labs to AWS. Both companies have customer relationships with major hyperscalers, creating clear synergy potential.

Credo + DustPhotonics = Why This Becomes a Moat

The strategic significance of this acquisition is not “they picked up another PIC.” When Credo’s existing technology meets DustPhotonics’ assets, a structural moat forms that other companies cannot easily replicate.

First, system-level optimization.

The legacy optical transceiver industry is built on a division of labor. One company makes the DSP, another makes the PIC, and yet another assembles them into a transceiver module. Each player optimizes within its own domain, but inefficiencies emerge at the boundaries. If the DSP’s output characteristics do not perfectly match the PIC’s input characteristics, signal quality suffers and additional correction circuitry becomes necessary.

Credo now designs SerDes, DSP, and PIC all in-house. They can co-optimize the DSP output with the PIC input from the ground up. With PILOT software under the same roof, the scope for co-optimization between DSP and PIC is structurally larger. There is a fundamental difference between chip designers, optical designers, and software engineers building the same product within one company, versus separate companies exchanging spec sheets and trying to match up.

Second, extending ZeroFlap reliability into optical.

What Credo built through years of AEC deployment is not just “a chip that works well.” It is system-level engineering know-how for predicting and preventing link flaps. Signal drift patterns under temperature changes, performance degradation curves from aging, intermittent failure patterns in large-scale clusters. This kind of knowledge can only come from years of real-world deployment data.

DustPhotonics’ architecture structurally makes it easier to extend this ZeroFlap capability into the optical domain. In the ELS configuration specifically, there is only one laser, so light source management is simplified. Instead of monitoring 8 individual lasers, you only need to manage 1 laser and 8 PIC channels. Even in the integrated laser model, the laser count drops from the conventional 8 down to 2, and the same principle applies. The number of variables that PILOT needs to monitor shrinks. When Credo transplants the reliability engineering it built in AEC to optical transceivers, DustPhotonics’ PIC architecture makes that transplant easier.

Third, this is a “controlling light” business, not a “making light” business.

DustPhotonics’ PIC supports both integrated and external lasers, but the ELS configuration is strategically important because the laser sits outside the PIC. Credo does not need to make lasers themselves. Whether it is Lumentum or Coherent, they can use the best laser available at any given time. What Credo owns is not the light source itself, but the control layer: splitting light (PIC), processing signals (DSP), and ensuring system stability (PILOT). This is a strategy of owning only the high-value-add stages that are difficult to commoditize.

When these three elements come together, the completed technology stack looks like this:

SerDes IP → DSP → Silicon Photonics PIC → Optical Transceiver → PILOT Software

Credo now designs every stage that data passes through, from leaving one chip to arriving at another. The company projects optical revenue alone will exceed $500M in FY27 post-integration. They were preparing this acquisition while the stock was in the $80s, and the stock jumped 20% immediately after the announcement. With $1.3B in cash and short-term investments and essentially zero net debt as of FY26 Q3, this was a strategic move made from a position of strength.

4. Who Else Is Playing This Same Game?

If you had to name the companies that share Credo’s ambition of tackling the big picture of AI infrastructure connectivity, it comes down to Astera Labs and Marvell. All three companies go beyond selling a single chip or cable. They are all looking at the same problem: how to connect AI servers to racks, and racks to racks, faster and more reliably.

But they start from different places, which means they are each strong in different areas. Understanding these differences is how you see where Credo is strong and where it is vulnerable.

Astera Labs: Starting Inside the Server, Climbing Up to the Rack

The easiest way to understand Astera is this: originally, this company was closer to one that keeps signals from breaking inside a server. When GPUs, CPUs, memory, and accelerators connect to each other, PCIe and CXL signals weaken and distort as they travel across the board. Astera’s Aries retimer is a chip that cleans up those weakened signals mid-path and extends their reach, essentially a relay for high-speed signals that cannot survive long board traces. Astera solved the problem inside the AI server first. In official materials, Aries is described as a product that significantly improves signal integrity and extends reach in PCIe and CXL environments.

But Astera did not stop there. A company that used to focus on internal server connections is now moving up to the question of how to tie entire racks together. The Taurus smart cable module adds management and diagnostic capabilities inside the cable to improve reliability. Scorpio goes further, acting as a smart fabric switch that handles data flow between multiple GPUs and accelerators. The key point is that Astera is not trying to build “a faster switch.” This company layers software and telemetry on top, aiming to continuously monitor where bottlenecks form and where data gets stuck when multiple AI servers are linked. COSMOS is ultimately that management layer. In other words, Astera’s core identity is less about the medium and more about making connections visible and operationally manageable.

This is where the difference from Credo becomes clear. If Credo is a company that digs deep into link-level reliability and the internals of the optical stack, Astera is closer to a company building a “structure for managing traffic flow more effectively across servers and racks” at the system level. Astera’s recent announcement of adding optical-based switch-to-accelerator connectivity in the Scorpio X Series to extend to multi-rack is part of the same theme. In December 2025, they acquired aiXscale Photonics, stepping into the optical domain. But Astera is not trying to become an optical components company. It looks more like a company that wants to weave its own fabric on top, even as optical enters the picture.

Marvell is a different animal from Astera or Credo. Calling it simply an optical DSP company is underselling it. The position Marvell is really going after is the seat at the table when hyperscalers design their AI systems from day one. That is why they talk about custom XPU, custom HBM architecture, advanced packaging, NVLink Fusion, and CPO all in the same breath. Marvell is not trying to sell a chip to a customer. It is moving toward co-designing the entire AI system that the customer wants.

Technically, Marvell’s strength is its scale and the breadth of adjacent capabilities. Credo is a company that excels at links. Astera is a company that excels at intra-server and intra-rack fabric. Marvell, by contrast, also gets involved in “how to build the custom AI accelerator that sits on top.” The recent announcement of NVLink Fusion collaboration with NVIDIA is an extension of that trajectory. Marvell said it would provide custom XPUs with NVLink Fusion compatible scale-up networking, which effectively means that when a hyperscaler architects an AI cluster, Marvell can enter not as a component vendor but as a design partner. That is a different kind of leverage than what Credo or Astera bring.

On the optical side, Marvell is not just a DSP-only player either. The company has shown custom AI accelerator architectures with CPO built in, and has emphasized that its silicon photonics technology already has several generations of field history. On top of that, they have released a TIA and laser driver chipset for 1.6T LPO. Marvell does not see the optical transition as “one new market.” It sees it as the question of how to embed optical connectivity inside custom XPU and rack-scale AI designs. So the real threat from Marvell is not product-level competition. It is the possibility that a customer locks into the Marvell stack from the design stage.

The Two Companies Credo Faces Are Actually Different from Each Other

What matters here is that Astera and Marvell are not the same company either. Astera is building a company that manages connections more efficiently inside servers and racks. Marvell is building a company that co-designs entire AI systems. Credo, among these three, is closest to a company that makes high-speed links more stable and wants to manage those links through the optical era with its own hands. Credo’s ZeroFlap, PILOT, optical DSP, and the PIC internalization through DustPhotonics all point in the same direction.

So if you put all three companies in the same table and compare them only by “who has DSP and who has PIC,” you actually blur the key distinction. The real difference is not the parts list. It is which control point each company is trying to seize.

Astera is going after data flow and observability. Marvell is going after custom AI architecture. Credo is going after high-speed link reliability and the optical transition control point. These three overlap, but they are different.

And that intersection is the real essence of the competition.

Who Has the Edge, and Who Is Most Vulnerable?

The way I see it, declaring an outright winner is too simplistic. Which company has the advantage depends on which type of connection becomes the bottleneck first.

If traffic management and switch architecture within AI servers and at rack-scale scale-up become more important, Astera could gain strength.

If hyperscalers want to design their own structure from scratch, bundling custom XPU, packaging, and CPO together, Marvell becomes the more natural fit.

On the other hand, if link flaps, operational complexity, and the pain of optical transition become more critical as AI clusters grow, Credo’s position strengthens. To my eye, the market has not yet fully priced in this last problem. Credo is smaller than Marvell but more focused, and it has gone deeper into the optical stack than Astera. That is both Credo’s opportunity and its risk.

Why Credo is the one I favor most among these three, and what the basis and limits of that judgment are, is what I will unpack in the next section.

5. How I See Credo

From here on, this is my interpretation beyond publicly available analysis.

The Market Still Sees Credo Through a Product Lens

The way I see it, the market is still looking at Credo too much at the product level. Before, they saw it as an AEC company. After the DustPhotonics acquisition, they are starting to see it as an optical expansion play. Neither of those is completely wrong. But neither fully explains this company.

The way I see Credo’s essence is simpler. This is not a company that sells copper. It is not a company that sells optical. It is a company that solves the most painful problems in data center connectivity.

In high-speed connectivity, the reason customers actually pay is not because the medium is copper or optical. At the end of the day, customers pay for connections that are more reliable, easier to deploy, and easier to scale. As AI clusters grow, the importance of all three only increases. That is why I am bullish on Credo.

Many investors still view interconnect through a media-centric lens. Copper company if it is copper, optical company if it is optical. But the actual pain points for hyperscalers are not that simple. What they are buying is not cables but cluster uptime. Not transceivers but operational predictability. The solution that has fewer failures, enables faster root-cause analysis when failures do happen, and keeps operational complexity from exploding during generational speed transitions is the one that ultimately wins.

The Core of the DustPhotonics Deal Is Not Revenue, It Is Control

So if you read the DustPhotonics acquisition as simply adding one more optical business, you are reading it too small. The way I see it, the core of this deal is not optical revenue. It is control.

Credo was already strong in DSP and link reliability. But relying on external sources for PIC means you cannot fully control the cost curve, yield curve, and product cadence of your optical products. Ultimately, in the optical era, part of the company’s fate would hinge on an external component vendor’s roadmap. The DustPhotonics acquisition is closer to a declaration that Credo intends to reduce that dependency and go deeper inside the optical stack.

This matters because the optical era might actually favor a company like Credo. Many people view the optical transition as a crisis for copper companies. I see it the other way around. As connectivity goes optical, systems become more sensitive, and how reliably you can manage the entire link matters more than the performance of any individual component. As the medium shifts to optical, the value of companies that excel at integration and control, rather than just component supply, could grow.

The real shift I see in Credo right now is exactly this. It is not that an AEC company is becoming an optical company. It is that a component vendor is becoming an architecture vendor. Not a company that sells one part well, but a company that defines how customers design and operate their data center connectivity. Once AEC, DSP, retimer, gearbox, optics, and the telemetry layer on top start bundling together, customers are no longer buying a cable. They are designing their connectivity the Credo way.

Why I Favor Credo Over Its Competitors

Astera and Marvell are clearly strong companies. Astera has advantages in managing data flow more efficiently inside servers and racks. Marvell has the scale and adjacent capabilities to co-design entire AI systems with hyperscalers. But the reason I favor Credo right now is that, among the three, it is the one most directly digging into the operational problems of data center connectivity.

As AI clusters grow, what matters is not simply faster links. What matters more is a structure where failures happen less, root causes are found faster when they do, and operational complexity does not balloon during the optical transition. On this front, Credo is moving in one coherent direction: ZeroFlap experience from AEC, PILOT, optical DSP, and PIC internalization through DustPhotonics.

Marvell has the strongest scale. Astera has the most interesting system-level extensibility. But at this particular moment, the most misunderstood company, and therefore the one with the most room for reinterpretation, is Credo. The reason I am bullish is not simply optical exposure. It is the possibility that Credo will grip the connectivity control point even more tightly in the optical era.

What to Watch Going Forward

Of course, full confirmation of this transition will take time. The optical revenue mix needs to grow. The DustPhotonics integration needs to translate into actual product competitiveness. And whether a management layer like PILOT creates real customer lock-in remains to be seen. So it is hard to say everything has been proven right now.

But the way I see it, the market is still evaluating Credo on results, while the company is already moving to restructure its foundation. The time gap between those two is the biggest opportunity right now.

My upside scenario is straightforward. The moment the market starts seeing Credo not as a fast-growing AEC company but as a connectivity platform company that can handle both copper and optical, the entire valuation framework could shift. If this thesis is wrong, the reason will be equally clear: either the DustPhotonics integration fails to deliver the expected cost and product competitiveness, or Credo gets trapped in component-level competition during the optical transition instead of establishing itself as the control layer.

What matters going forward is not optical exposure per se. It is whether Credo can hold the control layer in the optical era.
