Silicon photonics foundry. Still an unfamiliar term for many, but right now it’s one of the hottest keywords in the semiconductor industry. A factory that mass-produces chips that transmit data using light. Tower is currently standing at the front of that line. It is the de facto sole volume supplier of 1.6T (1.6 terabits per second) PICs (Photonic Integrated Circuits), and it serves as the primary foundry for both pluggable transceivers and CPO (Co-Packaged Optics, the next-generation technology that places optical modules inside the chip package).

1. What Does Tower Semiconductor Actually Do

Tower Semiconductor (TSEM) is an Israeli-headquartered semiconductor foundry. A foundry doesn’t design chips itself; it manufactures chips designed by other companies. Samsung and TSMC are the most recognizable examples, and those two companies primarily produce ultra-fine digital chips: smartphone APs, AI accelerators, the kind of products locked in a race to 3nm, 2nm.

Tower doesn’t play that game. What this company does well is what the industry calls “specialty analog”: SiGe (silicon-germanium, a material for high-speed communications), RF CMOS (high-frequency communication chips), BCD (power management chips), and CMOS image sensors (camera sensors). Think of it this way: if the digital world’s giants build the “brain,” Tower builds the “sensory organs” and “muscles” that handle electrical signals, detect light, and regulate power.

Its fabs are distributed across the globe. There’s a 200mm fab in Migdal Haemek, Israel; a 200mm fab in Newport Beach, California (the former Jazz Semiconductor facility, which has roots going back to Rockwell); and fabs in Japan through a joint venture called TPSCo running both 200mm and 300mm lines. In Italy, Tower shares a 300mm fab with ST Microelectronics.

The Intel relationship deserves a direct mention. In February 2022, Intel announced it would acquire Tower for $5.4B. The deal collapsed in August 2023 after failing to clear Chinese regulatory approval. The fact that Intel was willing to spend $5.4B to acquire Tower is itself proof of how irreplaceable its specialty analog technology and fab infrastructure are. After the merger fell apart, the two companies maintained a partnership called a “capacity corridor,” but according to industry sources, the practical integration of their different process systems has been difficult to execute. Intel has acknowledged challenges in fulfilling the arrangement. Tower, rather than depending on Intel, appears to have redirected its strategy toward expanding its Japanese TPSCo fabs.

FY2025 revenue came in at approximately $1.44B. Q4 2025 revenue hit $440M, a record quarter. By this point alone, Tower looks like a solid mid-sized foundry.

But the reason the market is excited about Tower right now is something else entirely: silicon photonics.

2. Why the Stock Is Surging

The biggest catalyst dropped on February 5th. Tower announced a collaboration with NVIDIA on 1.6T silicon photonics. Silicon photonics means placing optical components (devices that generate, modulate, and detect light) on silicon so that data can be transmitted using light instead of electricity. The announcement said Tower’s SiPho technology would be used in NVIDIA’s AI datacenter networking, and the stock jumped 12.6% that day.

In March, partnership announcements came every week: Oriole Networks (ultra-low-latency optical switching for AI), NLM Photonics (next-generation optical modulator validation), Lightwave Logic (high-speed polymer modulator PDK integration), Salience Labs (photonic switching for large-scale AI infrastructure), and Xscape Photonics (a startup backed by NVIDIA and Cisco that demonstrated on-chip multi-wavelength lasers). All of it runs on Tower’s PH18 platform.

The timing is sharp. OFC 2026 (Optical Fiber Communication Conference, the largest event in the optical communications industry) is running March 17 through 19 in Los Angeles, and Tower is there presenting its SiPho roadmap. Tower’s message at OFC 2026 is clear: “Our platform covers both Scale-Out (rack-to-rack connectivity) via pluggable transceivers and Scale-Up (intra-server GPU-to-GPU connectivity) architectures via CPO.”

The numbers back the story up. Q4 2025 revenue of $440M was up 14% year-over-year, with net income of $80M beating expectations. More importantly, look at the silicon photonics revenue trajectory: SiPho revenue roughly doubled from approximately $106M in FY2024 to $228M in FY2025. Adding SiGe, the combined SiPho plus SiGe revenue went from $241M in FY2024 to $421M in FY2025. These two technologies now account for roughly 30% of Tower’s total revenue, and that share is climbing fast.

Management is going all-in. What was originally a $650M investment plan for SiPho and SiGe has been raised to $920M, a 40% increase. The target is to grow monthly SiPho production capacity fivefold by Q4 2026 versus the prior year. More than 70% of new SiPho capacity is already reserved by customers through 2028.

The factory isn't fully built yet and the seats are already filled. A contact of mine who works at Tower tells me the volume surge has been dramatic enough that the team is genuinely stretched thin right now. Industry sources corroborate this: fab utilization is near full, with a backlog stretching years out.

3. What TSEM Actually Builds: PIC, EIC, and 3D Stacking

Tower builds three things.

First, PICs (Photonic Integrated Circuits). This is the core product. On silicon, Tower places waveguides (light pathways), modulators (devices that encode data by modifying the intensity or phase of light), photodetectors (devices that convert optical signals back to electrical signals), and in some configurations, lasers. These PICs go into pluggable optical modules, CPO, LiDAR, and quantum computing applications.

Second, EICs (Electronic Integrated Circuits). These are chips made using Tower’s SiGe BiCMOS process: drivers (circuits that power the modulators) and TIAs (transimpedance amplifiers, which convert optical signals to electrical). If the PIC handles the light, the EIC controls it.

Third, wafer-level 3D stacking of PIC plus EIC. Tower took the 3D wafer bonding technology it has run in volume production for camera sensors (BSI, Back-Side Illumination) and extended it to SiPho and SiGe. A PIC wafer and an EIC wafer are bonded vertically into a single 3D-IC. Tower formally announced this CPO foundry capability in November 2025 and built out a 3D-IC design flow together with Cadence. Core CPO enablers like TSV (Through-Silicon Via) and microring resonators are also part of the platform.

What Tower does not do is final module assembly. Wafer-level stacking of PIC and EIC happens inside the fab, but everything after that, including dicing (cutting the wafer into individual chips), fiber attach (connecting optical fiber to the chip), and final packaging, goes to OSATs (outsourced semiconductor assembly and test companies like ASE or Amkor).

What is PH18

Now for the PH18 platform specifically. PH18 is Tower’s silicon photonics foundry platform, operated out of its Newport Beach 200mm fab. “PH” stands for Photonics, “18” refers to the 180nm process node. Several variants exist.

PH18M (Metal) is the baseline. It provides the core optical building blocks: waveguides, modulators, photodetectors. PH18DA adds direct integration of InP (indium phosphide) lasers on silicon. OpenLight used this PH18DA platform to receive the first volume production order for laser-integrated 800G and 1.6T PICs. It proves that a single chip can both generate light and modulate it for data transmission, without an external laser source, and do it in volume. PH18DB goes further, integrating GaAs quantum dot lasers and SOAs (semiconductor optical amplifiers), a world first.

The defining characteristic of PH18 is that it is an open platform. Any fabless company that designs chips can come to Tower and manufacture on this platform. Tower runs regular MPW (Multi-Project Wafer) shuttles, allowing customers ranging from startups to large companies to prototype quickly and transition directly to volume production.

Current volume customers running on Tower include InnoLight (the world’s largest optical transceiver company), Coherent, and OpenLight/NewPhotonics. Tower is also working with Scintil Photonics on volume production of DWDM (Dense Wavelength Division Multiplexing) lasers for CPO on the Tower platform.

In late 2024, Tower also released a 300mm SiPho process as a standard offering. This is not a migration away from 200mm to 300mm. Tower is keeping PH18 on 200mm as the primary platform and adding 300mm as an option. With the Intel capacity corridor effectively stalled, Tower is placing the weight of its capacity expansion on the 300mm fab expansion at TPSCo in Japan.

4. The Optical Foundry Map: How Tower, TSMC, and GF Differ

Who manufactures silicon photonics chips? Answering that question requires separating out what is being made. An optical system has multiple layers: the PIC die, the EIC die, the integration of those two, and final packaging. Each foundry covers a different portion of that stack.

Tower: The Dominant Force in PIC Die Volume Production

Tower’s territory is PIC die, EIC die, and wafer-level 3D stacking. Final module packaging goes to OSATs. Tower is currently the de facto sole volume supplier of 1.6T SiPho PICs, and it produces the core EIC components (driver, TIA, PD) in-house using its SiGe process.

These PIC dies go into pluggable transceivers and CPO alike. The PIC itself doesn’t know whether it will end up in a pluggable module or a CPO package. It is simply an optical chip. When Tower announced at OFC 2026 that it covers both Scale-Out (pluggable) and Scale-Up (CPO), that is precisely what it meant. 650 Group’s Alan Weckel noted that scale-up networking will represent a growing share of the projected $200B AI networking market by 2030, and that alignment between foundries and vendors is the key to opening the CPO market.

Tower’s strengths are clear: proven volume production track record (InnoLight, Coherent, OpenLight all running volume orders), open platform access, PDK maturity validated through years of mass production, and the ability to manufacture both PIC and EIC under one roof.

TSMC: Full-Stack CPO in One Stop

TSMC is playing a completely different game. TSMC’s COUPE (Compact Universal Photonic Engine) is a full-stack solution that covers PIC die manufacturing, EIC die manufacturing, heterogeneous integration of the two, and packaging, all in one place.

The key difference is the EIC. TSMC can manufacture EICs on advanced logic nodes like N6 (6nm) or N4 (4nm). Tower’s SiGe BiCMOS is fast, but its integration density doesn’t compare to advanced CMOS logic. As lane speeds in CPO continue to increase, EIC performance becomes more critical, and TSMC has a structural advantage there.

On the packaging side, TSMC leads as well. TSMC is the only foundry to have successfully demonstrated die-to-wafer hybrid bonding at a meaningful scale, with a track record of shipping AMD hybrid bonded chips in actual volume. It can apply advanced packaging like CoWoS directly to PIC plus EIC integration.

The first COUPE-based product was the optical engine NVIDIA announced at GTC 2025, applied to its Quantum-X switch platform. Ayar Labs also announced it is transitioning its next-generation products from GF Fotonix to TSMC COUPE.

That said, TSMC COUPE is accessible only to the highest-tier customers, at the NVIDIA level. Mid-size and smaller fabless companies cannot easily get in.

One critical point needs emphasis here. Tower and TSMC are targeting the same market from different layers of the stack. TSMC offers a full stack (PIC plus EIC plus packaging). Tower is a specialized foundry supplying PIC dies (plus EIC dies and wafer-level stacking). This is not competition; it is coexistence. A scenario where Tower PICs end up inside a TSMC COUPE package cannot be ruled out, and the large number of customers who cannot access TSMC COUPE will use Tower’s open platform.

An analogy: TSMC is a vertically integrated automaker that delivers a finished vehicle. It builds the engine, the transmission, and assembles the whole car. Tower is a specialist engine manufacturer. Some automakers build their own engines; many others buy from a dedicated engine supplier. Both are needed.

GlobalFoundries: The Monolithic Path

GF takes a third approach. It uses monolithic integration, placing optical devices, RF circuits, and CMOS logic on a single silicon wafer. The GF Fotonix platform is 300mm, built on 45nm SOI.

But monolithic processing has a ceiling. Photonics processes cannot scale below 35nm, which puts a cap on EIC performance and makes it difficult to handle the high lane speeds required in CPO. That is exactly why Ayar Labs moved from GF monolithic to TSMC COUPE’s heterogeneous approach.

GF acquired AMF (Singapore’s SiPho specialty fab) in November 2025, gaining 200mm IP and personnel. Its SiPho revenue sits at around $75M-plus for 2026, roughly one-third of Tower’s.

The Challengers

UMC has licensed the iSiPP300 process from imec and is targeting risk production starting in 2026 to 2027. Samsung has opened a SiPho R&D center in Singapore and is recruiting former TSMC engineers. SilTerra (Malaysia) has expanded its 200mm SiPho fab to 46,000 wafer starts per month capacity. ST Microelectronics re-entered SiPho in early 2025.

The Landscape in Summary

Three paths coexist.

The TSMC full-stack path: PIC plus EIC plus packaging, all resolved at TSMC. Chosen by top-tier customers like NVIDIA.

The Tower specialized die path: Tower produces PIC dies (plus EIC plus wafer-level stacking); the customer takes those dies and handles packaging themselves or through an OSAT. Chosen by optical module companies like InnoLight, Coherent, and OpenLight, as well as fabless startups.

The GF monolithic path: PIC and EIC integrated on a single chip. Technically elegant but limited by EIC scaling constraints.

These three paths are not substitutes for one another. When the market grows, all three grow. The key point is that Tower is currently dominant in the common denominator: PIC die volume production.

5. NVIDIA, Marvell, and Where Tower Sits

To understand Tower’s position in this ecosystem, you need to look at its relationships with the companies that actually deploy the chips.

The NVIDIA relationship is the most direct. In February 2026, Tower announced it was scaling 1.6T SiPho optical modules optimized for NVIDIA’s networking protocols. Tower PICs are used in both pluggable and CPO applications within the NVIDIA ecosystem. NVIDIA’s own CPO product (the Quantum-X switch) uses TSMC COUPE, but the optical modules in the broader NVIDIA ecosystem (produced by companies like InnoLight) use Tower PICs. Tower and TSMC coexist inside the same NVIDIA ecosystem at different layers of the stack.

The relationship with InnoLight is older and deeper. InnoLight is the world’s largest optical transceiver company and NVIDIA’s largest optical module supplier. InnoLight’s 400G and 800G products are built on Tower’s PH18 platform. Coherent, one of the top global optical companies, has also begun placing volume production orders on Tower’s 1.6T platform. Tower is a central node in the entire optical supply chain.

Marvell acquired Celestial AI for $5.5B in 2025, internalizing optical I/O technology. Where Marvell will direct its SiPho foundry work going forward remains fluid. GF, TSMC, and Tower are all in the running.

The conclusion: Tower is not a captive supplier to any single company. As an open platform, it is accessible to NVIDIA camp customers and others alike. Wherever PIC dies are needed, Tower can supply them.

6. Companies That Rise With Tower

A foundry is a platform. When the platform grows, the players on top of it grow too.

InnoLight (300308.SZ) : Tower PH18’s largest customer. As Tower expands capacity, InnoLight’s supply security improves and its dominance in the 800G and 1.6T optical module market strengthens. InnoLight is targeting over 50% global market share in the 1.6T market, and Tower’s volume production capability is the foundation beneath that goal.

Coherent (COHR) : A global optical company that has started placing volume orders on Tower’s 1.6T platform. A direct beneficiary of Tower capacity expansion.

Cadence (CDNS) : The EDA partner providing exclusive support for Tower’s 3D-IC CPO design flow. Every design stacking Tower’s SiPho and SiGe in 3D runs on Cadence tools.

Scintil Photonics (private) : A partner manufacturing DWDM lasers for CPO on Tower PH18. As scale-up networking grows, DWDM CPO demand grows with it, and the Tower plus Scintil combination benefits accordingly.

Lightwave Logic (LWLG) : A company with high-speed polymer modulator technology. It provides PDKs to both Tower PH18 and GF Fotonix. Growth in the overall SiPho foundry market is direct upside for LWLG.

Xscape Photonics (private) : A startup backed by NVIDIA and Cisco that achieved the world’s first on-chip multi-wavelength laser on Tower PH18.

POET Technologies (POET) : Not in a direct supply relationship with Tower, but POET’s optical interposer technology is an indirect beneficiary of the expanding SiPho foundry market. When Tower produces PIC dies, someone needs to integrate them with lasers, drivers, and other components into a complete optical module. POET’s Optical Interposer is designed to automate and reduce the cost of exactly that integration step. A different layer of the supply chain, but a related one.

7. How a Foundry Engineer Reads Tower

From here, this is my personal view. I am a DFT (Design for Test) engineer working in Silicon Valley. Having come through Samsung Foundry, Qualcomm, and AMD, I have seen the inside of both foundry and fabless operations and watched chips get designed, manufactured, and tested from the inside. That experience is the lens I am using to evaluate Tower.

Is 200mm a Weakness

This is the first question non-engineers tend to ask. “Why are they still on 200mm?” A 300mm wafer has 2.25 times the area, which lowers cost per die and improves compatibility with back-end equipment. But silicon photonics operates differently. Optical components sit at dimensions ranging from hundreds of nanometers to several micrometers; they do not require extreme process scaling, and process maturity and yield matter more than wafer size.

Tower’s 200mm PH18 platform has been running in volume production for years. According to industry sources, SiPho process yield has reached production-viable levels, and current focus has shifted to reducing lot-to-lot and wafer-to-wafer variation in the stabilization phase. That is consistent with a platform that has entered production maturity.

Migrating to 300mm also means changing the PDK. Equipment characteristics differ, which changes optical parameters like waveguide loss and modulator response speed, requiring silicon validation to restart from scratch. GF Fotonix is natively 300mm, yet it trails Tower significantly in volume production track record. The maturity gap explains the difference. Long-term, 300mm expansion is inevitable, but with the Intel corridor effectively non-functional, the TPSCo Japan expansion carries much more of the strategic weight.

The Real Value of an Open Foundry

TSMC COUPE is powerful, but it is only accessible to top-tier customers at the NVIDIA level. GF also skews toward large custom engagements like Cisco. Tower PH18 is a general-purpose platform, shared by large companies like InnoLight and startups like Xscape Photonics.

In the foundry business, that difference is significant. A broad customer base keeps fab utilization stable and distributes risk far better than dependence on one or two accounts. The volume of diverse designs flowing through the platform accelerates its own maturity, creating a virtuous cycle.

Tower’s Real Position: The TSMC of PIC Dies

What I find most important about Tower is the clarity of its position. Tower is not trying to beat TSMC on CPO full-stack. The way TSMC manufactures digital logic dies for any customer who needs them, Tower manufactures optical PIC dies for any customer who needs them.

TSMC COUPE says: “We make the PIC, we make the EIC, and we do the packaging.” Powerful, but access is restricted. Tower says: “We make the PIC die (plus EIC and 3D wafer-level stack) at scale and with high yield. What you do with it from there is your choice.” More flexible, more accessible.

This flexibility remains relevant in the CPO era for a concrete reason. CPO packaging architectures have not yet standardized. Different customers are using different approaches. Tower PIC dies can be combined with any packaging path. TSMC COUPE’s dies, by design of the full-stack offering, are tied to TSMC’s packaging. Tower PICs are not locked in.

And there is a path for Tower to participate even within TSMC COUPE ecosystems. DWDM CPO lasers (the Scintil Photonics collaboration, for example) could be components inside CPO packages produced under TSMC COUPE. Tower and TSMC do not need to be adversaries.

Overall Assessment

From a foundry engineer’s standpoint, Tower’s core strengths come down to three things. First, it is the undisputed leader in PIC die volume production. It is the de facto sole volume supplier for 1.6T PICs, and yield is stabilizing on a production maturity trajectory. Second, it can handle PIC, EIC, and wafer-level 3D stacking all under one roof. Third, it is growing an ecosystem through an open platform.

The risks are real too. The structural trend of TSMC full-stack absorbing large customers, competitive pressure from GF, UMC, and Samsung, and the reconfiguration of capacity expansion after the Intel corridor fell apart.

Tower is “the TSMC of PIC dies” specifically.

The question is whether Tower can become the indispensable infrastructure of the optical era the same way TSMC became the indispensable infrastructure of the AI chip era through digital logic production. Whether the $920M investment is deployed effectively across 300mm expansion at TPSCo Japan, customer diversification, and non-datacenter market development in LiDAR and quantum computing will be the defining story over the next two to three years.

Closing

The copper wall in AI infrastructure is becoming a hard reality. Industry consensus is that 2026 is the commercialization year for silicon photonics. 800G and 1.6T optical module shipments are projected to double, with SiPho penetration reaching 50 to 70 percent.

Tower sits at the center of that transition. PIC dies going into pluggable transceivers, CPO, DWDM lasers, and LiDAR. The number one optical foundry. Whether it can hold that position will be determined by the execution of a $920M bet.
