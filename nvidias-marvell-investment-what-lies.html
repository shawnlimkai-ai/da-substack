Section 1: The Marvell $2B Investment

Surface: “Ecosystem Expansion”

On March 31st, NVIDIA took a $2B stake in Marvell Technology, representing roughly 3 to 4 percent of Marvell’s market cap.

A non-controlling strategic investment. Jensen Huang went on CNBC and said,

“Marvell is a marvelous investment. Been dying to say that.”

On the surface, it looks simple. NVIDIA wants to integrate Marvell’s custom ASICs and networking gear into its own systems via a platform called NVLink Fusion. Marvell’s stock jumped 13% that day, and analysts moved their price targets up to $135.

The formal partnership structure, as announced, breaks down like this:

  1. On top of the NVLink Fusion platform, Marvell provides custom XPUs and NVLink Fusion-compatible scale-up networking.

  2. NVIDIA contributes the full infrastructure stack including the Vera CPU, ConnectX NIC, BlueField DPU, NVLink interconnect, and Spectrum-X switches.

  3. Add to that silicon photonics co-development, advanced optical interconnects, and NVIDIA Aerial AI-RAN collaboration to convert 5G/6G telecom networks into AI infrastructure.

Marvell’s FY2026 datacenter revenue came in at $6.1B, up 46.5% year-over-year. Datacenters now account for 74.4% of total revenue. The FY2028 guidance targets $15B.

CEO Matt Murphy told CNBC that customer demand remains solid despite geopolitical headwinds.

That’s the surface.

The Subtext: The Real Target Isn’t Marvell. It’s Celestial AI.

To actually understand this deal, you have to go back to December 2025.

On December 2nd, Marvell announced it would acquire Celestial AI for $3.25B, with earn-out provisions that could push the total to $5.5B. Celestial AI is a startup that developed a technology called Photonic Fabric.

Here is what Photonic Fabric does.

Conventional Co-Packaged Optics (CPO) creates optical connections at the edge of a processor. Photonic Fabric creates optical connections from anywhere on a processor die. A single chiplet delivers 16 Tbps of bandwidth, which is ten times the current state-of-the-art 1.6T port. More importantly, this architecture allows compute and memory to be disaggregated. The physical constraint that forces GPUs and HBM to live in the same package gets broken by optics. As Marvell put it, “the shift from copper to optical already happened between racks. The next step is within the rack, within the system, and eventually within the package.”

With this acquisition, Marvell added a scale-up layer (GPU-to-GPU connectivity) to a portfolio that previously covered scale-out (Ethernet/Inphi DSP) and scale-across (DCI). Without it, Marvell had no way into NVIDIA’s NVLink-controlled territory.

Three months after the Celestial AI deal closed, NVIDIA wrote Marvell a $2B check.

My read: NVIDIA did not invest in Marvell. NVIDIA bought access to Celestial AI’s Photonic Fabric. By pulling Marvell’s custom XPUs into its ecosystem through NVLink Fusion, NVIDIA simultaneously brought next-generation optical scale-up technology inside its own walls.

The UALink Dimension

This deal also has an ecosystem sovereignty angle. AMD, Broadcom, Intel, Google, Meta, Microsoft, and AWS are all backing a competing open interconnect standard called UALink, a consortium that is essentially “everyone except NVIDIA.”

Hyperscalers are pushing it as an escape route from NVLink’s lock-in. From NVIDIA’s perspective, it’s the scenario where its own ecosystem fragments.

NVLink Fusion is the answer. “You want to use custom ASICs? Fine. Use them on our platform.” It embraces competitors’ chips while drawing them inside NVIDIA’s gravitational field. Marvell was chosen because Marvell has the deepest technical bench in optical DSPs and silicon photonics, and the Celestial AI acquisition made that case even stronger.

FinancialContent’s analysis captures it well

“NVIDIA is internalizing semi-custom infrastructure through NVLink Fusion while converting potential rivals into partners. Marvell went from potential competitor to the critical bridge between NVIDIA’s ecosystem and the custom ASIC world.”

Section 3: The Three Layers of NVIDIA’s Optical Empire

Past the individual deals, here is NVIDIA’s optical architecture decomposed into three layers.

Layer 1: Scale-Out (rack-to-rack) —> Already Secured

This is the segment connecting racks within a datacenter. The copper-to-optical transition here is already done. Pluggable transceivers at 800G and 1.6T are mainstream. NVIDIA’s Spectrum-X Ethernet and Quantum-X InfiniBand switches handle this tier.

NVIDIA’s move in this layer was CPO:

Placing pluggable transceivers directly onto the switch ASIC package. NVIDIA’s own data puts the power efficiency gain at 3.5x versus pluggable. The COUPE (Compact Universal Photonic Engine)-based CPO revealed at GTC 2025 is scheduled to ship from 2026 to 2027.

Who makes the lasers for that CPO? Lumentum and Coherent. That is why NVIDIA put $4B into those two companies and layered multi-billion purchase commitments on top.

Lumentum’s R300 OCS is also central here. The 300x300 port optical circuit switch claims a 65% reduction in total network power and 5 to 10x lower latency in a 100,000-XPU cluster. Samples are already in the hands of multiple hyperscalers and cloud builders.

Layer 1 summary:

NVIDIA has this tier essentially wrapped up. Coherent for SiPh, Lumentum for lasers and OCS, TSMC COUPE for packaging.

Layer 2: Scale-Up (within the rack, GPU-to-GPU) —> Currently Being Built

This is the hottest front right now.

NVIDIA’s current NVL72 uses copper NVLink to connect GPUs within a single rack. Seventy-two GPUs, copper, one rack. The problem is that copper hits physical limits above 800 Gbps: maximum 2-meter transmission distance, mandatory retimers, and rapidly escalating power consumption.

The next-generation Rubin architecture needs bandwidth that copper cannot deliver. This is where optical scale-up interconnects become necessary. And the most advanced technology in this space is Celestial AI’s Photonic Fabric, now owned by Marvell. Marvell has announced that it will combine Celestial AI’s optical scale-up interconnect with its UALink scale-up switch roadmap to build what it calls “the industry’s first all-optical scale-up solution.”

This is why NVIDIA put $2B into Marvell. The first-generation semi-custom reference design combining Marvell’s 1.6T optical interconnect with NVIDIA’s next-generation Rubin GPU architecture is expected in Q3 2026. Full revenue contribution from Photonic Fabric is projected for late 2028, with a $1B annual run-rate target by end of 2029.

Layer 2 summary:

Marvell (Celestial AI Photonic Fabric) plus NVIDIA NVLink Fusion is the core axis for optical scale-up.

Layer 3: In-Package (within the chip) —> A Future Bet

This is the most forward-looking layer. The ultimate goal is to replace the connections inside the GPU die, or between the die and HBM, with optical.

One point that’s easy to confuse here: the products Ayar Labs and TSMC COUPE are shipping starting in 2026 are “CPO optical engines that attach to switches.” This is actually an evolution of Layer 1 (scale-out) — moving pluggable transceivers onto the switch package, not putting optics inside the GPU die.

However, this CPO technology becomes the foundation for Layer 3. Ayar Labs’ TeraPHY chiplet provides optical I/O that sits directly on the processor substrate. The first generation starts as switch-side CPO, but the same technology progressively migrates toward the GPU. In March 2026, Ayar Labs closed a $500M funding round with both NVIDIA and AMD participating, at a $3.75B valuation. TSMC’s COUPE platform serves as the manufacturing infrastructure for this technology.

Lightmatter is also worth watching. They’ve developed the Passage M1000, a 3D photonic interposer claiming 114 Tbps bandwidth. Valued at $4.4B with $822M raised to date.

To summarize the timeline:

Scale-out CPO (2026–2027, Layer 1 evolution) → Scale-up optical (2028–2029, Layer 2) → true in-package optical (2029+, Layer 3).

The closer the connection you’re replacing with optics, the higher the technical difficulty — which is why the higher the layer number, the later it arrives.

Section 4: How NVIDIA’s Investment Formula Actually Works

Section 2 mentioned Lumentum’s Greensboro fab acquisition in one line. Now let’s actually unpack it. This is where NVIDIA’s investment mechanics become visible.

NVIDIA $2B → Lumentum → Greensboro Fab

NVIDIA’s Lumentum investment was announced March 2nd. Lumentum’s Greensboro acquisition was announced March 26th. Twenty-four days apart.

Go back to the original March 2nd investment statement: “NVIDIA is investing $2 billion in Lumentum to support R&D, future capacity and operations as the company builds out its U.S.-based manufacturing capabilities in a new fab.” The new fab was already explicitly named. Then in the Greensboro announcement, Lumentum referenced “previously announced strategic agreements.”

The fab acquisition was not a sudden decision triggered by the NVIDIA investment. The NVIDIA $2B package was designed with this fab as a prerequisite. Negotiations with Qorvo’s facility were almost certainly already in progress before the $2B deal was signed.

The Greensboro facility is 240,000 square feet. Lumentum will use it to produce CW and UHP laser chips from InP wafer inputs, with mass production targeted for mid-2028. NVIDIA VP of Operations Debora Shoquist noted that “stable access to high-performance optical components is essential.” The announcement included plans for hundreds of millions in additional investment and over 400 American manufacturing jobs.

Once this fab reaches full production, Lumentum’s laser manufacturing capacity expands substantially. And a significant portion of that expanded capacity will already be committed to NVIDIA’s multi-billion purchase agreement.

The Greensboro Fab Has Its Own Upstream Bottleneck

The Greensboro fab makes laser chips. Laser chips require InP (Indium Phosphide) wafer substrates as raw material. Who makes those?

Sumitomo Electric (Japan, approximately 60% market share) and AXT (U.S.-listed but manufactured in Beijing, approximately 35%). These two companies supply over 95% of global InP substrates. The market is in severe shortage: 2025 demand at roughly 2 million units against production capacity of 600,000. A 70% supply gap. In February 2025, China enacted export controls on indium-related compounds including InP, adding further instability to substrate supply.

NVIDIA is spending $6B to build a Western-based optical supply chain, but the InP wafers going into the Greensboro fab are 60% from Japan and 35% from China. The top of the chain is still exposed.

NVIDIA $2B → Coherent → InP Substrate Vertical Integration

The answer to this bottleneck is already in motion. Coherent, a recipient of the NVIDIA $2B investment, is simultaneously the world’s first company building a 6-inch InP wafer production line, supported by $33M in CHIPS Act funding to develop 150mm InP substrate capacity.

If NVIDIA’s $2B accelerates this vertical integration, Coherent ends up producing not just laser chips but the InP substrates that feed them. Lumentum could move in the same direction.

The Investment Formula

These two cases reveal a pattern.

Investment → manufacturing infrastructure expansion → NVIDIA becomes the preferential customer for the expanded capacity.

These three steps are a single package. And this package does not stop at one supply chain layer. It repeats as you go upstream. From lasers to substrates. Potentially from substrates to raw materials.

Once you understand this formula, reading future NVIDIA investments becomes more predictable.

When NVIDIA writes a $2B check to a company, expect a manufacturing expansion announcement from that company within weeks, with much of the expanded capacity already committed to NVIDIA. Conversely, if a supply chain company that NVIDIA has not yet invested in suddenly announces a major manufacturing expansion, that could be a signal for where the next check is headed.

If you followed the logic to this point, a question naturally surfaces. NVIDIA’s $2B into Marvell is not the end of the chain either. Marvell has its own supply chain to secure and expand. The company at the end of that chain is what the next sections pursue.

Section 5: The Gaps in NVIDIA’s Optical Stack, and Where the Next Checks Go

There’s one number we need to address first.

Currently, about 60% of NVIDIA’s transceiver volume comes from Chinese companies. Innolight and Eoptolink have captured the majority of incremental 800G orders.

Knowing this, NVIDIA’s $6B optical investment reads differently. Coherent (US), Lumentum (US), Marvell (US), Scintil (France). All Western companies. Not a single Chinese company among them.

This is not a coincidence. With US-China semiconductor tensions escalating, NVIDIA’s 60% dependence on Chinese optical supply chains is a structural risk. Under current “substantial transformation” trade rules, modules made by Innolight in Thailand can avoid tariffs. But what if US policy shifts from country-of-manufacture to an entity-based framework? At that moment, 60% of supply is at risk.

NVIDIA’s $6B optical investment is not just about securing technology. It’s about building a Western-based alternative supply chain from the ground up.

Layering this context onto NVIDIA investment formula

In Section 4, we derived NVIDIA’s investment formula:

invest → expand manufacturing → secure priority access to expanded capacity.

And we confirmed this formula repeats layer by layer across the supply chain.

Add the China risk axis to this, and the criteria for the next investment target narrow to two conditions: a company sitting in a gap in the supply chain, and Western-based.

Here’s what’s already been filled: lasers (Lumentum, Coherent), InP substrates (Coherent vertical integration), silicon photonics (Coherent), optical DSP (Marvell), Photonic Fabric (Marvell/Celestial AI), WDM laser (Scintil), OCS (Lumentum, nEye), optical I/O chiplets (Ayar Labs). All Western companies.

So where are the remaining gaps? Let’s examine them one by one against these two conditions.

Gap 1: Optical Module Assembly — Fabrinet (NASDAQ: FN)

NVIDIA has secured lasers and SiPh.

Who assembles those components into finished modules?

Currently about 60% goes to Chinese manufacturers Innolight and Eoptolink. NVIDIA’s own production line (15 to 20% of volume) is assembled by Fabrinet (Thailand) in a co-development arrangement with TFC Optical Communications (Chinese-listed, 300394.SZ). The rest comes from Western companies like Coherent and Lumentum.

The problem is visible. NVIDIA is spending $6B to build a Western supply chain, but the module assembly step is 60% Chinese, and even the design partner for NVIDIA’s own production line is a Chinese company. Can this structure survive the CPO era?

This is where Fabrinet’s strategic value rises. Fabrinet is a Thailand-based, non-Chinese contract manufacturer. It is the sole source assembler for NVIDIA’s own transceiver production and sits outside the China risk zone. NVIDIA’s stated goal of pushing its own production share above 50% in the 1.6T era is, read in reverse, a commitment to reducing Chinese ODM dependency below 50%.

Fabrinet is currently building Building 10 in Chonburi, Thailand. Two million square feet, $3B in capacity. At OFC 2026, CEO Seamus Grady called CPO and OCS “very exciting areas” and confirmed the company is extending its silicon photonics precision packaging capabilities into CPO. The AWS relationship is also expanding, with a target quarterly run rate of $200M.

The core inference: apply the Lumentum-to-Greensboro pattern.

If NVIDIA wants to scale up Western-based module assembly, it needs to secure Fabrinet’s capacity. A direct $2B equity stake? Fabrinet has consistently positioned itself as a pure-play contract manufacturer that never makes its own products, so a long-term volume agreement seems more likely than a stock purchase. But a meaningful share of Building 10’s $3B capacity is probably already committed to NVIDIA and AWS. As China risk becomes more real, Fabrinet’s strategic value only increases.

Market cap roughly $30B. Revenue up 36% year-over-year. The only publicly traded company sitting at the intersection of NVIDIA’s optical self-manufacturing expansion and Chinese supply chain diversification simultaneously.

Gap 2: The Analog Core — MACOM Technology (NASDAQ: MTSI)

An optical module contains more than lasers and SiPh. It needs a driver to convert electrical signals to optical, and a TIA (Transimpedance Amplifier) to convert optical signals back to electrical. This is the analog semiconductor layer.

Here is a striking fact: MACOM is currently the sole supplier of drivers and TIAs for NVIDIA’s own 1.6T optical module production. The GB200 system alone incorporates four MACOM products: linear equalizers for 1.6T/800G ACC, TIAs and drivers for 800G LPO modules, and drivers for 1.6T DSP modules.

Why MACOM instead of Marvell?

Marvell also has competitive driver/TIA technology, but Marvell bundles those components with its own DSPs. NVIDIA uses its own DSPs, so it needs drivers and TIAs that come independent of someone else’s DSP. MACOM fits that requirement. And now that NVIDIA has formally tied Marvell into NVLink Fusion as a strategic partner, asking Marvell for standalone driver/TIA components while rejecting their bundled DSPs gets even more awkward. MACOM’s independent position actually strengthened with this announcement.

The quantitative case for why the NVIDIA investment formula applies to MACOM: NVIDIA’s current self-production share of transceivers is 15 to 20%.

The 1.6T target is above 50%. That is roughly a 3x increase in MACOM volume. Add the China dependency angle. The volume moving away from Innolight and Eoptolink has to go somewhere. It goes to NVIDIA’s own production line, which runs on Fabrinet (assembly) and MACOM (drivers/TIAs). MACOM does not scale, and NVIDIA’s China diversification strategy does not work. The entire de-China strategy is contingent on MACOM’s capacity.

MACOM is a U.S.-based company, which matters in the context of NVIDIA building a Western supply chain. The structural parallel to the Lumentum situation is exact: sole supplier, demand expected to triple, capacity expansion needed, American-based. Market cap roughly $10B, which also fits the range of NVIDIA’s typical investment scale.

CPO does not eliminate the need for drivers and TIAs. They migrate into the switch ASIC package. MACOM already has a CPO driver product line in development. The pluggable-to-CPO transition is an opportunity for MACOM, not a threat.

Gap 3: SiPh Foundry — Tower Semiconductor (NASDAQ: TSEM)

NVIDIA’s investment pattern has one consistent principle: dual sourcing. Lasers went to both Lumentum and Coherent, not one. OCS went to both Lumentum’s R300 and nEye. Concentration on a single supplier is avoided.

But for SiPh foundry, TSMC COUPE is currently the only source. NVIDIA’s CPO optical engine, Ayar Labs’ next-generation TeraPHY. Everything rides on COUPE. If TSMC stops, there is no fallback.

Tower Semiconductor is the natural candidate for a second source. An Israel-based independent specialty foundry, Tower and GlobalFoundries are the two main non-TSMC SiPh foundry players. Tower’s Q3 2025 SiPh revenue was approximately $52M, up 70% year-over-year. Full-year 2025 SiPh revenue guidance is above $220M, with a Q4 annualized run rate target above $320M.

One fact stands out.

Tower is currently spending $650M of its own money to triple SiPh output by mid-2026. Compare to the Lumentum pattern: Lumentum was already in advanced negotiations for the Greensboro fab before NVIDIA’s $2B was announced publicly. The NVIDIA investment came in as an accelerant to an expansion already underway. Tower’s $650M self-funded build-out could be at exactly that stage, just not yet connected to NVIDIA capital.

Intel attempted a $5.4B acquisition of Tower in 2022, which collapsed in 2023 when Chinese regulatory approval never came. Tower survived as an independent company. Had Intel succeeded, NVIDIA would have had no access to Tower’s foundry. The failed Intel acquisition left Tower on the table as an open option.

Tower currently handles CPO manufacturing for the Broadcom ecosystem. As an independent company, it can serve both sides. If NVIDIA pulls Tower in as a second SiPh foundry source, that is the same dual-sourcing logic applied to a new layer.

The Marvell variable adds another angle. Celestial AI’s Photonic Fabric also needs SiPh fabrication. Marvell cannot rely exclusively on TSMC for that. As NVIDIA’s $2B flows through Marvell into Photonic Fabric production scale-up, Tower could become one of the manufacturing partners. In that case, Tower captures benefit from both the direct NVIDIA ecosystem and the Marvell ecosystem simultaneously.

The scenario: NVIDIA strategic investment in Tower plus preferential SiPh foundry capacity access. Tower’s $650M expansion gets capital reinforcement, NVIDIA gets a TSMC COUPE backup. Market cap roughly $6B, well within NVIDIA’s investment range.

GlobalFoundries (GFS) is worth naming in the same context. The Fotonix SiPh platform already manufactures for Ayar Labs, PsiQuantum, and Ranovus, and GFS is U.S.-based which removes the China risk. The constraint is that GFS capacity is already tight in multiple areas, making it harder to carve out NVIDIA-dedicated volume compared to Tower.

Gap 4: WDM Ecosystem Expansion

NVIDIA’s investment in Scintil signals that WDM CPO is on the roadmap. First-generation CPO is single-wavelength. Second-generation is multi-wavelength WDM, which increases bandwidth density and improves OCS compatibility.

The Marvell connection runs through here too. Photonic Fabric’s first generation focuses on scale-up interconnects. A higher-bandwidth successor will likely integrate WDM. NVIDIA investing simultaneously in Scintil (WDM lasers) and Marvell (Photonic Fabric) may not be a coincidence. It could be a designed convergence.

WDM CPO requires multi-wavelength lasers (Scintil: secured) and per-wavelength modulators and photodetectors. The technology to watch is TFLN (Thin-Film Lithium Niobate) modulators, which are faster and more power-efficient than silicon-based alternatives.

A notable connection: POET Technologies is jointly developing 400G-per-lane TFLN modulators for integration into its Optical Interposer platform in a project with Quantum Computing Inc., targeting a 3.2 Tbps optical engine. This is exactly the kind of TFLN integration that WDM CPO needs, and it is built on a platform that can pair with Scintil’s WDM lasers.

Follow the chain:

NVIDIA → Scintil (WDM laser) → second-generation CPO → TFLN modulator required → POET Optical Interposer as the TFLN integration platform. The Scintil investment opens another path back to POET.

Gap 5: Optical I/O Chiplet — The Next Front Line

Ayar Labs ($3.75B valuation) is functionally in the “secured” column after NVIDIA and AMD both participated in its $500M March 2026 funding round. But this is not the end of the story.

Look at the other side. Lightmatter ($4.4B valuation, $822M total raised) has not received NVIDIA investment. Google GV is among its investors. Google makes its own TPUs. Google competes with NVIDIA.

This dynamic echoes the NVLink vs. UALink structure at the optical I/O chiplet layer.

The scenario: Lightmatter’s Passage 3D photonic interposer gets adopted as the interconnect for next-generation Google TPUs. It becomes the UALink coalition’s optical I/O standard in partnership with Broadcom’s CPO. NVIDIA then needs Ayar Labs fully locked down, not just as a participant in a funding round but as a committed supply chain partner.

NVIDIA moving beyond the $500M round to a Lumentum/Coherent-style $2B strategic investment plus purchase commitment is conceivable, especially at the moment Ayar transitions to TSMC COUPE manufacturing. NVIDIA is already TSMC’s deepest customer relationship. A reference design combining Ayar chiplets and NVIDIA GPUs on COUPE could become the “CUDA moment” for optical I/O.

For investors: Ayar Labs is not publicly traded, so direct exposure is not available. Indirect exposure exists through GlobalFoundries, which manufactures first-generation TeraPHY. GFS also serves PsiQuantum and Ranovus, so the SiPh foundry demand thesis holds regardless of Ayar specifically. On the other side, Lightmatter’s L200/L200X uses UCIe SerDes from Alphawave Semi (LSE: AWE), which is one indirect proxy for the competing ecosystem.

When Ayar Labs and Lightmatter eventually IPO, probably in 2027 to 2028, the optical investment theme gets another major catalyst. NVIDIA potentially adding to its Ayar stake at IPO is a reasonable expectation.

The Full Map: What Comes Next

Fabrinet (FN): Sole source module assembler, Thailand-based non-Chinese alternative. Reducing Chinese ODM share from 60% below 50% requires Fabrinet capacity. Building 10’s $3B in capacity is the central variable.

MACOM (MTSI): Sole supplier for NVIDIA’s 1.6T self-production. U.S.-based analog semiconductor. Direct beneficiary of optical self-manufacturing scale-up. The most structurally similar candidate to the Lumentum strategic investment plus purchase commitment pattern.

Tower Semiconductor (TSEM): SiPh foundry second source. Serves both the NVIDIA and Broadcom ecosystems. Independent company unconstrained by either camp. $650M self-funded expansion already underway.

GlobalFoundries (GFS): Non-TSMC SiPh foundry. Key manufacturing partner for Ayar Labs and other optical I/O chiplet companies. U.S.-based.

POET (POET): TFLN modulator integration opening the Scintil connection. Malaysian manufacturing base, entirely outside the Chinese supply chain. Six separate scenarios, analyzed in Section 6. Every one of them remains valid.

And above all of these gaps, the question from Section 4 remains open.

NVIDIA’s $2B went into Marvell. Marvell holds Celestial AI. If the investment formula holds, Marvell will use that capital to reinforce its own supply chain. Who is at the end of that chain? Section 6 traces it.

Scenarios: Where POET Goes From Here

Disclaimer: The following is the author’s speculation and reasoning based on publicly available information, and should not be taken as confirmed fact. This is intended as reference material only, not as a basis for investment decisions.

These are analytical scenarios based on available facts, not confirmed outcomes.

Scenario A: Marvell acquires or makes a strategic investment in POET

Recall the investment formula. NVIDIA $2B into Lumentum, 24 days later the Greensboro InP fab gets acquired. Capital flowing into supply chain reinforcement in rapid succession.

Apply that formula to Marvell. NVIDIA $2B flows into Marvell, Marvell uses that capital to strengthen the Photonic Fabric supply chain by acquiring or investing in its key component supplier. That supplier is POET.

The logic is direct. Marvell spent $3.25B on Celestial AI and the Photonic Fabric technology that came with it. That technology’s external light source comes from POET.

Lumentum acquired a fab to vertically integrate its laser supply after receiving the NVIDIA investment. Marvell could execute the same playbook with POET to secure its ELS supply chain. POET’s market cap in the $600M range is entirely manageable for Marvell. Short of a full acquisition, a purchase commitment plus preferential capacity access of the kind in the Lumentum deal is also possible.

Probability: Medium.

Marvell completing its Celestial AI integration and the visibility of Photonic Fabric production scale-up in 2027 to 2028 is the likely timing window. Though the Lumentum precedent shows this can happen faster than expected. NVIDIA’s $2B entered Marvell only days ago.

Scenario B: Coherent acquires POET

A scenario several Seeking Alpha analysts have raised. Coherent has silicon photonics and laser technology but lacks a wafer-scale optical engine integration platform like POET’s Optical Interposer. Acquiring POET gives Coherent laser plus SiPh plus optical interposer, a complete optical stack. The NVIDIA $2B gives Coherent substantial acquisition capital.

Probability: Medium to High.

Scenario C: NVIDIA makes a direct strategic investment in POET

An extension of the $2B pattern. POET’s market cap makes a $2B investment oversized, but a smaller strategic investment plus purchase commitment structure is achievable. Potentially undisclosed, like the Scintil investment.

Probability: Low to Medium.

NVIDIA accessing POET indirectly through Marvell or Coherent may be more capital-efficient than a direct investment. But NVIDIA’s Scintil investment shows it will write checks to small companies when the technology is strategically necessary.

Scenario D: The Back Door Through Kyber

The most discussed rumor in community circles.

FIT (Foxconn Interconnect Technology) has been named in some reports as a major module supplier for NVIDIA’s next-generation Kyber rack. According to Seeking Alpha analysis, FIT’s 102.4T ELSFP module specifications align precisely with POET Starlight ELS. If the Kyber rack uses ELSFP modules, FIT builds those modules, and POET Starlight goes inside them, then POET enters NVIDIA’s infrastructure without a direct investment or acquisition.

NVIDIA does not approve supply chains it is unaware of. If FIT’s ELSFP module gets confirmed in the Kyber rack with POET Starlight inside, that is effectively NVIDIA’s indirect endorsement of the technology.

Probability: Medium.

FIT’s Kyber selection is still at the rumor stage, but FIT and POET’s partnership is a documented fact. Confirmation of this scenario would be the single largest near-term catalyst for POET.

Scenario E: The Anti-NVIDIA Coalition’s Counter-Move

Not all scenarios originate from the NVIDIA side.

As NVIDIA locks up Coherent, Lumentum, and Marvell optical volume, Broadcom, AMD, and hyperscalers with custom AI ASICs (Google TPU, Amazon Trainium, Meta MTIA) find themselves with fewer supply chain options. They also need optical interconnects. They also need lasers. They also need optical engines.

POET’s partner ecosystem sits entirely outside the NVIDIA ecosystem. LITEON, Lessengers, Luxshare, FIT: none of these are aligned with NVIDIA’s primary supply chain. As NVIDIA tightens its optical lock-up, non-NVIDIA players need alternative supply chains. POET is positioned to serve as that alternative.

Probability: Low to Medium.

Scenario F: NVIDIA’s $2B Flows Through Marvell to POET

The most realistic near-term scenario, and the one that the March 31st NVIDIA-Marvell announcement directly improved the probability of.

Here is why.

NVIDIA’s $2B into Marvell is not just capital. It is an official certification of Marvell as an NVLink Fusion partner. When Marvell walks into AWS or Google with Photonic Fabric, the pitch now includes: “NVIDIA invested $2B in us and integrated this technology into NVLink Fusion.” That changes the sales dynamic entirely.

The AWS connection was already visible. When Celestial AI’s acquisition was announced, AWS VP Dave Brown issued a direct public endorsement. Add the NVIDIA name and hyperscaler adoption decisions accelerate.

Faster adoption means faster volume orders for POET Starlight.

The magnitude shift matters. When Celestial AI was a startup, POET received small-lot orders. POET’s current early production orders are at the $5.6M level. When the same technology flows through Marvell’s $100B-plus revenue pipeline to hyperscalers, orders operate at a completely different scale.

Marvell is targeting a $1B annual Photonic Fabric run-rate by end of 2029. Add NVLink Fusion deployments and the total addressable volume exceeds Marvell’s standalone projection. If POET’s ELS captures even 5 to 10% of that, the number is in a different category than $5.6M.

The timing accelerant is the March 31st announcement. The first-generation semi-custom reference design was already announced for Q3 2026. Initial production orders will precede that by some months.

Probability: Medium to High.

The most plausible near-term path, tied to the Photonic Fabric production ramp timeline of 2028 to 2029, though initial volume activity begins earlier.

POET Risks

Fair accounting requires the risks.

POET is still in early commercialization. Q3 2025 early production orders were at the $5.6M level, with quarterly net losses of $9.4M. Cash position of roughly $430M provides adequate runway, but meaningful revenue scale takes time. Stock volatility is extreme.

The Celestial AI relationship’s continuation and strengthening post-Marvell acquisition is not guaranteed. Marvell could develop an internal ELS or find a different supplier. Production-scale yield and performance have not yet been validated at volume. Once again, all of this is purely my own speculation.

Despite these risks, the documented POET-to-Celestial AI-to-Marvell-to-NVIDIA supply chain connection and the structural InP efficiency advantage are facts. Whether that foundation supports one of the six scenarios above is the investment judgment question.

Conclusion

What NVIDIA Is Building Is an “Optical CUDA”

Take one step back and look at the full picture.

NVIDIA’s GPU market dominance was not built by the GPU chip itself. It was built by CUDA, a software ecosystem that made leaving NVIDIA’s platform increasingly costly for developers. CUDA is the foundation of NVIDIA’s $3T market cap.

What NVIDIA is doing in optics today follows the same pattern.

Coherent and Lumentum to secure lasers and SiPh. Marvell and Celestial AI to bring scale-up Photonic Fabric into the fold. TSMC COUPE to standardize manufacturing infrastructure. Scintil, nEye, and other smaller investments to build a next-generation technology pipeline.

Wrap all of it inside NVLink Fusion as the platform, and you have an optical interconnect ecosystem. When operating inside this ecosystem becomes the most efficient way to build chips, networks, and AI clusters, customers find it increasingly difficult to leave.

The next war in semiconductors is the war over light. As every connection in the datacenter transitions from copper to optical, who controls the supply chain for that transition will determine the semiconductor ecosystem for the next decade.
