1. What Burn-In Is, and Why It Matters Again Now

The semiconductor test flow after wafer sort, simplified, looks like this.

Step 1: Wafer fabrication

The foundry prints hundreds or thousands of identical die across a silicon wafer. Everything stays on the wafer until the individual chips are singulated later in the process.

Step 2: Wafer probe test

Probes make contact with the pads on each die while it is still on the wafer, checking for basic functional operation. Completely dead die and obvious electrical failures get caught here. Probe test is optimized for speed and electrical verification, though, which means it is not designed to catch defects that look fine today but will fail under real-world operating stress over time.

Step 3: Burn-in test

Elevated temperature and voltage stress are applied to the chip for a defined period, intentionally accelerating failures that would otherwise only show up later in the field. Think of it as weeding out parts that pass today but would not survive actual operation.

Step 4: Packaging

Die that pass screening go into the package. This stage has become significantly more complex as CoWoS, HBM stacking, and chiplet integration have grown as a share of the mix.

Step 5: Final (package) test

The packaged part gets tested again to confirm it is ready to ship. This step checks for any issues introduced during assembly and validates end-product functionality.

When I was doing yield improvement work, there was a problem I ran into constantly while digging through wafer sort data. Die that passed probe would fail at final test after packaging. Or they would pass final test and come back as customer returns (called as RMA). Failure analysis usually pointed to the same root cause: latent defects.

What is Latent defect?

A hidden defect that does not surface during initial test but converts to an actual failure over time when the device is exposed to heat and voltage stress in real operating conditions.

Classic examples include weak gate oxide, voids inside vias, or metal lines vulnerable to electromigration. Under short room-temperature testing, these all look fine. But under accumulated thermal and electrical stress, they fail, sometimes within hours, sometimes after hundreds of hours.

Burn-in test can catch a significant portion of these defects before they reach the field. It has always mattered, but historically the emphasis was concentrated in automotive and other high-reliability segments. Applying burn-in to every chip is expensive and time-consuming, and for the AP products I was working with, the more common approach was to run burn-in reactively: reproduce a field failure in the lab, trace it back to a process issue, and drive an improvement.

_ The situation is different now._

Blackwell-class AI GPU s are already operating above 1 kW, and the trajectory points higher. The problem is not just higher power consumption. Higher power means higher current density and more thermal stress inside the die. The faster a latent defect converts to a real failure under operating conditions, the more valuable early screening becomes. That dynamic makes burn-in qualitatively more important for AI accelerators than it was for previous product generations.

Silicon photonics adds another dimension. Here, the case for burn-in is not just about electrical latent defects. Photonic devices that include a transmitter go through an output drift period early in operation, where optical power and wavelength are not stable. Aging the device under controlled current and temperature stress accomplishes two things at once: die with latent defects fail out, and the surviving die have their optical characteristics stabilized through the drift period. For conventional logic, burn-in is purely a screening process.

For silicon photonics, it is simultaneously a screening process and a pre-production stabilization step.

That dual function makes burn-in qualitatively more embedded in the silicon photonics manufacturing flow.

Advanced packaging raises the stakes further. When a defective die ends up inside a CoWoS package or a hybrid-bonded 3D stack, the failure is no longer a single die loss. It can take down the entire package or the entire stack. As integration moves toward CPO architectures that combine optical die and switch ASICs in a single package, a defect in one photonic die can become a system-level loss. Sorting known-good die before packaging is no longer just a cost-saving measure. It is the mechanism that protects yield and unit economics across the full assembly chain.

This is where WLBI, wafer level burn-in, comes in. It is the capability that lets manufacturers screen for known-good die at the wafer stage, before packaging locks in the cost. And beyond the wafer stage, there is PPBI, packaged part burn-in, which applies burn-in stress to the completed device after packaging.

AEHR currently offers solutions in both WLBI and PPBI, and holds a leading position in both AI processor and silicon photonics markets.

What makes the business model structurally interesting is that revenue does not stop at equipment shipment. The contactor is a consumable that wears out and must be replaced as long as production runs, creating a razor-and-blade dynamic.

In FY2024, contactor revenue of $37.6M already exceeded system revenue of $24.2M.

Burn-in is not a new process. But the economics of burn-in have changed in a way that is structural, not cyclical. AEHR is the company that got there first.

2. How Silicon Photonics, Hybrid Bonding, and 3D Stacking Amplify Burn-In Demand

Silicon Photonics: Why the Case for Burn-In Is More Direct Here

Silicon photonics ICs integrate optical components such as lasers, modulators, photodetectors, and waveguides directly on silicon wafers. They are the core building blocks of 800G, 1.6T, and 3.2T optical transceivers in AI data centers, and the foundation of CPO and optical I/O architectures.

The reasons these devices need burn-in are different from conventional logic. Breaking it down by component:

Laser (light source). The most sensitive element in a silicon photonics transceiver. Whether the laser source is an external component such as an EML or CW laser, or a III-V material integrated heterogeneously on the chip, optical power fluctuates during early operation. Wavelength shifts with temperature. This is a physical property of lasers, and there is an aging period during which the device stabilizes. If that aging does not happen at the wafer level before packaging, finished devices can end up with optical power outside spec. In AEHR’s FOX-XP system, this aging process runs concurrently with burn-in. Current and temperature stress eliminates die with latent defects, and the surviving die emerge with stable optical characteristics.

Modulator (electro-optic modulator). Mach-Zehnder interferometer-based modulators drift at their bias point as a function of temperature and time. At data rates above 100G per lane, that drift directly degrades eye diagram quality. Burn-in stress applied before production lets manufacturers sort out parts where the bias drift has not yet stabilized.

Photodetector (light receiver). Germanium photodetectors have dark current, the leakage current that flows without any light input, that is highly sensitive to temperature. Burn-in can identify die where dark current is abnormally elevated before they make it into a package. Only die that maintain in-spec dark current after high-temperature stress make the cut as KGD.

Waveguide (optical waveguide). Waveguides are passive structures and are not themselves the target of active burn-in. But when tested on the wafer alongside active components like lasers and modulators, lot-to-lot variation in insertion loss and coupling efficiency becomes visible. Waveguides are passive structures and are not themselves the target of active burn-in. But when tested on the wafer alongside active components such as lasers and modulators, variation in insertion loss and coupling efficiency can be evaluated as part of an integrated screening flow that uses both electrical and optical test resources

AEHR’s 2021 materials reference “photonics transmitters stabilized” as a key output of the system, and the 2025 Annual Report describes WaferPak and DiePak as the interface that connects FOX system electrical and optical test resources to the device. FOX-XP can deliver independent current to up to 8,192 photonic devices per wafer simultaneously, running burn-in and stabilization as a single integrated step.

From an investment standpoint, silicon photonics may actually be the better recurring revenue driver for AEHR compared to AI processors.

AI processor die counts per wafer are lower and contactor prices are higher, but design cycles tend to be longer. Silicon photonics has thousands of die per wafer, and the pace of transceiver generation changes from 400G to 800G to 1.6T means device designs are updated frequently, driving higher WaferPak contactor replacement rates. My read is that silicon photonics represents a more predictable and durable source of repeat revenue for AEHR.

Hybrid Bonding and 3D Stacking: The Economics of Known-Good Die Have Changed

Hybrid bonding is one of the most consequential technology shifts in semiconductor manufacturing since EUV. TSMC’s SoIC-X and Intel’s Foveros Direct are the primary examples. By replacing solder bumps with direct copper-to-copper bonding, interconnect pitch drops below 10 micrometers and bandwidth scales up several times over.

The reason this drives structural demand for burn-in goes beyond simple KGD cost savings.

W2W bonding: no rework option. In wafer-to-wafer hybrid bonding, two full wafers are bonded together. A defective die on one side destroys the good die facing it. If each wafer has 95% yield, the combined yield of the bonded pair drops to 90.25%. With conventional solder bumps, it was theoretically possible in extreme cases to reflow and separate die. With Cu-to-Cu direct bonding, once they are together, they stay together. Pre-bonding screening is the last opportunity to remove latent defects before the cost structure locks in. WLBI is that last gate.

D2W bonding: once stacked, individual die are inaccessible. Die-to-wafer hybrid bonding singulates individual die and bonds them one by one onto a wafer. TSMC SoIC and AMD 3D V-Cache use this approach. According to SemiAnalysis, building a single AMD 3D V-Cache requires five bonding steps. Once the 3D stack is complete, test access to internal die is essentially gone. Probing die buried in the middle of a stack is physically impossible; only indirect testing through external I/O is available. Pre-stack wafer-level screening is the only point where each die can be individually verified. That makes it both the last opportunity and the most important one.

HBM stacking: the bonding process itself generates new stress. HBM3E is at 12-hi stacking, and HBM4 is on a path toward 16-hi. Die at 30 to 50 micrometers thick, per Semiconductor Engineering, are stacked sixteen layers high. Process steps including wafer thinning and bonding annealing at 200 to 400 degrees Celsius can activate new defects in die that were previously clean. Verifying that KGD qualified by WLBI before bonding remains good after bonding may require additional burn-in screening at the stack level. Intel Foundry has already formally adopted the strategy of moving burn-in stress earlier in the flow.

The summary is that burn-in’s role in the hybrid bonding and 3D stacking era goes well beyond cost reduction insurance. Rework is off the table, so pre-bonding burn-in becomes the last gate. Post-stacking, individual die are inaccessible, so pre-stacking burn-in becomes the only individual screening opportunity. And the bonding process itself introduces new stress, creating demand for burn-in both before bonding at the wafer level and after bonding at the package level. That AEHR has FOX-XP for WLBI and Sonoma for PPBI is not incidental to this picture.

3. AEHR’s Products and Technical Moat

The Equipment Lineup

AEHR’s product portfolio has two main pillars: the FOX platform for wafer level burn-in and the Sonoma platform for packaged part burn-in.

FOX-XP. AEHR’s flagship system. It handles up to 18 300mm wafers simultaneously using independent internal test slots called Blades. Each Blade has its own power supply, thermal control, and test resources. The platform spans SiC, GaN, silicon photonics, AI processors, and flash memory. CEO commentary on earnings calls has put the system price at $4 million to $5 million per unit.

FOX-NP. The lower-cost entry model. It uses the same Blade architecture as the FOX-XP, which means customers who evaluate on the NP can scale to the XP without retooling. It functions as the primary entry point for new customer relationships.

WaferPak Contactor. The full-wafer contactor that interfaces with FOX systems. A single touchdown contacts tens of thousands of pads across the entire wafer simultaneously. Because WaferPak design is customized to each device layout, a design change or end of contactor life means a new purchase. This is the blade in the razor-and-blade model, and in FY2024 contactor revenue of $37.6M already exceeded system revenue of $24.2M.

DiePak Carrier. A carrier for running burn-in on singulated die or modules after wafer dicing. Each DiePak handles up to 1,024 die in parallel.

FOX WaferPak AutoAligner. Automated equipment for aligning and loading wafers into WaferPak contactors. Supports FOUP-based 300mm wafer handling and SECS/GEM factory communication. Combined with FOX-XP, it enables lights-out production burn-in without manual intervention.

Sonoma. The packaged part burn-in platform AEHR acquired through the Incal Technology acquisition in August 2024. It handles packaged AI processors, GPUs, and HPC processors at up to 2,000W per device based on FY2026 Q1 8-K filings. Multi-zone thermal management provides independent temperature control across different regions of the package. In January 2026, AEHR announced a next-generation model with integrated fully automated loader/unloader capability.

AEHR is, at present, one of the only suppliers offering both WLBI via FOX-XP and PPBI via Sonoma for AI processors, per the 2025 Annual Report. As described in Section 2, the hybrid bonding era creates burn-in demand on both sides of the bonding step. Having both platforms matters.

The FOX-XP Moat

Ultra-high power delivery. Up to 3,500W per wafer, and over 2,000A per wafer in AI processor configurations, per FY2025 Q1 8-K. This is a fundamentally different power architecture than conventional ATE.

Precision thermal control. Direct thermal conduction using high-thermal-conductivity heat transfer fluid, not forced air convection. The system controls temperature independently per wafer across a range from ambient to 150 degrees Celsius while managing the trade-off between overkill (stressing good die to failure) and underkill (missing latent defects) for high-power die.

WaferPak technical specs. Simultaneous contact across more than 50,000 pads per wafer in a single touchdown, per AEHR’s website. Investor presentation materials cite cycle life of over 100,000 contacts; the 2024 Annual Report puts usable life at two to seven years. Per-contact current capacity of up to 4A, compatibility with Al, Cu, Au, and solder ball pad materials, and support for remote voltage and ground sensing.

Universal Channel Architecture. Each channel independently supports I/O, power supply, clock, and PPMU functions. A single Blade handles up to 2,048 Universal Channels, enabling BIST and DFT-based functional test during burn-in. As a DFT engineer, this is the part I find most technically impressive. This is not a burn-in oven. It is a system that makes real-time die-level pass/fail decisions while burn-in is running.

The Sonoma Moat

Packaged part burn-in is not a new market. Advantest, Chroma, and DI Corporation have all offered PPBI equipment. What differentiates Sonoma is its design orientation toward ultra-high-power AI processor-class devices.

2,000W per device. Most existing PPBI equipment was designed for devices in the tens to hundreds of watts range. AI accelerators that exceed 1 kW in a single package require a completely different design philosophy for power delivery, thermal removal, and overcurrent protection. Sonoma was built for that operating point from the ground up.

Multi-zone thermal management. Thermal output within an AI processor package varies significantly across different die regions. A single temperature set point cannot maintain accurate burn-in conditions across the full package. Sonoma manages temperature across multiple independent zones simultaneously.

The customer relationships Incal brought. Incal Technology had direct commercial relationships with major AI chip companies before the acquisition. That customer access and the long-term roadmap visibility that comes with it are assets that are at least as valuable as the hardware. Management noted that more than ten companies visited the Fremont facility to review Sonoma after the acquisition closed.

Turnkey consumable structure. Sonoma carries the same consumable model as FOX-XP. Burn-in modules and device-specific sockets are purchased alongside the system and replenished as long as production runs. Equipment sale is not the end of the revenue relationship.

AEHR’s Moat in Silicon Photonics

AI processor WLBI is where AEHR has the clearest lead today, but as the market grows, larger players will eventually attempt to enter. The silicon photonics moat is a different kind of advantage.

Burn-in and stabilization in a single step. As covered in Section 2, silicon photonics burn-in is not purely defect screening. Optical characteristic stabilization has to happen in the same process. That requires sustained independent current delivery to each die over a multi-hour long soak. The question is how to do this economically. Soaking packaged devices one at a time after assembly does not work on cost or time. FOX-XP handles up to 18 wafers simultaneously over extended periods, driving thousands of die per wafer in parallel, and that parallelism is what makes the economics of wafer-level long soak viable. Based on publicly available information, I am not aware of another commercially visible platform with the same combination of multi-wafer capability, high power delivery, and wafer-level parallelism in this niche.

8,192 devices per wafer in parallel. AI processor die are large, so a 300mm wafer typically holds 50 to 100 of them. Silicon photonics die are small, and a single wafer can carry thousands. FOX-XP’s ability to independently drive up to 8,192 photonic devices per wafer while simultaneously running burn-in and stabilization reflects an architecture designed around that die density. General-purpose burn-in equipment cannot achieve this level of parallelism.

Power headroom for next-generation devices. As silicon photonics ICs move from 800G to 1.6T and 3.2T, integration density is rising and next-generation devices require two to four times the power of their predecessors, per AEHR 2023 and 2024 press releases. FOX-XP’s 3,500W per wafer capacity covers that trajectory. Whether competing equipment can handle this power level at the wafer level is not verifiable from public sources.

Production track record and switching cost. AEHR has been building silicon photonics WLBI production history since 2019 and 2020. The 2025 Annual Report references multiple customers, and multiple WaferPak designs are already qualified at customer fabs. Once a burn-in system is qualified into a production process, switching is extremely difficult. A new platform requires rebuilding all burn-in recipes, pass/fail criteria, and yield correlation data from scratch. That switching cost is the most durable protection for AEHR’s installed base.

4. Business Structure and Profitability

As of FY2024, AEHR's revenue mix shows a classic razor and blade model, with consumable contactors already accounting for a larger share than systems. More recently, the slowdown in SiC has put meaningful pressure on both revenue and margins.

However, FY2026 H2 bookings guidance stands at $60M to $80M, which is above AEHR's full year FY2025 revenue, and management said that most of it is tied to AI processors. In the end, the core investment question is whether AI and photonics can drive new equipment revenue growth and restore the company's damaged gross margin.

5. Market Opportunity

The silicon photonics market is projected to grow from $3.1B in 2025 to about $9.65B to $10.4B by 2030. AEHR has already secured relevant customers and follow on orders, so silicon photonics could become a meaningful revenue contributor in FY2027 and FY2028 as 800G and 1.6T production ramps.

If advanced packaging continues to expand and memory WLBI reaches volume production, AEHR's TAM could widen materially.

6. Risks

Customer concentration.

In FY2025, per the 10-K, Customer A represented 38.6% of revenue and Customer B represented 15.1%. That has improved from Customer A at 67.3% in FY2024, but concentration remains significant. More importantly, most of the new AI-related customers are still at the evaluation or early-order stage and are not yet generating the repeat revenue that defines a mature production relationship. The silicon photonics customer base beyond the lead customer also needs to demonstrate actual production ramp timing. Adding customers to the logo list and building a durable production revenue base are two different things.

Margin recovery is unconfirmed.

As detailed in Section 4, whether gross margin can recover from the current 30% range back above 40% remains an open question. A shift in revenue mix toward contactors relative to systems would support margin improvement, but the timeline for that shift is uncertain.

Bookings are not revenue.

The $60M to $80M bookings guidance is based on customer forecasts. Tariffs, macro uncertainty, or hyperscaler capex adjustments can delay realization. AEHR has previously withdrawn guidance, which is worth keeping in mind.

Valuation.

For a company currently running at a loss, that kind of valuation means the market is already pricing in a substantial portion of the FY2027 and beyond growth story. If the $60M to $80M bookings guidance converts to revenue and FY2027 clears $100M, the current price can be justified. Until that happens, the stock is running on expectations.

Competition.

AEHR is the de facto leader in commercial WLBI today, but once the TAM is large enough, M&A or internal development at a larger ATE company is inevitable. Permanent monopoly is not part of this thesis.

Conclusion

Burn-in is not a new process. It has been around for decades. What has changed is the economic logic behind it, and the change is structural.

AI processor power density has increased to the point where latent defects convert to failures faster than previous product generations. Silicon photonics adds a stabilization requirement on top of the standard screening function, creating dual demand from a single process step. Hybrid bonding and 3D stacking have expanded the cost consequence of a single defective die from the die itself to the full package or stack. All three of these are happening simultaneously, and together they are raising the economic value of wafer-level KGD screening to a level that has no real precedent.

AEHR sits at the intersection of all three. FOX-XP is a platform that is technically difficult to replicate. WaferPak provides recurring revenue throughout the production life of every design win. And the company is accumulating design wins in both AI processors and silicon photonics at the same time.

That said, the thesis still requires proof. Whether the $60M to $80M bookings guidance translates to revenue. Whether gross margin recovers. Whether evaluation-stage customers convert to volume production. Until those three things are confirmed, this remains a thesis, not a result.

The Q3 earnings on April 7 will be the first real data point.
