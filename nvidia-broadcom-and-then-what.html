1. What Cisco Is, and Why Now

For a long time, the market has thought of Cisco as a relic of the dot-com era. The company that made the routers and switches data had to pass through, holding roughly 75% of the global router market at its peak in the mid-1990s.

In March 2000, Cisco briefly became the most valuable company in the world at around $555B in market cap. The P/E was 220x. Then the bubble burst. The stock fell 89%, from $80 down to $8.60. The company survived, but the stock spent over 20 years never recovering its all-time high.

That same Cisco broke out above its dot-com era closing high in December 2025, 25 years later. On February 9, 2026, it hit an all-time high of $86.78.

What changed?

  1. Silicon One, its internally designed chip.

  2. The Acacia acquisition, which brought coherent optical technology in-house.

  3. A strategic partnership with NVIDIA.

  4. Splunk, which gave Cisco a security and data platform.

Revenue is still anchored in networking, but the Cisco of today is a company trying to own the entire connectivity layer of AI infrastructure.

Two structural shifts are creating the window.

First, the bottleneck in AI infrastructure is migrating from GPUs to the network.

You can stack as many GPUs as you want. If the fabric connecting them can’t keep up, the whole system gets throttled. Running a 128,000-GPU cluster requires three layers of high-speed interconnect: scale up within the rack, scale out between racks, and scale across between data centers. Very few companies can cover all of that with their own silicon and optics.

Second, AI infrastructure demand is spreading beyond the hyperscalers.

Sovereign clouds, neoclouds, and many enterprise customers often lack the capability to design and operate AI data center networks the way AWS or Azure does. That drives growing demand for a validated turnkey stack where chips, systems, networking, optics, security, and operational tooling come bundled together. As geopolitical tension increases, sovereign AI infrastructure demand at the national level only gets stronger.

In this environment, Cisco offers a full-stack AI infrastructure proposition built around networking and security.

2. Is Demand Actually There?

Good technology that doesn’t sell doesn’t matter. So let’s start with whether it’s selling.

Q2 FY2026 results, reported February 11

Revenue of $15.3B, up 10% year over year. Non-GAAP EPS of $1.04, up 11%. Both came in above the high end of guidance.

AI infrastructure orders from hyperscalers alone hit $2.1B in a single quarter, up sharply from $1.3B the prior quarter and roughly equal to all of FY2025’s AI orders combined. The company is guiding for total AI orders to exceed $5B in FY2026, with recognized AI infrastructure revenue exceeding $3B.

The composition of demand is worth noting. Hyperscaler AI infrastructure revenue runs roughly 60% systems, 40% optics. This isn’t a one-and-done switch sale. The optical connectivity demand that follows keeps regenerating. Systems and optics grow together in a bundled structure. At the Morgan Stanley TMT Conference on March 5, management confirmed product order growth accelerating at 18% and projected this to be the best revenue year in company history.

Beyond the hyperscalers, sovereign and enterprise momentum is real. The neocloud, sovereign, and enterprise pipeline is above $2.5B, with $350M in orders from that segment in Q2 alone. Saudi Arabia’s HUMAIN 1GW AI infrastructure JV, the UAE G42 partnership, and Australia’s Secure AI Factory are concrete examples.

I want to add a personal experience here.

Recently, a hiring manager from Cisco’s Silicon One team reached out to me directly and asked whether I would be interested in interviewing. Silicon One is Cisco’s in house networking ASIC family. It is the core silicon behind Cisco’s AI infrastructure strategy, designed to unify routers, switches, and service provider equipment under a single chip architecture.

Anyone who has prepared for a job move in the United States will know that it is actually quite rare for a hiring manager to contact you directly. That was exactly why I became curious and decided to take a short call. During that conversation, I heard several things that left a strong impression on me.

After that call, I went back and re examined Cisco’s public data from the ground up. And I came to the conclusion that the market is still missing something. A detailed breakdown of Cisco’s technical moat follows in the next section.

3. Cisco’s Technical Moat

Silicon One: The In-House Networking Chip

Cisco has been designing its own ASICs for more than 40 years. The latest result is the Silicon One G300: 102.4Tbps of bandwidth, supporting 64 ports at 1.6Tbps each. 750 switches are enough to connect 128,000 GPUs. The previous generation required 2,500.

Most networking vendors (Arista, Nokia, Dell) buy Broadcom’s Tomahawk. Cisco broke that dependency with Silicon One. Because it’s their own chip, they can optimize the whole system around it. The P4-programmable NPU means new workloads can be addressed in software. The G300’s “Intelligent Collective Networking” has every switch in the fabric sharing real-time congestion state and optimizing packet paths across the entire network. Cisco claims 33% better utilization and 28% faster job completion in their own simulations.

They have also opened the door to merchant silicon deployments. Meta has publicly discussed deploying the Cisco 8501 based on Silicon One G200. Cisco has also highlighted collaboration with Microsoft Azure around Silicon One based infrastructure.

Acacia: Five Years In, Now a Core Asset

In 2021, Cisco completed the Acacia acquisition. The amended deal value was about $4.5B, while Cisco later reported $4.983B of purchase consideration in its fiscal 2021 filings. That acquisition gave Cisco silicon photonics alongside a coherent DSP: the technology to generate light, and the brain to read it.

The product strategy built on top of that is Routed Optical Networking. Plug an Acacia optical module directly into a router port and connect data centers hundreds of kilometers apart without separate transmission equipment. Less gear, less power, less operational complexity.

In Q2 FY2026, Acacia posted its best quarter ever. Bookings grew triple digits. 800G is ramping now, and 1.6T plus a power-50%-reduced 800G LPO module are both targeting second-half shipment.

The NVIDIA Alliance: Strategic Partner, and Early Tension

In February 2025, Silicon One became the only external partner chip included in NVIDIA’s Spectrum-X platform. At GTC, NVIDIA also announced the Cisco N9100 switch with NVIDIA chips. The “Cisco Secure AI Factory with NVIDIA” is a full-stack turnkey solution bundling GPUs, networking, storage, and security together.

That’s a unique position. More broadly, Ethernet based architectures give Cisco a more neutral posture than a closed proprietary fabric, which could matter if customers want flexibility across different GPU or custom silicon roadmaps.

There is tension building, though. NVIDIA’s $4B in optical investments across Lumentum and Coherent signal they’re not planning to stay dependent on Cisco/Acacia exclusively. The G300 at 102.4Tbps is double the bandwidth of Spectrum-4 at 51.2Tbps, which means if open Ethernet expands into the scale-up domain, it starts threatening NVLink’s high-margin business. The partnership works well right now, but the larger the market gets, the wider the competitive surface becomes.

Splunk: You Can’t Sell AI Infrastructure Without Security

In March 2024, Cisco acquired Splunk for $28B, the largest acquisition in the company’s history. That deal brought in a platform that analyzes data flowing through the network and detects security threats in real time. As geopolitical tension rises, demand for sovereign AI infrastructure that comes with built-in data sovereignty and security doesn’t go away. That’s the context behind deals like Saudi HUMAIN, UAE G42, and Australia’s Secure AI Factory.

Why the Full-Stack is Structural Moat

Cisco’s real moat isn’t any individual product spec. It’s the structure: a switching ASIC (Silicon One), coherent silicon photonics and DSP (Acacia), a security and observability platform (Splunk), and the system integration capability to bundle it all together. Being able to design and deliver the entire connectivity layer of AI infrastructure, from chip to optics to security, under one roof. That combination is what gives Cisco a real structural advantage here.

The vertical integration produces concrete results. Plug an Acacia 800G pluggable directly into a G300 switch port, and because the chip and optics are designed within the same company, the G300 can process signals directly without a separate DSP, enabling 800G LPO at 50% lower power. That kind of optimization doesn’t come easily when the chip and optics are sourced from different vendors. Cisco/Acacia has shipped more than 650,000 coherent ports to date and holds roughly 25% of global deployed capacity according to Cignal AI (optical market research firm).

Where this full stack matters most is the sovereign AI market. Countries building their own AI infrastructure prefer getting everything from a single vendor rather than assembling a complex multi-vendor stack. The “Cisco Secure AI Factory with NVIDIA” is the concrete productization of this full-stack approach.

4. OFC 2026 and the Shifting Competitive Landscape

OFC 2026, which wrapped last week, gives a clear picture of where Cisco is headed. It also surfaced two developments that could change the competitive dynamics in a meaningful way.

What Cisco Showed

Start with the numbers. Cisco’s Bill Gartner, SVP/GM of Optical Systems and Optics, presented at OFC. His data: compared to traditional WAN links, AI workloads require 504x more bandwidth for scale up, 56x for scale out, and 14x for scale across. Five hundred and four times. Connectivity is now as demanding and as complex as the GPUs themselves.

Cisco’s answer was three new Acacia-based products.

The Open Transport 3000 Series is a multi-rail open line system that consolidates multiple parallel fiber pairs onto a single line card. Cisco claims 75% lower power and 80% reduction in rack space. It’s aimed squarely at hyperscaler scale-across demand: running multiple data centers as one distributed GPU cluster.

The NCS 1014 800G pluggable transponder line card delivers 12.8T of capacity in 1RU. Twice the density of the prior 400G generation, with 38% lower power.

The Acacia Bright QSFP28 100ZR 0dBm coherent pluggable draws under 6W and reaches 100 to 150km. It extends the coherent pluggable strategy that started in DCI out to the access and edge.

All three point in the same direction. Optical connectivity in AI infrastructure is expanding from inside the data center to between data centers to the edge. Cisco/Acacia is positioning Routed Optical Networking to cover the entire span. They already have more than 450 customers, including every top hyperscaler.

New Variables from the Competition

At the same OFC, and in the week just before it, two announcements came out that could shift the landscape.

First, NVIDIA committed $2B to Lumentum and another $2B to Coherent, totaling $4B in direct optical investments. At OFC, Marvell and Lumentum jointly demonstrated a rack-level optical system combining Marvell DSPs with Lumentum’s R300 Optical Circuit Switching. NVIDIA capital, plus Marvell DSPs, plus Lumentum photonics, starts looking like a competing full stack that goes head-to-head with Acacia’s silicon photonics and coherent DSP combination.

Second, on March 12, just before OFC, Arista announced the XPO (eXtra-dense Pluggable Optics) MSA. A single module at 12.8Tbps, with 204.8Tbps per OCP rack unit. Four times the front-panel density of OSFP. 45 founding members signed on, and a Microsoft VP offered public support. Volume production is 2027, but this is Arista declaring that it intends to lead the next-generation optics form factor, not just networking. That’s a direct collision with Cisco/Acacia’s OSFP-based pluggable strategy. Bechtolsheim already proved with OSFP that once you own a form factor standard, that ecosystem lasts a decade.

OFC 2026 showed Cisco both opportunity and threat at the same time.

5. Structural Risks

The more AI revenue grows, the more margin gets compressed. This is the most immediate risk. Q2 Non-GAAP gross margin came in at 67.5%, down 120 basis points from 68.7% a year ago. Q3 guidance calls for a further decline to 65.5 to 66.5%. The cause is a worse mix as AI hardware grows as a share of revenue, combined with a sharp jump in memory prices. Management described the memory price increase as “unprecedented.” The stock fell more than 10% after February earnings because of this margin shock. As AI revenue becomes a bigger part of the mix, this pressure isn’t going away.

G300 needs to ship on time. GA is scheduled for second-half 2026. Tomahawk 6 has been shipping since 2025. That’s roughly a one-year gap. Hyperscalers need 102.4Tbps-class silicon now. If Cisco slips, design wins go elsewhere.

Arista isn’t slowing down. They’re taking share in hyperscaler AI networking, EOS software quality is excellent, and the XPO MSA is a declaration that they intend to own the next-generation optics form factor too.

Tariffs and geopolitics. The Q3 guidance already bakes in tariff exposure. Tariffs and geopolitics remain a risk. Cisco has said it already reduced China related component tariff exposure by about 80 percent, and current trade policy assumptions are already embedded in guidance. The indirect effect of oil above $90 on data center power costs and customer capex sentiment is also a real factor.

6. What’s Coming Next

There are events around Cisco that the market hasn’t fully priced in yet.

The HUMAIN JV: Not a Gear Sale. A Business Partnership.

In November 2025, AMD, Cisco, and HUMAIN (under Saudi Arabia’s PIF) announced a joint venture targeting up to 1GW of AI infrastructure by 2030. Phase 1 is 100MW with construction set to begin in 2026. Luma AI has already contracted the entire 100MW of Phase 1 capacity. This JV is starting with its first customer already signed.

The important part is Cisco’s role. This isn’t a switch delivery contract. Cisco is a founding investor and exclusive networking partner with minority equity in the JV. It’s a profit-sharing structure. Chuck Robbins said Cisco’s own sales team will sell HUMAIN data center capacity directly to global customers. Equipment revenue, plus JV economics, plus recurring service demand. Three separate income streams from a single deal.

The target market covers Asia, Europe, India, the Middle East, and Africa. A potential reach of 4.5 billion people. This is the clearest concrete example of why Cisco’s full-stack turnkey approach works in the sovereign AI market.

Cisco AI Summit: Positioned at the Center of the AI Ecosystem

Look at the speaker lineup from the Cisco AI Summit 2026, held in San Francisco on February 3: Jensen Huang from NVIDIA, Sam Altman from OpenAI, Lip-Bu Tan from Intel, Matt Garman from AWS, Amin Vahdat from Google, Marc Andreessen from a16z, Kevin Scott from Microsoft, Mike Krieger from Anthropic. Every major player in AI infrastructure was on Cisco’s stage.

Lip-Bu Tan’s comment stood out. He said the biggest bottleneck in AI is memory and there’s no relief until 2028. That’s directly connected to what’s compressing Cisco’s margins right now. Management called the memory price surge “unprecedented.” If that lasts until 2028, this isn’t just Cisco’s problem. It’s a structural margin headwind across every AI infrastructure hardware company. Cisco is in the same boat as the rest of the industry.

Intel is also listed as a strategic technology partner in the G300 announcement, alongside AMD and NVIDIA. On the Unified Edge platform, they’re co-marketing edge AI running on Intel Xeon 6. Cisco is positioning itself as a neutral infrastructure layer spanning both the GPU world (NVIDIA) and the CPU world (Intel).

Cisco President Jeetu Patel said at the Summit that Cisco AI Defense will be

“100% written by OpenAI Codex.”

That’s a signal that Cisco itself is becoming an AI-native software company. If software development costs come down, there’s a path to structural margin improvement over the long term.

The Axonius $2B Acquisition Rumor

In January 2026, Israeli outlet Calcalist reported that Cisco was in negotiations to acquire cybersecurity asset management firm Axonius for $2B. Axonius denied it, and TD Cowen said it didn’t appear imminent. But the timing is meaningful. ServiceNow had just acquired competitor Armis for $7.75B, and what Axonius does (unified management of network-connected assets, automated security gap detection) fills the exact missing piece in the Splunk plus Hypershield stack.

This would be the biggest cybersecurity acquisition since Splunk at $28B. If it closes, the “chip to security” full-stack story becomes a lot more complete.

The Biggest Long-Term Optionality: If Ethernet Takes Scale-Up

The events above will play out over the next one to two years. But there’s a larger picture underneath all of it.

Scale out has always been Ethernet’s domain. Scale up has been NVIDIA NVLink’s domain. But open standard efforts like UALink and the Ultra Ethernet Consortium are gaining real traction, and Dell’Oro noted that Ethernet surpassed InfiniBand in AI backend networking in 2025. Sameh Boujelbene wrote that scale up could represent the largest TAM expansion in the history of the networking industry. The G300 was introduced as a chip targeting not just scale out but scale up as well.

It’s too early to call the end of NVLink’s dominance. But the push to crack open that closed architecture is real and accelerating. If that transition continues, the size of the market Cisco can participate in becomes a fundamentally different number than what’s priced in today. That’s upside that isn’t in the stock yet.

Conclusion

While the market has been rattled by Iran war risk and oil prices, the fact that Cisco has been quietly grinding higher isn’t random. Capital may be rotating into a company that offers both defensive characteristics and a credible AI growth story at the same time.

But there are still things that need to be confirmed. Margin defense and G300 commercialization matter more right now than order volume growth. The May earnings call is the inflection point. If margins stabilize within guidance and AI order acceleration holds, the case that this stock is still undervalued becomes much easier to make.

Why is Cisco the AI semiconductor company to look at after missing NVIDIA and Broadcom? Because it is one of the few incumbents offering an integrated AI infrastructure stack spanning networking, optics, and security, while still trading at roughly 19 to 20 times forward earnings
