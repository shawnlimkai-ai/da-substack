OSAT: The Final Bottleneck in AI Chips

Is the Market Really Distinguishing What Happens Inside the Back End?

May 11, 2026

Likes: 33

The re-rating of the AI semiconductor value chain has happened in the order of visibility. First it was GPUs. Then foundries. Then memory. Stock prices moved in the order the market recognized each bottleneck.

So where is the next re-rating?

There is one final stage in semiconductors that the market doesn’t know well. Even after design, fabrication, and memory attachment, a chip is still not finished. It has to be diced, stacked, connected, and tested through backend processing before it becomes a chip. The companies that handle this segment are OSATs (Outsourced Semiconductor Assembly and Test).

For a long time, the market looked at OSAT through a narrow lens. Backend outsourcers. A commodity where it doesn’t matter who does the work. But as the AI era arrived, that label stopped fitting. Even within OSAT, some revenue generates 10% margins while other revenue produces EBITDA margins above 40%. I believe this segment is the last part of the AI semiconductor value chain that will re-rate.

In this article, I decompose the revenue of 12 publicly listed OSATs to analyze how the profit structures of low-margin assembly and high-margin test differ, whether the market has priced in this difference, and which OSATs retain investment value along the time axis.

What Is OSAT

The semiconductor value chain broadly divides into design, manufacturing, and backend processing. Fabless companies design, foundries etch circuits onto wafers, and the final step turns those wafers into finished products through backend processing.

Backend processing splits into two categories. Assembly and test.

Assembly is the process of dicing wafers into individual chips, mounting them onto substrates (die attach), creating electrical connections (wire bonding or flip chip), and encapsulating them for protection from the external environment (molding). Only after these steps does a circuit on a wafer become a “chip.”

Test is the process of verifying that the finished chip operates as designed. It includes wafer probing at the wafer level, final test after packaging, burn-in under high temperature for extended periods, and SLT (System Level Test) that runs full inspection in actual system environments. As chips grow more complex, test stages multiply and costs rise.

Who handles this backend work? IDMs like Samsung and SK hynix package much of their own memory in-house, and TSMC directly handles high-value advanced packaging like CoWoS. But processing all backend in-house is inefficient. Investing tens of billions to install EUV equipment and develop 3nm processes versus dicing commodity chips and attaching them to substrates are fundamentally different in value-add. So a significant portion of backend is outsourced to specialists. These specialists are OSATs.

But right now, the nature of the work OSAT receives is changing. You need to understand why it’s changing now.

Why Now

OSAT was a commodity for decades. Why is it suddenly different? The answer lies in the physical structure of AI chips.

Traditional semiconductors had one die in one package. Wire bond it, wrap it in plastic, done. Any OSAT could do this, which is why it was a commodity.

AI GPUs are different. Take Nvidia Blackwell as an example. A single package contains 2 compute dies and 8 HBM3E stacks. Blackwell adopted CoWoS-L technology, which uses local silicon bridges instead of the conventional single silicon interposer (CoWoS-S).

Dies are connected through localized silicon bridges, bonded with microbumps, and mounted onto a substrate. This is a different dimension of technology from wire bonding. Whether interposer or bridge, connecting dies on silicon requires foundry-grade cleanrooms, lithography equipment, and sub-micron precision.

The problem is that extremely few can do this. CoWoS is a process that TSMC developed and TSMC dominates. But demand is exceeding TSMC’s capacity.

Industry estimates put TSMC’s CoWoS capacity at roughly 75,000 to 80,000 wafers per month currently, expanding to 120,000 to 130,000 by the end of 2026. Nvidia is estimated to account for more than half of global CoWoS demand, with AMD, Google, Amazon, and various custom ASIC players competing for the rest.

Reports indicate that both CoWoS-L and CoWoS-S lines are fully booked, suggesting demand substantially exceeds capacity. Building a new CoWoS line takes 18 to 24 months, so this gap will persist through at least 2027 to 2028.

How does TSMC handle the excess? It hands it to OSAT. In the past, TSMC kept the high-value CoW (Chip-on-Wafer) bonding in-house and outsourced only the relatively lower-margin oS (on-Substrate) assembly and test to OSATs. But the demand surge has changed things. Recently, some CoWoS-related processes and comparatively simpler RDL structures have begun flowing to OSATs.

Some industry estimates even mention the possibility of expanded outsourcing of CoW-related processes. The scope of work OSAT receives is gradually widening. Sell-side estimates suggest CoWoS-related volume outsourced to OSATs reaches roughly 40,000 wafers per month (480,000 annually).

One more thing. Hyperscalers have begun voluntarily diversifying their supply chains. Nvidia, Google, and Amazon all want to reduce their 100% dependence on TSMC as a single source. Once an OSAT is qualified for CoWoS-related processes, customers allocate volume to OSAT directly, separate from what TSMC hands off. On top of the overflow from TSMC’s capacity shortage, there’s structural demand from customer risk management.

To sum up, the change happening in OSAT right now is not a simple cyclical upturn. The physical structure of AI chips has made advanced packaging mandatory, demand has exceeded what TSMC can handle alone, and the nature of the work OSAT receives is itself changing. Starting from oS assembly and gradually moving to higher-value processes, from conventional test to AI chip SLT. The quality of revenue is shifting.

Four Types of OSAT

Completely different businesses are running under the same OSAT label.

Type 1: Advanced Packaging OSAT

CoWoS, 2.5D interposer assembly, fan-out wafer-level packaging. Direct beneficiaries of TSMC overflow. Since new CoWoS lines take 18 to 24 months from groundbreaking to production, this tailwind is valid through at least 2027 to 2028.

Type 2: Memory Packaging OSAT

Backend processing for DRAM, NAND, and HBM. When resources concentrate on HBM, conventional DRAM/NAND packaging overflows to OSAT. A two-way beneficiary structure. The risk is dependency on IDM outsourcing policy.

Type 3: Test-Specialized OSAT

SLT (System Level Test) is the core. As chiplet architectures become standard, test points increase exponentially. SLT demand is growing structurally alongside rising AI chip complexity.

Type 4: Optical Packaging OSAT

Assembly, alignment, and test of CPO and silicon photonics chips. 2026 to 2027 is early deployment; volume production comes after 2028. ASE, Amkor, JCET, and some other global OSATs are currently investing in this capability.

This is why OSAT should no longer be viewed as a simple back end step in the AI semiconductor era.

The key question is which OSAT companies are actually sitting at the bottleneck, which types of revenue can translate into higher margins, and which parts of the market are still underappreciated.

In the section below, I will focus on why OSAT margins can improve in the AI semiconductor era, where the market may still be missing the opportunity, and which companies deserve closer attention under this framework.

Same OSAT Revenue, Different Quality

Lump OSAT into a single industry and you miss something crucial. Even within OSAT, packaging revenue and test revenue contribute to profits in fundamentally different ways. In packaging, as volume grows, material costs and process input costs rise in lockstep.

In test, once you’ve installed the ATE, profits accrue with no incremental cost as utilization climbs. The same revenue growth generates more profit on the test side. That’s the operating leverage difference.

Take ASE, the world’s #1 OSAT. FY2025 consolidated financials show a gross margin of 17.7% and operating margin of 7.9%. But ASE is really two businesses bundled into one stock price. ATM (packaging + test) accounts for 60% of revenue with a 23.5% gross margin. EMS (electronics manufacturing services) makes up 40% with a 9.1% gross margin.

The market evaluates the consolidated 17.7%, but if you strip out the actual OSAT business alone, it’s 23.5%, and it climbed to 26.0% in Q1 2026. The number the market sees and the actual OSAT business number are different.

Mix shift within ATM is also underway.

LEAP (Leading-Edge Advanced Packaging) revenue surged 6.4x over three years, from $250 million in 2023 to $1.6 billion in 2025, and management guided at least a doubling to $3.2 billion or more for 2026. Test revenue grew +32% in FY2025, nearly twice the speed of packaging (+17%).

Management has set a structural ATM gross margin target of 24% to 30%, and with Q1 2026 at 26.0%, there’s still room to the upper bound (30%). What fills that room is the rising share of LEAP and test.

ASE is a case where packaging and test are mixed within the same OSAT. So what kind of margin does a test-only OSAT produce?

KYEC (2449.TW) makes the difference even starker. A test-specialized OSAT with approximately 97% of revenue from test services. FY2025 EBITDA margin was 47.3%, and Q4 2025 alone hit 50.2%.

FY2025 revenue grew more than 30% year over year, and February 2026 monthly revenue accelerated to +41% YoY. ASE ATM’s Q1 2026 gross margin is 26.0%. KYEC’s EBITDA margin as a test pure play is 47% to 50%. The metrics are at different levels, so direct comparison requires care, but the direction is clear.

ASE and KYEC are not just a story about two companies. They represent opposite ends of a structural difference that applies across the entire OSAT industry. Packaging-heavy OSATs see margins move linearly even as revenue grows.

Test-heavy OSATs see profits compound nonlinearly as utilization rises. Regardless of which OSAT company you’re looking at, the share of test within that company’s revenue determines margin quality.

The takeaway is this. When evaluating OSAT, don’t look at revenue scale. Look at what kind of revenue is growing within that scale. Once you understand the operating leverage difference between packaging and test, you can avoid the mistake of throwing all 12 OSAT names into the same basket.

Has the Market Actually Priced In This Difference?

OSAT stocks have moved. That’s true. ASE’s trailing PE sits around 45x to 53x depending on the source (as of May 2026). That’s at least 4x its 5-year average of 12x. Amkor went from 17.6x at end of 2024 to roughly 39x. KYEC is up 236% over the past year. This is not a completely undiscovered trade.

But look at the content of this re-rating. It has been entirely packaging-driven. CoWoS overflow, TSMC outsourcing, advanced packaging capacity shortage. These keywords pulled up ASE and Amkor share prices. The market put money into OSAT because of the “packaging bottleneck,” not “test leverage.”

It’s not that the test narrative is entirely absent. But the market’s primary OSAT narrative is still CoWoS and advanced packaging, and the perspective of isolating test as a separate operating leverage story has not yet become mainstream. KYEC’s stock price has risen, but did that rise come from the recognition that “test has a structurally different profit profile from packaging”? Or did KYEC simply get lifted as the entire AI semiconductor value chain went up? My read is that it’s closer to the latter.

Recall the numbers from earlier. Packaging-heavy OSATs see margins move linearly as revenue grows. Test-heavy OSATs see profits compound nonlinearly as utilization climbs. KYEC’s 47% to 50% EBITDA margin is the evidence. If the market begins assigning a separate premium to this structural difference, the multiple for test pure plays can move to a different level from where it is today.

And there’s a structural tailwind supporting this direction. As chiplet architectures become standard, test points are multiplying exponentially. When the number of dies inside a single package increases, the test items required to verify die-to-die interconnects surge. This is not a cycle. Unless the trajectory toward more complex chips reverses, test demand rises structurally. As AI ASIC production ramps in earnest, SLT will inevitably emerge as a bottleneck.

Optical Packaging Has Not Yet Been Confirmed in the Numbers

If test is the gap that exists “now,” optical packaging is the gap that opens “next.”

When CPO (Co-Packaged Optics) enters full-scale data center deployment, sub-micron precision alignment and assembly of optical components, along with testing, will need to be performed at massive scale. The required precision and test infrastructure differ from conventional wire bonding OSAT lines. Dedicated equipment, separate alignment algorithms, and optical characterization test infrastructure are needed.

CPO is an area where foundry and OSAT overlap. Foundries are not exiting this space. TSMC is already developing a silicon photonics foundry platform, and wafer-level silicon photonics, interposer, and advanced integration remain in foundry territory. But for CPO to reach volume, the steps that follow become the bottleneck. Optical engine assembly, fiber attach, optical alignment, package-level optical test. This segment doesn’t end with traditional front-end foundry capabilities alone. This is where the OSAT opportunity opens.

Existing advanced packaging OSATs have structural advantages in this area. First, the high-precision assembly expertise built through 2.5D/3D integration can be repurposed for optical alignment. Second, their multi-chip module experience overlaps with CPO’s heterogeneous integration requirements. Third, large-scale production infrastructure (cleanrooms, automated lines, test floors) is already in place, making it likely more efficient to expand existing infrastructure than to build from scratch.

The back-end segment of the CPO value chain that I cover in my separate optical article series is exactly this area. 2026 to 2027 is the early deployment phase, and volume production follows after 2028. The market is not completely blind to CPO. But because CPO-related revenue and margin haven’t been separately identified yet, it’s hard to say that CPO has been independently priced as an earnings driver within OSAT valuations. Once the numbers start appearing, a new gap will open between OSATs that can do optical packaging and those that can’t.

Buying Today’s Margin: KYEC, ChipMOS

KYEC’s (2449.TW) margin structure was covered above. What I’ll add here is who this company works with.

KYEC is known in the industry as a core partner holding a very high share of Nvidia AI GPU final test, SLT, and burn-in. The exact share is not officially disclosed by the company, but the majority of Nvidia AI chip testing is estimated to be handled by KYEC. Since the days when Nvidia was still a small PC GPU company, KYEC prioritized engineers and production schedules for Nvidia. Nvidia chips have a characteristic of including embedded memory, and KYEC possessed the dual capability to test both memory and logic simultaneously. ASE and SPIL were relatively weaker in memory testing capability and couldn’t capture Nvidia volume. This technical edge plus a 20-year-plus partnership form KYEC’s 해자. When Nvidia GPU shipments grow, KYEC revenue grows directly.

Risks are geographic concentration in Taiwan (production bases concentrated in Hsinchu and Miaoli) and customer concentration. If Nvidia internalizes testing or diversifies to other partners, KYEC takes a direct hit. But given the 20-year-plus partnership and dual test capability, near-term replacement is unlikely. KYEC is less a cheap stock and more a stock in the process of having the market redefine the multiple it assigns to a test pure play.

ChipMOS (IMOS, NASDAQ) is the name accessible on NASDAQ among memory OSATs with meaningful test exposure mixed in. It doesn’t disclose customers, so you can’t build a thesis around a specific customer relationship the way you can with KYEC, but the revenue mix tells a directional story. Memory revenue share climbed from 38.8% in Q1 2025 to 49.9% in Q4 2025, and Q4 memory revenue was +55.3% YoY. AI datacenter DRAM/NAND demand is the driver. Management stated they have customer demand visibility through all of 2026, and at a PE of roughly 36x with +30% revenue growth, the PEG is about 1.2. Customer concentration risk is low since there’s no dependency on a single customer.

Buying Structural Catalysts 2 to 3 Years Out: ASE, Amkor

The case for buying ASE (ASX) rests on two things: CPO optionality and ATM mix shift.

Starting with CPO. ASE co-leads the Silicon Photonics Industry Alliance alongside TSMC and is investing $200 million in CoWoP (panel-level packaging). Management has stated that CPO early production will begin in 2026. As discussed in the earlier section, when CPO reaches the production stage, new revenue opportunities open for OSATs capable of optical packaging, and ASE is at the front.

The ATM mix is also shifting intentionally. ASE is pushing to increase test share. Of the $3.4 billion machinery capex in 2025, $1.14 billion (33%) went to test. In 2026, the company raised capex to $8.5 billion with wafer testing capacity expansion as a core priority. Test revenue accelerated from NT$49.8 billion in FY2023 to NT$54.5 billion in FY2024 (+9%) to NT$71.9 billion in FY2025 (+32%), and its share within ATM rose from 15.8% to 16.7% to 18.5%.

To be fair, the bigger driver of ASE’s margin improvement is the overall LEAP share expansion (from 6% to 13% to 20%+) rather than test share alone. ASE’s real investment thesis is the convergence of three things: LEAP share expansion, test share expansion, and CPO optionality. EMS dilutes consolidated margins at 40% of revenue, but if ATM grows +20% while EMS flatlines, time takes care of the rest.

Amkor (AMKR) is the only large-scale OSAT based in the US. It has an Arizona fab (operational 2028) and up to $400 million in CHIPS Act grants lined up, and it has secured a primary partner position in TSMC CoWoS-S networking outsourcing. On the CPO front, Amkor has formally positioned itself through an announced partnership with Lightmatter on 3D photonics packaging. Right now, it’s early in the capex cycle, with Q1 2026 gross margin at 14.2% and negative FCF. As the company disclosed, Arizona costs may dilute operating margin by 1 to 2 percentage points starting 2027, but at full scale from 2028 onward, it converts into a margin expansion driver.

Betting on a Specific Scenario: Hana Micron

Hana Micron (067310.KQ) is not a proven AI advanced packaging play. The optionality I see is the indirect benefit that emerges when Samsung’s backend capacity priority shifts.

In its 1Q26 earnings call, Samsung said it can deliver “everything from HBM to foundry, packaging, and SiPh under one roof.” The Tesla AI chip is the concrete example. The more this strategy works, the more advanced packaging (AVP) capacity gets allocated to turnkey products, structurally crowding out backend capacity for commodity memory. The more Samsung Foundry succeeds, the more commodity memory OSAT outsourcing grows.

The primary recipient of that outsourcing is Hana Micron. The company has both Samsung Electronics and SK hynix as customers, with a Vietnam subsidiary running a dedicated SK hynix line. The trigger is the point when Samsung’s 2nm production ramp locks AVP capacity into turnkey, combined with signals of expanded commodity memory outsourcing. On 2026 consensus, revenue is roughly 2 trillion won (+30% YoY), operating income 231.6 billion won (+74% YoY). At a PER of 12x to 14x, a company growing operating income 74% looks compressed.

The risk is the 2023 precedent where Samsung/SK hynix pulled back outsourcing volume significantly. If Samsung Foundry doesn’t recover as expected, the AVP displacement thesis doesn’t hold.

Same Thesis, Different Access Points

Doosan Tesna (131970.KQ) shares the same test thesis as KYEC. At a PER of 59x and PBR of 4.9x, the valuation is demanding, but if you want to bet on test OSAT within the KRX, it’s essentially the only option. It has M&A firepower (FY2024 EBITDA of roughly 223.5 billion won) and turnkey service expansion, and as long as the test thesis holds, there’s a scarcity premium in the Korean market.

Chinese OSATs (JCET 600584.SS, Tongfu 002156.SZ, HT-Tech 002185.SZ) are attractive on growth rates and AI exposure but are all A-share listed. SFA Semiconductor (036540.KQ), LB Semicon (061970.KQ), and Powertech (6239.TW) are closer to cycle plays.

Counterargument: “Won’t the Foundries Just Do Everything?”

This question always comes up when discussing OSAT investments. TSMC is pushing its Foundry 2.0 strategy, offering one-stop service from design to packaging. Doesn’t that eventually make OSAT irrelevant?

Directionally, yes. TSMC, Intel, and Samsung are all moving to internalize backend. But reality is more nuanced.

As covered earlier, TSMC is capacity-short enough that it’s outsourcing some CoWoS-related processes and simpler RDL volume to OSAT. That’s a short-term phenomenon. There’s a more structural reason.

OSAT exists not just because of capacity shortages. It exists because customers want it. For hyperscalers, depending 100% on TSMC for packaging and test is a supply chain risk. Once an OSAT is qualified, customers intentionally diversify volume. This structural demand doesn’t disappear even if TSMC’s capacity becomes sufficient. Nvidia, Google, and Amazon are already moving in this direction.

Another point. Does TSMC even have the incentive to handle all backend internally? TSMC’s gross margin is in the 50s. Within CoWoS, high-value-add silicon interposer fabrication and CoW bonding fit that margin profile.

But oS assembly and conventional test carry much lower margins. From TSMC’s perspective, it’s more rational to focus capacity on high-margin front-end and CoW, and outsource the rest. Even as capacity expands, this incentive structure doesn’t change.

What about Intel EMIB and Samsung I-Cube? Both are proprietary advanced packaging technologies, but neither has displaced CoWoS. Intel EMIB is an alternative for customers pushed out of the CoWoS queue, and Samsung I-Cube still primarily serves in-house chips. All three foundries are trying to internalize backend, but they’re not keeping up with the pace of demand growth.

That said, this structure won’t persist in exactly the same form forever. The CoWoS overflow thesis in particular has an expiration date. The reason OSAT holds a position of strength right now is that demand exceeds supply. After 2028, as TSMC’s CoWoS capacity expands sufficiently and Intel and Samsung advanced packaging capabilities mature, the pricing power OSAT derives from simple overflow volume could weaken.

But this doesn’t mean the end of the entire OSAT thesis. Packaging overflow may ease with time, but test complexity and optical packaging operate on different time horizons. As chiplets, HBM, and custom ASICs proliferate, the burden of SLT and burn-in keeps growing. When CPO ramps up, new backend bottlenecks emerge in optical alignment, fiber attach, and optical test.

That’s why OSAT investment is not a single thesis. For 2026 to 2028, CoWoS overflow and advanced packaging capacity are the core story. Beyond that, test complexity and CPO packaging capability may become more important.

Checkpoints for the OSAT Thesis

One. TSMC CoWoS lead time. Currently 6 months or more. If this drops below 3 months, the CoWoS overflow trade weakens. But this doesn’t mean the end of the test or CPO thesis.

Two. OSAT utilization. If ASE and Amkor ATM/advanced segment utilization falls below 90%, that’s a signal of weakening packaging pricing power. In this case, multiples for broad advanced packaging OSATs could face pressure.

Three. TSMC’s outsourcing direction. Currently, some CoWoS-related processes are flowing to OSAT. If this reverses and TSMC pulls outsourcing back, the overflow thesis shakes. Conversely, if outsourcing grows in CPO, optical packaging, and advanced test, the thesis continues in a different form.

Four. OSAT capex vs. revenue growth rate. Both ASE and Amkor are in heavy capex mode. If capex runs ahead of revenue growth and gross margin declines for two consecutive quarters, that’s an overinvestment signal. Conversely, if test revenue share or optical packaging revenue rises, the mix shift thesis remains intact.

These four are not signals to “sell OSAT.” They’re signals to confirm which OSAT thesis remains valid. Even at the point where CoWoS overflow weakens, test complexity or CPO packaging could emerge as the new bottleneck. The point is not to hold onto the OSAT label no matter what, but to follow where the bottleneck migrates. The first checkpoint is TSMC’s next quarterly earnings call.

Closing

The market still calls OSAT “backend.” But the actual bottlenecks are forming within the backend. The label hasn’t changed, but the location of the bottleneck has.

The label “OSAT” is too big. Inside it, there’s low-margin assembly, high-margin test, companies currently spending but potentially repricing 2 to 3 years from now through CPO and US onshoring, and Korean OSATs that get busier the more Samsung Foundry recovers, though the numbers haven’t confirmed this yet.

What you should be looking at right now is not the label. It’s the quality of revenue. And you need to plot that revenue quality on a time axis.
