The Real Game Behind Terafab

Why Reading This as Just "Good News for Intel" Gets It Wrong

Apr 08, 2026

Likes: 34

On the morning of April 7, Intel posted a single line on X. “Intel is proud to join the Terafab project with SpaceX, xAI, and Tesla to help refactor silicon fab technology.”

It was a strangely quiet announcement.

No press release.

No SEC filing.

On the surface, it looks simple.

  1. Intel joined Terafab, and the market liked it.

  2. Intel could get Tesla volume, so the stock went up. End of story.

But this deal has too many weird angles for that kind of read.

SpaceX filed a confidential IPO with the SEC on April 1. The target valuation was reported at over $1.75 trillion, with a fundraising goal around $75 billion.

Meanwhile, Samsung had already locked in a $16.5 billion long-term AI6 chip supply contract with Tesla.

And then Intel slid into the middle.

What makes it more interesting is Intel’s own position. Intel Foundry posted $17.8 billion in revenue for 2025 but recorded $10.3 billion in operating losses, and external customer revenue was just $307 million.

Why would a company in that shape react so sharply to this deal?

Reading this as simply “good news for Intel” is one-dimensional.

Behind the surface announcement, a much bigger game is playing out.

In this piece, I want to unpack the real calculus behind the Intel and Terafab news, drawing on publicly available facts and what I’m hearing on the ground in Silicon Valley.

Why Intel Foundry Is All-In on This Deal

According to Intel’s annual report, Intel Foundry’s external customer revenue in 2025 was $307 million. That is 1.7% of its total foundry revenue of $17.8 billion. The rest is essentially internal volume. Annual operating loss was $10.3 billion. With a structure like that, it is very hard to convince the market that the foundry can stand on its own without external customers. Whether it is a spinoff or an IPO, what the market ultimately wants to see is an answer to one question:

“Is this really a foundry that other companies would trust with their chips?”

So what Intel Foundry needs most right now is not a technology demo.

It needs customers. Not names on a slide, but anchor customers who commit volume over time and generate real revenue. Microsoft is already public as an Intel Foundry customer, and AWS announced a collaboration on custom AI chips based on Intel 18A in 2024. But those are still more symbolic than substantial. To genuinely shift the narrative for Intel Foundry, it needs large-scale, sustained external demand.

In that context, Terafab is not just another collaboration headline for Intel. It is the closest thing to the anchor customer that Intel so desperately needs right now. Of course, there are caveats. The information that has been made public so far amounts to a “joining” announcement. Key contract details like wafer volumes, capex sharing, and long-term capacity guarantees are nowhere to be seen.

The phrase in Intel’s post, “ _ help refactor silicon fab technology_ ,” suggests something deeper than simple wafer supply. But the fact that this was announced through a single X post with no press release or SEC filing also suggests this has not yet reached the stage of a formal contract.

So you should not read this as a confirmed order.

At this stage, Terafab is closer to a possibility than a contract.

So What Does Intel Actually Get Right Now?

If there is no formal contract yet, what does Intel actually gain from this deal? I see two things.

The first is credibility. This may not translate to revenue right away, but for Intel Foundry, credibility is what matters most at this moment. Under Lip-Bu Tan, Intel has been restructuring its manufacturing control and capital structure. On April 1, Intel repurchased a 49% stake in its Ireland Fab 34 from Apollo for $14.2 billion, regaining full control of the fab. Add CHIPS funding and government capital on top of that, and Intel has at least secured enough staying power along with the image of being a national strategic asset.

The technology side is not standing still either. The perception that “Intel is coming up faster than expected” has started to form in parts of the market, and it is at that exact moment that the Terafab name got attached. That sends a signal to both the market and Washington: “Intel has at least secured a seat at the table for the U.S. AI manufacturing hub.”

Here is my bigger read on this. The short-term value of Terafab is not about how many wafers Intel prints right away.

It is about repositioning Intel Foundry as

“the U.S. AI manufacturing platform that Tesla and SpaceX put their names on.”

For a foundry starved of external customers, this alone can function as a qualification event. Real large-scale revenue is a later problem. What Intel needs first is a reason for other potential customers to take a second look. In that sense, Terafab is both a pipeline story and a sales story.

The second is packaging. This is far more tangible than credibility. If you only look at Terafab through the lens of process nodes, you miss the point. The area where Intel can contribute most immediately, and monetize most quickly, is likely advanced packaging.

The bottleneck in the semiconductor industry has shifted. For a long time, almost everything came down to how small you could make a transistor. That’s no longer the whole story. Increasingly, what matters is how fast and efficiently you can connect multiple chips inside a single package. Packaging has become a critical competitive axis.

Read full story

Intel’s Foveros and EMIB are still considered top-tier capabilities in the industry. Meanwhile, the real bottleneck in the market has been showing up more often in advanced packaging than in the node itself. TSMC’s CoWoS being capacity-constrained is practically an open secret, and even Nvidia has dealt with shipment bottlenecks due to packaging capacity limits. What matters in the AI chip race right now is not just cutting transistors smaller. It is how you attach HBM, how you connect chiplets, how you manage power delivery and thermals. Those are the factors that determine real-world performance and shipment volumes. In that sense, Intel’s packaging capabilities are an asset that can convert to revenue faster than 18A itself.

So the short-term picture probably looks more like this. AI6 wafer production is already pointed at Samsung’s Taylor facility, and AI5 is reportedly going to TSMC. Intel enters initially through packaging services and process co-development. Rather than a sudden flood of external wafer revenue, the progression is more likely to start with advanced packaging and enablement, with logic wafer opportunities following after.

Reading this as Intel taking over Terafab’s manufacturing operations at this stage would be getting ahead of the story.

The Timing Lines Up With the SpaceX IPO

On April 1, SpaceX filed a confidential IPO with the SEC. The target valuation was reported at over $1.75 trillion, with a fundraising target around $75 billion. Musk personally pushed back on reports of a $2 trillion valuation, calling them “BS.”

Six days later, Intel announced it was joining Terafab.

This timing may not be a coincidence. There is no hard evidence yet that Terafab is a direct use of IPO proceeds. But what is clear is that this announcement helps build the SpaceX narrative. It makes SpaceX look like more than a launch vehicle company. It positions SpaceX as a company that intends to own AI infrastructure directly. “We are not just a satellite company. We are looking at data centers and chips ourselves. And Intel is already in.” That is a message that would play well on an IPO roadshow.

It makes even more sense when you think about the valuation. Based on reported estimates, SpaceX’s 2025 revenue is around $15 billion to $16 billion, with EBITDA of roughly $8 billion. To justify a valuation above $1.75 trillion on those numbers, Starlink alone is not enough. Investors need “the next decade’s story.” Terafab and the orbital data center concept are exactly the kind of material that fills in that story.

In fact, SpaceX has applied to the FCC for authorization to launch up to one million AI data center satellites. It is worth noting that Terafab’s demand axis may not be limited to Tesla vehicle chips. It could also connect to SpaceX’s orbital data center vision.

Take it one step further and an interesting question emerges. If SpaceX moves toward designing dedicated chips for orbital data centers, the radiation-hardened AI silicon market comes back into focus. Today that market is dominated by players like BAE Systems and Microchip, operating at relatively low volumes in a closed ecosystem. If SpaceX-scale demand actually materializes, the structure of that market could change entirely. This is still speculative. There is not enough public information to pin down specific products or supply structures.

But even from public job postings, SpaceX is actively hiring for RFIC, silicon photonics, and ASIC verification. The direction toward deeper internalization of at least parts of the RF and silicon stack is clear.

A Negotiating Card That Squeezes Samsung

Let’s look at Tesla’s chip supply structure first.

Samsung has been producing Tesla’s AI4 chips, and TSMC is expected to handle AI5. AI6 goes to Samsung’s Taylor facility in Texas. The contract is worth $16.5 billion and runs through 2033. Samsung Foundry CEO Han Jin-man confirmed the target of second-half 2027 mass production at the March 2026 shareholders’ meeting.

Then Intel entered the picture. From this moment, the dynamics change. Tesla can now place TSMC, Samsung, and Intel on the table simultaneously. This is not simple supply diversification. It creates a structure where foundries compete against each other. It resembles what Apple has done for years. Never fully locked in to one supplier, always holding the other as a negotiating card.

From Samsung’s perspective, this is clearly uncomfortable. Samsung fought to win a $16.5 billion contract, reportedly accepting losses to do so, and before that contract has even fully stabilized, Intel has publicly appeared as a new card. From this point, Tesla has one more piece of leverage at the negotiation table with Samsung. It could push to revisit pricing terms. It could apply more pressure on timelines and yield requirements. Whether Samsung gets squeezed out of Terafab entirely or ends up going even deeper is still unknown. But the balance of power at the negotiating table has shifted.

Layer the yield narrative on top of that. Market perceptions around Intel 18A versus Samsung SF2 may be running ahead of reality, but the important thing is that a narrative has started circulating among customers and investors: “Intel might actually be better than Samsung.” That in itself becomes leverage for Tesla. What matters more than the exact yield percentage is that the direction of the narrative has started to shift.

But here is where it gets interesting. This story does not necessarily end as “bad news for Samsung.”

Samsung’s Cards Are Getting Bigger Too

I recently heard that a VP-level executive from Samsung, my former employer, traveled to Silicon Valley to meet with the Tesla team.

Normally, when it comes to technology collaboration discussions, senior engineers or director-level people move first. When a VP moves in person, it suggests that conversations have gone beyond technical review and into business-level territory. Word around the circle is that the discussion was related to Terafab. I could not get details beyond that, but the timing is too interesting to just brush off.

From this point on, this is my scenario. Not confirmed fact, but an interpretation built on public information and what I am hearing in the field.

Samsung is already the foundry partner most deeply connected to Tesla. It built AI4, and AI6 is locked into a long-term contract for 2nm production at Samsung’s Taylor facility. In other words, Samsung is not a spent card for Tesla. It is a card that is already deeply embedded. On top of that, there are very few options for advanced logic production on U.S. soil, which makes Taylor not just a Tesla-dedicated fab for Samsung but a strategically important foothold for capturing high-end logic demand within the United States. The U.S. government finalizing support for Samsung’s Texas expansion underscores the strategic value of that foothold.

So here is how I read it. Intel joining Terafab is a negotiating card that pressures Samsung in the short term. But over the medium to long term, as Terafab grows, it could lift Samsung’s strategic value as well. Right now, Intel’s arrival as a new card has clearly made things uncomfortable for Samsung. But assuming Samsung gets pushed out of the game entirely is also too simplistic. As this game gets bigger, and as Tesla and SpaceX demand more advanced capacity and more options on U.S. soil, the Samsung card could become important again.

One additional read I would add is this. Samsung’s real value may not be simply “the company that makes AI6.” In the longer run, it could be that Samsung is one of the very few large-scale players capable of providing an advanced AI stack option on American soil that is not TSMC. As Terafab scales, and as the Musk camp becomes more sensitive to supply chain concentration risk, the incentive to keep Samsung as a strategic option rather than discarding it entirely only grows.

What People Are Missing: What About Terafab’s Memory?

Everything so far has been about logic and foundry. But if you want to read Terafab as a real project, there is a missing puzzle piece.

It is Memory.

Terafab reads as a project that ties together logic, memory, and advanced packaging. But Intel, the name that has officially joined, can offer logic and packaging but not memory. It already sold off its NAND business and has no DRAM. Follow the announcement text literally, and as Terafab scales into an actual production project, the question of who handles memory inevitably resurfaces.

The reason this question matters is simple.

In today’s AI infrastructure, memory is not a component. It is a bottleneck. You do not get there just by making great compute chips. Whether it is HBM or DRAM, memory supply, pricing, and long-term availability all have to align for the system to work. Samsung’s recent earnings surge was driven primarily by strong demand and pricing for AI data center DRAM and HBM. If Terafab is truly aiming at a larger AI compute project, memory sourcing is likely to become just as important as logic.

At this point, Samsung’s card value extends beyond foundry into memory.

There is no basis right now to say “Samsung memory is going into Terafab.” That would be overstating it.

But Samsung is already deeply connected to Tesla through foundry, and simultaneously a core player in AI memory supply. As Terafab grows, Samsung’s strategic value can reasonably be interpreted more broadly than just 2nm logic capacity.

If Terafab starts operating as a structure that demands logic, memory, and packaging together, Samsung becomes a card on a different dimension entirely. Viewed through foundry alone, Samsung looks pressured. Add memory to the picture, and its strategic presence could actually expand.

Ultimately, reading this deal as purely an Intel win or a Samsung loss makes the picture far too simple.

Terafab Is Also a Message to Washington

One more thing. If you look at this deal as purely a manufacturing story, you miss an important axis. Washington. At the very least, the current version of Terafab is not just a factory story. It is a project that translates extremely well into the language of American industrial policy.

Government money is already deeply embedded in Intel. According to Intel’s own disclosures, the company has already received a portion of its CHIPS grants, and in August 2025, it announced an $8.9 billion U.S. government common equity investment backed by $5.7 billion in outstanding CHIPS funds and $3.2 billion from the Secure Enclave program. Per Intel’s statements, the government holds a 9.9% stake, and total government investment stands at $11.1 billion. In other words, Intel is already firmly inside the frame of “a U.S. manufacturing asset that the government has a stake in preserving.”

The bigger picture is similar. The core of CHIPS funding has been allocated to large-scale U.S. production projects like Intel, TSMC Arizona, and Samsung Taylor. Intel secured its final manufacturing subsidy, and Samsung received final approval for its Texas expansion. Washington has already committed to the direction of funding advanced semiconductor production on American soil.

Terafab enters at this exact moment. American companies Tesla and SpaceX partner with Intel, an American foundry already backed by government capital, to build AI chip production and packaging on American soil.

This is a powerful narrative for investors, but it is equally powerful as a policy narrative. TSMC and Samsung are already making massive investments in the U.S., and that is true. But the combination of Intel, Tesla, and SpaceX creates a far more direct message of “ a Made-in-America AI manufacturing ecosystem.”

In my view, Terafab is both a manufacturing project and a ready-made justification for pulling in follow-on policy support. With the SpaceX IPO underway and Terafab starting to look like a U.S. strategic project that spans AI data centers and robotics, it becomes much easier for Washington to layer additional resources onto an existing American manufacturing platform.

The timing of SpaceX filing its confidential IPO on April 1 followed by Intel’s Terafab announcement just days later makes it hard to dismiss that reading entirely.

Here is how I see it. Terafab is not just about where chips get made. It is a project that ties together capital, policy, supply chain, and the American manufacturing narrative in a single package. So this deal is a fab project, but it is also a message aimed at Washington. Whether that message converts into actual dollars remains to be seen. But the structure is certainly there for it to be read that way.

Conclusion

Based on what is publicly known right now, Terafab looks less like Tesla building a greenfield fab from scratch and more like a structure where the Musk camp layers its demand, capital, and narrative on top of Intel’s existing manufacturing and packaging base. What has been confirmed so far is Intel’s “joining.” The critical numbers, wafer volumes, capex sharing, long-term capacity reservations, additional government support, are all still missing.

There are three things to watch going forward.

Whether binding capacity reservations materialize.

Whether Intel’s role stays limited to packaging and enablement or expands into wafer fabrication.

And whether follow-on policy support or government-linked funding actually attaches.

Until those three things become visible, Terafab is closer to a signal that big players are starting to pivot in the same direction than it is to a confirmed contract. What is needed now is not a knee-jerk reaction to a single headline, but a lens that reads where this entire board is moving.
