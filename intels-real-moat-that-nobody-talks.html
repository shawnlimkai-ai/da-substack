Intel’s Real Moat That Nobody Talks About: STCO

The five-layer simultaneous optimization game, from transistors to optics

May 05, 2026

Likes: 38

Intel is back at the center of the market.

On April 24, Intel’s stock surged 24% in a single day. It was the largest one-day gain since 1987. For the month of April, shares rose 114%, one of the strongest monthly rallies in Intel’s history, touching $100 intraday. That’s a price level not seen since the dotcom bubble of 2000.

The surface-level trigger was Q1 earnings. Revenue came in at $13.6B, with non-GAAP EPS of $0.29, blowing past the Street consensus of $0.01. Data center and AI revenue hit $5.1B, up 22% year over year. Q2 revenue guidance of $13.8B to $14.8B also topped the $13.07B consensus.

But a move of this magnitude is hard to explain with one quarter of results. The good numbers were just the trigger. What the market started re-evaluating wasn’t Intel’s earnings per se, but the structural shift behind them.

So what exactly is the market starting to see differently?

I previously wrote an article titled “Intel Foundry: The Last Chance.” As a former foundry engineer, I covered the technical challenges Intel Foundry needed to overcome, drawing on my own foundry knowledge and looking from the outside in.

But I felt my knowledge alone wasn’t enough to explain what’s changing at Intel right now. So I met with and interviewed someone currently working on Intel’s foundry research team. It gave me a direct look at Intel’s technology strategy and internal engineering perspective that public materials alone don’t capture.

Intel’s strength isn’t about having one good process node or one good package. It’s about the ability to translate a customer’s software-level requirements into actual technology knobs, and to bind transistors, circuits, packages, power delivery, memory, optics, and system architecture into a single optimization loop.

That structure has a name, STCO, System Technology Co-Optimization.

The central question of this article is straightforward. Is Intel just an old CPU company enjoying a temporary bounce? Or is the market beginning to re-rate it as a full-stack semiconductor company that AI actually needs?

The answer becomes visible when you unpack Intel's STCO assets layer by layer. This article first defines what STCO is, then walks through five layers of technical assets: transistors, packaging, heterogeneous devices, standards and infrastructure, and system architecture. From there, it examines four paths along which Intel's profitability is actually improving, identifies signals that strengthen or weaken the thesis, and finally lays out the value chain beneficiary map that STCO opens up.

1. What STCO Is and Why It’s Intel’s Moat

STCO didn’t appear out of thin air. It’s easier to understand if you start with DTCO.

DTCO stands for Design Technology Co-Optimization.

Instead of treating process and design as separate tracks, you optimize them together. In the old model, the process team would define transistor characteristics, design rules, and interconnect specs. Then the design team would draw cells and circuits within those constraints. But as nodes shrank, this approach hit limits. Making transistors slightly smaller wasn’t enough anymore. Teams needed to see earlier how process choices affected real circuit performance, power, and area. So the process team and the design team started sitting at the same table. That’s DTCO.

STCO widens that table.

Now the optimization scope doesn’t stop at transistors and circuits. It extends to packaging, power delivery, memory integration, optical interfaces, and system architecture. The problem isn’t just how to make a good chip. It’s how to architect the entire system the chip goes into.

Inside Intel, STCO is less a marketing term and more an engineering framework. It starts with the customer’s software-level requirements for performance, power, bandwidth, and latency, then translates those into specific process choices, package structures, power delivery schemes, and interface specs. Put simply, it’s the work of converting a customer’s workload requirements into actual silicon and package technology knobs.

Here’s what that looks like concretely. Say a customer wants to reduce latency on an AI inference workload. Memory bandwidth is tight, the power budget is limited, and the package can’t grow indefinitely. STCO takes those requirements and translates them into a set of simultaneous questions. Which transistor library? How many chiplets? How much SRAM, and should FeRAM or other memory blocks be in the mix? How many HBM stacks, and where? Attach them laterally with EMIB, or stack vertically with Foveros? Is frontside power delivery enough, or is backside power needed? Is electrical I/O sufficient, or does this need optical I/O? And how do all of these choices affect system-level PPA?

STCO is the process of coordinating these questions under a single system PPA target, rather than having each team solve them independently. The technical assets are the raw materials. STCO is the methodology for combining them. That’s why this article unpacks the technology layer by layer. Each layer is a knob that STCO turns, and whether those knobs can move together inside the same loop is the core of the Intel thesis.

2. The Transistor Layer: RibbonFET and PowerVia

This figure is an AI generated example intended to illustrate the overall concept.

At the very bottom of STCO sits the transistor. Every system optimization ultimately starts with how well you control current and how efficiently you deliver power.

For the past decade, the industry’s workhorse transistor structure has been FinFET. As the name suggests, FinFET uses thin, fin-shaped channels that the gate wraps around on three sides. The industry rode this structure from the 16nm and 14nm generation through 5nm. But as nodes shrank, the gate’s ability to control the channel started weakening. Leakage current became harder to contain, and balancing performance against power grew increasingly difficult.

That’s why the industry is transitioning to the next-generation structure: GAA, Gate-All-Around. As the name implies, the gate wraps the channel on all sides. Where FinFET covered three faces, GAA covers top, bottom, and both sides. It enables more precise current control and finer tuning of performance and power characteristics by adjusting channel width.

Intel calls its GAA implementation RibbonFET. TSMC introduces GAA starting with N2, and Samsung adopted it earlier at 3nm, though production stabilization took time. Up to this point, the entire industry is heading in the same direction. Intel’s unique story starts with what comes next.

That card is PowerVia.

Look at a cross-section of a conventional chip. Transistors sit at the bottom, with multiple metal layers stacked above. These metal layers carry two things simultaneously: data signals and power. The problem is that as nodes shrink, wiring space gets tighter. Signal lines and power lines share the same real estate, and they start interfering with each other. Thicker power lines crowd out signal routing. More signal lines compromise power delivery.

PowerVia changes this structure. Data signals still go out through the front side of the chip. But power is delivered from the back side. After fabricating the wafer, the backside is processed to lay down dedicated power delivery metal, feeding power directly to transistors from below. The industry term for this is BSPDN, Backside Power Delivery Network.

The benefits are fairly intuitive. Removing power lines from the front-side metal layers frees up space for signal routing. Routing becomes easier, and the same functionality fits into a smaller area. Power also reaches transistors through a shorter path, reducing voltage drop (IR drop). Lower voltage drop means more stable performance at the same power budget, and it’s easier to pack more transistors into the same area.

Intel’s published numbers show cell utilization exceeding 90% with PowerVia, a 5% to 10% improvement over baseline. Performance gain at iso-power is up to 4%. Looking at the full 18A node, Intel claims 15% better performance per watt and 30% higher density compared to Intel 3.

The important thing here isn’t the 4% number itself. PowerVia is not a technology the process team can deliver alone. To feed power from the backside, the transistor layout, frontside signal routing, backside power routing, and package power pin placement all have to align. An optimization on one side can create a conflict on the other. For example, the locations where backside vias need to be drilled might overlap with where the frontside signal routes want to go. Or the package power feed points might not match the chiplet floorplan.

This is why PowerVia isn’t just a process technology. It’s an STCO-level technology. For foundry customers, the implications shift too. It’s no longer just about whether this node is a few percent faster than TSMC. It’s about whether you can co-optimize power delivery architecture, package pin maps, and chiplet floorplans together. For AI chips where power and routing are simultaneous bottlenecks, this difference can be significant.

That said, the decision to introduce both RibbonFET and PowerVia simultaneously at 18A was not the easy path. Layering a new transistor architecture with a new power delivery architecture in the same node multiplies the variables to debug. When something goes wrong, isolating whether the root cause is the transistor structure, the backside power delivery, routing, or package interaction becomes harder. Introducing them one generation at a time might have made yield ramp smoother.

The fact that 18A goes into Intel’s own Panther Lake product before external customers also makes sense in this context. Intel can run a full silicon cycle on its own product first, stabilizing the process-design-package interactions. The data generated in this process feeds into future foundry customer support. The IDM data loop mentioned earlier shows up here again.

TSMC is heading in the same direction. TSMC calls its backside power structure Super Power Rail and plans to pair it with nanosheet transistors at the A16 generation. Intel’s advantage isn’t a permanent moat. It’s closer to a time gap before TSMC catches up. But TSMC’s customer base and execution track record are formidable. So how long this time gap lasts depends on how many external customers Intel can win and how much it can prove with real products in the interim.

At 14A, things go a step further. If PowerVia delivers power from the chip’s backside, PowerDirect connects backside contacts more directly to the transistor’s source and drain. Fewer intermediate steps mean lower resistance, yielding additional efficiency and area benefits. Intel has indicated that the RibbonFET plus PowerDirect combination could deliver 15% to 20% improvement in performance per watt.

The node Tesla mentioned in its Terafab concept is this same 14A. Ultimately, 14A isn’t just the next process node. It’s the stage where Intel has to prove RibbonFET, backside power, advanced packaging, and the external foundry model all at once. PowerVia and PowerDirect are the transistor-layer cards that underpin the entire thesis from the bottom up.

3. The Package and Substrate Layer: Attaching Sideways, Stacking Upward

This figure is an AI generated example intended to illustrate the overall concept.

AI chips can no longer be built as a single large die. There’s the reticle limit of lithography equipment, and there are yield concerns. So functions like compute, IO, memory controller, and HBM interface get split into multiple chiplets, then reassembled inside a package to behave as one system. What determines performance isn’t just which chiplets you use, but how closely, broadly, and reliably you connect them.

Intel’s packaging assets can be viewed along two axes. One is the technology for attaching chiplets side by side. The other is the technology for stacking them vertically.

Let’s start with side-by-side. The natural comparison is TSMC’s CoWoS. CoWoS lays down a large silicon interposer and places GPU die and chiplets like HBM on top of it. NVIDIA’s H100 and B200 were built on this approach. Maturity and reliability are very high, but using a large interposer creates cost and capacity burdens. The reason CoWoS capacity bottlenecks keep getting mentioned is precisely this.

Intel’s EMIB takes a different approach to the same problem. Instead of laying a big silicon interposer across the entire floor, EMIB places small silicon bridges only where chiplets need to connect, embedding them within the substrate. It creates high-density connections only where they’re needed. Think of it not as placing all chiplets on a massive silicon slab, but as building short bridges only between the parts that need to talk to each other.

This structure reduces silicon usage and increases packaging design flexibility. Adding more chiplets doesn’t necessarily require scaling up the entire interposer. You just add bridges at the required connection points. EMIB-T extends this by adding TSVs, moving toward handling not just signals but also power delivery. This becomes particularly meaningful when attaching memory like HBM that demands both high bandwidth and high power simultaneously.

EMIB’s significance isn’t about completely replacing CoWoS. The more practical angle is as a second source. If CoWoS capacity remains a bottleneck, NVIDIA and hyperscalers can’t afford to depend entirely on TSMC for all their advanced packaging volume. Intel’s EMIB could become an alternative. NVIDIA’s $5B investment in Intel, with mention of CPU manufacturing and packaging collaboration, fits within this narrative.

If the technology for attaching side by side is EMIB, the technology for stacking vertically is Foveros.

Foveros places chiplets not just horizontally, but stacks them vertically. The first implementation in Lakefield used 55μm pitch micro-bumps to connect upper and lower chiplets. This shrank to 36μm in Meteor Lake, and was validated in production through Lunar Lake and Arrow Lake.

The next step is Foveros Direct. Conventional micro-bump approaches place solder bumps between chiplets. Foveros Direct eliminates this intermediate step, bonding copper pads directly to each other. This is generally known as hybrid bonding. As bump pitch shrinks, more connections fit in the same area, and data movement between the two chiplets becomes much denser.

Intel’s roadmap targets 3μm pitch. Going from 36μm to 3μm means, by simple math, a 144x increase in connections per unit area. At that density, two vertically stacked chiplets stop behaving like separate components in a package and start acting almost like a single large die. Stacking cache or memory on top of logic, or closely combining logic dies from different process nodes, becomes possible.

Foveros Omni extends this further. Beyond vertical stacking, it enables attaching additional chiplets laterally as well. In a single package, some chiplets connect side by side, some stack vertically, and other memory or IO blocks attach in yet another direction.

This combination is the strength of Intel’s packaging layer. EMIB solves the side-by-side problem, and Foveros solves the vertical stacking problem. Used together, a packaging designer can choose different connection methods based on each chiplet’s characteristics. Chiplets needing high bandwidth get placed closer together. Cost-sensitive chiplets go in a different position. Memory and IO get placed in whichever direction makes sense.

But as you attach more chiplets, another limit emerges: the substrate.

The substrate is the base plate that chiplets sit on. Today, most use organic substrates. But as packages grow and chiplet counts increase, organic substrates face increasing strain in terms of planarity, thermal expansion, and wiring density. Larger packages warp more, and running fine-pitch wiring reliably gets harder. This problem compounds as packages exceed the 100mm × 100mm class.

Glass substrate is the leading candidate to push past these limits. Glass offers a very flat surface and thermal expansion characteristics that can be better matched to silicon. It’s advantageous for building larger packages, placing more chiplets, and running denser wiring. Intel has set a target of glass substrate mass production in the latter half of the decade, alongside a long-term vision of fitting one trillion transistors in a single package.

If EMIB and Foveros address how to connect chiplets, glass substrate addresses how large a system you can fit in a single package. If AI chips are heading toward 20 or 30 chiplets in one package, the substrate isn’t just a component. It becomes the infrastructure that sets the ceiling on system size.

So Intel’s packaging layer doesn’t end with EMIB and Foveros. It extends from attaching sideways, to stacking upward, to reliably placing all those chiplets on a larger substrate. From the STCO perspective, packaging isn’t a back-end process layered on top of transistors. It’s the second design space that determines AI system performance and scalability.

4. The Heterogeneous Devices Layer: Non-Standard Function Blocks on the Package

If transistors and packaging build the foundation, the next question is what else you can put inside the package.

AI chips don’t run on logic alone. They need more on-chip memory, more sophisticated power delivery, and faster optical I/O. In the past, these functions lived outside the chip, on the board or elsewhere. But as advanced packaging has matured, some of these functions can now move next to or on top of logic chiplets.

The candidates to watch in this layer are FeRAM, GaN power chiplets, and silicon photonics OCI. They address different problems. FeRAM is memory, GaN is power, and silicon photonics is optical I/O. But they share a common trait: all three are heterogeneous function blocks that can be integrated with logic chiplets inside the same package.

Let’s start with memory.

This figure is an AI generated example intended to illustrate the overall concept.

Cache inside CPUs and GPUs is mostly built from SRAM. SRAM is fast but expensive. Storing a single bit requires six transistors, consuming significant die area. The bigger issue is that SRAM scaling is slowing down. At leading-edge nodes, logic transistors keep shrinking, but SRAM cells don’t shrink nearly as well. As cache consumes a growing share of die area, this creates pressure on both cost and performance.

The industry has been searching for memory candidates to complement SRAM. Technologies like eDRAM, MRAM, 3D SRAM, and FeRAM fall into this category. But they’re on different timelines. eDRAM has prior commercialization history. Monolithic 3D SRAM requires more distant transistor integration technologies like CFET. MRAM is attractive in certain niches, but it’s not yet a general-purpose card for replacing large caches in AI accelerators.

FeRAM is interesting because it promises much higher density than SRAM and is non-volatile. FeRAM stores data using the polarization state of a ferroelectric material. In simple terms, it uses the alignment direction of internal charges to represent 0s and 1s. Unlike SRAM’s six-transistor-per-bit structure, it offers a significant area advantage.

But claiming FeRAM will replace all SRAM would be an overstatement. SRAM still dominates in speed and endurance. Directly swapping out L1 or L2 cache with FeRAM isn’t realistic. FeRAM’s sweet spot is different: applications that need more capacity than SRAM can provide, but at lower latency than HBM.

This is where the LLC, Last Level Cache, becomes a candidate. As AI chips approach the reticle limit, there’s growing pressure to add more SRAM cache, but die area runs out. If part of the LLC can be built as FeRAM-based memory blocks, you can fit more on-chip memory in the same area. Structurally, this opens the door to a new memory hierarchy: SRAM L1, SRAM L2, FeRAM LLC, HBM.

This picture gets more interesting when combined with chiplets. The logic chiplet is made at the most advanced node. The FeRAM memory chiplet is made at a separate, appropriate node. Then the two are stacked using something like Foveros Direct. Instead of forcing logic and memory to coexist on the same die, you choose the right process and structure for each, then join them in the package. Because this requires looking at memory, logic, and packaging together, it connects directly to the STCO framework.

The second candidate is the GaN power chiplet.

When a single AI chip draws over 1,000W, power delivery itself becomes a major part of the design. Data center power arrives at high voltage and steps down through multiple stages, ultimately reaching the ~1V level that logic actually uses. The farther this conversion happens from the chip, the more loss there is and the more board space it consumes.

GaN, Gallium Nitride, is a strong material for power conversion. It handles higher voltages and faster switching than conventional silicon, and can deliver the same power conversion in a smaller, more efficient form factor. Until now, GaN power devices have mostly been discrete components on the board. But if you can build them as thin chiplets and place them near the logic, the story changes.

The direction Intel has shown is integrating GaN power transistors and CMOS control circuitry into a single GaN-on-Silicon chiplet. Attach this chiplet next to the logic chiplet via EMIB or Foveros, and part of the power conversion can move inside the package. This reduces board-level power losses and enables delivering power closer to the chip’s various voltage domains.

This isn’t simply a power device problem either. You need to know how the logic cores are divided into voltage domains, where the package feeds power, how to avoid thermal hotspots, and where to place the power chiplet. Power delivery is entering the domain where it must be co-designed with transistors, packaging, and the floorplan.

The third candidate is silicon photonics OCI.

Data movement in AI clusters is becoming an ever-larger bottleneck. As compute gets faster inside GPUs and accelerators, data movement between chips and between racks grows more critical. Electrical signals face mounting loss and power issues at higher speeds. That’s why attempts to bring optical I/O closer to the chip continue.

Intel has deep production experience in silicon photonics. It has manufactured 100G and 400G optical transceivers at scale, and at OFC 2024, it demonstrated a 4Tbps-class OCI, Optical Compute Interconnect chiplet. The goal of this direction is to attach the optical engine near the host chiplet, converting the signal to optical before it travels a long electrical path through the package and board.

Of course, silicon photonics OCI isn’t at a stage where it immediately drives large-scale revenue. Packaging, fiber attach, laser source, thermal control, and test cost all need solutions. But over the long term, if data movement in AI systems constrains power and performance, optical I/O will likely move closer to the package.

Intel’s story doesn’t come from viewing these technologies in isolation. It comes from whether it can simultaneously determine how to place logic chiplets, memory blocks, power chiplets, and optical I/O in the same package, which process to use for each, which interface to connect them with, and which system requirements to target.

This layer isn’t one that explains revenue this year or next.

It’s closer to a 5-to-10-year option. But if AI chips keep getting bigger, hotter, and need to move more data, the number of heterogeneous function blocks that must fit inside a package will only grow. This is also why Intel’s STCO can carry long-term significance.

5. The Standards and Infrastructure Layer: The Gateway for External Chiplets

Looking at what we’ve covered so far, Intel’s STCO asset base is broad. At the bottom sit transistor technologies like RibbonFET and PowerVia. Above that are packaging technologies like EMIB and Foveros. Higher still are heterogeneous function blocks like FeRAM, GaN power chiplets, and silicon photonics.

But having a lot of technology doesn’t automatically make a foundry business. From an external customer’s perspective, there’s a more important question.

Can I actually put my chiplet into an Intel package? Does that process require a fully custom project built from scratch every time? Or can I integrate it through a reasonably standardized workflow?

The layer that answers this question is standards and infrastructure.

In the chiplet era, the rules by which chiplets communicate with each other are just as important as good process and good packaging. Even if logic chiplets, IO chiplets, memory controller chiplets, and accelerator chiplets all sit inside one package, integration becomes difficult if they each speak a different data language. If every company builds its own proprietary interface, each new combination requires re-doing PHY, protocol, validation, and test flow from scratch. This erodes the very advantages of chiplets: reusability and combinability.

UCIe, Universal Chiplet Interconnect Express, is the standard designed to solve this problem. Simply put, it’s a common language for chiplets. Regardless of who made a chiplet, if it follows the same interface specification, it can connect with others inside a single package. If PCIe was the board-level standard for connecting multiple devices, UCIe is the package-level standard for connecting chiplets.

Intel led the launch of UCIe, and participants include AMD, ARM, Qualcomm, Samsung, TSMC, Microsoft, Meta, Google, and NVIDIA. This roster matters. A chiplet standard is meaningless if only one company pushes it. CPU companies, GPU companies, foundries, hyperscalers, and IP companies all need to participate for a real ecosystem to form.

Having UCIe doesn’t mean every chiplet plugs in with zero effort. Power, thermal, signal integrity, test, and packaging design rules still need alignment. But a common interface changes the starting point. Instead of building a fully custom connection from scratch, you start from an agreed-upon specification. For external customers, design risk and verification burden both decrease.

For Intel, UCIe carries significance beyond just participating in a standard. Intel has EMIB and Foveros as packaging assets, and UCIe can become the gateway that pulls external chiplets into them. When external customers design UCIe-based chiplets, Intel can prepare integration flows to place them inside EMIB or Foveros packages. The wider UCIe adoption spreads, the larger the pool of chiplet candidates that can enter Intel’s packaging ecosystem.

The addition of 3D stacking and hybrid bonding considerations in UCIe 2.0 aligns with this trajectory. As chiplets move from side-by-side to vertical stacking, the interface standard needs to evolve accordingly. From Intel’s perspective, technologies like Foveros Direct and the evolution of UCIe are looking in the same direction.

If standards build the road, the infrastructure to actually place chiplets on that road is also needed. That’s where the Foundry Chiplet Alliance comes in.

Announced at Intel Foundry Direct Connect 2025, the Foundry Chiplet Alliance is a mechanism to pull external chiplet IP companies into Intel’s packaging ecosystem. The concept is simple: pre-validate chiplets for IO, memory controller, security, accelerator, and interconnect functions, and prepare them in forms that integrate easily into Intel’s EMIB or Foveros packages.

External customers no longer need to build everything from scratch. They can focus on their own logic or accelerator chiplets and pull the remaining standard function blocks from a verified chiplet library. This reduces design time and NRE burden. NRE, non-recurring engineering cost, is the upfront development expense of designing and validating once. As you move to advanced nodes and advanced packaging, this cost grows significantly, making a reusable chiplet library quite meaningful for customers.

This structure is also important for Intel Foundry’s ability to win external customers. For a customer to come to Intel, “our process is good” isn’t enough. They need to see whether the required IP exists, whether validated chiplets are available, whether a packaging design flow is in place, and whether test and validation infrastructure is ready. The Foundry Chiplet Alliance is Intel’s signal that it intends to build that ecosystem.

Government and defense markets being mentioned as early targets is natural. This market has relatively low potential for conflict with Intel’s own CPU and AI accelerator businesses. At the same time, demand for secure supply chains, trusted manufacturing, and chiplet reuse is high. Customer conflict is lower than with external hyperscalers or GPU companies, and these customers value domestic U.S. manufacturing and packaging capabilities, making it a natural entry point for Intel.

Ultimately, this layer’s role is clear. If transistors and packaging form Intel’s technical foundation, UCIe and the Foundry Chiplet Alliance are the gateways for external customers to enter that foundation. No matter how good the packaging is, if external chiplets can’t easily enter, the foundry business struggles to grow. Conversely, if a standard interface and validated chiplet infrastructure accumulate, Intel’s EMIB and Foveros can evolve from internal product packaging into a platform that serves external customers.

If STCO is the internal optimization loop, this layer is the mechanism that opens that loop to the outside. If Intel can build a structure that absorbs external chiplets on the basis of process, package, and system data it has accumulated from its own products, the foundry business expands from selling process to offering package-level system integration.

6. The System Architecture Layer: Top-Down Meets Bottom-Up

The fifth layer sits above the other four and ties them together.

Each of the preceding four layers answers a different question. The transistor layer sets the basic unit of current and power. The package layer determines how closely chiplets can be connected. The heterogeneous devices layer determines whether functions like memory, power, and optical I/O can be pulled inside the package. The standards layer determines whether external chiplets can enter this structure.

The system architecture layer is the one that sets direction from above these four. It takes the performance, power, latency, bandwidth, and package density targets demanded by the customer’s workload and pushes them down into technology knob selections across the layers below. At the same time, actual silicon results from the lower layers flow back up to adjust next-generation system requirements. Requirements flow downward from the top. Real data flows upward from the bottom. It’s a bidirectional loop.

This loop is the essence of STCO. In Section 1, I described STCO as “the methodology for combining raw materials.” This layer is what sets the reference point for that combination. If a customer wants to reduce latency in AI inference, that requirement simultaneously drives transistor library selection, chiplet partitioning, memory hierarchy, power delivery scheme, and I/O architecture. Going the other direction, if running PowerVia on 18A reveals that IR drop is worse than expected at a specific floorplan configuration, that result feeds into next-generation package pin map and power domain design.

The fact that Intel can run this loop internally is the IDM differentiator. Silicon data from Xeon feeds directly into the next-generation Xeon’s STCO framework. For external foundry customers, the outputs of this loop get delivered in the form of PDKs, design kits, and packaging design rules. The system architecture layer isn’t a visible technology. It’s the process of aligning the other four layers under a single objective. And whether this process actually works is the ultimate test of the STCO thesis.

7. Where Is Intel’s Profitability Improving?

Having a lot of technology assets doesn’t make an investment thesis if they don’t convert to revenue. Intel’s problem has long been right here. The STCO layers covered above show Intel’s technical moat, but the market didn’t believe these would translate into revenue and profit. The 60% stock decline in 2024 and Pat Gelsinger’s departure were the peak of that skepticism.

What’s changed in 2026 is that the connection is starting to become visible. Intel’s profitability isn’t improving through a single path. It shows up first in internal products, creates an entry point for external customers through advanced packaging, and can lead to foundry revenue through Intel 3 and 14A. On top of all this, balance sheet recovery and capex discipline must hold for the market to view this as a structural turn rather than a one-time bounce.

The first path: product margin.

This is where the numbers show up first. Q1 2026 revenue of $13.6B, non-GAAP EPS of $0.29, non-GAAP gross margin of 41.0%. DCAI revenue of $5.1B, up 22% year over year. Six consecutive quarters of guidance beats.

The notable shift in Q1 is that the CPU is regaining a role within AI infrastructure. If you look only at AI training, the GPU is central. But as the focus moves to inference and agentic AI, the CPU’s responsibilities grow. Within a GPU cluster, the CPU handles orchestration, memory access, security, and workload distribution. The announcement that Xeon 6 will serve as the host CPU in NVIDIA’s DGX Rubin NVL8 supports this direction. Google signing a co-development deal for Xeon 6 and custom IPUs, despite having its own TPUs, points the same way.

Xeon 6 P-core uses a chiplet architecture that separates compute tile and IO tile. The compute tile can sit on a more advanced node while the IO tile uses a more mature node, enabling mix-and-match price-performance that a monolithic die can’t deliver.

That said, it would be wrong to attribute DCAI growth solely to STCO. Intel’s CFO also noted that tight supply in Q1 led to sales of previously shelved legacy products, and that benefit may not repeat in Q2. Q2 non-GAAP EPS guidance is $0.20, and gross margin guidance is 39.0%, both lower than Q1. Extrapolating Q1 numbers forward carries risk. What matters is whether DCAI growth is one-time or a structural trend driven by AI infrastructure demand, and that needs to be confirmed in coming quarters.

Still, the investment point on the product side is here. As long as Xeon keeps selling, Intel accumulates process, design, package, and system data through its own products. That data feeds back into the next-generation STCO framework. It’s a structure where learning happens first in the internal product loop, then gets confirmed through margin and design wins.

The second path: advanced packaging.

Having external customers commit to Intel’s full process stack from the start is a big ask. They’d need to adapt to a new PDK, design flow, customer support model, and accept supply risk. Packaging, by contrast, can be a lower-barrier entry point. Customers can keep using TSMC for the main die while exploring partial package integration or second-source capacity with Intel.

In an environment where CoWoS capacity remains a bottleneck, NVIDIA and hyperscalers can’t afford to depend on a single supplier for all packaging capacity. If Intel sufficiently demonstrates EMIB, Foveros, and test capacity, it can build packaging revenue and strategic account relationships before even capturing foundry wafer share. This path can monetize faster than 14A.

NVIDIA’s $5B investment in Intel reads in this context as well. What’s been officially confirmed is collaboration on custom x86 CPUs and PC SOCs. But NVIDIA’s mention of Intel’s process, manufacturing, and advanced packaging capabilities alongside the deal suggests it should be read as a broader strategic option than a simple CPU supply contract.

The third path: foundry customer revenue.

This is the biggest upside, but also the slowest and riskiest. That said, foundry revenue doesn’t necessarily have to start at 14A.

There’s Intel 3. It’s the industry’s last FinFET node. Not every customer wants the latest leading edge. In segments like networking, IoT, defense, and automotive, a proven FinFET process is often a better fit. Intel 3 can serve as a cash cow node in this market. If external foundry customers come in on Intel 3, foundry revenue can start flowing even before 14A ramps. The fact that Intel created a dedicated organization called MDCE (Manufacturing Development and Customer Engineering), focused on yield and customer communication, also connects to this trajectory. It’s an operational signal that Intel is actually building out foundry customer support infrastructure.

14A comes after that. Tesla disclosing plans to use Intel 14A in its Terafab concept was the first signal. Reuters reported that this contract could make Tesla Intel 14A’s first major customer. However, many details around cost sharing, operational structure, and ramp timing remain unclear.

So Terafab is better viewed as a credibility event than a revenue model input at this stage. In the foundry business, the first external customer is about trust more than numbers. The mere fact that “an external customer is genuinely considering and planning to use Intel 14A” is what’s needed to convince the next customer. If 14A succeeds, Intel can be re-priced not as a CPU company but as a leading-edge foundry option. If it fails, the STCO thesis remains confined to a product-side recovery story.

The fourth path: balance sheet and capital discipline.

Intel has agreed to repurchase Apollo’s 49% stake in the Ireland Fab 34 JV for $14.2B. Apollo originally invested $11.2B in 2024 in a deal structured to provide financial flexibility. The Apollo buyback is closer to a consequence of the STCO thesis than a cause. It signals that Intel’s financial health has recovered and that it can regain full control of a core manufacturing asset.

Lip-Bu Tan’s strategic shift matters here. His approach of building 14A capacity carefully, tied to confirmed customer commitments, differs from the old Intel playbook of “build first, fill later.” What the market wants to see isn’t aggressive capex but capex linked to customer commitments. If this discipline holds, Intel’s foundry investment gets interpreted not as fixed costs that widen losses, but as growth investment with option value.

To summarize, Intel’s profitability is improving through four stages. First, it gets confirmed through margin and design wins in Xeon and internal products. Then advanced packaging becomes the entry point for external customers. Intel 3 lays the first stepping stone for near-term foundry revenue, and after that 14A needs to generate actual leading-edge foundry revenue. Finally, all of this has to happen without destroying the balance sheet. All four have to align simultaneously for Intel’s re-rating to stick.

8. The Verification Cycle: Signals That Strengthen or Break the Thesis

This thesis will be continuously tested over the next 6 to 12 months. What the market has started pricing in is the possibility that “Intel can once again become a necessary player in AI infrastructure.” If this possibility gets confirmed by numbers, the re-rating continues. If it doesn’t, the stock could revert to a level that reflects only the short-term earnings beat.

The first thing to watch is 14A customer validation. Terafab was an important first signal, but details are still thin. The next data points matter more. Does a second external 14A customer emerge after Tesla? Do customer comments on 14A PDK and design kit maturity improve? Does the 2027-to-2028 timeline hold? 14A is partly a result of originally planned 18A external customer timelines slipping, so further delay risk should be left on the table.

Second is the foundry loss trajectory. Q1 showed recovery on the product side, but on a GAAP basis, losses remain large (GAAP EPS of negative $0.73). Foundry 18A and 14A ramp costs, depreciation, and customer support investment come first. The only condition under which the market tolerates these losses is if the loss trajectory is visibly narrowing. If revenue grows but losses don’t shrink, the market may interpret it as “structural underutilization” rather than “growth investment.”

Third is the time gap with TSMC. Intel’s backside power advantage is not a permanent moat. TSMC combines nanosheet transistors with Super Power Rail at A16. Intel’s window isn’t “TSMC can’t do it.” It’s “how many customers can Intel lock in before TSMC catches up.” If TSMC’s A16 ramp accelerates and major AI customers flock there, Intel’s transistor-level differentiation weakens. Conversely, if Intel secures additional 14A customers and bundles packaging in the interim, it can compete on system integration rather than pure process comparison.

Fourth is product-side durability. Q1 DCAI growth was strong. But as Intel’s CFO noted, Q1 included some one-time sales effect from tight supply. Q2 non-GAAP EPS guidance of $0.20 and gross margin guidance of 39.0% are both lower than Q1. Whether Xeon 6 design wins convert to actual volume, whether DCAI growth repeats, and whether margin quality holds all need verification in coming quarters.

Fifth is customer conflict and firewalls. Intel collaborates with NVIDIA while competing in the AI accelerator market. It works alongside AMD in the x86 ecosystem while competing with Xeon versus EPYC. Government and defense customers present relatively low conflict, but as the customer base extends toward hyperscalers and AI silicon buyers, this problem grows. This risk doesn’t show up directly in numbers; it surfaces through customer composition. If the first external customers stay in low-conflict segments, it’s safe but limits upside. If hyperscalers come in, upside grows but firewall credibility becomes more critical.

Last is valuation. Intel’s stock has already priced in a lot of expectations. A 114% gain in April, touching $100 intraday. From here, good news alone isn’t enough. EPS revisions need to follow. If DCAI growth, foundry loss reduction, additional 14A customers, and packaging revenue signals don’t translate into actual estimate upgrades, valuation pressure may surface first.

Signals that strengthen the thesis come down to three. A second external 14A customer emerges beyond Tesla. Foundry losses narrow. DCAI and packaging-related revenue improve simultaneously.

Signals that weaken the thesis are also three. The 14A timeline slips. Foundry losses don’t shrink. Q1’s DCAI strength turns out to have been driven by inventory benefits or one-time pricing.

9. The STCO Value Chain: A Layer-by-Layer Beneficiary Map

This section is independent of the Intel employee interview and is based on publicly available information and the author’s own technical judgment. No information from the interview is included.

When translating the Intel STCO thesis into stock picks, the most important thing to avoid is narrowing it into a “find Intel’s suppliers” game. STCO isn’t about any single component. It’s an industry structural shift where AI chips must co-optimize transistors, packaging, memory, power, optical I/O, and software requirements simultaneously.

So beneficiaries need to be split into two categories. One is INTC, where Intel makes money directly. The other is picks-and-shovels companies whose demand grows as this structural shift accelerates, regardless of whether Intel succeeds or not.

Picks and shovels: beneficiaries whether Intel succeeds or TSMC goes the same direction.

The highest-conviction external beneficiaries are in advanced packaging equipment, inspection, and chiplet EDA/IP.

On the packaging side, the key names are

BESI (most direct exposure to hybrid bonding equipment),

AMAT (hybrid bonding process equipment and the Kinex system co-developed with BESI),

KLA (process control for advanced packaging), and

Onto Innovation (3D metrology and substrate inspection).

As AI chips grow and chiplet counts rise, bonding yield and package inspection become bottlenecks. Whether Intel’s STCO succeeds or TSMC’s CoWoS remains dominant, advanced packaging equipment and inspection grow either way. This area has defensive value even if the Intel thesis proves wrong.

On the EDA/IP side, the key names are

SNPS (UCIe IP, controllers, PHY, verification IP),

CDNS (UCIe IP, 3D IC design flow, package planning),

RMBS (HBM, DDR, PCIe, CXL, and UCIe-related memory interface IP).

As chiplet counts increase, die-to-die IP, verification, thermal signoff, and package planning challenges necessarily grow. This isn’t just an Intel problem. It’s shared by TSMC, Samsung, AMD, NVIDIA, and every hyperscaler.

Substrate supply chain: as packages grow, the base plate becomes the bottleneck.

IBIDEN, Shinko, Unimicron, and AT &S are positioned on the large AI package substrate side, while Corning holds long-term glass substrate optionality.

Given Intel’s stated goal of glass substrate mass production by 2030, this area is worth keeping on the long-term map.

Long-term optionality: 5-to-10-year time horizon.

This area connects to the heterogeneous devices layer. On the optical I/O side, the candidates are COHR, LITE, MRVL, and Fabrinet.

On the GaN power side, Infineon, Navitas, and Vicor. On compound semiconductor equipment, VECO. However, because Intel has strong internal capabilities in silicon photonics and GaN-on-Silicon, these companies won’t necessarily become direct Intel suppliers.

As AI systems move toward optical I/O and on-package power, these are long-term themes that apply across the industry.

System customers: catalysts, not supply chain beneficiaries.

NVIDIA, Google, and Tesla are customers of or catalysts for Intel’s STCO, not pure supply chain beneficiaries.

The moment these companies actually use Intel’s process, packaging, CPU, and system capabilities, Intel’s STCO transitions from a technology narrative to a revenue possibility.

The external beneficiary list I’m most bullish on is BESI, AMAT, KLA, Onto, SNPS, CDNS, and RMBS. These companies sit on industry-wide themes of chiplet, hybrid bonding, packaging inspection, UCIe, and memory interface. They aren’t tied to the Intel thesis alone, so even if the thesis is wrong, they have defensive value as long as the direction is right.

Closing: Three Stages of Intel’s Re-Rating

Understanding Intel’s re-rating requires breaking it into three stages.

Stage 1 is product recovery. Xeon 6, DCAI growth, gross margin recovery. This stage was partially confirmed in Q1 2026. Six consecutive beats, with NVIDIA and Google design wins starting to show up in the numbers.

Stage 2 is packaging option value. EMIB, Foveros, and advanced packaging customer signals. This stage is an area the market hasn’t yet fully priced in. The longer CoWoS bottlenecks persist, the higher the second-source value of Intel’s packaging rises.

Stage 3 is foundry re-rating. External 14A customers, yield, and foundry loss reduction. This is both the biggest upside and the biggest risk. If this stage gets confirmed, Intel gets priced not as a CPU company but as an AI infrastructure company. If it doesn’t, valuation stays at Stage 1 levels.

Intel’s stock currently reflects Stage 1 strongly and has started pricing in parts of Stage 2. Stage 3 remains unproven. So the investment decision from here isn’t simply “has Intel gotten better?” It depends on whether the company can transition from product recovery through packaging option value to foundry re-rating.

On a 1-year trading horizon, the valuation is stretched. Intel is no longer a cheap turnaround stock. On a 5-year structural change horizon, the market is just beginning to reprice Intel’s STCO assets. The answer changes depending on which time horizon you’re looking through.
