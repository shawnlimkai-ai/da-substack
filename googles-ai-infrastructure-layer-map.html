Google’s AI Infrastructure Layer Map

Where Is the Next Bottleneck?

At Google Cloud Next ‘26, held in Las Vegas on April 22, 2026, Google unveiled its 8th-generation TPU.

The most striking change is that the 8th-gen TPU is now clearly split into TPU 8t for training and TPU 8i for inference.

Earlier generations had use-case-specific optimizations like v5p and v5e, but presenting training and inference as two main axes within the 8th generation is a meaningful shift.

The more important thing, though, isn’t the TPU itself.

With this announcement, Google laid out how AI data center infrastructure operates under the name AI Hypercomputer. The AI Hypercomputer concept itself has existed since 2023 and gets updated each year at Cloud Next, but this was the broadest announcement so far, with every part of the infrastructure moving to the next generation at the same time.

For reference, when Google officially explains AI Hypercomputer, it splits into 3 layers:

(1) Performance-optimized hardware (compute, networking, storage)

(2) Open software (framework, OS, orchestration)

(3) Consumption options (provisioning model).

That’s the breakdown when the user receives it as a cloud service. But to analyze the infrastructure itself, you have to look separately at how the accelerators inside the hardware, the host CPU, IPU, memory, communication, data path, storage, and physical infrastructure all operate, and where the bottlenecks emerge. In this article I’ll re-divide it into 6 layers in my own way, which is easier to follow.

This is a different cut from Google’s official categorization.

This article walks down from the top layer to explain the entire AI infrastructure structure as Google sees it. Then it places other hyperscalers like AWS, Microsoft, Meta, and NVIDIA on the same layer-by-layer axis for comparison.

I’ll lay out where standards are aligning at which layer, where bottlenecks emerge that create new investment opportunities, and how the other hyperscalers will repaint this picture in their own colors over the next 24 to 36 months.

Layer 1. Workload + Software stack: The center of AI work shifts from training to inference

For the past few years, AI infrastructure centered on training. The game was about training larger models for longer, and hardware design followed suit. Compute throughput in a single pass and memory bandwidth. These two were the variables of the game.

Now the center of gravity is shifting to inference. Training is the work of building a model. Inference is the work of using that built model with real users. Training a model once still costs a lot, but in commercial AI services, the cost of inference, which means calling the trained model every day, is becoming an increasingly important variable. As services like ChatGPT and Gemini keep answering questions from users worldwide, costs accumulate not from a single training run but from the inference calls that repeat every day.

On top of that, with agents and reasoning models going mainstream, a single user request no longer ends with a single line of response. An agent is a structure where the AI takes one user question, runs searches, calls external tools, looks at intermediate results, and judges again, taking multiple steps on its own. A reasoning model doesn’t return an answer right away. It expands its thought process internally over a long sequence and then produces an answer. Both follow a “one question → multiple intermediate processing steps → final answer” structure rather than “one question → one answer,” so the number of tokens the model has to process per user request grows quickly. A token is the unit by which a model processes text. To put it simply, it’s a chunk of a sentence broken into small pieces. One English word, or a few Korean characters or word fragments, can be processed as a single token.

Training and inference demand different things from hardware. Training is a workload that processes large batches at once, so throughput matters. Training chips work best when bundled in the thousands and run for days, pushing massive matrix operations through in one go. Inference, on the other hand, has to produce answers while users wait at their screens, so latency matters. Inference chips work better when designed to repeatedly process short requests fast. That’s why TPU 8t and 8i split the way they did. They’re both 8th-gen TPUs, but the training one is closer to a throughput machine and the inference one closer to a latency machine.

But within inference, the bottleneck isn’t a single thing either. The stage of reading the long prompt the user sent in one go and the stage of generating answer tokens one by one have different characteristics. The first stage is usually called prefill, the second is called decode. Prefill takes the entire user prompt at once and builds the model’s internal state, which is compute-heavy. Decode is the stage that generates answer tokens one at a time from that state, and each token requires referencing all the prior calculation results. So prefill is compute-heavy, and decode is memory-access-heavy.

Here the concept of KV cache enters. As the model processes the prompt, it accumulates intermediate calculation results in memory. This is called KV cache. K stands for key, V for value. Think of it as a kind of notepad the model references when predicting the next token. During decode, every time a new token is created, it has to keep referring back to that notepad. So the longer the user’s prompt, and the longer the answer, the bigger the notepad gets, and the larger the data that has to be read each time. If you put a 10,000-token document in as a prompt, 10,000 tokens worth of KV cache piles up in memory, and each time a single answer token is generated, the model has to keep referencing information equivalent to that 10,000 tokens.

The most important variable at this layer is token cost. AI service costs ultimately accumulate in proportion to the number of tokens the model processes. As inference usage grows, the margins of hyperscalers and AI labs are determined by how cheaply they can process one token. And token cost isn’t determined by chip performance alone. With the same hardware, actual costs vary significantly depending on how the software distributes user requests, how well it manages KV cache, and how busy it keeps the accelerators.

That’s why Google didn’t talk only about chips in this announcement and emphasized the software stack alongside. Software in the inference era isn’t a side feature. It’s closer to a full operations system that takes user requests, connects agents to external tools, distributes work across multiple TPU pods, and translates model code so the TPU can execute it well. To lower token cost, the chip itself has to be fast, but you also need software that keeps that chip fed.

From this perspective, Gemini Enterprise, A2A, MCP, vLLM TPU support, and GKE Inference Gateway are software at different layers. Gemini Enterprise is the platform where enterprises connect Gemini models to their own data. A2A is the protocol for multiple AI agents to communicate. MCP is the protocol for AI to access external tools and data. vLLM is an open-source engine that efficiently distributes inference requests across multiple chips, and GKE Inference Gateway is the gateway that routes inference requests across multiple TPU pods. The names differ but the direction is the same. They’re software layers built to handle more inference requests at lower cost.

On top of that, Google didn’t only design the chip. JAX, PyTorch, vLLM, XLA, and Pathways are co-designed with hardware in the same stack. This is part of the same flow. JAX and PyTorch are frameworks for building models, XLA is the tool that compiles that model code into instructions the TPU can understand, and Pathways is the system that distributes a single model across many chips. Making the TPU faster and getting software to feed the TPU properly are the same problem. No matter how fast the chip is, if the software can’t distribute requests properly, only some of the chips work and the rest spend their time waiting on data.

AI infrastructure competition is shifting from a fight to build faster chips to a stack competition to drive down token cost. When workloads change, software changes. When software changes, the hardware design beneath it changes. The split between TPU 8t and 8i is a sign that this shift has reached the silicon.

Layer 2. Compute complex: A compute system bundling accelerator, host CPU, and IPU into one

In actual AI data centers, a single TPU chip doesn’t run alone. Next to it sits a host CPU coordinating the work, and an IPU handles networking and storage communication on its behalf. The compute complex, where accelerator, host CPU, and IPU bundle into one, is the real compute system. The foundation of what Google calls AI Hypercomputer also lives here.

In this structure, the most visible part is the TPU. But the TPU is also no longer a chip with a single purpose. Google split the 8th-gen TPU into training and inference variants. TPU 8t is the training chip, TPU 8i is the inference chip. They both look like AI accelerators, but the bottlenecks the two chips target are different.

TPU 8t is the training chip. It bundles 9,600 chips into a single superpod, providing 121 FP4 exaFLOPS of compute and a 2PB shared HBM pool. Training is the work of dividing massive models across thousands of chips and running them for a long time. So beyond individual chip performance, what matters is whether thousands of chips move stably as if they were a single system.

The number I find most important about TPU 8t isn’t peak FLOPS.

It’s 97% goodput.

In large-scale clusters, problems with chips, links, memory, and thermals always erupt somewhere. Failure isn’t an exception, it’s a normal operating condition. The competitiveness of next-generation AI infrastructure doesn’t end with building the fastest chip. The ability to quickly detect failed chips, links, and slowed paths, then route around them while keeping training going, is becoming increasingly critical. Fault telemetry, automatic rerouting, and OCS-based recovery all live here. From my view, this reliability and diagnosis layer is one of the hidden bottlenecks of AI infrastructure.

TPU 8i is the inference chip. It bundles up to 1,024 active chips into one pod, with 288GB HBM and 384MB on-chip SRAM per chip. Inference has to repeatedly process short responses quickly while users wait. Especially in reasoning and MoE workloads, chip-to-chip communication, cache access, and synchronization determine latency. TPU 8i is designed to reduce latency rather than maximize peak compute. The Boardfly topology shortens inter-chip communication distance, and CAE accelerates the work of multiple chips collecting and synchronizing results. The point isn’t more compute. It’s about cutting away the memory-access, inter-chip communication, and synchronization bottlenecks that emerge during inference.

Looking at it this way, TPU 8t is a throughput machine for training that pushes long compute runs, while TPU 8i is a latency machine for inference that processes short requests quickly. When the workload changes, the bottleneck changes. When the bottleneck changes, the silicon design changes too. The split between 8t and 8i is a signal that this shift has reached the chip architecture itself.

But TPU alone doesn’t complete the compute complex. Alongside it sits the Axion N4A host CPU and the next-generation IPU being co-developed with Intel. The host CPU handles agent runtime, the control plane, and general computing tasks. The IPU takes networking, storage, and security work off the host CPU. No matter how fast the accelerator, if the data doesn’t arrive on time, it goes idle. The host CPU and IPU are essential components for keeping accelerator utilization up.

What Google calls AI Hypercomputer isn’t a strategy of just making TPUs faster. It’s a structure where the TPU handles compute, the CPU coordinates work, and the IPU handles data movement and infrastructure tasks, all driving up the utilization of the entire system. The competitiveness of AI data centers now comes not from individual accelerator performance but from how smoothly these components fit together.

The fact that Google also announced A5X at the same event has to be read in this context. A5X is a bare metal instance based on NVIDIA Vera Rubin. This is closer to a strategy of running both Google’s own TPU and NVIDIA GPUs inside Google’s cloud, rather than a strategy of completely replacing NVIDIA with TPU. Whether the customer uses TPU or NVIDIA, the networking, storage, software, and power infrastructure beneath them runs on Google Cloud. Google’s real strategy isn’t a specific chip. It’s running multiple compute engines inside its own infrastructure at the highest possible utilization.

The design partner setup here is also interesting. The training TPU appears to continue with Google’s long-time ASIC design partner, while MediaTek is reported to be involved in the inference TPU according to Bloomberg and The Information. There’s no official announcement, but the direction itself matters. MediaTek brings low-power design experience, SerDes capability, and TSMC process integration accumulated from mobile SoCs. Inference chips need cost, power, and latency optimization more than training chips do. If Google is moving toward not putting all TPU designs through one channel and instead using different partners by workload, that’s a signal that AI accelerator design is becoming more segmented.

The Information also reported that Marvell is in discussion with Google not only for additional inference TPUs but also for assistant chips that sit alongside the TPU and reduce memory bottlenecks. Neither company has officially confirmed. So this part is safer to read as a signal of Google’s multi-vendor design direction rather than a confirmed supply chain.

What to look at in this report is the memory bottleneck. In AI workloads, the bigger and bigger bottleneck isn’t compute itself. It’s where the data sits, how it’s brought in, and how often it gets moved. If you can process some compute near memory or reduce the data movement itself, another axis emerges for driving up accelerator efficiency. This point connects naturally to the next Layer 3, memory hierarchy.

Layer 3. Memory hierarchy: The bottleneck shifts from memory capacity to where the data sits

In the past, you could understand AI accelerator memory roughly with HBM at the center. Model weights, activations, and KV cache sat in HBM, and the data needed for compute got pulled into the chip’s SRAM for processing. HBM is a large, fast warehouse attached next to the accelerator, and SRAM is a small workbench right next to the compute units.

But as inference workloads grow, this simple structure is hitting limits. Especially in reasoning models and long context workloads, KV cache grows fast. KV cache is the intermediate calculation results the model built while processing prior tokens. Every time it generates an answer token, it has to keep referencing this information. So the longer the prompt, and the longer the answer, the more time the model spends reading data from memory rather than computing.

This is the memory wall.

The compute units inside the accelerator have gotten faster, but if the data they need doesn’t arrive on time, the compute units have to wait. Inference performance isn’t decided by FLOPS alone. How close the data sits and how fast it can be re-read are what determine latency.

The reason TPU 8i bumped on-chip SRAM up to 384MB is here.

If data that’s used often can be processed inside the chip without going all the way back to HBM each time, latency drops and HBM traffic drops. At the same time, TPU 8i provides 288GB of HBM per chip. So Google’s direction isn’t simply attaching more HBM. It’s splitting the role, with the most frequently used data sitting in SRAM and larger model data and active KV cache sitting in HBM.

In TPU 8t, memory means something a bit different.

TPU 8t is the training chip. In training, what matters isn’t a single user’s latency but whether thousands of chips move stably as one giant system. Google explained that the TPU 8t superpod offers 9,600 chips and 2PB of shared memory. More than the memory of an individual chip, what’s key is the ability to bundle the HBM scattered across thousands of TPUs into a single massive training memory pool.

As a result, TPU 8i and TPU 8t use memory differently. TPU 8i, being for inference, prioritizes keeping hot KV cache close to the chip. TPU 8t, being for training, prioritizes binding the HBM of thousands of chips into one large memory system. The same HBM is a resource for reducing latency in inference and a resource for sustaining large-scale distributed training in training.

A more important shift one level deeper is that KV cache isn’t a problem solved only inside the chip. Google also mentioned a structure in the serving system that splits KV cache across multiple tiers like RAM, Local SSD, Cloud Storage, and Lustre. You can’t put all KV cache in the most expensive memory. What’s used often goes nearby, and what’s used less often goes down to bigger and cheaper storage tiers.

The more important question in memory hierarchy isn’t “which memory is fastest.” It’s which data is used most often. Which data must sit close to the chip. Which data is fine in slightly slower locations. And how this placement should be done to lower token cost.

The shift visible in Google’s announcement is clear. The bottleneck of AI infrastructure isn’t simply a shortage of HBM capacity. In inference, where to put KV cache matters more, and in training, how to bind the HBM of thousands of chips into a single system matters more. Memory is no longer a part attached to the accelerator. It has become the structure that determines the cost and performance of AI workloads as a whole.

And as data spreads across SRAM, HBM, RAM, Local SSD, and Lustre, the importance of the network connecting these tiers grows too. So the next bottleneck flows naturally into network fabric.

Layer 4. Network fabric: The communication network tying tens of thousands of chips into one computer

In an AI data center, data doesn’t always stay inside the same chip. Some data sits in another accelerator within the same pod, some sits in another rack, and in larger training clusters it can sit in another data center. So the network isn’t simply wiring that connects servers. It’s the fabric that ties tens of thousands of accelerators, racks, pods, and data centers together as one giant computer.

As AI models grow, you can’t finish all the compute inside a single chip or a single server. Model weights, activations, gradients, KV cache, and checkpoint data keep moving between devices. If the network is slow or unstable, even the fastest accelerator stops while waiting on data. In large-scale AI systems, a slow network is a slow accelerator.

AI networks split broadly into two stages. The first is scale up. This is the very fast communication between accelerators inside the same pod. NVIDIA calls it NVLink, Google TPU calls it ICI. They’re short, fast connections meant to bundle multiple chips into one big accelerator.

The second is scale out. This is the communication between pods, between racks, and between data centers. In this stretch, just having fast chip-adjacent links isn’t enough. You have to connect more devices farther and more reliably. That’s why Virgo Network matters in this Google announcement.

Virgo Network is the scale out fabric Google announced for large-scale AI clusters. Google explained that Virgo provides 4x higher bandwidth than the previous generation and has a structure that reduces the scaling tax, where communication efficiency drops as scale grows. To put it simply, it’s a network that makes performance scale linearly as you add more chips. Just buying lots of fast chips isn’t enough. Those chips have to exchange data with each other and move as one system.

Looking at the scale tells you how far Google is trying to push the network. Google said that with Virgo Network and TPU 8t, 134,000 TPUs can be tied into a single fabric within one data center, and across multiple data centers, more than 1 million TPUs can be tied into a single training cluster. They want to make multiple data centers look like one supercomputer. AI training clusters going forward will struggle to stay inside a single building. Power, cooling, and site constraints make it likely they get split across multiple data centers, and a network is needed to bind them into one training system.

What’s interesting is that Virgo doesn’t end as a TPU-only fabric. Google said Virgo Network also applies to A5X, the NVIDIA Vera Rubin NVL72-based instance. In that case, the structure supports up to 80,000 GPUs in a single data center and up to 960,000 GPUs across multiple sites. This shows Google’s strategy isn’t to push a single chip. It’s to bind everything, whether TPU or NVIDIA GPU, into one fabric inside its own cloud.

On the TPU 8i side, the meaning of network is a bit different. While TPU 8t is structured to run massive training clusters for long stretches, what matters for TPU 8i is reducing short latency in inference and reasoning workloads. Especially in MoE or reasoning models, chip-to-chip communication happens often depending on which token goes to which expert. When network paths that take the long way around become frequent, tail latency grows. Users feel the slowest response more than the average speed, so reducing this tail latency matters.

That’s why Google applied Boardfly topology to TPU 8i. The existing 3D torus structure works efficiently for workloads where neighbors communicate frequently like large-scale training, but in workloads like MoE and reasoning where many chips need to communicate widely, hop counts can grow. Boardfly uses more direct connections to flatten the network, reducing the maximum hop count from 16 to 7 in the same 1,024-chip pod. Google explained that this structure can improve latency by up to 50% in communication-intensive workloads.

OCS gets attached on top. OCS stands for Optical Circuit Switching. Simply put, it’s a device that switches the path the optical signal travels. The TPU 8i pod is designed so that multiple groups are connected via OCS, with chip-to-chip communication landing within a maximum of 7 hops. The important point isn’t that OCS is just “optical technology.” In large-scale clusters, link problems always emerge. To keep training or inference from stopping, you have to be able to avoid the failed path and route traffic around it. This is why the goodput and reliability mentioned in Layer 2 connect directly to network fabric.

Falcon is part of this picture too. In AI workloads, a transport layer that moves large amounts of data reliably and with predictable latency matters. Google said it’s co-developing Falcon networking protocol with NVIDIA through OCP, and that A5X implements multiple Falcon concepts. This is Google pulling NVIDIA-based systems into the networking philosophy of its AI Hypercomputer.

Google’s network announcement focuses on cluster scale and reliability rather than link speed. With TPU 8t, Virgo binds hundreds of thousands of TPUs into one training fabric. With TPU 8i, Boardfly and OCS reduce reasoning inference latency. And with A5X, Virgo and Falcon bring NVIDIA GPUs into the same fabric strategy. The network is no longer a part attached behind the chip. It’s a core system that determines AI cluster performance and utilization.

In large-scale AI clusters, the network is the layer that converts chip performance into actual system performance. No matter how fast the chip, if the data doesn’t arrive on time, performance doesn’t show. The reason Google emphasized Virgo Network, Boardfly, OCS, Falcon, and multi-data-center clusters together lies here. AI infrastructure competition is shifting from a contest of individual chip speed to a fight over how to bind tens of thousands of chips and multiple data centers into one system.

Layer 5. Storage + Data path: Storage becomes the system that feeds the accelerator

No matter how fast the accelerator and network are, if the needed data doesn’t arrive on time, the AI system stops. So in this Google announcement, storage shows up not as simple storage space but as a system that pushes data into TPUs and GPUs to keep them working.

In training, this problem shows up most clearly in checkpoints. When training a giant model with thousands or tens of thousands of accelerators, you have to keep saving intermediate states. That’s a checkpoint. If you don’t save checkpoints often, you lose a lot of training time when something fails. On the flip side, if checkpoint saving is slow, the expensive accelerators sit waiting during the save. In large-scale training, storage speed connects directly to training efficiency and recovery speed.

That’s why Google emphasized Managed Lustre. Lustre is a parallel file system long used in supercomputers. If regular storage is a single-line cashier, a parallel file system opens many cashiers so that many servers can read and write data at the same time. AI training is now similar to supercomputer workloads. Many TPUs and GPUs have to access the same dataset, checkpoint, and intermediate data at the same time.

Google said Managed Lustre has been scaled up to 10 TB/s bandwidth and 80 PB capacity. This number matters not because the storage itself got bigger but because it stops accelerators from waiting on data. If checkpoints can be saved quickly during training, and recovery after a failure is quick, the idle time of the entire cluster drops. Google explained that a large compute cluster is ultimately only as effective as the storage system feeding it data.

Rapid Buckets sits in the same flow. If Managed Lustre is a high-speed file system that many compute nodes access simultaneously, Rapid Buckets is the high-performance object storage on the Google Cloud Storage side. Google explained that Rapid Buckets provides sub-millisecond latency and 20 million requests per second, reducing GPU blocked time during large-scale checkpoint and recovery, and speeding up data loading. As training grows, storage isn’t a backup space anymore. It becomes part of the training loop.

But fast storage alone isn’t enough. The path the data takes from storage to accelerator memory has to be short too. In existing server structures, data doesn’t go directly from storage to TPU or GPU. It usually passes through the host CPU and host DRAM. The CPU receives the data, places it in memory, then hands it over to the accelerator. Through this process, data copies repeat and CPU and DRAM bandwidth gets consumed. As accelerators get faster, this intermediate path becomes the bottleneck.

This is where RDMA matters. RDMA stands for Remote Direct Memory Access. In simple terms, it’s a way for one device to read and write data directly to another device’s memory without much CPU involvement. Typical data movement has the CPU receiving, processing, and forwarding data, while RDMA lets the network device access memory directly, cutting unnecessary copies and CPU involvement. In environments like AI clusters where thousands of devices keep exchanging data, this difference is large. The CPU doesn’t have to spend time on data transport, and the accelerator gets the data it needs faster.

Google’s TPU Direct RDMA and TPU Direct Storage are technologies in this direction. TPU Direct RDMA lets data move directly between TPU HBM and the NIC, bypassing host CPU and DRAM. TPU Direct Storage enables direct memory access between high-speed storage like Managed Lustre and the TPU. Google explained that this structure reduces host bottlenecks, increases bandwidth for massive data transfers, and keeps the MXU busy when handling large multimodal datasets.

This shift matters for inference too, not just training. In long context and agent workloads, KV cache grows. You can’t keep all KV cache in the most expensive HBM forever. Frequently used cache stays in nearby memory, and less frequently used cache goes down to lower tiers like RAM, Local SSD, GCS, or Lustre, then gets pulled back when needed. Google announced Automatic KV Cache storage tiering in GKE, with RAM offload reducing time to first token by more than 40% and improving throughput by 50%, and Local SSD offload improving throughput by nearly 70% based on a 50K system prompt.

Z4M is a side piece of this layer. While Managed Lustre is Google’s complete high-speed storage offering, Z4M is a server type that lets customers or storage software companies build their own high-performance storage clusters on Google Cloud. It provides large local SSDs and RDMA cluster configurations to run parallel file systems like VAST Data or Sycomp. Large AI customers may want different storage structures by workload, so Google keeps both managed storage and custom storage options open.

This layer can’t separate storage from data path. In training, datasets and checkpoints have to move quickly. In inference, KV cache and long context data have to come back up quickly. Fast storage matters, but more importantly, the data has to get from CPU and DRAM to accelerator memory without being blocked along the way.

If this supply chain is slow, expensive TPUs and GPUs wait. If it’s fast, the same chips can handle more training and more tokens. The next bottleneck drops down to the physical infrastructure that has to actually house all this equipment.

Layer 6. Physical infrastructure: Power, cooling, racks, data center construction

The last layer is the physical data center. No matter how good the accelerators and networks are, if you can’t supply power, can’t pull heat out, and can’t install the rack, the cluster doesn’t turn on.

The first problem you hit in an AI data center is power density. Past data center racks were designed at the level of a few kW to a few tens of kW. Today’s AI racks already cross 100 kW, and next-generation systems like NVIDIA Rubin Ultra go into megawatt territory. As single accelerator power grows and the number of accelerators and network gear in a rack grows together, rack-level power demand climbs fast.

Looking at it just as “using more electricity” misses the picture. Pushing more power into the same space causes problems with current, heat, power conversion loss, cable thickness, and busbar design. If you distribute power within the rack at low voltage like before, the current gets too high, and losses and heat go up. So in next-generation AI racks, higher voltage DC power distribution matters more.

Here higher voltage DC power distribution comes in. Power is the product of voltage and current. To send the same power at higher voltage, the current needed drops. When current drops, power loss and heat drop, and the same power can travel through a thinner, more efficient path. To get AI racks into the megawatt range, attaching bigger power supplies isn’t enough. The power distribution structure inside the rack itself has to change.

Two camps split here. Google published a ±400 VDC-based high-voltage DC distribution spec at OCP, and NVIDIA is pushing an 800 VDC architecture as the center of its next-generation AI factory power ecosystem. The notation differs but the direction is the same high-voltage DC, and power infrastructure companies like Vertiv and Eaton align with both flows. Google emphasizing integrated power management and watt-per-performance improvements in this announcement fits the same context. In AI infrastructure, system-level power efficiency matters as much as chip performance. Accelerators, host CPUs, IPUs, memory, network, and cooling all share power within the same rack, so the power supply and conversion structure determines cluster efficiency.

The second problem is cooling. Power eventually turns into heat. As rack power climbs, air cooling alone hits limits. Air has limited capacity for moving heat, so it can’t sufficiently cool high-density accelerator racks. So next-generation AI data centers are moving to liquid cooling.

The most realistic first step is liquid direct-to-chip. This is a method where coolant runs near a cold plate on top of the chip, pulling heat directly from the source. It gets much closer to the heat source than blowing air from far away, so it can handle higher power density in the same space. Immersion cooling is discussed as a next step, but in large-scale hyperscaler environments, direct-to-chip likely deploys widely first due to maintenance, standardization, and reliability issues.

Google publishing the Project Deschutes line of liquid cooling specs through OCP is part of the same flow. AI racks can no longer be designed differently by each company. Rack power, coolant flow, CDU (Cooling Distribution Unit), cold plate, manifold, and serviceability need to be standardized to a degree to deploy quickly at hyperscaler scale. That’s why NVIDIA’s rack-scale systems like GB200 and GB300, and Google’s next-generation AI infrastructure, are converging on the same direction of cooling standards.

The third problem is power supply to the entire data center. Even if you distribute power well within the rack and cool the chips well, if the external grid can’t pull in enough power, the data center can’t run. AI cluster power demand is growing even on a single-building basis, and multi-gigawatt scale doesn’t fit on a single site. So the criteria for choosing data center sites is shifting from network latency or land price to grid capacity and power connection feasibility.

At this point, the time axis of AI infrastructure shifts. Chips can move on 18 to 24 month cycles for design and production, but grid expansion, substations, transmission lines, cooling equipment, and data center construction take longer. Even if AI chip supply opens up, if grid connection and construction capacity can’t keep up, actual deployment slips. Physical infrastructure is the slowest layer in the AI capex cycle.

Finally, construction capacity is also a problem. AI data centers aren’t scaled-up server rooms. High-density power distribution, liquid cooling pipes, CDU, heat rejection, backup power, switchgear, transformers, and fiber routing all have to be designed together. Mechanical and electrical construction get much more complex, and the contractors and skilled workforce that can build this at scale are limited. Physical infrastructure bottlenecks aren’t problems you solve by buying more equipment.

The last bottleneck is the most physical. If electricity doesn’t come in and heat can’t leave, even the most expensive chip is just turned-off silicon. The AI race starts with software and silicon, but in the end the physical data center sets the speed.

Hyperscaler and NVIDIA stack strategies through the AI infrastructure 6 layer view

When you place five companies on the same 6 layers, it becomes clear who has aligned how far and where each depends on external partners.

It’s a map for seeing which layer of the AI infrastructure stack each company directly controls and which layer it leaves to external partners. Five industry trends emerge from the table.

Google has the most visibly listed external partners across all 6 layers among hyperscalers.

AWS has a strategy of going almost entirely with in-house design across Layers 2 to 5 since acquiring Annapurna Labs, so external partner exposure is low. Microsoft is strong on the software side (Foundry, Copilot Studio) but has high NVIDIA dependence in accelerators and the data path. Maia is moving toward self-sufficiency as it ramps, but it’s not at the stage of replacing NVIDIA. Meta runs a multi-vendor portfolio of MTIA + AMD MI450 + NVIDIA + in-house Arm CPU on the infrastructure side, and with the April 2026 release of Muse Spark, the software strategy expanded to closed-source models. NVIDIA isn’t a hyperscaler but responds by building its own seat in all 6 layers on top of GPU.

Layer 2 (Compute complex) became multi-tiered across all 5 companies at the same time.

ARM-based in-house host CPUs are deployed across all 4 hyperscalers. Graviton, Axion, Cobalt, Vera. In the same seat, IPU/SmartNIC also entered through different solutions per company. AWS Nitro, Microsoft Azure Boost DPU, NVIDIA BlueField, Google + Intel IPU collaboration. Shipping a single accelerator chip alongside a host CPU + IPU has become the universal pattern.

Layer 5 (Storage + Data path) is aligning across all 5 companies in the host bypass direction.

TPUDirect, EFA, GPUDirect, and Azure GPUDirect RDMA aren’t exactly the same technology layer, but the direction of reducing host CPU and DRAM involvement and connecting accelerator/NIC/storage more directly is common. The flow of parallel file system managed services becoming standard above is the same. AWS FSx Lustre, Azure Managed Lustre, and Google Managed Lustre all converged on Lustre. The standard for the AI data path has solidified in one direction across all hyperscalers.

Layer 6 (Physical) is the clearest standard convergence point.

Google published a ±400 VDC-based high-voltage DC distribution spec at OCP, and NVIDIA is pushing an 800 VDC architecture as the center of its next-generation AI factory power ecosystem.

The notation differs but the direction is the same high-voltage DC, and power infrastructure companies like Vertiv and Eaton align with both flows. Add Meta publishing the OCP Helios rack-scale architecture at OCP together with AMD, and the rack design for AMD MI450’s first 1GW deployment lands on top of OCP too. OCP is becoming the governance layer where hyperscaler next-generation data center standards converge.

Multi-vendor diversification in accelerator design channels is happening simultaneously at all 4 hyperscalers.

Google has a 4-vendor setup with the training design partner + MediaTek + Marvell discussions + Intel IPU, AWS distributes Trainium design across Marvell + Alchip + GUC and others, Microsoft uses multiple partners for Maia, and Meta co-develops MTIA with external partners while running AMD MI450 custom + NVIDIA GPU + in-house Arm CPU in a multi-vendor portfolio.

The trend of the ASIC design services market expanding through the multiplication of hyperscaler × chip line × vendor is happening across the entire industry.

Where do bottlenecks form, and where are the investment opportunities

When 6 layers move as one cycle, if one layer gets blocked, the ramp-up of other layers shakes too. On the flip side, when one layer opens up, the cycles of other layers swell together. So when tracking the industry cycle, it matters where the ceiling is and where the opening is. Five spots emerge.

Bottleneck 1. ASIC design services market: hyperscaler × chip line × vendor multiplication

The meaning of Google extending TPU design from a single channel to multiple channels goes beyond supply chain diversification. As each design partner takes a different segment of chip, a cycle begins where the hyperscaler ASIC market expands as multi-vendor. Training chips go to existing design partners, inference chips to MediaTek, additional chip designs in discussion with Marvell, and IPU co-developed with Intel.

The same flow shows across hyperscalers. Meta runs the MTIA roadmap (Gen 4: 300/400/450/500) on a release cycle every 6 months while simultaneously running an AMD MI450 custom 6GW contract, several million NVIDIA GPU chips, and in-house Arm CPU as a multi-vendor portfolio. AWS distributed Trainium design across Marvell, Alchip, GUC, and others, and Microsoft also uses multiple design partners for Maia.

From the design partner’s view, taking different chip lines from one hyperscaler builds revenue stability, and taking chip lines from other hyperscalers (AWS Trainium, Microsoft Maia, Meta MTIA, AMD custom) lets multi-channel cycles ramp at the same time. The starting point is that the ASIC design services market expands not as the sum of one company’s one chip but as the multiplication of vendor × chip × hyperscaler.

Add to that the fact that host CPU and IPU are also tying into the accelerator cycle. A single accelerator ramp swells the three axes of accelerator + host ARM CPU + IPU at the same time. The fast growth of Arm adoption in hyperscaler in-house CPUs is the direct sign of this. Companies to watch in this layer are ASIC design services companies (Marvell, MediaTek, Alchip, GUC), Arm IP licensing, and IPU/SmartNIC design services.

Bottleneck 2. HBM mass production capacity: bonders and their supply chain

As the memory bottleneck shifts from a simple HBM capacity issue to a question of how to place data across multiple tiers like on-chip SRAM, HBM, host RAM, SSD, and parallel file systems, the segments of the memory industry have diversified. That said, on the mass production capacity side, the HBM bottleneck doesn’t sit in one place. DRAM wafer capacity, TSV, stack, packaging, test, and customer qualification are all locked up across the advanced packaging supply chain at the same time.

In HBM4, the possibility of hybrid bonding being delayed beyond expectations has grown. JEDEC’s thickness relaxation and microbump process improvements let HBM4 go further on microbump. But as we move into 16-high, 20-high, and HBM5 generations, bump pitch, stack height, thermal, and yield problems grow again, so hybrid bonding isn’t a vanished transition point but a delayed one. Hybrid bonding equipment is concentrated in a few suppliers, and as this transition kicks in, cleaning, deposition, inspection, and metrology become important alongside bonding tools. The HBM mass production capacity bottleneck spreads across the entire advanced packaging supply chain rather than concentrating on a single bonder company.

Memory company share fights are zero-sum, but the bonder and advanced packaging seats are positive-sum, scaling with the entire market ramp. Companies to watch in this layer are hybrid bonding equipment leaders, advanced packaging equipment megacaps, and HBM inspection/test equipment companies.

Bottleneck 3. Connectivity: the multiplication effect of accelerator SKU diversification

If NVIDIA expands lines with Rubin + Rubin CPX + additional accelerator SKUs and Google goes from 8t/8i to a 9th-gen prefill-dedicated chip, having 3 to 4 different types of accelerator chips in one inference system becomes standard. KV cache flows between chips with every inference request.

When accelerator SKUs grow from 1 to 4, chip-to-chip links grow from 1 to 6 (n(n-1)/2 when n=4). The growth of accelerator types acts as multiplication on the connectivity market. Add to that the simultaneous transitions of PCIe Gen5 to Gen6 and 800G to 1.6T optics, and the cost per link rises too. The trend of host CPU networking burden shifting to IPU/SmartNIC happens in the same cycle, and connectivity component usage jumps at once. Three effects enter the same cycle simultaneously.

In optical components, there’s an additional variable. As we move from 800G to 1.6T, both the cost per transceiver and the size of the optical components market expand at the same time. Add OCS, a new category, climbing into the optical switching standard seat, and a separate segment gets added. NVIDIA’s $2 billion investments in Coherent and Lumentum in 2025 to lock in capacity is a move to strategically secure future optics capacity. Companies to watch in this layer are PCIe retimer companies, AEC companies, 800G/1.6T optical transceiver companies, and OCS MEMS component companies.

Bottleneck 4. Reliability and RAS: a newly rising hidden layer

There’s a reason you can’t dismiss the 97% goodput number as a bragging point about stability. In a 100K (100,000) chip fabric, chip failures, broken links, memory errors, and thermal throttling are no longer exceptions. With something always broken somewhere, having work continue is the normal operating condition.

The real difference in next-generation AI infrastructure shifts from peak FLOPS to the ability to quickly detect, isolate, and route around faults while keeping work going. From this view, OCS-based automatic rerouting, fault telemetry, RAS circuits, and automatic diagnosis technologies are all rising as a hidden infrastructure layer. To run a 100K chip fabric, these capabilities become more critical variables than chip performance.

From an investment perspective, two seats are worth watching. The first is OCS and optical switching components. As a core mechanism of fault recovery, they’re likely to take up a bigger seat inside hyperscaler fabrics. The second is advanced test/diagnosis equipment. The ability to quickly diagnose which chip failed how in a 100K chip fabric determines data center operating cost. This seat used to be classified just as memory/logic test market, but it’s climbing on top of the AI infrastructure cycle as a separate category.

Bottleneck 5. 800V DC + liquid cooling OCP standard convergence

Two standard convergences are happening simultaneously in Layer 6. NVIDIA officially announced the 800 VDC architecture in May 2025, and Vertiv and Eaton entered as power infrastructure partners on the same standard. Eaton officially announced Beam Rubin DSX, an NVL72-direct integrated module, at NVIDIA GTC 2026 (March 16). Google published a ±400 VDC-based high-voltage DC distribution spec at OCP, aligning in the same direction with different notation. At the same time, liquid cooling converges on the same OCP. Google published the Project Deschutes line of liquid cooling specs through OCP, and 5th-gen Project Deschutes-based CDU products are coming up in the OCP ecosystem too. nVent is listed as a reliable provider of CDU mass production based on that spec, and the same company also goes into NVIDIA GB200/GB300 cooling. Add Meta + AMD publishing the OCP Helios rack-scale architecture at OCP, and the rack design standard for AMD MI450’s first 1GW deployment also lands on top of OCP.

Two things become clear here. The power and cooling of next-generation data centers run on top of OCP standards. Centered on data centers using NVIDIA rack-scale systems, the high-voltage DC standard spreads, and the adoption of OCP-listed standard-compliant solutions multiplies by the number of hyperscalers. And OCP is becoming the governance layer where hyperscaler next-generation data center standards converge. Google’s ±400 VDC and Project Deschutes cooling, Meta + AMD Helios rack-scale, and Falcon all get published through OCP, structuring a place where the 4 hyperscalers meet on the same standards.

Companies to watch in this layer are liquid cooling leaders (Vertiv as market #1, nVent as OCP Deschutes listed), high-voltage DC power infrastructure (Eaton’s Beam Rubin DSX, Vertiv’s 800 VDC portfolio), and CDU (Cooling Distribution Unit) companies. On top of that, the most fundamental ceiling is power supply itself. US hyperscaler data center site decisions are already determined by grid capacity, not chips. With major utility PPA requests sitting in 5-year waiting lines, seats like onsite power generation, microgrids, and transformers are rising as new investment categories.

What pictures are other hyperscalers painting

Google publishing a full stack at one event on April 22 is a signal that other hyperscalers will repaint the same picture in their own colors. AWS has its slot at re:Invent in late November every year, Microsoft at Ignite in November every year, and NVIDIA at GTC in spring and fall. Points I want to confirm at each company:

AWS: The extreme of full-stack ASICs in-house

AWS has the highest self-sufficiency of the five. After acquiring Annapurna Labs in 2015, the strategy is to go almost entirely with in-house design across Layers 2 to 5, and Trainium, Graviton, Nitro, EFA, and FSx Lustre are all in-house stack. Points I want to confirm at the next re:Invent:

  • L2: Trainium3 mass production ramp in earnest, Graviton5 (192 cores) production, the next generation of Nitro. Whether the share of Annapurna in-house design grows further

  • L4: EFA Gen 4 (synced with the 1.6T optical transition), the next-generation evolution of Nitro-based network offload

  • L5: Additional jump in FSx Lustre throughput, S3 Express expansion, depth of vector workload integration

  • L6: Standardization degree of in-house cooling and in-house power. Whether the NVIDIA 800 VDC standard partially applies to GPU instances

The AWS message will likely be about driving token cost lower through its own stack without NVIDIA dependence. The weakness of this strategy is that the software stack (Bedrock, Nova family, AgentCore) has weaker mindshare compared to Google Gemini Enterprise or Microsoft Foundry. Nova is a Bedrock-only model lineup filling all the Micro/Lite/Pro/Premier price tiers, propping up Bedrock’s price competitiveness, but the frontier model seat still depends on Anthropic Claude. The picture of catching up in the enterprise agent platform seat will get stronger.

Microsoft: Reducing NVIDIA dependence while booking revenue through software

Microsoft is strongest on the software side. With Azure AI Foundry, Copilot Studio, and AutoGen, it competes directly with Google Gemini Enterprise in the enterprise agent seat. On the infrastructure side, NVIDIA dependence is the highest. Most OpenAI workloads run on NVIDIA GPUs, and even as Maia ramps, it’s not at the stage of replacing NVIDIA. Points I want to confirm at the next Ignite:

  • L2: Maia 200 mass production, Cobalt 200 (Neoverse CSS V3, 132 cores) production, the next generation of Azure Boost DPU. The pace of reducing NVIDIA dependence

  • L4: Azure HPC fabric, InfiniBand Gen 8 mass production, Spectrum-X integration

  • L5: Azure Managed Lustre expansion, NetApp Files successor, possibility of an in-house parallel file system

  • L6: The degree of immersion cooling moving into earnest (Microsoft was the first to attempt immersion in this space)

The Microsoft picture is a hybrid strategy of booking revenue through software while gradually reducing NVIDIA dependence in infrastructure. Maia replaces NVIDIA in part, but most OpenAI workloads run on NVIDIA, so they run both sides.

NVIDIA: A response of building all 6 layers on top of GPU

NVIDIA isn’t a hyperscaler but it responds by building its own seat across all 6 layers. CUDA + AI Enterprise + NIM (L1), Vera Rubin + Rubin CPX + Vera CPU + BlueField (L2), HBM4 + GDDR7 (L3), NVLink + Spectrum-X + InfiniBand (L4), DGX SuperPOD reference architecture + GPUDirect Storage/RDMA (L5), 800 VDC architecture + Vera Rubin DSX reference + Eaton Beam Rubin DSX (L6). Points I want to confirm at the next GTC:

  • L2: Rubin Ultra ramp (2027), Rubin CPX (long context inference, with strong prefill character at the center of context processing) mass production ramp

  • L3: HBM4E adoption, GDDR7 in earnest, CXL 3.0 exposure

  • L4: Spectrum-X Ethernet in earnest, NVLink Gen 6

  • L6: 800 VDC architecture mass production shipments in earnest, Eaton/Vertiv collaboration expansion, possibility of immersion cooling standards

What NVIDIA targets isn’t selling GPUs alone but a platform strategy of locking in all hyperscalers through full-stack reference architecture. DGX SuperPOD, NVL72, and Vera Rubin NVL144 CPX rack are all full-stack references, and when a hyperscaler installs NVIDIA, every component going in alongside aligns with the NVIDIA standard.

Meta: In-house infrastructure + multi-vendor portfolio + software strategy shift

Meta sits in the most interesting seat. While other hyperscalers vertically integrate around in-house ASICs, Meta is going multi-vendor portfolio. Looking only at the infrastructure deals announced in 2026, the direction is clear.

The 5-year 6GW contract with AMD in February 2026 (estimated around $60 billion based on reporting). MI450-based custom GPU + EPYC Venice CPU + ROCm software + AMD Helios rack-scale architecture in one bundle. The first 1GW deployment starts in 2H 2026. Add to that millions of NVIDIA GPU chips deployed, in-house Arm CPU design, AWS Graviton being introduced for selected agentic workloads (April 2026), and external GPU rentals from CoreWeave and Nebius.

The in-house MTIA roadmap also runs on a cycle of new chip releases every 6 months (MTIA 300/400/450/500), with a 2nm process-based multi-gigawatt rollout planned through 2029. A significant portion of the $115 billion to $135 billion 2026 capex guidance (including finance lease) goes into AI infrastructure.

There’s a shift on the software side too. Meta Superintelligence Labs released a new model, Muse Spark , breaking from the Llama open-weight tradition and going closed model. Muse Spark is a multimodal reasoning model that went into the Meta AI app and meta.ai, with API offered as private preview to select partners.

After the Scale AI investment ($14.3B, 49% stake) and Alexandr Wang’s appointment as the first Chief AI Officer, this model fits naturally with the flow of Meta reorganizing its superintelligence team. At least based on Muse Spark, Meta’s frontier model strategy appears to have moved from open-weight-centric to closed-model-centric. That said, how Muse and Llama will run in parallel going forward hasn’t been confirmed in any public statement. PyTorch remains an open-source framework as is.

Points I want to confirm in the next Meta announcement: deployment scale of MTIA 400/450/500 (especially the timing of 2nm process mass production in earnest), the actual operating timing of AMD MI450’s first 1GW deployment, the spread of Helios rack-scale OCP standards, the workload split between AMD vs. MTIA within the multi-vendor portfolio, and whether the next Muse series model maintains the closed-source stance or returns to partial open-weight. Meta shifted software strategy, but in the seats of accelerator + memory + network + data center standards under OCP governance, it still moves the most actively.

Availability split: when does what become usable

When tracking the industry cycle, what often confuses people is whether something is usable now or just announced. In Google’s announcement this time, the timing varies item by item.

What this table shows is clear. The two years of 2026 to 2027 are the window where step-function transitions happen simultaneously across all 6 layers. Accelerator multi-channel design alignment, HBM4 mass production in earnest, simultaneous PCIe Gen6 + 800G/1.6T transitions, host bypass data path standardization, and 800 VDC + liquid cooling OCP standardization all bundle into the same 24-month window. These transitions don’t happen separately. They come in together at the same time, lifting the entire AI infrastructure industry up a step.

Closing

When Google laid out the entire AI infrastructure picture on April 22, that was more than a victory lap on its own stack. It was a reference map for where the whole industry needs to go. The more layers you align inside a single company's stack, the more room you have to bring token costs down. That is the core message of this announcement.

This piece walked through that picture across six layers: the path each hyperscaler is taking, the five bottlenecks that show up along the way, and the 24 to 36 month timeline that ties them together. When the next round of hyperscaler announcements comes, lining them up against this framework should make it clearer where each piece fits in the cycle and where the real investment opportunities live.
