Earnings: surface numbers and what’s underneath

Headline-wise, this print wasn’t bad.

Revenue $10.6B, Non-GAAP EPS $2.65. Versus prior guidance, revenue landed mid-range and EPS at the high end. Slightly above Street consensus. As a standalone print, it’s a clean beat.

The reason the market initially turned cold was also clear. Next quarter’s guide came in light. Q3 revenue was set at $9.2B to $10B, below the StreetAccount consensus of $10.19B. The weakness was concentrated in one place. Handsets.

Handsets revenue was $6.024B, down 13% year over year. Memory tightness, rising LPDDR and NAND prices, and softer demand from Chinese OEMs all hit at once, pressuring premium smartphone shipments and mix. The largest contributor to Qualcomm’s revenue still hasn’t bottomed. The market reflexively selling first was natural enough.

So why did the stock turn around?

A while back, I mapped the CPU cycle into six scenarios and flagged QCOM as the main investment bet in two of them. One was AI inference explosion, the other was Auto SoC. What’s striking is that this print touched both at the same time.

What that one line actually means

The sentence in the earnings release was this.

“We are equally excited by our entry into the data center, where a leading hyperscaler custom silicon engagement is on track for initial shipments later this calendar year.”

The phrase to focus on is custom silicon engagement. It can mean at least three different things.

Scenario A: Standard rack purchase.

The hyperscaler buys AI200/AI250 as off-the-shelf product and integrates it into its own data centers. Same structure as the HUMAIN 200MW deal. In this case revenue math is straightforward: ASP times volume.

Scenario B: Semi-custom derivative.

The hyperscaler hands over its workload spec, and Qualcomm builds a derivative chip on top of the AI200/AI250 base, customizing some blocks. NRE (non-recurring engineering) revenue gets layered in, and margin runs better than standard. Design-win lock-in extends.

Scenario C: Pure custom ASIC.

The hyperscaler provides the full chip spec, and Qualcomm composes a customer-specific ASIC by combining its IP blocks. This is closer to the business model of established ASIC design service providers. Customer lock-in and long-dated revenue visibility are far stronger than simple product sales. Amon himself used the phrase “multi-generation engagement” on the call. And it’s the case where keeping the customer’s name confidential is most natural.

In my read, the most likely answer sits somewhere between B and C. If it were a plain standard rack purchase, there’d be little reason to keep things anonymous. Deals with high PR value, like HUMAIN, usually go out with the name attached. Insisting on anonymity points to one of two things: (1) the hyperscaler doesn’t want to telegraph anything to competitors, or (2) it wants to position this carefully relative to its existing ASIC partnerships. Either case fits a customized engagement better than a standard product sale.

Amon also got more specific about timing in the prepared remarks. “We expect initial shipments in the December quarter.” That’s an entire quarter, not a single month, of meaningful shipments potentially starting. Investor Day on June 24 is roughly two months out. There’s a reasonable chance more detail on the engagement structure and the customer’s identity surfaces in that window.

Which leaves the question. Who is the anonymous hyperscaler.

The next sections work toward that picture step by step. Why LPDDR is central to this game, how the Alphawave and Ventana acquisitions form the skeleton of a customer-specific ASIC business, and most importantly, who that anonymous hyperscaler is most likely to be, drawing together what’s circulating in the field and what’s in the public record.

LPDDR and the ASIC full stack

Why LPDDR

When I first looked at Qualcomm AI200, the part that stood out as odd was the memory.

Say AI accelerator and most people picture HBM first. NVIDIA H200 carries 141GB of HBM3e, B200 carries 192GB of HBM3e. On bandwidth, HBM is overwhelming. Qualcomm AI200, though, went with 768GB of LPDDR per card. The capacity is far larger, but the choice broke from the conventional AI accelerator playbook. So the question naturally comes up: why LPDDR instead of HBM.

CES 2026 sharpened the answer when NVIDIA unveiled Vera Rubin. Vera CPU carries 1.5TB of LPDDR5X memory per card. Together with Micron and SK hynix, NVIDIA introduced a new memory module form factor called SOCAMM. LPDDR5X, mostly seen in mobile devices until now, was pulled into the data center server.

Looked at across the full NVL72 rack, the structure gets more interesting. The CPU side carries 54TB of LPDDR5X, the GPU side 20.7TB of HBM4. By capacity, LPDDR is 2.6x larger than HBM. Even NVIDIA doesn’t believe everything can be solved with HBM alone. HBM remains the fastest memory, but agentic AI and long context windows demand a larger memory tier.

This is where Qualcomm AI200’s choice reads differently.

AI200 carries 768GB of LPDDR per card. That’s far more memory capacity than H200 or B200. At launch, the spec might have looked unfocused. After Vera Rubin, the direction reads differently. Qualcomm isn’t going head-to-head with HBM. It’s staking out a different position: a high-capacity LPDDR-based inference accelerator.

That choice also lines up with the workload Qualcomm is targeting.

LLM inference splits into prefill and decode. Prefill processes the input prompt and builds the KV cache. This phase is compute-heavy. Decode generates tokens one at a time. There, memory capacity, memory access efficiency, and tokens per watt matter as much as raw compute.

In disaggregated serving, prefill and decode aren’t forced onto the same hardware. Each gets the hardware that fits it. The lane Qualcomm is targeting is closer to decode. Rather than going toe-to-toe with massive GPU clusters, the play is to carve out the inference segment where memory capacity and power efficiency matter most.

So LPDDR isn’t a cost-cut choice. From Qualcomm’s standpoint, it’s a way of pulling the memory ecosystem it knows best up into the data center. Qualcomm has spent years working on LPDDR controllers, PHYs, power management, thermal design, and the broader memory subsystem inside mobile SoCs. If NVIDIA is moving into this territory through SOCAMM, Qualcomm is closer to a company that already lives there.

AI250 pushes this direction one step further.

Qualcomm has said AI250 will boost effective memory bandwidth by more than 10x through a near-memory computing-based memory architecture. That 10x figure is less about physically multiplying raw pin bandwidth and more about cutting the data movement bottleneck on real workloads to lift effective throughput.

To pull that off, just stacking more LPDDR on the board isn’t enough. The physical distance between compute logic and memory has to shrink, the data paths have to get shorter, and the software has to align memory access patterns with the hardware structure. Near-memory computing isn’t a memory-capacity problem. It’s a problem where packaging, interconnect, compiler, and runtime all have to come together.

When I saw a job posting shared on LinkedIn back in February by a former Qualcomm manager, the part that caught my eye was right here. The role description bundled “3DIC and DRAM” together. That phrasing suggests Qualcomm isn’t just thinking about packing more memory onto a board, but is looking at shrinking the distance between logic and DRAM inside the package itself.

Of course, a single job posting alone doesn’t pin down product structure or tape-out timing. But seeing “3DIC,” “DRAM,” “diagnosis,” and “yield” appear together in one role hints that Qualcomm’s near-memory computing isn’t a marketing keyword. It’s tied to the actual problems of productization. Given that AI250 is targeting a 2027 launch, the hiring timing isn’t out of place either.

So Qualcomm’s LPDDR strategy has to be read in three stages.

First, AI200 sidesteps the HBM fight and stakes out a different position as a high-capacity LPDDR-based inference accelerator.

Second, that position fits well with decode-centric disaggregated serving.

Third, AI250 uses near-memory computing to compensate for LPDDR’s bandwidth ceiling at the packaging and architecture level.

Seen this way, AI200’s 768GB of LPDDR isn’t an odd choice. It’s Qualcomm carrying its mobile-side vocabulary of memory and power efficiency into data center inference. And Vera Rubin showed that this direction isn’t an isolated Qualcomm bet. It’s connected to the broader shift in memory hierarchy across AI infrastructure.

Alphawave and Ventana: the skeleton of an ASIC business

Looking only at AI200 and AI250, Qualcomm’s data center strategy reads like an inference accelerator story. But going to hyperscaler custom silicon takes more than building one good NPU.

A data center chip ultimately has to solve three things at once. Compute, memory, and data movement. No matter how good the compute core is, the product doesn’t ship if HBM, LPDDR, PCIe, Ethernet, and chiplet interfaces don’t connect properly. Hyperscaler custom silicon especially has to fit the customer’s rack architecture, network fabric, and memory hierarchy. What you need here is high-speed connectivity IP.

This is where the Alphawave acquisition matters.

Alphawave owns high-speed connectivity IP including SerDes, PHY, HBM controllers, UCIe, PCIe Gen6, and Ethernet SerDes. When you build an ASIC, the compute core is only one piece. To attach HBM, you need HBM PHY and controller. To go chiplet, you need a die-to-die interface like UCIe. To connect card to card and rack to rack, you need PCIe and Ethernet SerDes.

If you keep sourcing this IP externally, the differentiation in a custom silicon business weakens. Adapting fast to customer-specific structures gets harder, and some of the margin leaks out. The reason established custom silicon vendors hold this market isn’t just that they design chips on the customer’s behalf. It’s because they own deep SerDes and connectivity IP in-house.

Qualcomm buying Alphawave can be read as filling that gap. If AI200 and AI250 are Qualcomm’s compute-side entry, Alphawave is closer to the connective tissue that mounts those chips into the data center system.

Ventana fills a different gap.

Ventana is a RISC-V-based data center CPU IP company. It owns the Veyron, a server-class RISC-V CPU core. Putting a data center accelerator into a real system requires CPU IP for the host or control plane. Continuing to lean on ARM or x86 for that piece brings licensing costs and strategic constraints.

Qualcomm has already been through ARM and Nuvia litigation. So over the long run, having a CPU option that isn’t fully locked to ARM matters. RISC-V is open at the ISA level, which gives more flexibility to configure CPUs per customer in data center custom silicon. The Ventana acquisition secures that option.

Looked at this way, the direction of recent acquisitions is fairly consistent.

AI200 and AI250 provide Hexagon NPU-based inference compute. Alphawave fills in the connectivity layer with SerDes, PHY, UCIe, PCIe, and Ethernet. Ventana adds a RISC-V-based CPU option. Combined, Qualcomm has the basic ingredients to be in the conversation for data center custom silicon, not just as another accelerator vendor.

That doesn’t mean Qualcomm immediately stands shoulder to shoulder with the established custom ASIC players. The hyperscaler ASIC business takes customer relationships, software stack, validation, supply chain, and long-running production experience.

Which is why this piece matters in Qualcomm’s data center strategy. AI200 is a product, AI250 is the next product. Alphawave and Ventana are structural preparation for stretching beyond product sales into a customer-specific silicon business. This quarter’s one line suggests that preparation may already be at the customer engagement stage.

Who is the anonymous hyperscaler

Nothing past this point is officially confirmed.

What Qualcomm disclosed stops here. A custom silicon engagement is underway with an unnamed leading hyperscaler, and initial shipments are slated to start later this year. The customer’s name, the product structure, the shipment volume, and revenue recognition timing are all undisclosed.

So what follows is inference, drawing on public materials, each company’s existing silicon strategy, the direction of data center architecture, and what’s circulating in the field. It can be wrong. Until the company officially names the customer or fills in more detail at Investor Day, this should be read as a hypothesis.

The candidate set narrows to four. AWS, Google, Microsoft, Meta. Oracle could fit if you stretch the definition of hyperscaler, but given what’s known about its in-house AI silicon strategy and the lack of a clear connection to Qualcomm, the priority is lower. Chinese hyperscalers are unlikely given U.S. export controls and customer disclosure risk.

Starting with AWS, the odds look low. AWS already has Trainium and Inferentia, and has been running an in-house silicon group for years through Annapurna Labs. That doesn’t mean it never uses outside partners, but giving Qualcomm a major new custom silicon engagement seems less likely than the other candidates.

Google sits in a similar place. Google has the longest-running in-house AI accelerator line in TPU. TPU is already offered as a GCP product to external customers. It can’t be ruled out that Google would diversify partners, but it isn’t the cleanest fit for Qualcomm’s anonymous engagement.

Microsoft is a much more plausible candidate. Maia 100 is already public, and OpenAI plus Azure inference workloads put Microsoft in a position where it needs both internal silicon and external partner silicon. There’s a real motivation to reduce NVIDIA dependence, and at the same time the workload is too large to solve entirely in-house. There’s reason to keep things anonymous as well. Microsoft remains a strong candidate.

That said, my bet leans toward Meta.

The biggest reason is the nature of Meta’s workload.

Meta isn’t a cloud service provider, but it operates one of the largest data center fleets in the world. AI capex is massive, and inference demand is enormous. Ad ranking, feed ranking, Reels recommendation, Meta AI, and proprietary model services (Llama 4, Muse Spark) all require huge inference infrastructure. This isn’t a problem you solve simply by buying the fastest GPUs in volume. Latency, throughput per dollar, throughput per watt, and memory capacity all matter.

Qualcomm AI200’s positioning lines up with this. AI200 isn’t an HBM-based training accelerator. It’s a high-capacity inference accelerator with 768GB of LPDDR. The disaggregated serving and decode-oriented architecture Qualcomm talks about point in the same direction. A company like Meta, optimizing massive inference workloads across multiple tiers, has a real reason to keep a separate inference slice outside NVIDIA GPUs.

The second reason is network architecture.

Qualcomm AI200 and AI250 racks are built around PCIe scale-up and Ethernet scale-out. Meta has long been an aggressive user of Ethernet-based networking in large data centers. Unlike NVIDIA’s NVLink and InfiniBand-centric structure, Qualcomm’s approach is closer to fitting into the Ethernet infrastructure hyperscalers have already laid down. This applies to both Meta and Microsoft, but it reads more naturally on the Meta side.

The third reason is Meta’s drive to control its own AI stack.

Meta announced Llama 4 in April 2025, but the market reception was weak. In April 2026, Meta Superintelligence Labs unveiled a new proprietary model called Muse Spark, effectively overhauling the entire model lineup. As part of that shift, 2026 AI capex guidance was set at $115B to $135B. Roughly twice last year’s level.

At that capex scale, single-sourcing on NVIDIA means giving up control. Running proprietary models efficiently on proprietary infrastructure requires distributing the hardware backend. MTIA is the first answer, but on its own it can’t absorb all of this capex pressure. The space for a non-NVIDIA inference accelerator like Qualcomm sits in that gap.

The fourth reason is the existing relationship between Qualcomm and Meta.

The two companies have collaborated for years on consumer devices like Quest and Ray-Ban Meta. That alone doesn’t make Meta a data center ASIC customer. XR chips and data center chips are entirely different businesses. But the fact that the two have a working engineering, roadmap, and product collaboration relationship is a small plus. It’s a supporting reason, not a decisive one.

The fifth reason is Meta’s need to diversify hardware.

Meta runs huge AI infrastructure capex and is heavily NVIDIA-dependent. At the same time, it’s developing MTIA in-house. There’s a key counterargument here. Meta is already co-developing the next-generation MTIA with an existing ASIC partner across multiple generations. So if Qualcomm is Meta’s customer, the picture is unlikely to be Qualcomm replacing the MTIA mainline.

The more natural reading is dual-track. Meta keeps NVIDIA and its existing ASIC partner for training and general-purpose inference, and adds a separate silicon slice for specific inference workloads. Qualcomm could take the memory-heavy inference portion, the decode-oriented serving portion, or part of the edge-to-cloud AI stack. That doesn’t conflict with the existing MTIA partnership. It actually reads as Meta further segmenting its silicon stack by workload.

So my conclusion is this.

AWS and Google are unlikely. Microsoft is fully plausible. But based on public materials and the circumstantial picture so far, Meta looks most likely. Again, this is inference. Until the customer name is officially disclosed, it shouldn’t be treated as confirmed Meta.

From an investment standpoint, the customer name alone isn’t the most important variable. The bigger question is the nature of the engagement.

Whether this is a plain AI200 rack purchase, a semi-custom product based on AI200 or AI250, or a deeper custom ASIC engagement that uses Qualcomm’s Hexagon NPU, Alphawave’s connectivity IP, and Ventana’s RISC-V CPU IP, changes the meaning entirely.

A confirmed Meta name alone may produce a positive market reaction.

But the real re-rating will come from the engagement structure, not the customer name.

If this turns out to be closer to a customer-specific silicon platform than a standard product sale, Qualcomm gets repriced as a company entering the hyperscaler custom silicon supply chain, not just a vendor selling one product into the data center.

Risks and checkpoints

For this picture to hold, several things have to actually be proven. It also helps to lay out the signals that can confirm them in the near term. This market doesn’t reward announcements alone.

Software stack production validation

This is the largest underlying risk. AI200 and AI250’s hardware spec is interesting enough. 768GB of LPDDR, disaggregated serving, near-memory computing, Ethernet scale-out. The direction is good. But in data center accelerators, hardware spec alone doesn’t pull customer production workloads in. Real models have to run reliably, the compiler and runtime have to hold up, and PyTorch, vLLM, scheduling, profiling, and debugging all have to come along.

NVIDIA’s moat isn’t GPU performance alone. It’s CUDA and the software ecosystem. For Qualcomm to take a position in this market, it has to be overwhelmingly cheaper on specific workloads, dramatically more power-efficient, or fit cleanly into hyperscalers’ internal stacks. The signal to watch isn’t Qualcomm’s own benchmarks. It’s signals coming from customers or the developer ecosystem. References to actual production readiness on PyTorch, vLLM, Meta's proprietary models like Muse Spark, and disaggregated serving workloads matter most.

The nature of the initial shipment

Qualcomm said the leading hyperscaler custom silicon engagement begins shipping in the December quarter. But “initial shipment” is a wide term. It could mean small-volume customer qualification shipments, a limited pilot deployment, or the start of a meaningful volume ramp.

From an investment standpoint, what matters isn’t the fact of shipment itself. It’s whether the customer actually puts production workloads on it, whether next fiscal year’s guidance starts to show data center contribution, and whether this engagement is a single product sale or a multi-generation custom silicon relationship. The phrasing to watch in subsequent earnings calls is data center revenue contribution, customer ramp, and follow-on engagement.

June 24 Investor Day

The fastest confirmation trigger. Whether the customer name is disclosed matters, but the nature of the engagement matters more. Whether it’s an AI200 rack sale, a semi-custom product based on AI200/AI250, or a deeper custom silicon structure incorporating Hexagon NPU, Alphawave connectivity IP, and Ventana RISC-V CPU IP is the dividing line.

Whether the customer is Meta or Microsoft obviously matters too. But the customer name alone doesn’t complete the thesis. If it’s confirmed as Meta but only at the qualification level, the meaning is limited. If it’s confirmed as Microsoft but as a multi-generation custom silicon structure, the meaning is much larger.

The gap between handset erosion and the new revenue ramp

Qualcomm is still a handset company. The Apple modem revenue runoff, softer Chinese OEM demand, and rising LPDDR and NAND prices are still a drag. Auto and IoT are growing and the data center optionality has emerged, but they can’t immediately offset handset weakness. If a gap opens up between the Apple modem cliff and the data center ramp somewhere in 2027 to 2028, the stock could revert to the existing handset discount.

The company expects Chinese OEM revenue to bottom in Q3 and start a sequential recovery from Q4. If that scenario plays out, Qualcomm gets to absorb the data center and Auto optionality in a window where handset pressure is easing. If handset recovery slips beyond Q3, riding the data center expectation alone won’t be enough to hold the stock up.

NVIDIA’s pricing pressure on inference

Qualcomm is targeting inference, not training, and specifically the memory-heavy inference workload. NVIDIA is also pushing inference efficiency. If the Rubin generation lifts tokens per watt and tokens per dollar significantly and the pricing comes in aggressive, Qualcomm’s TCO advantage shrinks. If AI250’s near-memory computing doesn’t deliver a meaningful difference in production, the broader thesis weakens.

In the end, this thesis isn’t simply “Qualcomm enters the data center.” More precisely, it’s whether the data center and Auto ramp can become visible to the market faster than the handset erosion. The first answers start to come at the June 24 Investor Day and the December quarter shipment.

Closing

Not much is locked in yet. We don’t know if the customer is Meta or Microsoft. We don’t know if this is AI200-based, semi-custom, or a deeper custom ASIC structure. We don’t know if the December quarter shipment is qualification or volume ramp.

Even so, this print created a reason to look at Qualcomm again.

Until now, the market viewed Qualcomm as a handset modem and AP company. The question going forward is different. Can Qualcomm build a meaningful second and third P&L engine outside of handsets. Auto is already starting to show up in the numbers. Data center is entering its first validation window.

This quarter is not a conclusion. It’s the start of validation. Whether Qualcomm can move out of the handset discount gets decided at the June 24 Investor Day and the December quarter shipment.
